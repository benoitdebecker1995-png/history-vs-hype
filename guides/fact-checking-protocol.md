# Fact-Checking Protocol for History vs Hype

## Core Principle
**Maintain historical integrity above all else.** Educational content requires verified claims from credible sources.

## Primary Source Hierarchy

### Tier 1: Most Reliable
1. **Primary documents** - original treaties, census data, government archives
2. **Peer-reviewed academic publications** - university press books, scholarly journals
3. **Expert historians specializing in the topic** - cited extensively in literature

### Tier 2: Secondary Sources
4. **Respected journalists with expertise** - Thomas de Waal, Barbara Tuchman types
5. **International organization reports** - UN, ICJ, verified NGOs
6. **Declassified government documents** - with context about bias

### Tier 3: Use with Caution
7. **Credible news sources** - for modern events, always verify with multiple sources
8. **Documentary evidence** - photographs, footage (verify authenticity and context)

### Avoid
❌ Social media claims
❌ Propaganda from involved parties (without clearly labeling as such)
❌ Secondary citations without checking original source
❌ Wikipedia (use as starting point only, verify all claims)

## Modern Consensus Priority (2010-Present)

**For Tier 1-2 secondary scholarship:**
- Prioritize peer-reviewed publications from 2010-present
- Pre-2010 classic works: Note as "foundational" and verify against recent scholarship
- Academic consensus shifts over time - using modern scholarship ensures current understanding

**Timeless sources (no recency check):**
- Primary documents (treaties, archives, census data)
- Contemporary eyewitness accounts
- Statistical datasets

**When citing pre-2010 scholarship:**
- Check if recent work (2010+) has revised the thesis
- Note: "Scholar X's foundational 19XX work showed... though recent scholarship by Y (20XX) has qualified this by..."

**Counter-arguments:**
- Actively search for academic rebuttals (not just supporting evidence)
- Steel-man opposing views (strongest versions, not strawmen)
- Document where legitimate scholarly debates exist

## Literature Review for YouTube Videos (CRITICAL)

**You are NOT doing academic research. You are making educational YouTube videos.**

### What Academic Historians Do (NOT You):
- Read primary sources (treaties, archives, letters) firsthand
- Form original interpretations and arguments
- Contribute new knowledge to the field
- Spend months/years on a single topic
- Write peer-reviewed papers or dissertations

### What You Do (YouTube Research):
- **Find what historians have ALREADY concluded**
- **Use their analysis** (with proper citation)
- **Show primary sources on screen** as evidence (but rely on historians' interpretations)
- **Synthesize existing scholarship** into accessible video format
- **Produce content in days/weeks, not months/years**

### Example - The Right Approach:

**WRONG (Academic Research):**
> "I'm going to read the 1899 Anglo-Egyptian Condominium Agreement myself and form my own interpretation of what it means for Bir Tawil."

**RIGHT (YouTube Research):**
> "I'm going to find what historians like M.W. Daly and reports like Carnegie Endowment say about the 1899 treaty, then use their analysis. I'll show the treaty text on screen for evidence."

### Your Literature Review Process:

**Step 1: Search for Existing Analysis**
- Academic books that explain the topic
- Peer-reviewed articles analyzing the issue
- Credible reports (Carnegie, Brookings, etc.)
- Expert journalism from specialists

**Step 2: Extract Historians' Conclusions**
- What do experts say happened and why?
- What is the scholarly consensus?
- Where do historians disagree?
- Use NotebookLM to extract key findings

**Step 3: Use Primary Sources as Evidence**
- Show treaties, documents, maps on screen
- But cite historians' interpretation of what they mean
- Don't try to interpret complex legal/historical documents yourself

**Step 4: Cite Properly**
- "According to historian M.W. Daly..."
- "Carnegie Endowment's 2020 report found..."
- "The 1899 treaty states... [show on screen], which historians interpret as..."

## Visual Source Presentation (CRITICAL FOR CHANNEL BRAND)

**The channel's core differentiator:** Showing primary sources ON SCREEN, not just citing them verbally.

### Why This Matters

**Most history channels:**
- Narrator: "Historians say the USSR grew rapidly"
- B-roll: Stock footage of factories, generic historical images
- Problem: Viewer must trust narrator

**This channel:**
- Narrator: "Robert Allen analyzed Soviet GDP data showing 4.1x growth 1928-1970"
- B-roll: Display the actual GDP statistical table Allen used
- Result: Viewer sees the evidence, can evaluate the interpretation themselves

**This is "democratizing historical methodology":**
- Academics read primary sources in archives → You show them on screen
- Academics cite evidence in footnotes → You display evidence as B-roll
- Academics allow peer review → Viewers can verify claims visually

### Pre-Production Source Verification

**Before writing script, verify:**

1. **Can we display this source on screen?**
   - Treaties: Available as scanned documents? Readable?
   - Statistical data: Can we create clear table/chart?
   - Maps: High-resolution, archival quality?
   - Letters/documents: Legible text?

2. **If citing historian's conclusion, what did they analyze?**
   - Don't just show the historian's book cover
   - Show the underlying data/documents they used
   - Example: Allen's *Farm to Factory* → Show Soviet statistical tables, not his book

3. **Attribution standards:**
   - Source of document (which archive, library, database?)
   - Date of document
   - Verification it's authentic (not fake/misattributed)

### B-Roll Source Standards

**For each displayable source, document:**

**Primary Documents (Treaties, Laws, Government Records):**
- Archival source (e.g., "British National Archives, 1859")
- Document title/number
- Specific article or page shown
- Translation if not in English (show original + English subtitle)

**Example B-roll note:**
```
[0:55-1:12] Display 1859 Wyke-Aycinena Treaty, Article 7
Source: Guatemala National Archives
Show: Original Spanish text + English translation overlay
Highlight: "cart road" clause
```

**Statistical Data (Tables, Charts, Graphs):**
- Original source (government agency, academic study)
- Year(s) of data
- Create clean, readable visual (not tiny scanned table)
- Attribution on-screen (small text: "Source: Buringh & Van Zanden 2009")

**Example B-roll note:**
```
[6:15-6:30] Manuscript Production Data Table
Data: Buringh & Van Zanden (2009), "Charting the 'Rise of the West'"
Display: Clean table showing 24,191 (6th-7th c.) → 245,444 (8th-9th c.)
On-screen text: "915% increase"
```

**Maps:**
- Historical maps: Archival source, date created
- Modern maps showing historical borders: Source the boundary data
- Never use maps without attribution

**Photographs/Images:**
- Archive source (Wikimedia Commons, Library of Congress, etc.)
- Date photograph taken
- Verify authenticity (reverse image search, archival records)

### Integration with Script

**When writing scripts, include B-roll source notes:**

**Format:**
```markdown
[Narration text]

**[B-ROLL 2:15-2:30]: Display ICJ Statute Article 38**
- Source: UN International Court of Justice official text
- Show: Article 38 text on screen
- Highlight: "international custom, general principles"
- Attribution: Small text "Source: ICJ Statute"
```

**This ensures:**
- B-roll team knows exactly what to create
- Sources are properly attributed
- Visual evidence matches narration
- Viewers can verify claims

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Showing historian's book cover instead of their source data**
- Bad: "Allen says GDP grew 4x" → Show *Farm to Factory* book
- Good: "Allen analyzed Soviet data showing 4x growth" → Show the actual GDP table

❌ **Using stock footage when primary sources exist**
- Bad: "The 1859 treaty promised a road" → Show generic historical road image
- Good: "Article 7 promised a road" → Show actual treaty text

❌ **Citing sources without visual verification**
- Bad: "Statistics show..." (but no table displayed)
- Good: Show the statistical table on screen

❌ **Poor attribution**
- Bad: Just show document with no source label
- Good: Document + small on-screen text "Source: British National Archives, 1859"

### Quality Checklist

**Before filming, verify:**
- [ ] Every major claim has a displayable source identified
- [ ] B-roll notes specify exact document/data to show
- [ ] Sources are attributed with archival origin
- [ ] If citing historian, their underlying evidence is specified
- [ ] No claims rely on "trust me" (all visually verifiable)
- [ ] Maps/charts are readable on mobile screens
- [ ] Translation provided for non-English sources

### When Sources Can't Be Shown

**Sometimes sources genuinely can't be displayed:**
- Lost/destroyed documents (cite that fact)
- Classified materials
- Oral history/interviews
- Historiographical debates (interpretation, not documents)

**In these cases:**
- Clearly state why: "These records were destroyed in..."
- Use secondary sources: "According to historian X who accessed..."
- Be transparent about limitations

**But if core claim has no displayable evidence → reconsider including it.**

## Working with Limited Academic Literature

**Reality: Not every topic has deep academic coverage.**

### Topics with Rich Literature:
- Major wars (WWI, WWII, Vietnam)
- Colonial empires (British, French, Ottoman)
- Major territorial disputes (Israel-Palestine, Kashmir)
- Genocides (Holocaust, Armenia, Rwanda)

### Topics with Limited Literature:
- Minor territorial oddities (Bir Tawil)
- Obscure colonial boundaries
- Small-scale historical incidents
- Recent events (not yet studied by academics)

### When Sources Are Limited - Tiered Approach:

**Priority 1: Find What Exists**
- Search Google Scholar, JSTOR for ANY academic work
- Even 1-2 articles or book chapters are valuable
- Look for related topics (e.g., no Bir Tawil books, but Egypt-Sudan relations books exist)

**Priority 2: Use Credible Reports**
- Think tanks (Carnegie, Brookings, RAND)
- International organizations (UN, ICJ, AU)
- Government archives and official documents
- These are Tier 2 sources but acceptable when Tier 1 is limited

**Priority 3: Expert Journalism**
- Long-form journalism from specialists
- Documentary analysis
- Investigative reporting with sources cited
- Major outlets (BBC, Al Jazeera, NYT - when covering history/politics)

**Priority 4: General Context**
- Books on broader topics (colonial Africa, not specifically Bir Tawil)
- Use for background and framing
- Cite carefully ("In the context of colonial boundaries generally...")

### Minimal Viable Literature Review:

**For a credible 6-8 minute video, you need:**
- ✅ At least 1-2 academic sources (books or articles) on the topic OR closely related topics
- ✅ 1-2 credible reports/analyses (Carnegie, UN, etc.)
- ✅ Primary sources (treaties, documents) even if just shown on screen
- ✅ News coverage for modern aspects (verified from multiple outlets)
- ✅ Legal/definitional sources for framework (international law, etc.)

**Example - Bir Tawil (Limited Literature):**
- Academic: General Egypt-Sudan relations books, colonial Africa books
- Reports: Carnegie Endowment 2020 report (comprehensive)
- Primary: 1899 treaty text, maps
- News: TIME, Al Jazeera on Heaton claim
- Legal: Cornell Law on terra nullius definition
- **Result:** 6-8 sources total, credible video possible

**Example - Crusades (Rich Literature):**
- Academic: Multiple books on crusades, peer-reviewed articles
- Primary: Crusader chronicles (Fulcher of Chartres, Raymond d'Aguilers)
- Modern: Pete Hegseth news coverage
- **Result:** 10+ sources, very strong credibility

### When You Can't Find Enough Sources:

**Red flags that topic may not be viable:**
- Zero academic work on topic or related topics
- Only blog posts and social media claims
- Can't verify basic facts from multiple sources
- No primary sources available

**Solutions:**
1. **Reframe the topic** - Pivot to related topic with better sources
2. **Be transparent** - "Little academic research exists on X, but here's what we know..."
3. **Use hedging language** - "Reports suggest..." "Available evidence indicates..."
4. **Abandon the video** - If you can't verify core claims, don't make the video

### Documentation Requirements:

**For every video, document:**
- [ ] Academic sources found (even if limited)
- [ ] Why certain sources were used (if non-academic)
- [ ] Gaps in available research
- [ ] How you verified key facts despite limited sources

**Example note in research doc:**
> "Note: Very limited academic work on Bir Tawil specifically. Using Carnegie Endowment report (Tier 2) as primary analytical source, supplemented with general colonial boundaries books for context. All key facts verified across multiple sources."

## VidIQ Script Fact-Checking Process

### Step 1: Identify All Factual Claims
Go through VidIQ-generated script and highlight:
- Specific numbers (casualties, populations, dates)
- Historical events and their outcomes
- Cause-and-effect relationships
- Quotes from historical figures
- Legal or treaty provisions

### Step 2: Source Each Claim
For EVERY factual claim, find:
- At least 2 independent sources
- Preferably 1 academic source
- Original source if possible (not someone citing someone)

### Step 3: Flag Contested Information
When sources disagree:
- ✓ Present multiple perspectives clearly
- ✓ Explain why numbers/interpretations differ
- ✓ Use language like "Armenian sources claim X, while Azerbaijani sources claim Y"
- ✓ State scholarly consensus if one exists
- ✗ Don't present one side's numbers as fact

### Step 4: Verify with Claude/AI
- Feed sources to AI for cross-checking
- Ask AI to identify claims needing more evidence
- Have AI check for logical fallacies or unsupported conclusions
- Request verification of statistical claims

## Outdated Claims to Avoid

**Modern scholarship frequently revises old statistics and claims. Always check if commonly repeated figures have been updated.**

### Known Outdated Claims (Do NOT repeat):

**Justinian Plague (541-549 CE):**
- ❌ OLD CLAIM: "Killed 1/3 to 1/2 of Europe's population"
- ✅ MODERN SCHOLARSHIP: Significantly revised DOWN. Recent research (2019-2024) challenges the massive mortality figures
- **Why it changed:** DNA evidence and paleoclimatology studies show less widespread impact than Byzantine sources claimed
- **What to say instead:** "The Justinian Plague caused significant mortality in some urban centers, though recent scholarship debates the total impact"

**General Rule for Historical Statistics:**
- Statistics from sources written 100+ years ago need verification against recent scholarship
- Demographic claims from ancient/medieval sources are often exaggerated
- Check publication dates on your sources - pre-2000 figures may be outdated
- For disease/famine mortality: recent DNA, archaeological, and statistical modeling often revises old estimates

### Where This Shows Up:
- Plague mortality rates (Black Death, Justinian, etc.)
- Pre-modern battle casualties (often inflated)
- Colonial-era population estimates
- Ancient/medieval demographic data
- Famine death tolls (especially from biased contemporary sources)

**Best Practice:** When citing mortality statistics or demographic claims, check:
1. What's the most recent (last 10 years) scholarship?
2. Has DNA/archaeological evidence changed the picture?
3. Are you citing a primary source's claim or modern analysis?

## Red Flags to Watch For

### In Your Own Scripts
- Suspiciously precise numbers without clear source
- Absolutist language ("all historians agree," "always," "never")
- Conspiracy framing ("deliberate cover-up") without documentation
- Serious accusations (fraud, bias) without specific evidence
- Cherry-picked evidence that ignores counter-evidence

### In Sources You're Using
- Politically motivated sources without disclosure
- Citations that don't actually support the claim when checked
- Secondary sources that misrepresent primary sources
- Dated scholarship contradicted by recent research
- Sources with obvious agenda presenting one-sided narratives

## Specific Historical Topics - Key Considerations

### Nagorno-Karabakh / Armenia-Azerbaijan
- **Challenge:** Highly politicized with competing historical narratives
- **Required:** Primary sources (1823 survey, Ottoman records, Soviet archives)
- **Watch for:** Demographic numbers presented with false precision
- **Verify:** Academic fraud claims with original scholarship documenting it

### Armenian Genocide
- **Challenge:** Denial machinery creates false "controversy"
- **Required:** Rely on international historian consensus
- **Watch for:** False equivalency ("both sides" when one has evidence)
- **Verify:** Lobbying expenditure claims with official records

### Israel/Palestine
- **Challenge:** Contemporary political implications, strong partisanship
- **Required:** Multiple perspectives clearly labeled
- **Watch for:** Population statistics varying by date/boundaries used
- **Verify:** Specific historical events (evacuation orders, military operations) with archival sources

### Colonial History
- **Challenge:** Apologists vs. critics, both can cherry-pick
- **Required:** Primary colonial documents + indigenous perspectives
- **Watch for:** Minimization of atrocities OR exaggeration beyond evidence
- **Verify:** Specific policies (encomienda system) with legal documents and implementation records

## Comment Response Protocol

When someone challenges your facts:
1. **Never get defensive** - thank them for engagement
2. **Check their claim** - they might be right!
3. **If they're correct:** Acknowledge it, post correction
4. **If they're wrong:** Provide sources politely, don't be condescending
5. **If complex:** Acknowledge nuance exists, offer to research more

## Tools for Fact-Checking

### Research Databases
- Google Scholar (peer-reviewed articles)
- JSTOR (academic journals - need access)

- National Archives (various countries)

### For Modern Claims
- ProPublica (investigative journalism)
- Bellingcat (open-source investigations)

### For Primary Sources
- Library of Congress
- National Archives (US, UK, etc.)
- University repositories
- Declassified document databases

## Pre-Production Checklist

Before filming ANY video:
- [ ] Every number has a source
- [ ] Every quote is verified from original
- [ ] Contested claims are clearly labeled
- [ ] At least 2 sources for each major point
- [ ] Academic sources for historical interpretation
- [ ] Modern relevance connections are accurate
- [ ] No logical fallacies in arguments
- [ ] Bias acknowledged where it exists

## The Golden Rule
**If you can't verify it with credible sources, don't include it.**

It's better to have a shorter, fully verified video than a longer one with questionable claims that could undermine your credibility.
