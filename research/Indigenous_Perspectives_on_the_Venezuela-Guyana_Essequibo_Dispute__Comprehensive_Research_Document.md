### Timeline of Land Titling Process

**Colonial Era**:
- **1899** - Paris Arbitral Award (Essequibo to British Guiana)
- **1902** - Aboriginal Indians Protection Ordinance
- **1948** - Peberdy Report (recommended "Amerindian Districts" - never implemented)

**Post-Independence**:
- **1965** - Independence Agreement included land titling commitment
- **1967-1969** - Amerindian Lands Commission (recommendations never fully implemented; collective district title requests dismissed)
- **1976** - Amerindian Act 1976 passed; titles issued
- **1991** - Additional titles issued; APA established
- **2004-2007** - 17 communities titled, 6 extensions granted (74 → 91 titled communities)
- **2006** - **AMERINDIAN ACT 2006 PASSED** (primary current legislation)
- **2007** - Guyana endorsed UNDRIP (not legally binding; ILO 169 NOT ratified)
- **2013** - Amerindian Land Titling (ALT) Project commenced (USD $10.7-13.2M budget)
- **2019** - 21 demarcations completed, 18 titles issued
- **2020-2024** - 16,174 Amerindians gained land security; 15 villages demarcated, 10 titled
- **2024** - 16.48% of landmass titled to Indigenous peoples; Karasabai titled
- **2025** - $80M allocated for Amerindian Act revision (ongoing)

**Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

### Legal Status of Indigenous Land Under Guyanese Law

**Rights Titles Confer (Under Amerindian Act 2006)**:

1. **Communal Ownership** (Section 44): Inalienable—villages cannot dispose of land. Held communally by Village, administered by Village Council

2. **Management Rights** (Section 13): Manage and regulate use/occupation; sustainable use, protection, conservation; protect sacred/cultural sites

3. **Resource Use** (Sections 54-57): Residents use forest produce with Village Council permission; traditional rights over State lands/forests (subsistence, exercised sustainably); does NOT include mineral rights

4. **Consultation & Consent** (Sections 48, 55, 58):
   - Mining: Miners MUST obtain 2/3 consent at Village meeting; minimum 7% tribute (small/medium scale)
   - Forestry: Non-residents MUST obtain 2/3 consent
   - Protected Areas: NO protected area without Village consent
   - Violation = criminal offence

5. **Leasing - LIMITED** (Section 46): Max 10% of land; max 50-year term; 75% consent for non-residents

**CRITICAL LIMITATIONS**:

1. **Subsoil Resources Excluded**: Mineral rights remain with State; Indigenous peoples cannot control mining on titled lands

2. **Minister's Override Power** (Section 50): If Village refuses large-scale mining consent, Ministers can declare "public interest" and allow mining anyway

3. **Pre-Existing Concessions Remain**: "Save and except" clauses allow prior mining/logging to continue

4. **Individual Village Titles Only**: NO collective district titles allowed; described as "fragmentation of traditional territories"

5. **Insufficient Titled Areas**: 28 of 29 titled Villages (Regions 1-2) say title insufficient; 65% have settlements outside title; ALL have hunting/fishing grounds excluded

6. **No Protection for Untitled Customary Lands**: State can allocate to third parties without consent

7. **Weak FPIC Enforcement**: "None of the land titles were agreed through effective FPIC" (APA finding)

**Confidence Level**: CERTAIN (all provisions verified from Amerindian Act 2006)

## 4. INDIGENOUS MIGRATION HISTORY

### When and Why Different Groups Migrated

**General Timeline**:
- First human inhabitants arrived approximately 35,000 years ago, migrating from Asia
- Archaeological evidence confirms Indigenous presence from approximately 8,000-10,000 BCE
- **Confidence Level**: PROBABLE (archaeological estimates)

**Specific Migration Events**:

**WARAO - 1767 Mass Migration**:
- Originally inhabited Orinoco River delta (Venezuela)
- **1767**: "Great numbers of the Guarao migrated from the Orinoco region to the swamps of the Barima River of Guyana" due to "increasing harassment and ill treatment from the Spanish colonizers"
- This was Dutch-controlled territory at the time; remained after British takeover in 1803
- **Source**: Minority Rights Group, Williams (1928)
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**AREKUNA - After 1770**:
- Originally lived in upper regions of Caroni and Paragua rivers (Venezuela)
- **After 1770**: "Spanish Capuchin missions, with the support of the colonial authorities, began to forcibly resettle the Arekuna and other indigenous communities away from their traditional lands"
- Migrated into what is now Guyana as "late migrants"
- **Source**: Minority Rights Group
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**MAKUSHI & WAPISHANA - 1780s**:
- Fled to Guyana in the 1780s "to escape colonial resettlement policies in Brazil"
- Makushi occupied northern half of Rupununi Savannah; Wapishana the southern half
- **Source**: Minority Rights Group
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**WAIWAI - By 1837**:
- Migrated to Guyana Acarai Mountains "most likely because of pressure for resettlement from Portuguese missionaries in the Rio Branco region of Brazil"
- **Source**: Minority Rights Group
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

### Reasons for Migration

1. Harassment and violence from Spanish colonizers (Warao)
2. Forced resettlement by Spanish missions (Arekuna)
3. Escape from colonial resettlement policies in Brazil (Makushi, Wapishana)
4. Pressure from Portuguese missionaries (Waiwai)
5. Resource availability and environmental factors
6. Territorial expansion and population movements

**Confidence Level**: CERTAIN for documented migrations

### Historical Relationships with Colonial Powers

**DUTCH (1616-1814)**:
- Established first European settlement in 1616 - trading post 25km upstream from Essequibo River mouth
- Lokono (Arawak) employed by Dutch in fishing/salting operations and recapturing fugitive slaves
- By 1771, Spanish Governor reported Lokono "had already been working together with the Dutch for many years...including intermarriage"
- Dutch "pushed Indians out of their homelands" through superior military technology
- **Source**: Multiple historical sources
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**SPANISH**:
- Spanish colonization caused "almost unrelieved tragedy for the Indian"
- Spanish harassment of Warao led to 1767 mass migration
- After 1770, Spanish Capuchin missions forcibly resettled Arekuna
- Kalinago/Karinya peoples "resisted Spanish encroachment into the 'Wild Coast'...confirming their status as the de facto first defenders of the territory"
- **Source**: Historical scholarship
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**PORTUGUESE**:
- Portuguese missionaries in Brazil pressured Waiwai to migrate to Guyana by 1837
- Makushi and Wapishana fled Portuguese colonial resettlement policies in 1780s
- **Source**: Minority Rights Group
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**BRITISH (1796-1966)**:
- British takeover in 1796 was bloodless
- Indigenous people viewed as "protectors of the lands, or their borders, from claims by Spain and France"
- British believed Indigenous people "would be important as a form of security in times of slave uprisings"
- Lokono employed as laborers in sugar plantations
- Warao "encouraged to work on the estates"
- Indigenous peoples "divested of their traditional territorial rights and rendered 'cattle' that could be rounded up and consigned to reservations" (Williams, 1936)
- **Source**: Historical scholarship
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

### Pre-Colonial Territorial Concepts vs. Colonial Borders

**CRITICAL FINDING**: Indigenous territorial concepts were fundamentally different:

1. **Based on language and kinship** rather than fixed geographic lines
2. **Permeable and relational** - allowing for movement, trade, shifting alliances
3. **Not cartographically defined** - no written maps or surveyed boundaries
4. **Linked to resource use patterns** - seasonal movements, hunting/fishing territories
5. **Spiritual/cultural connections** to land rather than property ownership
6. **Transborder/fluid** - people moved across what became colonial borders

**Key Quote**: "For us there are no borders" - Thomas Devroy, former Akawaio village chief (AFP, 2023)

**Academic Analysis**: "Indigenous nations 'never adopted a nation state' but instead were conscripted into its 'conceptual arrays' of settler sovereignty, and domesticated by its 'state-created identities'"
- **Source**: University of Alberta research, citing multiple scholars
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

## 5. INDIGENOUS PARTICIPATION IN 1899 ARBITRATION

### CRITICAL VERIFICATION: Were Indigenous Peoples Consulted?

**ANSWER: NO - Indigenous peoples were NOT consulted or represented in the 1899 Paris Arbitration Tribunal**

**PRIMARY EVIDENCE**:

1. **Tribunal Composition**: Only 5 jurists (all European/American); NO Indigenous representatives
   - Baron Herschell/Lord Russell of Killowen (British)
   - Sir Richard Henn Collins (British)
   - Melville Weston Fuller (US, representing Venezuela)
   - David Josiah Brewer (US, representing Venezuela)
   - Frederic de Martens (Russian, presiding arbitrator)
   - **Source**: UN International Arbitral Awards, Vol. XXVIII (official treaty text)
   - **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

2. **Legal Framework**: 1897 Washington Treaty had NO provision for Indigenous participation; only "government agents and counsel" represented the parties
   - **Source**: 1897 Washington Treaty, Article V
   - **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

3. **Scholarly Confirmation**: "Remarkably, while busy presenting factual evidence at the Hague arbitral proceedings of the 1890s, neither Venezuela nor Great Britain 'acknowledged that […] imperialist initiatives had taken place and were continuing on the unceded territories of Indigenous nations'" (Bulkan, 2021)
   - **Source**: University of Alberta research, citing Bulkan (2021)
   - **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

4. **Legal Basis Excluded Indigenous Rights**: Arbitration was tasked to determine territories "belonging to, or that might lawfully be claimed by, the United Netherlands or by the Kingdom of Spain" - NO mention of Indigenous territorial rights
   - **Source**: 1897 Washington Treaty, Article III
   - **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

5. **Doctrine of Discovery**: Both Britain and Venezuela based claims on "Successor in title" through Dutch "effective occupation" or Spanish "first discovery" - rooted in terra nullius, treating Indigenous territories as "empty" lands
   - **Source**: University of Alberta research
   - **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**INSTRUMENTALIZATION, NOT REPRESENTATION**:
- Britain used Indigenous alliances as EVIDENCE for British claims ("trump card")
- Indigenous people were used as EVIDENCE, not consulted for CONSENT
- "In 1899, the Hague tribunal...used evidence that by the accepting British sovereignty, the traditional lands of those tribes were thus a part of British Guiana"
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

### Impact of 1899 Border on Indigenous Communities

**WARAO PEOPLE PARTITIONED**:
- "According to Williams (1928), 'Guaranos' is one of the many spellings for the Warau peoples...who because of the arbitrary border demarcations...have been partitioned, some 'under the government of Venezuela, and some under that of British Guiana'" (p.203)
- 1897 map shows "Guaranos" overlapping and transgressing the border
- **Source**: Williams (1928); University of Alberta research
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**AKAWAIO PEOPLE** live across Guyana, Venezuela, and Brazil
- Thomas Devroy: Essequibo was "the land of the Akawaio" Indigenous people
- **Source**: AFP interview, 2023
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**BROADER IMPACTS**:
- "National borders are 'impositions' which 'undermine the sovereignty of indigenous polities' by restricting transborder movements of Indigenous peoples" (Sullivan, 2022)
- Border disrupted traditional economies and transit systems
- Indigenous communities faced with incomplete land titles in disputed territory
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

## 6. CURRENT INDIGENOUS CONCERNS (2023-2025 ESCALATION)

### Specific Fears About Venezuelan Annexation

**Loss of Land Rights** (CERTAIN):
- Romario Hastings: "If [President Nicolás] Maduro has his way, it will jeopardize the steps we have already taken as Indigenous peoples, which have been years and years of struggle" (Mongabay, January 2024)
- Only 20 of 68 land demarcation cases completed 2013-2024
- 24-year legal battle in Upper Mazaruni for land recognition

**Loss of Livelihoods** (CERTAIN):
- Derrick John (NTC Chairman): "If Venezuela should have its way, then we will lose everything we have achieved, we have fought for and we have protected for centuries" (Climate Tracker, December 2023)
- Melena Pollard: "If Venezuela is to come over, that is going to put our lives into jeopardy, our livelihood into jeopardy" - concerns about mining jobs, fishing, river access (Climate Tracker, December 2023)

**Loss of Cultural Heritage** (CERTAIN):
- South Rupununi District Council: "Our ancestors are buried in those lands and there are petroglyphs marking the presence of our ancestors in the area" (Guyana Chronicle, December 2, 2023)
- Essequibo called "Chiipi Wa'o" in Wapichan

**Carbon Credits and Climate Programs Threatened** (CERTAIN):
- Rivers' View community using carbon credit funds from Hess Corporation deal for infrastructure
- Toshao Pollard fears Venezuelan control would jeopardize these programs
- **Source**: Climate Tracker, December 2023

### Economic Impacts: Mining, Oil, Environmental Degradation

**MERCURY CONTAMINATION** (CERTAIN):
- Parabara Village recorded highest mercury levels among Indigenous communities in Guyana
- 92% of Yanomami tested in areas closest to illegal mining had unsafe mercury levels
- Hair mercury levels in Parabara comparable to most contaminated communities in South America
- Mercury crosses placenta, exposing fetuses to concentrations up to 7x higher
- Mining camps near Marudi Mountain more than doubled from 20 to 50 camps (January-November 2024)
- Community leader: "Our health, our land, our water is life. And today it is all polluted….I am a mother for my community and for my youth, and I know they are at risk"
- **Source**: Rainforest Foundation US 2025; PMC study 2020; WHO findings
- **Communities Affected**: Parabara, Kako, Isseneru, Masakenari
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**MALARIA LINKED TO ILLEGAL MINING** (CERTAIN):
- Sharp rise in malaria cases "disproportionately affecting Indigenous communities across the Guiana Shield"
- Mining creates stagnant water where mosquitoes breed
- **Source**: Rainforest Foundation US 2025; CSIS report 2025
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**VENEZUELA'S PLANNED RESOURCE EXPLOITATION** (CERTAIN):
- December 2023: Maduro ordered "immediate creation" of divisions for oil and mining companies
- Instructed PDVSA to "immediately grant operating licenses for the exploration and exploitation of oil, gas and mines" in Essequibo
- Lennox Percy (Paruima Toshao): "Our way of living is sustainable. We don't want that kind of industry in this area" (Mongabay, January 2024)
- Venezuela's PDVSA has poor environmental record: 50+ year old infrastructure, rising oil spills, Lake Maracaibo pollution
- **Source**: Mongabay 2024; Observatory of Political Ecology of Venezuela 2022
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**EXISTING GUYANESE MINING IMPACTS** (CERTAIN):
- River and drinking-water contamination by mercury
- Decline of fishing and game
- Mario Hastings (Kako Toshao): "The [Guyanese] government proposes plans and implements them without our full participation...They continue to grant concessions, forest concessions and mining concessions, when we do not agree" (Mongabay, January 2024)
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**OFFSHORE OIL CONCERNS** (PROBABLE):
- ExxonMobil discovered 11 billion barrels offshore Essequibo in 2015
- Jean La Rose: "My community lives close to the coast. We depend on the sea for fish and crab...We don't know the effects that an oil spill would have" (Nia Tero, December 2023)
- **Confidence Level**: PROBABLE (expressed concerns, no documented impacts yet)

### Security Concerns: Violence, Families Fleeing, Border Threats

**VIOLENCE AGAINST INDIGENOUS GUYANESE IN VENEZUELA** (CERTAIN):

**January 2018**: Young Indigenous Guyanese miner from Hosororo tortured, had ears cut off, then beheaded by Venezuelan "Sindicato" mining group. Graphic video posted online. National Toshaos Council condemned "in the strongest terms, the murder of one of our indigenous sons"
- **Source**: Kaieteur News, January 2018

**Other Documented Killings**:
- October 2017: Davinda Saywack (Guyanese) killed execution-style
- July 2017: Three Guyanese miners killed: Vernon Eudoxie (51), Cologne Solomon (23), Samuel Moses (19)
- **Source**: Kaieteur News
- **Communities Affected**: Border communities in Region 1 (Barima/Waini)
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**VENEZUELAN GANG ACTIVITY NEAR BORDERS** (CERTAIN):
- May 2025: Indigenous leaders report "growing concern over Venezuelan gang activity near borders, a new threat after years of peace"
- Toshao Andre Mohabir fears "annexation or oil-related disruption could destroy their heritage"
- **Source**: Ashes on Air, May 2025; Stabroek News
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**MILITARY ESCALATION** (CERTAIN):

**March 1, 2025**: Venezuelan naval vessel entered Guyana's EEZ, approached ExxonMobil's platform; Guyana deployed military
- Indigenous response: "In Essequibo's indigenous villages, Arawak and Makushi residents expressed concern that their lands could face conflict" (Ashes on Air, May 2025)

**May 19, 2025**: NTC Chairman at conference: "These threats [are] driving fear in a lot of the communities, especially those on the border region"

**May 20, 2025**: GDF Chief to Toshaos: "I want you to be vigilant...we need your support" - called on border communities to share intelligence
- **Source**: News Room Guyana, May 2025
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**FEAR-DRIVEN DISPLACEMENT** (PROBABLE):
- December 2023: APA reported "some families have already moved away from their villages in search of security"
- However, APA noted this was "mostly due to a lack of access to credible information" and "information blackout from the government while misleading information was spread on TikTok"
- Jean La Rose posted message urging peoples to "remain in their homes and guard them"
- Lloyd Perreira: "We were very scared when we saw the referendum" (Al Jazeera, January 2024)
- **Source**: Mongabay, Al Jazeera, Nia Tero
- **Communities Affected**: Northwestern communities near Venezuelan border
- **Confidence Level**: PROBABLE (some displacement documented but unclear scale; partly driven by information gaps)

### Are Indigenous Peoples Being Consulted Now?

**BY GUYANA GOVERNMENT** (CERTAIN - YES):
- **National Toshaos Council Annual Conference**: 17th conference held May 2025 with 250+ Toshaos
- Border controversy was major topic
- President Ali, VP Jagdeo, and military leadership addressed Indigenous leaders
- GDF requesting intelligence sharing from border communities
- NTC Chairman attended COP28 (December 2023) to discuss Essequibo concerns internationally
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**BY VENEZUELA** (CERTAIN - NO):
- December 3, 2023 referendum held in Venezuela only; no vote held in disputed region
- "There has been no similar consultation conducted...by Venezuela...with the indigenous people of the region"
- Venezuela's Ministry of Foreign Affairs "did not comment on the land rights and future of Amerindian communities" (Mongabay, January 2024)

**March 2025**: Venezuela's Diosdado Cabello claimed community assemblies held in Essequibo on March 15, 2025
- **NTC Response (March 21, 2025)**: "Hundreds of Toshaos...categorically rejected these claims as 'completely false,' confirming 'no such community assemblies took place'"
- **Source**: News Room Guyana, Guyana Chronicle, March 2025
- **Confidence Level**: CERTAIN

**BY INTERNATIONAL COURT OF JUSTICE** (UNCLEAR):
- ICJ case filed by Guyana in 2018, jurisdiction accepted December 18, 2020
- Case focuses on validity of 1899 Arbitral Award and state sovereignty
- No evidence of direct ICJ consultation mechanisms for Indigenous peoples
- Case structured as state-to-state dispute, not explicitly addressing Indigenous rights
- **Confidence Level**: UNCLEAR (no documented consultation mechanism)

## 7. VENEZUELAN WARAO REFUGEES TO GUYANA

**CRITICAL IRONY**: While Venezuela claims Essequibo, Venezuelan Indigenous peoples are fleeing TO Guyana

**SCALE** (CERTAIN):
- 24,500 total Venezuelan refugees/migrants in Guyana (2021-2024)
- Approximately 2,500 Indigenous Warao among them (10% of Venezuelan migrants)
- 250+ Warao found refuge in Anabisi, northern Guyana (more than half are children)

**CONDITIONS** (CERTAIN):
- UNHCR Multi-Country Representative (November 2021): Warao families facing "One meal a day, no shelter, and no drinking water"
- Most Warao have only one meal per day or less
- Lacking formal job opportunities
- Limited access to services due to remoteness
- Lloyd Perreira (Wakapoa Toshao, January 2024): "We are worried that these people are suffering. Their numbers are rapidly increasing"

**DISTURBING REPORT** (CERTAIN):
- May 2022: Approximately 1,500 Warao forced to work without pay in illegal gold mines in Guyana
- Recruited under false pretenses, taken to Kumaka mines
- Juan Carlos González (Warao man): "They tricked them and sent them straight to the mines to work like slaves"
- **Source**: InSight Crime, 2022

**WHY THEY'RE FLEEING**: Venezuela's humanitarian crisis, economic collapse, extreme poverty (75% of population), hyperinflation

**Confidence Level**: CERTAIN (extensively documented by UN agencies)

## 8. GAPS AND LIMITATIONS IN RESEARCH

### What We DON'T Know

1. **Exact displacement numbers**: Scale of Guyanese Indigenous peoples who fled villages in December 2023 unknown

2. **Specific service disruptions**: Limited documentation of how border tensions affected healthcare, education, or resource access in 2025

3. **ICJ Indigenous participation**: No clear information on whether ICJ has any informal consultation with Indigenous communities

4. **Venezuelan Indigenous perspectives from Essequibo**: Almost no direct testimony from Indigenous peoples living on Venezuelan side of border

5. **Current conditions post-May 2025**: Most recent detailed community-level information from May 2025 conference; current situation unclear

6. **Specific economic data**: No comprehensive statistics on mining revenues lost to illegal operations or precise economic impact on Indigenous households

7. **Detailed environmental monitoring**: Limited recent data on mercury levels across all affected communities

8. **Individual community responses**: Most information aggregated through NTC/APA; individual village-level decision-making less documented

### What Claims Are Made About Indigenous Peoples Without Their Direct Input

1. **Government claims on both sides**: Both Venezuela and Guyana have "instrumentalized Indigenous identity" in the dispute while Indigenous peoples face state-endorsed extractivism

2. **ICJ proceedings**: Case focuses on state sovereignty; Indigenous territorial rights not explicitly addressed despite Indigenous peoples being primary inhabitants

3. **Media coverage**: Some international coverage discusses Indigenous peoples as passive subjects rather than active political agents

4. **Historical narratives**: 1899 arbitration used Indigenous alliances as evidence without Indigenous participation

### Where Sources Are Unclear or Conflicting

1. **Romario vs. Mario Hastings**: Some confusion in sources about which Hastings is toshao vs. resident of Kako village

2. **Exact titled community numbers**: Regions 7, 8, 9 specific numbers not fully disaggregated in accessible sources

3. **Displacement scale**: APA reported families fleeing but also noted this was information-driven; actual numbers unclear

4. **Timeline discrepancies**: Some historical migration dates vary slightly between sources

5. **Population figures**: Pre-colonial population estimates and some current Indigenous population statistics vary between sources

## 10. KEY FINDINGS FOR EDUCATIONAL VIDEO

### Core Narrative Points

1. **Indigenous peoples have lived in Essequibo since "time immemorial"** - approximately 125,000 people, ~90% of Essequibo's population, representing 9 distinct nations

2. **They were completely excluded from the 1899 Paris Arbitration** that both countries now reference - no consultation, no representation, no consideration of Indigenous sovereignty

3. **Colonial borders split Indigenous nations** - especially Warao and Akawaio peoples now divided across artificial boundaries

4. **Indigenous organizations unanimously support Guyana's sovereignty** while also critiquing both governments' extractive industries

5. **"We do not recognize borders"** - Jean La Rose's powerful statement about Indigenous territorial concepts vs. colonial impositions

6. **Land rights remain incomplete after 60 years** - only 20 of 68 demarcation cases completed since 2013; 28 of 29 titled villages say titles are insufficient

7. **Venezuelan annexation threatens decades of Indigenous struggle** for land recognition

8. **Mercury poisoning is severe** - Parabara has highest levels in Guyana; mining camps doubled in 2024

9. **Violence against Indigenous peoples** - documented torture and beheadings of Guyanese Indigenous miners by Venezuelan gangs

10. **Ironic reversal**: 2,500 Venezuelan Warao Indigenous refugees fled TO Guyana while Venezuela claims to "protect" Essequibo

11. **Fear in border communities** - NTC Chairman explicitly stated threats "driving fear" in May 2025

12. **Venezuela has NEVER consulted Indigenous peoples** in Essequibo; false claims of community assemblies rejected by Indigenous leaders

13. **"Stay, guard it"** - Jean La Rose's message urging Indigenous peoples to remain on their ancestral lands

### Most Powerful Quotes for Video

1. Jean La Rose: "We do not recognize borders. We recognize the river that gets us to one place. We recognize the mountain we must cross to get to one place."

1. This is fundamentally about **COLONIAL CONTINUITY** - both countries base claims on European "discovery" while Indigenous sovereignty is ignored
