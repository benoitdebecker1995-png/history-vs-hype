# Quote Verification Protocol
## Enhanced Fact-Checking for Historical Attributions

**Purpose:** Prevent misattributed quotes from appearing in History vs Hype videos

**When to use:** EVERY TIME you include a quote from a historical figure, theologian, philosopher, or public figure

---

## The Golden Rule for Quotes

**If you can't find the specific document/page where someone said it, DON'T USE IT.**

Famous quotes are frequently misattributed. Without verification, you're repeating someone else's error.

---

## Mandatory Quote Verification Checklist

For EVERY quote in your script, complete ALL these steps:

### ☐ Step 1: Identify the Claim
- [ ] Who is being quoted?
- [ ] What exactly did they supposedly say?
- [ ] Where did I first encounter this quote? (note the source)

### ☐ Step 2: Find the Primary Source
- [ ] What is the specific work where this appears?
  - Book title
  - Letter to [person]
  - Sermon at [location/date]
  - Speech at [event]
  - Legal document/treaty
- [ ] What is the date/year of this work?
- [ ] What is the specific page/section/chapter?

### ☐ Step 3: Verify the Exact Wording
- [ ] Can I access the original source? (translation counts if original is not in English)
- [ ] Does the wording match what's claimed?
- [ ] Is this a direct quote or a paraphrase?
- [ ] Is the quote taken in context or cherry-picked?

### ☐ Step 4: Check for Common Misattributions
- [ ] Search "[quote] misattributed"
- [ ] Search "[person's name] never said"
- [ ] Check Quote Investigator or similar sites
- [ ] Search "[quote] actually said by"

### ☐ Step 5: Cross-Reference with Academic Sources
- [ ] Does a peer-reviewed academic source cite this quote?
- [ ] Does the academic source provide the specific citation?
- [ ] Can I verify the academic source's citation is correct?

### ☐ Step 6: Document the Citation
Format: **[Person], *[Work Title]* ([Year]), [Page/Section]**

Examples:
- ✅ James Madison, *Memorial and Remonstrance Against Religious Assessments* (1785), Section 1
- ✅ Aristotle, *Politics*, Book I, Part 4
- ✅ Pope Gregory XVI, *Mirari Vos* (1832), Section 14
- ❌ "Martin Luther said in the 1520s..."
- ❌ "James Thornwell, 1850s, says..."

## Red Flags: When to Be Extra Careful

### 🚩 Red Flag 1: Vague Attribution
**Examples:**
- "Luther said in the 1520s..."
- "Thornwell, writing in the 1850s..."
- "An ancient philosopher once wrote..."

**Why it's dangerous:** Suggests the person citing it doesn't know the actual source

**Action:** Find the specific work or don't use it

---

### 🚩 Red Flag 2: Suspiciously Perfect Quote
**Examples:**
- Quote that perfectly encapsulates a complex idea in one sentence
- Quote that seems designed for modern political argument
- Quote that uses anachronistic language

**Why it's dangerous:** May be fabricated, misattributed, or taken out of context

**Action:** Extra scrutiny - check multiple academic sources

---

### 🚩 Red Flag 3: Cross-Cultural Attribution
**Examples:**
- Greek philosophical concepts attributed to medieval theologians
- Enlightenment ideas attributed to ancient figures
- Modern phrasing attributed to historical figures

**Why it's dangerous:** Ideas often get conflated across time periods

**Action:** Verify the person lived at the right time and had access to these concepts

---

### 🚩 Red Flag 4: Only Found in Secondary Sources
**Examples:**
- Quote appears in modern books but not in primary documents
- Quote appears in blogs/websites but not in academic literature
- Multiple sources cite the quote but none provide original source

**Why it's dangerous:** This is how misattributions spread

**Action:** Don't use until you find the primary source

---

### 🚩 Red Flag 5: Politically Convenient Quote
**Examples:**
- Quote that perfectly supports your video's argument
- Quote that seems to settle a complex historical debate cleanly
- Quote from a figure whose views were actually more nuanced

**Why it's dangerous:** You might be cherry-picking or using a distorted version

**Action:** Research the figure's full position, not just one quote

---

## Common Misattribution Examples

Keep this reference handy - these are frequently misattributed:

| Commonly Attributed To | Actually Said By | The Actual Truth |
|----------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Martin Luther: "Slaves are tools with voice" | **Aristotle, *Politics*** | Aristotle described slaves as "animate tools" or "living instruments" |
| Einstein: "Insanity is doing the same thing..." | **Unknown** | No evidence Einstein ever said this |
| Jefferson: "The tree of liberty..." regarding blood of tyrants | **Jefferson DID say this** | But in a letter, not in founding documents - context matters |
| Burke: "The only thing necessary for evil to triumph..." | **Misattribution** | Burke never wrote this exact phrase |

## How to Handle Uncertain Attributions

If you can't fully verify a quote but it's important to your argument:

### Option 1: Hedge the Attribution
- "Luther is often quoted as saying..."
- "A statement attributed to Thornwell..."
- "What appears to be an Aristotelian concept that later theologians adopted..."

### Option 2: Focus on the Idea, Not the Quote
- Instead of: "Luther said slaves are tools with voice"
- Try: "Protestant reformers, including Luther, defended social hierarchy and condemned peasant rebellions against masters"

### Option 3: Don't Use It
- **Best option if verification fails**
- One less quote is better than one wrong attribution

## Special Cases

### Quotes in Translation
- [ ] Identify the translator
- [ ] Check if translation is contested
- [ ] Note that it's a translation: "In [Translator]'s translation..."

### Quotes from Oral Sources
- [ ] When was it recorded/transcribed?
- [ ] By whom?
- [ ] Is the recording/transcript available?

### Quotes from Religious Texts
- [ ] Which translation/version?
- [ ] Which verse/section?
- [ ] Is interpretation contested?

### Quotes from Legal Documents
- [ ] Full document name
- [ ] Article/Section number
- [ ] Year ratified/signed

## Integration with Production Workflow

### During Research Phase
- Create a **Quote Database** spreadsheet:
  - Column A: The quote
  - Column B: Attribution
  - Column C: Source (full citation)
  - Column D: Page/section
  - Column E: Verification status
  - Column F: Notes/context

### During Script Writing
- **Never write a quote without the citation already documented**
- Include citation in script draft: [Luther, [NEED SOURCE], says...]
- Mark unverified quotes in RED

### Before Filming
- **MANDATORY VERIFICATION PASS**
- Every quote must pass the full checklist
- No RED marks remaining in script
- All citations documented

### During Editing
- Consider showing citation on screen for major quotes
- Add source attribution to description with page numbers

---

## When Someone Challenges Your Attribution

### If You Receive a Comment Like:
"Luther never said that - that's actually Aristotle"

### Response Protocol:

1. **Thank them immediately**
   - "Thank you for the correction - this is exactly the kind of rigor we strive for"

2. **Verify their claim**
   - Check if they're correct (they often are)
   - Research the correct attribution

3. **If they're right:**
   - Pin a correction comment
   - Add correction to video description
   - Update your records
   - Consider re-upload if error is significant

4. **Update your systems:**
   - Add to Common Misattributions list
   - Review how the error got through
   - Strengthen verification for similar cases

## Enforcement

### This is Non-Negotiable

For a fact-checking channel, misattributing quotes is:
- ❌ Hypocritical (we fact-check others)
- ❌ Credibility-destroying (one error undermines everything)
- ❌ Preventable (with proper verification)

### Pre-Flight Checklist Item

Before EVERY video is considered complete:

**"QUOTE VERIFICATION: Every quote has been verified with primary source citation documented. Checked by: [Name] on [Date]"**

---

## Summary: The Three Verification Questions

Before using ANY historical quote, answer these:

1. **Where exactly did they say it?** (specific work, page, date)
2. **Can I verify it in the original source?** (or reliable academic citation)
3. **Is this commonly misattributed?** (search for debunking)

If you can't confidently answer all three: **Don't use the quote.**

---

*Remember: Your credibility takes years to build and one wrong attribution to destroy. Verify everything.*
