## Claim: How do ILC Articles 40 and 41 apply to Somaliland regarding self-determination?

### ✅ VERIFIED: Text of ILC Articles 40 and 41

**Source:** ILC Articles on Responsibility of States for Internationally Wrongful Acts (2001)

**Article 40 - Application of this Chapter**

1. This chapter applies to the international responsibility which is entailed by a serious breach by a State of an obligation arising under a peremptory norm of general international law.

2. A breach of such an obligation is serious if it involves a gross or systematic failure by the responsible State to fulfil the obligation.

**Article 41 - Particular consequences of a serious breach of an obligation under this chapter**

1. States shall cooperate to bring to an end through lawful means any serious breach within the meaning of article 40.

2. No State shall recognize as lawful a situation created by a serious breach within the meaning of article 40, nor render aid or assistance in maintaining that situation.

3. This article is without prejudice to the other consequences referred to in this Part and to such further consequences that a breach to which this chapter applies may entail under international law.

## ✅ VERIFIED: Self-Determination's Status as Peremptory Norm (Jus Cogens)

### Status: CONTESTED but increasingly recognized

**Evidence supporting jus cogens status:**

> "State practice illustrates how the self-determination principle is gradually becoming a peremptory norm of general international law"
> — ResearchGate study on Jus Cogens and Self-Determination

> "Self-determination has evolved from a political principle to a peremptory legal norm, i.e. jus cogens"
> — Multiple ILC sources

> "The UN International Law Commission lists the right of self-determination among peremptory norms"
> — ILC 2019 Report on Peremptory Norms

**Critical limitation - Chagos Islands precedent:**

> "The Court stopped short of holding that self-determination is a peremptory, or higher, norm of international law (jus cogens), a finding that would have triggered a duty of non-recognition on all states"
> — Analysis of ICJ Chagos Advisory Opinion (2019)

> "The Chagos advisory opinion played a significant role in the debate because of the relationship between erga omnes obligations and jus cogens and because the Court did not refer to self-determination as a norm of jus cogens"
> — EJIL: Talk! Analysis

**Key distinction:**
- The ICJ confirmed self-determination has "erga omnes character" (all states have legal interest)
- BUT it did NOT confirm it's jus cogens (peremptory norm)
- Judges Trindade and Robinson argued in separate opinions the Court SHOULD have endorsed self-determination's peremptory nature

## ⚠️ PARTIALLY APPLICABLE: How Articles 40 & 41 Could Apply to Somaliland

### The Legal Argument (Pro-Somaliland):

**IF self-determination is jus cogens (still contested), THEN:**

1. **Article 40 test:**
   - Was there a "serious breach" (gross or systematic failure)?
   - Somalia's 31-year denial of Somaliland's self-determination claim
   - Barre regime's violence against northern population (1980s)
   - Continued military opposition to restoration (1991-present)

2. **Article 41(1) obligations:**
   - All states should "cooperate to bring to an end through lawful means" the breach
   - Could support recognition as means of ending the violation

3. **Article 41(2) non-recognition:**
   - States should NOT recognize "as lawful a situation created by a serious breach"
   - Paradox: AU refuses to recognize Somaliland based on preserving Somalia's territorial integrity
   - But IF Somalia's territorial claim violates self-determination (jus cogens), then maintaining that situation would itself violate Article 41(2)

### Counter-Arguments (Pro-Somalia/Status Quo):

1. **Self-determination is NOT clearly jus cogens:**
   - ICJ refused to confirm this in Chagos (2019)
   - Only erga omnes, not peremptory
   - Articles 40-41 don't apply

2. **Self-determination already exercised in 1960:**
   - Somaliland voluntarily joined Somalia (Mahmoud: "wildly popular")
   - 31 years of practice legitimized the union
   - No ongoing violation

3. **Territorial integrity vs. self-determination:**
   - Somalia's territorial integrity is ALSO protected by international law
   - Uti possidetis doctrine (colonial borders) cuts both ways
   - AU applies it to 1960 unified Somalia, not 1960 British Somaliland

4. **Article 40 "gross or systematic" threshold not met:**
   - No genocide, no apartheid, no forcible denial
   - Somaliland functions as de facto state
   - Not comparable to active suppression cases

## Verdict for Response

**Conclusion:**

✅ Articles 40 and 41 raise important questions about Somaliland's case

⚠️ BUT they depend on self-determination being recognized as jus cogens — which the ICJ notably declined to confirm in Chagos (2019)

💭 Even if self-determination IS jus cogens, applying Articles 40-41 requires proving:
   - Breach is "serious" (gross or systematic)
   - Somalia is the responsible state
   - The situation violates the norm

🔴 Major obstacle: Somaliland's 1960 union was voluntary. Hard to argue ongoing "forcible denial" when initial merger was consensual and internationally recognized.
