## Executive Summary

**Three of four research questions are substantially verified** with high-quality academic sources, while one requires important corrections. ## Research Question 1: North American Indigenous Archaeology & Sacrifice

### A. Cahokia Mound 72 (~1050 AD)

**VERDICT: VERIFIED - HIGH CONFIDENCE**

**Archaeological Consensus:**
Current scholarship confirms substantial evidence of ritual killings at Mound 72. Excavations by Melvin L. Fowler (1967-1971) revealed approximately **272 bodies**, predominantly young women aged 15-30, in multiple mass burial contexts associated with Cahokia's founding period.

**Key Evidence:**
The site contains several distinct burial features: a central "Beaded Burial" with paired individuals on 20,000+ marine shell beads arranged in a falcon/thunderbird shape, a mass grave of 53 young women arranged in neat layers with evidence of strangulation or blood-letting, 39 individuals showing violent death including decapitation and fractured skulls, and four males with linked elbows missing heads and hands in ritually significant arrangement.

**Leading Specialists:**

**Thomas E. Emerson** (Illinois State Archaeological Survey) published the definitive reanalysis in American Antiquity 81(3):405-425 (2016) titled "Paradigms Lost: Reconfiguring Cahokia's Mound 72 Beaded Burial." Using bioarchaeology and AMS radiocarbon dating, Emerson's team dated activities to cal A.D. 991-1023 and argued ritual practices emphasize fertility and world creation rather than male warrior themes.

**Timothy R. Pauketat** (University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign) authored "Cahokia: Ancient America's Great City on the Mississippi" (2009) and estimates one group of girls was sacrificed every generation over Cahokia's 150-year peak, theorizing sacrifices may have contributed to the city's eventual decline.

**Direct Quote from Emerson et al. (2016):** The burials represent "ritualized human sacrifice tied to world renewal/fertility ceremonies" and "periodic commemorative events coinciding with Cahokia's founding."

**Other Significant Mississippian Sites:**
- **Moundville (Alabama)**: Evidence of 110 young women in one burial mound, likely sacrificial victims
- **Spiro Mounds (Oklahoma)**: Major ceremonial center (800-1450 CE) with extensive burial goods suggesting ritual practices
- **Etowah (Georgia)**: Mound C excavations revealed evidence of ritual violence and warrior culture

**Note on Sarah Baires:** Dr. Sarah E. Baires is a prominent Cahokia scholar (author of "Land of Water, City of the Dead," 2017), but research did not find her specifically named as a Mound 72 specialist—her expertise focuses on urbanism and sacred landscapes.

### B. Indigenous Nations Encountered by English Colonists

**VERDICT: NO EVIDENCE - HIGH CONFIDENCE**

**⚠️ CRITICAL CORRECTION NEEDED:** Archaeological evidence does NOT support human sacrifice among Powhatan, Wampanoag, Haudenosaunee, or Cherokee nations.

**Powhatan Confederacy (Virginia):**
Primary sources (Captain John Smith 1612, William Strachey 1618) describe sacrifices to the deity Okee consisting of **tobacco, blood, deer suet, and other offerings—NOT human sacrifice**. Archaeological burial evidence from sites like Chickahominy River shows standard burial practices without sacrificial killings. One violent incident—Powhatan's destruction of the Chesapeake tribe—was warfare/political execution, not ritual sacrifice.

**Wampanoag (Massachusetts):**
Only dubious folklore exists (the "Sacrifice Rock" legend near Bourne, MA, appearing in Scott Corbett's 1955 "Cape Cod's Way" as legend, not documented history). Christina J. Hodge's study "Faith and Practice at an Early-Eighteenth-Century Wampanoag Burial Ground" (Historical Archaeology 39(3):73-94, 2005) found no evidence of human sacrifice. **Dog sacrifice** rituals were documented for war protection and healing.

**Haudenosaunee/Iroquois Confederacy:**
No archaeological evidence of human sacrifice. The **White Dog Sacrifice** was documented ceremonial practice using animals. Treatment of war captives could be brutal (torture-killing), but this was warfare practice, not religious sacrifice. No evidence of ritual human sacrifice comparable to Mississippian sites.

**Cherokee:**
Animal and food sacrifices were practiced (deer tongue, bird sacrifices), but no human sacrifice. Archaeological studies at Garden Creek Mound, North Carolina, and Coweeta Creek show no evidence of human sacrifice. Mortuary evidence indicates status differentiation through grave goods, not sacrificial victims.

**Important Qualification:** Absence of archaeological evidence doesn't definitively prove these practices never occurred, but there is **no scholarly consensus or credible evidence** supporting human sacrifice claims for these specific nations.

### C. Increase Mather's 1676 Claims

**VERDICT: DOCUMENT CONFIRMED, SPECIFIC QUOTE NOT VERIFIED - MEDIUM CONFIDENCE**

**Primary Source Identified:**
"A Brief History of the Warr with the Indians in New-England" by Increase Mather, published Boston, 1676 (printer John Foster). Written during King Philip's War (1675-1676), this ~30,000-word, 89-page contemporary chronicle provides a day-by-day account of military engagements.

**Historical Context:**
King Philip's War (Metacom's War) was fought between English colonists and the Wampanoag, Narragansett, and other Indigenous nations. The devastating conflict resulted in near-extermination of hostile Native groups. Mather argued the war was defensive but also blamed English colonists for sins (apostasy, pride, drunkenness) that caused God to raise enemies against them.

**⚠️ CRITICAL FINDING:**
The specific claim about "devilish" sacrifices **could not be confirmed** in accessible portions of the text. Mather did characterize Indigenous religion as worship of "the Divell" (Devil) and described their god "Oke" as demonic—this was standard Puritan rhetoric equating non-Christian religion with devil worship, **NOT descriptions of observed human sacrifice rituals**.

**Assessment:**
- Document exists: YES
- Context (King Philip's War): YES
- "Devilish" language about Indigenous religion: LIKELY (standard Puritan rhetoric)
- **Specific claims about human sacrifice: NOT CONFIRMED**

**Recommendation:** The claim may be an inference from Mather's general characterization of Indigenous religion as "devilish," present in an unaccessed section, a conflation with other colonial writings, or an exaggeration. The full text should be examined for verification.

**Citation:** Mather, Increase. 1676. A Brief History of the Warr with the Indians in New-England. Boston: John Foster. Electronic edition edited by Paul Royster, University of Nebraska-Lincoln Digital Commons.

## Research Question 2: Papal Condemnations of Rights (19th Century)

### A. Pope Gregory XVI, Mirari Vos (1832)

**VERDICT: CONFIRMED - HIGH CONFIDENCE**

**Exact Latin Text (Paragraph 14):**
"Atque ex hoc putidissimo indifferentismi fonte absurda illa fluit ac erronea sententia, **seu potius deliramentum**, asserendam esse ac vindicandam cuilibet 'libertatem conscientiae'."

**Authoritative English Translation:**
"And from this most foul font of indifferentism flows that absurd and erroneous proposition, **or rather insanity [deliramentum]**, that freedom of conscience must be asserted and vindicated for everyone."

**What Was Being Condemned:**
Gregory XVI specifically targeted French priest Félicité de Lamennais and his newspaper L'Avenir, which advocated separation of church and state, universal freedom of conscience, unlimited freedom of the press, and democratic reforms. Cardinal Avery Dulles explains in First Things (December 2001) that Gregory XVI "condemned the extreme liberalism of Félicité de Lamennais, which would allow all kinds of unfounded, libelous, and subversive opinions to be circulated without any legal restrictions."

The encyclical condemned religious indifferentism (the view that one religion is as good as another), unlimited freedom of conscience (each person's conscience as sole arbiter of truth independent of God), absolute freedom of opinion (unrestricted liberty without moral restraints), and radical secularist separation of Church and State.

**Full Citation:**
Pope Gregory XVI. Mirari Vos (Encyclical Letter). August 15, 1832. Vatican.va official archives: https://www.vatican.va/content/gregorius-xvi/en/documents/encyclica-mirari-vos-15-augusti-1832.html

### B. Pope Pius IX, Syllabus of Errors (1864)

**VERDICT: CONFIRMED - HIGH CONFIDENCE**

**Relevant Condemned Propositions:**

**Proposition 15 [CONDEMNED]:**
"Every man is free to embrace and profess that religion which, guided by the light of reason, he shall consider true."
(Source: Allocution "Maxima quidem," June 9, 1862)

**Proposition 16 [CONDEMNED]:**
"Man may, in the observance of any religion whatever, find the way of eternal salvation, and arrive at eternal salvation."
(Source: Encyclical "Qui pluribus," Nov. 9, 1846)

**Proposition 77 [CONDEMNED]:**
"In the present day it is no longer expedient that the Catholic religion should be held as the only religion of the State, to the exclusion of all other forms of worship."

**Proposition 78 [CONDEMNED]:**
"Hence it has been wisely decided by law, in some Catholic countries, that persons coming to reside therein shall enjoy the public exercise of their own peculiar worship."

**Proposition 79 [CONDEMNED]:**
"Moreover, it is false that the civil liberty of every form of worship, and the full power, given to all, of overtly and publicly manifesting any opinions whatsoever and thoughts, conduce more easily to corrupt the morals and minds of the people, and to propagate the pest of indifferentism."

**Structure:** The Syllabus is a list of condemned propositions (errors). Each represents an ERROR—the Catholic teaching is the contradictory, not the contrary.

**Historical Context:**
Directed at specific mid-19th century European situations: Italian unification movement's seizure of Papal States, Piedmontese government closing monasteries, Spanish proposals to disestablish the Catholic Church, and anticlericalism in Latin America. English historian E.E.Y. Hales notes regarding Proposition 77: "The Pope is not concerned with a universal principle, but with the position in a particular state at a particular date."

**Full Citation:**
Pope Pius IX. Syllabus Errorum (Syllabus of Errors). December 8, 1864. Papalencyclicals.net/pius09/p9syll.htm

**Important Nuance:**
These condemnations opposed religious freedom **based on relativism and indifferentism**, not all concepts of religious liberty. Vatican II (1965) later affirmed religious freedom as civil immunity from coercion while maintaining that truth subsists in the Catholic Church.

## Research Question 3: Harry Laughlin's 1924 Congressional Testimony

### A. Laughlin's Congressional Testimony

**VERDICT: VERIFIED - HIGH CONFIDENCE**

**Three Major Congressional Testimonies:**

1. **"Biological Aspects of Immigration"** - April 16-17, 1920, House Committee on Immigration and Naturalization, 66th Congress, 2nd Session. Published: Washington: Government Printing Office, 1921. Available: Library of Congress, Internet Archive, HathiTrust.

2. **"Analysis of America's Modern Melting Pot"** - November 21, 1922, House Committee on Immigration and Naturalization, 67th Congress, 3rd Session, Serial 7-C. Published: Washington: Government Printing Office, 1923.

3. **"Europe as an Emigrant-Exporting Continent and the United States as an Immigrant-Receiving Nation"** - March 8, 1924, House Committee on Immigration and Naturalization, 68th Congress, 1st Session, Serial 5-A. Published: Washington: Government Printing Office, 1924.

**Official Appointment:**
After his 1920 testimony, Committee Chairman Albert Johnson appointed Laughlin as the committee's **"Expert Eugenics Agent"** (position held 1921-1931).

**Exact Language from Testimony:**

From the 1920 testimony: "At present, not inferior nationalities but **inferior individual family stocks** are tending to deteriorate our national characteristics." Laughlin discussed **"hereditary physical, mental, and moral or temperamental traits"** as determining "the character of the nation," warned against admitting **"degenerate 'blood'"**, and called for preventing immigration of those deemed **"socially inadequate."**

From the 1922 testimony: Discussed immigrants from southern and eastern Europe showing higher rates of **"all types of social inadequacy, including feeblemindedness, insanity, criminality, and dependency."** Presented statistical data purporting to show disproportionate rates of mental illness from Italy (23.1%), Russia and Poland (12.6%), versus England/Wales (5.5%) and Scotland (0.2%).

**⚠️ IMPORTANT NUANCE ON EXACT PHRASING:**
The term "genetically inferior" per se does not appear in available excerpts. However, Laughlin used **functionally equivalent eugenic language**: "inferior hereditary qualities," "inferior family stocks" with hereditary defects, "defective germ-plasm," and "bad stock" with hereditary traits. The concept of **hereditary/biological inferiority** was clearly present even if the specific modern phrasing wasn't used verbatim.

**Scholarly Verification:**
Mae M. Ngai in "Impossible Subjects: Illegal Aliens and the Making of Modern America" (Princeton University Press, 2004) documents how Laughlin provided "scientific rationale" for restriction based on eugenic theories about racial hierarchies. Paul Lombardo documented that Laughlin "argued that the 'American' gene pool was being polluted by a rising tide of intellectually and morally defective immigrants—primarily from eastern and southern Europe."

**Impact:**
The Immigration Restriction Act of 1924 directly resulted from Laughlin's testimonies, reducing immigration from southern and eastern Europe to only 9% of total quotas while northern and western European countries received 86% of quotas.

### B. English-Only Education Laws

**VERDICT: VERIFIED - HIGH CONFIDENCE**

**Primary Scholarly Source:**
Ester de Jong, "Foundations for Multilingualism in Education: From Principles to Practice" (Philadelphia: Caslon Publishing, 2011), Chapter 5, pp. 126-138.

**Exact Quote:**
"Several states passed legislation that prohibited the teaching of foreign languages to young children and **37 states passed laws making English the official language of the state during this period**" [WWI era through the 1920s].

**Time Period:**
WWI era through the 1920s, with peak activity 1919-1920 when 15 states passed English-only laws in just two years.

**What These Laws Mandated:**
English as the basic language of instruction in public schools, prohibition of foreign language instruction to young children (particularly under age 10), required English proficiency for graduation, penalties for teachers who used foreign languages, and in some states, English-only textbooks and curricula.

**Historical Context:**
WWI nationalism and anti-German sentiment drove the Americanization movement. Over 30 states passed Americanization laws between 1917-1922, closely connected to nativist/eugenic movements supporting immigration restriction.

**Court Response:**
Meyer v. Nebraska (1923): U.S. Supreme Court struck down Nebraska's law banning foreign language instruction to children under 10 as unconstitutional, ruling it violated parents' rights. However, English-only instruction continued as de facto practice despite this ruling.

**⚠️ MINOR VARIANCE:**
Some sources cite 34 states rather than 37. The San Francisco Public Press notes "34 states—15 between 1919 and 1920—enact laws that declare English as the basic language of instruction." The difference likely stems from varying classifications of law types or timeframes. Both numbers confirm the widespread nature of these laws.

**Citation:**
de Jong, Ester. 2011. Foundations for Multilingualism in Education: From Principles to Practice. Chapter 5: "Language Policy in the United States," pp. 126-138. Philadelphia: Caslon Publishing.

## Research Question 4: Yewden Villa "Brothel Babies" Archaeology

### Overview

**VERDICT: YEWDEN VILLA IS THE MOST CITED SITE, BUT "BROTHEL BABIES" INTERPRETATION IS THOROUGHLY DEBUNKED - HIGH CONFIDENCE**

### A. Yewden Villa, Buckinghamshire

**What Was Discovered:**
97 infant burials excavated 1912-1921 by Alfred Heneage Cocks at a 1st-4th century CE Roman villa. Original remains were rediscovered in 2008 by Jill Eyers in Buckinghamshire County Museum, stored in tobacco boxes and shotgun cartridge containers. Only 35-40 of the original 97 remains have been relocated for modern analysis.

**Current Archaeological Interpretation:**
The "brothel" interpretation has been thoroughly challenged and is NOT supported by current archaeological consensus.

**Critical Evidence Against Brothel Theory:**

**Temporal Context:** The 97 burials occurred over **300 years** (not all at once), making this a normal mortality rate for the period.

**DNA Evidence Contradicts Theory:** Hassan et al. 2014 analyzed 33 infants, successfully sexed 12. Result: 7 female, 5 male (balanced sex ratio), with no shared mtDNA haplotypes (all from different mothers). This directly contradicts the hypothesis that male infants were selectively killed while females were raised as prostitutes.

**Geographic Implausibility:** The site location is too remote from major population centers to sustain a brothel business.

**Alternative Explanation - Obstetric Complications:** Mays et al. 2012 found cut marks on one femur consistent with embryotomy (obstetric procedure for obstructed labor), suggesting deaths from childbirth complications.

---

### B. Other Commonly Misidentified Sites

**Ashkelon, Israel (Roman-era bathhouse):**
97-100 infant remains found in sewer beneath late Roman/early Byzantine bathhouse (excavated 1988). Originally interpreted as brothel infanticide, but DNA analysis (Faerman et al. 1997/1998) showed 14 males, 5 females—**opposite of expected pattern**. Recent reinterpretation suggests possible baptistery rather than brothel.

**Other Roman Britain Sites:**
Piddington, Northamptonshire (17 perinatal/infant burials); Shiptonthorpe & Hayton, East Yorkshire (careful, ritualized infant burials); Poundbury, Dorset (large cemetery with normal mortality patterns); Barton Court Farm, Oxfordshire (infants near corn driers in agricultural context).

### C. Specific Studies

**⚠️ CRITICAL CORRECTION: Sian Halcrow (2017)**

This is **NOT a peer-reviewed journal article** but an authoritative scholarly blog post titled "Why were so many babies murdered in the past?" published September 20, 2017, on The Bioarchaeology of Childhood blog.

**Halcrow's Exact Conclusions:**
"There is no contextual evidence that links this burial site to a brothel." She notes that "97 infant deaths over a few hundred years is not an excessively high mortality rate" and that "the assumption that a high rate of infant mortality around the time of birth equals infanticide is problematic." Peak mortality at birth is "the most critical time in a baby's life"—this is normal, not evidence of murder.

**Halcrow's Credentials:**
Professor, University of Otago, Department of Anatomy; specialist in bioarchaeology of childhood; co-editor of "The Mother-Infant Nexus in Anthropology" (2019) with Rebecca Gowland.

**⚠️ CRITICAL CORRECTION: "Milella et al. (2015)"**

There is NO "Milella et al. 2015" study in Britannia about infant burials. The requested study is:

**Millett, M., and Gowland, R. (2015)** "Infant and child burial rites in Roman Britain: a study from East Yorkshire"
- Journal: Britannia, Volume 46, pages 171-189
- DOI: 10.1017/S0068113X15000100

**Exact Conclusions from Millett & Gowland 2015:**

"The burial of neonatal infants followed a careful age-specific funerary rite"—NOT unceremonious disposal. Evidence from East Yorkshire sites shows infants were deliberately placed in specific locations (corners of rooms, near hearths, along boundaries) with spatial patterning suggesting gendered domestic space, strong preference for eastern sides of buildings associated with women and childbirth, and burials contemporaneous with occupation.

**Direct Quote:** "We trust that this evidence disposes once and for all with the suggestion that infanticide was the norm in Roman Britain."

The study emphasizes that different age groups received different funerary treatment, evidence suggests infants not viewed as "separate entities" but as "indivisible part of the mother," and reliance on Ashkelon parallels is "probably unwise" given "very considerable evidence that religious beliefs and burial practices showed significant regional and interprovincial variation."

### D. Current Archaeological Consensus

**Leading Bioarchaeology Specialists Who Have Critiqued "Brothel Babies":**

**Professor Sian Halcrow** (University of Otago): "Whole cemeteries devoted to murdered infants seem fictitious when we consider a more contextually nuanced approach."

**Professor Rebecca Gowland** (Durham University): Infant burials show "careful age-specific funerary rite," not disposal. Her 2002 work with Chamberlain exposed methodological flaws in infanticide claims.

**Dr. Martin Millett** (Cambridge University): Infant burials demonstrate "careful choices and decisions," not unceremonious disposal.

**Current Scholarly Consensus (2010s-2020s):**

**Rejection of "Brothel Babies" Narrative:**
- No archaeological evidence for brothels (no architectural features, too remote, no erotic artwork)
- Natural mortality patterns (peak at 38-40 weeks is normal and expected)
- Methodological problems (regression-based age estimation creates artificial clustering; small DNA samples insufficient)

**Positive Evidence for Ritual Burial Practices:**
- Spatial patterning (deliberate placement in specific locations with corner burials, boundary burials, hearth associations)
- Age-specific differentiation (neonatal infants in domestic contexts, premature/older infants elsewhere)
- Evidence of care and ceremony (wrapped in cloth, covered with tiles, associated with feasting deposits)

**Alternative Explanations:**
Normal funerary practice for specific age cohort, obstetric complications (embryotomy evidence), stillbirths and neonatal mortality from maternal health problems and difficult labor, religious/ritual significance (mother goddess cults), and preservation of mother-infant bond.

**⚠️ INACCURACIES IN "BROTHEL BABIES" CLAIMS:**

1. "Mass grave" language is misleading—burials accumulated over 300 years
2. "Brothel" identification has no archaeological evidence
3. "Infanticide" certainty conflates natural perinatal mortality with deliberate killing
4. Ashkelon parallels are questionable with different cultural context
5. Sex-selective killing claims contradict DNA evidence showing balanced ratios
6. "Disposal" language contradicts evidence of careful, ritual placement
7. Media sensationalism (BBC documentaries) popularized theory despite lack of evidence
