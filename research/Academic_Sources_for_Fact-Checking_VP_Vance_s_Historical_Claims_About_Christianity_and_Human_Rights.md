## Claim 1: Christianity invented human rights

The historical record reveals a stunning reversal: the Catholic Church spent the 19th century explicitly condemning the very rights concepts it now claims to have invented. This wasn't mere disagreement—these were formal papal condemnations of liberty of conscience, freedom of press, and religious freedom as dangerous "errors."

### Essential primary sources that demolish the invention claim

**Pope Gregory XVI's Mirari Vos (1832)** remains the smoking gun. Available free at papalencyclicals.net, this encyclical condemns liberty of conscience as "that absurd and erroneous proposition" that "spreads ruin in sacred and civil affairs" (§14). Gregory XVI specifically attacks freedom of the press as spreading "monstrous doctrines and prodigious errors" (§15) and opposes separation of church and state (§20). These aren't isolated statements but systematic rejections of what we now consider fundamental human rights.

**Pope Pius IX's Syllabus of Errors (1864)** escalates the condemnation to encyclopedic levels. This document lists 80 condemned "errors" of modern civilization, including Error #15 (religious freedom), Error #55 (church-state separation), Error #79 (civil liberty of worship), and the famous Error #80 condemning reconciliation with "progress, liberalism and modern civilization." The full text at papalencyclicals.net provides devastating evidence that 19th-century Catholicism positioned itself against, not as the source of, modern rights discourse.

### The 1940s invention thesis: Samuel Moyn's essential scholarship

**Samuel Moyn's Christian Human Rights (University of Pennsylvania Press, 2015, ISBN: 978-0-8122-4818-0)** provides the definitive academic account. Moyn argues that "Christian human rights" emerged not from ancient tradition but as conservative Christian responses to fascism and communism in the 1930s-1940s. The book demonstrates how Christian discourse emphasized "human dignity" and "the human person" as alternatives to secular individualism—not the individual rights tradition itself. Chapter 2 shows Christian Democratic parties used rights language to "impose moral constraints on individuals, support conservative family structures, and preserve existing social hierarchies," not to liberate. His earlier **The Last Utopia: Human Rights in History (Harvard, 2010)** argues modern human rights only emerged in the 1970s, further undermining claims of deep Christian roots.

### Multiple non-Christian origins: The Ishay and Hunt counter-narrative

**Micheline R. Ishay's The History of Human Rights: From Ancient Times to the Globalization Era (UC Press, 2004/2008, ISBN: 978-0-520-25641-5)** traces rights values to Hindu, Buddhist, Confucian, and Islamic sources alongside Greek and Roman philosophy—comprehensively demolishing Christian exclusivity claims. Available through JSTOR at jstor.org/stable/10.1525/j.ctv1xxscm, Chapter 1 documents "Early Ethical Contributions to Human Rights" from Mesopotamian Codes of Hammurabi through ancient Indian texts. Her key question—"Why did the European vision of human rights triumph?"—receives a materialist answer: capitalism and colonialism, not inherent Christian values.

**Lynn Hunt's Inventing Human Rights: A History (Norton, 2007, ISBN: 978-0-393-06095-9)** argues human rights emerged from secular novels creating empathy—Richardson's Pamela, Rousseau's Julie—not religious tradition. Hunt explicitly contrasts the U.S. (where Jefferson invoked "Creator") with France where rights were "deduced from entirely secular sources of nature, reason, and society." The critical passage: "Human rights could only flourish when people learned to think of others as their equals, as like them in some fundamental fashion" through reading novels, not religious instruction (p. 58).

### Pre-Christian sources worth showing on screen

The **Cyrus Cylinder (539 BCE)** at the British Museum demonstrates ancient Persian concepts of religious freedom and slave emancipation predating Christianity by 500+ years. While serious scholars caution against calling it the "first charter of human rights," it definitively proves tolerance concepts existed before Christianity. Ancient Stoic philosophy (Cicero, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius) developed concepts of natural law and universal human dignity in the classical world.

### Counter-argument you must address

**Brian Tierney's The Idea of Natural Rights (Eerdmans, 1997, ISBN: 978-0-8028-4854-3)** argues subjective rights language first developed in 12th-century canon law. This pro-Christian argument actually supports your position: it shows rights emerged in late medieval period through legal developments, not from ancient Christian teaching. Use this to show even pro-Christian scholars can't push the origin back to Christianity's early centuries.

## Claim 2: Founders wanted Christianity in public life

The founding generation's profound disagreements on religion in public life reveal not consensus but bitter division. The narrative of Christian founders imposing Protestant hegemony dissolves when confronted with Madison's radical separationism, widespread persecution of religious minorities, and the complex legal history of the Establishment Clause.

### David Sehat's moral establishment thesis: Your essential framework

**David Sehat's The Myth of American Religious Freedom (Oxford, 2011/2015, ISBN: 978-0-19-024721-8)** winner of the 2012 Frederick Jackson Turner Award, demolishes both liberal and conservative founding myths simultaneously. Sehat's core argument: while the First Amendment applied only to federal government, Protestant Christianity remained "intimately intertwined with the state" at state level. This **"moral establishment"** used government power to punish Catholics, Jews, Mormons, agnostics, and freethinkers through moral laws enforcing religious orthodoxy. Not until Everson v. Board of Education (1940) did the Supreme Court extend First Amendment protections to states, beginning the dismantling that triggered today's culture wars. The 2015 updated edition extends analysis through contemporary debates. Available through Oxford University Press and major libraries.

### Madison's radical separationism: Primary sources that matter

**Madison's Memorial and Remonstrance Against Religious Assessments (1785)** provides the most important founding-era statement on church-state separation. Available free at founders.archives.gov/documents/Madison/01-08-02-0163 with full National Archives editorial notes, this document opposed Patrick Henry's General Assessment bill with 15 detailed arguments. Key passages for your video:

Because #7 devastates claims of Christian necessity: "Experience witnesseth that ecclesiastical establishments...have had a contrary operation. During almost fifteen centuries has the legal establishment of Christianity been on trial. What have been its fruits? More or less in all places, **pride and indolence in the Clergy, ignorance and servility in the laity, in both, superstition, bigotry and persecution.**" Madison explicitly argues Christianity flourished "in spite of every opposition" from government support, not because of it.

Because #9 articulates the asylum principle: Madison warns against "departure from that generous policy, which, offering an Asylum to the persecuted and oppressed of every Nation and Religion" made America distinctive. The Memorial and Remonstrance led directly to passage of Jefferson's Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom (1786), establishing the separationist template before the Constitution itself.

### The Adams counterpoint: Founder disagreement on display

**John Adams presents the contrast.** His letter to Thomas Jefferson (28 June 1813, available at founders.archives.gov) states: "I then believed, and now believe, that those general Principles of Christianity, are as eternal and immutable, as the Existence and Attributes of God." Yet the same Adams signed the **Treaty of Tripoli (1797)** declaring "the government of the United States of America is not in any sense founded on the Christian Religion." Adams supported Massachusetts' religious establishment while Madison opposed all establishments—founder disagreement undermining any claim of consensus.

**John Witte Jr.'s peer-reviewed article "A Most Mild and Equitable Establishment of Religion: John Adams and the Massachusetts Experiment" (Journal of Church and State 41:213-252, 1999)** provides scholarly analysis of this divide, available through JSTOR and academic databases.

### The moral establishment's persecution record

Protestant hegemony enforced through violence and legal discrimination provides concrete evidence against Christian benevolence claims:

**Anti-Catholic persecution:** The 1844 Philadelphia Bible Riots saw pitched gun battles requiring martial law. The 1835 Charlestown Ursuline Convent burning followed Baptist minister Lyman Beecher's anti-Catholic speaking tour. State-level Blaine Amendments (1870s-1880s) banned government aid to religious schools, specifically targeting Catholic education. **Noah Feldman's Divided by God (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2005, ISBN: 978-0-374-53038-9)** documents Blaine Amendments as political weapons against Catholic political power.

**Anti-Mormon persecution:** Expelled from Missouri (1838), Illinois (1844, Joseph Smith murdered by mob), Mormons fled west only to face the 1857 Utah War when President Buchanan sent one-third of the U.S. Army against them. **Kenneth H. Winn's Exiles in a Land of Liberty (UNC Press, 1989)** frames this in terms of republican ideology threatened by Mormon communitarianism.

**Anti-Jewish persecution:** Leo Frank's 1915 lynching, 1920s KKK revival, and 1924 immigration restrictions targeting Eastern European Jews all occurred within the moral establishment framework. **Jonathan D. Sarna's American Judaism (Yale, 2004)** and the ADL's "Antisemitism in American History" resource (antisemitism.adl.org) document systematic discrimination.

### Everson and the incorporation moment

**Everson v. Board of Education, 330 U.S. 1 (1947)** marks the critical turning point. Justice Hugo Black's majority opinion incorporated the Establishment Clause against states via the 14th Amendment, declaring: "Neither a state nor the Federal Government can set up a church. Neither can pass laws which aid one religion, aid all religions, or prefer one religion over another." While the case upheld New Jersey's bus fare reimbursement as "public welfare," it established the "wall of separation" framework citing Jefferson's 1802 Danbury Baptist letter.

**Ellis M. West's encyclopedia article on Everson** (available at University of Richmond's scholarship repository, scholarship.richmond.edu/polisci-faculty-publications/131/) provides accessible scholarly analysis. The Cornell Law School Constitutional Annotation (law.cornell.edu/constitution-conan/amendment-1/early-cases-and-everson-v-board-of-education) and First Amendment Encyclopedia (firstamendment.mtsu.edu) offer comprehensive free resources.

### Essential books challenging the Christian nation narrative

**Isaac Kramnick and R. Laurence Moore's The Godless Constitution: The Case Against Religious Correctness (Norton, 1996/2005)** demonstrates the Constitution is "utterly secular"—no mention of God except Article VI prohibiting religious tests. Anti-Federalists opposed ratification precisely because it was a "godless document," proving the framers' secular intent.

**Steven K. Green's Inventing a Christian America: The Myth of the Religious Founding (Oxford, 2015)** shows "Christian America" was invented by the second generation after the Revolution, not by Founders themselves. This myth-making "sought to sanctify the recent past" while actual founding documents remained determinedly secular.

**Martha C. Nussbaum's Liberty of Conscience: In Defense of America's Tradition of Religious Equality (Basic Books, 2008, ISBN: 978-0-465-05164-9)** provides philosophical and constitutional framework, tracing the tradition from Roger Williams through Madison of granting "equal liberty of conscience to all."

### Counter-arguments requiring response

**Philip Hamburger's Separation of Church and State (Harvard, 2002, ISBN: 978-0-674-01374-2)** presents the conservative originalist position that "separation" has no 18th-century foundation and emerged from anti-Catholic prejudice. While influential in conservative legal circles, Mark McGarvie's H-Net review criticizes Hamburger's "misunderstanding of disestablishment history." Use this to acknowledge the conservative legal position while noting scholarly pushback.

The demographic argument—"America was 95% Protestant"—requires the response that demographics don't determine constitutional design. Madison's Memorial #9 explicitly protected minorities, and Article VI's prohibition of religious tests shows Founders rejected religious qualifications despite Protestant majorities.

## Claim 3: Christian civilization is uniquely moral

The claim collapses under 1,800 years of Christian theological defense of slavery, papal authorization of colonization, and systematic violence against heretics and non-Christians. Christianity's most influential theologians—Augustine, Aquinas—provided the intellectual framework for slavery, while popes issued bulls explicitly authorizing enslavement and territorial conquest.

### Augustine's slavery theology: The foundation

**Augustine's City of God, Book XIX, Chapter 15 (413-426 CE)** establishes the theological foundation: "The prime cause, then, of slavery is sin, which brings man under the dominion of his fellow,—that which does not happen save by the judgment of God." Free online at ccel.org and newadvent.org, this passage naturalizes slavery as divine punishment for sin.

**Toni Alimi's Slaves of God: Augustine and Other Romans on Religion and Politics (Princeton, 2024, ISBN: 9780691244235)** provides essential scholarly analysis. Finalist for the American Academy of Religion Book Award, this "game-changing" work demonstrates slavery was integral to Augustine's views on law, rule, accountability, and citizenship. Augustine related chattel slavery to slavery to God, embedding human bondage in theological anthropology. The Christian Century called it a work that "should occasion a reckoning."

**Matthew Elia's companion works**—The Problem of the Christian Master (2024) and his peer-reviewed article "Slave Christologies: Augustine and the Enduring Trouble with the 'Form of a Slave'" (Journal of Theology, 2021, DOI: 10.1177/0020964320961668)—demonstrate that "slavery is not simply one moral 'issue' among others but an abiding presence in Augustine's thought, providing a metaphor that animates his treatment of God, sin, Christology, order, desire, virtue, and freedom."

### Aquinas extends the tradition

**Thomas Aquinas's Summa Theologica** continues Augustine's work. In Secunda Secundae, Question 57, Article 3 (available free at newadvent.org/summa/3057.htm), Aquinas argues: "some are naturally slaves according to the Philosopher [Aristotle]... slavery belongs to the right of nations." Reply to Objection 2 states slavery is "natural in the second way"—socially useful rather than naturally necessary, but still divinely sanctioned. The Supplement, Question 52, discusses slavery's impediment to matrimony, normalizing slavery within Christian social order.

**Ralph Neill's "Slavery in the Writings of Thomas Aquinas" (St. John's University, 2011)** available as PDF at sju.ca provides comprehensive analysis. Aquinas followed Aristotle in accepting natural slavery while arguing it served divine purposes.

### Papal bulls authorizing slavery and colonization

Three papal documents provide irrefutable primary evidence:

**Pope Nicholas V's Dum Diversas (18 June 1452)** grants Portugal "full and free permission to invade, search out, capture and subjugate the Saracens and pagans and any other unbelievers and enemies of Christ wherever they may be...and to reduce their persons into perpetual slavery." Available with Latin text at doctrineofdiscovery.org/dum-diversas/, this bull authorized the Portuguese slave trade from West Africa.

**Pope Nicholas V's Romanus Pontifex (8 January 1455)** expanded authorization, granting Portugal monopoly on African trade with explicit right "to reduce their persons to perpetual slavery." Church leaders argued slavery served as "natural deterrent and Christianizing influence to barbarous behavior among pagans"—theological justification embedded in official Church teaching.

**Pope Alexander VI's Inter Caetera (4 May 1493)** established the Doctrine of Discovery, granting Spain rights to colonize the Americas: "give, grant, and assign to you and your heirs and successors, kings of Castile and Leon, forever...all islands and mainlands found and to be found, discovered and to be discovered." Full Latin and English translation available at doctrineofdiscovery.org/assets/pdfs/Inter_Caetera_Modrow&Smith.pdf. This authorized Christian European claims on land inhabited by non-Christians, providing legal framework for dispossession.

**Pius Onyemechi Adiele's The Popes, the Catholic Church and the Transatlantic Enslavement of Black Africans 1418-1839 (Olms Verlag, 2017)** provides the most comprehensive scholarly treatment. Available open access through OAPEN Library at library.oapen.org, pages 289-407 analyze papal documents empowering Portugal to enslave Black Africans, with Latin texts of bulls in appendices. This is your essential scholarly source for understanding how official Catholic teaching authorized the slave trade for over four centuries.

### Christian creation of race-based slavery

**Katharine Gerbner's Christian Slavery: Conversion and Race in the Protestant Atlantic World (UPenn Press, 2018, ISBN: 9780812224368)** demonstrates how Protestant missionaries helped create modern concepts of race and white supremacy. Gerbner shows "Protestant Supremacy" excluded enslaved people from Christian communities precisely to maintain slavery—contradicting claims that Christianity opposed slavery. Reviews note it "shines a light on Christianity's complicity in the development of modern racism" across Caribbean and North American colonies from 1600s-1800s.

**Jennifer A. Glancy's Slavery in Early Christianity (Oxford, 2002/2006, ISBN: 9780195136098)** provides the first comprehensive analysis of early Christian ideology and practice of slavery. Glancy argues scholars drastically underestimated slavery's impact on early church structures, showing churches grew assuming slaveholding was the norm. Her focus on bodily experience of subjection and violation reveals how deeply slavery permeated early Christian communities.

**Charles Irons's The Origins of Proslavery Christianity (UNC Press, 2008)** examines theological arguments whites employed to justify slavery in Virginia, showing how Nat Turner's 1831 rebellion led to restrictions on black church attendance. Christian theology of slavery intensified rather than diminished as abolitionist pressure grew.

### Doctrine of Discovery: Christian colonialism codified

**Robert J. Miller et al.'s Discovering Indigenous Lands: The Doctrine of Discovery in the English Colonies (Oxford, 2010, ISBN: 9780199579815, DOI: 10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199579815.001.0001)** shows how England used Discovery doctrine to justify sovereignty over indigenous lands across Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and the United States. This papal-originated legal framework became international law governing colonial conquest.

**Mark Charles and Soong-Chan Rah's Unsettling Truths: The Ongoing, Dehumanizing Legacy of the Doctrine of Discovery (InterVarsity Press, 2019, ISBN: 9780830852987)** provides the most accessible comprehensive treatment, combining history, theology, and cultural commentary. This demonstrates how Discovery doctrine institutionalized white supremacy and American triumphalism with explicit Christian theological justification.

**Steven T. Newcomb's Pagans in the Promised Land: Decoding the Doctrine of Christian Discovery (Fulcrum, 2008)** offers essential Indigenous scholar perspective, attacking the presumption that American Indian nations are subject to U.S. plenary power based on Christian conquest theology.

**Steve Heinrichs and Cheryl Woelk's edited collection Yours, Mine, Ours: Unravelling the Doctrine of Discovery (Mennonite Church Canada, 2016)** includes essays by Miller, Andrew Fitzmaurice, and others, with comprehensive timeline of discovery and dispossession.

### Christian imperialism: Missionary complicity

**Emily Conroy-Krutz's Christian Imperialism: Converting the World in the Early American Republic (Cornell, 2015, ISBN: 9780801453533)** focuses on the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions (1812-1846), demonstrating American evangelical Protestants were motivated by explicit "Christian imperialism"—belief that Christian nations had duty to use colonial and commercial power to spread Christianity. Reviews note its "innovative approach to understanding Protestant triumphalism."

**Jean and John L. Comaroff's two-volume Of Revelation and Revolution (University of Chicago Press, 1991/1997)** provides foundational scholarship on how African subjectivity was fashioned by encounters with colonialism and Christianity in South Africa. The Comaroffs demonstrate creation of "rationalistic competitive individualism" through missionary activity inseparable from colonial domination.

### Christian violence: Crusades, Inquisition, religious wars

**"Christian Violence against Heretics, Jews and Muslims" (Chapter 23, The Cambridge World History of Violence, 2020, DOI: 10.1017/9781316661291.024)** provides comprehensive scholarly treatment showing violence against Jews, Muslims, and heretics was embedded in Latin-Christian theology, not aberrations from it.

**Jonathan Riley-Smith's "Christian Violence and the Crusades" (in Religious Violence between Christians and Jews, Palgrave Macmillan, 2002, DOI: 10.1057/9781403913821_1)** analyzes anti-Jewish violence during Crusades as theologically justified.

**Megan Cassidy-Welch's Crusades and Violence (Arc Humanities Press, 2020, ISBN: 9781641894753)** and **Jennifer Kolpacoff Deane's Medieval Heresy: Inquisition, Repression and Popular Movements** examine how Church developed inquisitorial procedures and generated fictional heresies through bureaucratic processes of persecution.

### Essential works challenging Christian exceptionalism

**Ned Blackhawk's The Rediscovery of America: Native American Peoples and the Unfolding of the Continent (Yale, 2023)**, winner of the 2023 National Book Award, provides essential counter-narrative recognizing Native Americans in U.S. history and addressing Doctrine of Discovery.

**Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz's An Indigenous Peoples' History of the United States (Beacon Press, 2014)** documents Christian justifications for colonization in accessible narrative form.

**Orlando Patterson's Slavery and Social Death (Harvard, 1982)** provides comparative study showing slavery existed across civilizations, challenging any civilization's claim to unique moral superiority.

### Key scholarly rebuttals for your video

The claim that Christian civilization is uniquely moral collapses under:

1. **1,800+ years of theological defense of slavery** by Christianity's most influential theologians
2. **Papal authorization of slavery and colonization** through official Church documents spanning four centuries
3. **Creation of race-based slavery** facilitated by Christian theology excluding converted enslaved people from religious community
4. **Doctrine of Discovery** as Christian legal framework for dispossession of indigenous peoples worldwide
5. **Systemic violence** through Crusades, Inquisitions, and religious wars embedded in Christian theology
6. **Colonial exploitation** carried out with explicit Christian missionary justification
7. **Moral progress often achieved despite or against Christian opposition**—abolition eventually emerged from secular Enlightenment values, not Christian teaching
