# Guatemala's Position on the Belize Territorial Dispute: Comprehensive Research for Documentary Balance

## Introduction: Understanding Guatemala's perspective

After comprehensive research into Guatemala's official position, academic scholarship, and neutral legal analysis, this report reveals that **Guatemala presents sophisticated legal arguments grounded in international law**—arguments significantly under-represented in English-language sources. While international legal experts believe Belize has the stronger case overall, Guatemala's position deserves serious documentary coverage to ensure balanced historical storytelling.

## 1. Guatemala's Legal Case at the International Court of Justice

### The Memorial and Reply submissions

On December 8, 2020, Guatemala filed its **500-page Memorial** with the ICJ, accompanied by 462 annexes, 8 maps, and 24 figures—a comprehensive legal brief Guatemala's Foreign Ministry called "historic." The Memorial claims **"all rights inherited from Spain at the moment of independence in 1821."** Guatemala's December 2022 Reply to Belize's Counter-Memorial reinforced these arguments with what the government described as "all factual and legal elements that bolster the Guatemalan claim."

Guatemala assembled a world-class legal team led by **Alain Pellet**, one of France's most distinguished international jurists and former member of the UN International Law Commission, signaling serious investment in making the strongest possible case.

### Core legal argument: Treaty of cession, not boundary demarcation

Guatemala's central contention reframes the entire dispute: **the 1859 Wyke-Aycinena Treaty was fundamentally a "treaty of cession" disguised as a boundary treaty**, not a simple border demarcation as Britain and Belize claim. This distinction is legally critical.

Guatemala argues the treaty had to be "disguised" because of the 1850 Clayton-Bulwer Treaty between the US and Britain, which forbade further British colonization in Central America. The 1859 treaty's preamble states the boundary "has not yet been ascertained," yet Article 1 declares boundaries "as they existed previous to and on the 1st day of January, 1850"—a contradiction Guatemala presents as evidence the treaty was actually territorial cession backdated to avoid violating Clayton-Bulwer.

### Article VII as condition precedent: Guatemala's strongest argument

The heart of Guatemala's legal case centers on **Article VII**, which committed both parties to "conjointly use their best efforts" to establish "the easiest communication" between the Atlantic coast near Belize and Guatemala City via cart-road or river routes.

**Guatemala's interpretation:**
- Article VII was **compensatory**—Guatemala's payment for ceding territory to Britain
- Road construction was a **condition precedent** for the treaty's validity
- Britain's failure rendered the entire treaty void under international law

The evidence Guatemala presents is substantial. In 1860, Captain Wray surveyed a route and estimated £145,000 cost over four years. Survey work began from Garbutt's Falls in 1861 but stopped. The **1863 Additional Convention** saw Britain agree to contribute £50,000—proving Britain initially recognized an obligation. Guatemala ratified this convention with "clarifications" that Britain rejected, causing it to lapse. By 1869, Britain declared itself "free from obligations of building the road" after Guatemala "flatly rejected" another negotiation attempt.

**The road was never built**. Guatemala argues this breach of an essential treaty provision invalidates the entire agreement under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, which permits termination for material breach of provisions "essential to accomplishment of object or purpose."

### Ambiguity favors Guatemala's interpretation

Significantly, **the treaty negotiators themselves disagreed** on Article VII's meaning. British negotiator Charles Lennox Wyke believed the burden split 50/50 between Britain and Guatemala. Guatemalan negotiator Pedro de Aycinena understood Britain bore full responsibility. Wyke added Article VII himself, surprising even the British government. Under treaty interpretation principles, ambiguity should be resolved **against the drafter**—Britain.

### What Guatemala is requesting from the ICJ

Guatemala seeks **territorial, insular, and maritime awards** far more extensive than commonly understood:

**Land territory:** The region between the **Sibun River and Sarstoon River**—approximately **11,030 square kilometers, representing more than half of Belize's territory**. This includes southern Cayo District and all of Stann Creek and Toledo Districts.

**Insular territories:** Guatemala claims **all cayes and islands** along Belize's coast, from Ambergris Caye in the north to the Sapodilla Cayes in the south. Vice Minister Rodrigo Vielmann stated in 2024: "We are claiming the entirety of the insular territory...on the basis that the Belizean population nor the British settlements never historically held title."

**Maritime zones:** All territorial seas, exclusive economic zones, and maritime areas associated with these claimed territories, potentially giving Guatemala permanent sovereign access to the Caribbean Sea.

**Underlying request:** An ICJ declaration that the 1859 treaty is **void ab initio** (void from the beginning), requiring territory to "revert" to Guatemala.

## 2. Guatemalan Government Official Statements (2020-2024)

### President Bernardo Arévalo's position (2024-present)

In his September 2024 UN General Assembly debut, President Arévalo emphasized Guatemala's commitment to international law: **"By opting for dialogue and international legal mechanisms, we have demonstrated that peaceful means and respect for multilateral institutions are the most effective way to resolve disputes."** He pledged to honor the ICJ ruling regardless of outcome, calling it a path toward "a just and lasting solution that respects the dignity and rights of both nations."

Following September 2024 military confrontations at the Sarstoon River, Arévalo stated: **"From the Guatemalan side, we need to be clear about the defence of sovereignty"** while preventing escalation. Guatemala's Foreign Ministry declared that Belize Defense Force units "entered Guatemalan national territory through the Sarstoon River without the required authorisation, in violation of the territorial integrity of Guatemala"—framing border incidents as defensive responses to foreign incursion into what Guatemala considers its own territory.

### President Alejandro Giammattei's position (2020-2024)

When filing the Memorial on December 8, 2020, Giammattei's government issued a historic declaration: **"The people of Guatemala can be assured that in the Memorial, all rights inherited from Spain at the time of independence in 1821 are being claimed."** Foreign Minister Pedro Brolo called it "a patriotic endeavor of the highest order," submitting 8 volumes totaling 4,813 pages to the ICJ.

Giammattei simultaneously expressed desire for improved bilateral relations, telling the OAS Secretary General: "There are many things that unite us more than fighting"—signaling Guatemala views the legal dispute as separate from opportunities for trade and tourism cooperation.

### Guatemala's historical narrative

The official Guatemalan government position rests on three pillars:

**Spanish colonial sovereignty (pre-1821):** The territory was under Spanish sovereignty as part of the Capitanía General de Guatemala. British presence was limited to specific logging concessions granted by Spain in treaties of 1783 and 1786—concessions that granted economic exploitation rights but **not sovereignty**.

**Inheritance through uti possidetis juris (1821):** Upon independence, Guatemala inherited all Spanish territorial rights based on uti possidetis juris, a foundational principle in Latin American international law. The territory of Belice was part of the Province of Verapaz and thus passed to independent Guatemala.

**British breach and treaty nullity (1859-1946):** Guatemala maintains it signed the 1859 treaty ceding territory **"in exchange for a road to the Caribbean Sea, but the United Kingdom did not fulfill the promise."** After nearly a century of British non-compliance, Guatemala's Congress declared the treaty null and void in 1946, with the 1945 Constitution declaring Belice part of Guatemalan territory.

### The 2018 referendum mandate

On April 15, 2018, **95.88% of Guatemalan voters** approved submitting the dispute to the ICJ (though turnout was only 25%). This overwhelming support demonstrates national consensus that Guatemala has legitimate claims deserving international adjudication. The government presents this as fulfilling a constitutional mandate and the democratic will of the Guatemalan people.

## 3. Guatemalan Academic and Legal Scholarship

### Leading scholars supporting Guatemala's position

**Alberto Herrarte González** remains the most prominent academic voice. His definitive work **"La Cuestión de Belice: Estudio Histórico-Jurídico de la Controversia" (2000)** is considered indispensable for understanding Guatemala's historical and legal arguments. Herrarte, affiliated with Universidad de San Carlos de Guatemala (USAC), provided extensive documentation of Britain's failure to fulfill Article VII obligations and Guatemala's continuous legal protests from 1884 onward.

**Dr. Armando de la Torre** of Universidad Francisco Marroquín argues forcefully: **"The United Kingdom has never exercised sovereign jurisdiction over the territory of Belize. Guatemala, on the contrary, has done so since 1821, and has never ceased to claim it."** His scholarship emphasizes continuous Guatemalan claims and the legal distinction between British usufruct rights (limited economic concessions) and genuine sovereignty.

**USAC academic research**, particularly Tikia Anaite Granados Martinez's 2015 thesis, applies international relations systems theory to argue the 1859 treaty was signed under **asymmetric power relations** and pressure. Granados documents that the territory from Río Sibún to Río Sarstún was "usurped" beyond original Spanish concessions, and Article VII constituted an essential condition whose breach nullified the treaty.

### The 1938 "Libro Blanco" (White Book)

Guatemala's Foreign Ministry published the comprehensive **"Libro Blanco: Relativo a la Cuestión de Belice"** in October 1938, compiling treaties, diplomatic correspondence, and legal arguments. This official government publication remains cited by contemporary scholars as authoritative documentation that Britain never had legitimate title to Belizean territory and systematically violated treaty obligations.

### Scholarly arguments on the 1931 Exchange of Notes

This remains a crucial evidentiary issue. In 1931, Guatemala and Britain exchanged notes confirming border markers at Garbutt Falls and Gracias á Dios Falls—which Belize argues demonstrates Guatemala recognized and implemented the 1859 treaty 80 years later.

**Guatemalan scholars counter** with three arguments:

1. **Not properly ratified:** The notes were never ratified by Guatemala's Congress, as required for territorial matters under Guatemalan constitutional law
2. **Limited scope:** The notes dealt only with specific boundary markers, not validation of the entire treaty's legitimacy  
3. **Article VII still unfulfilled:** Accepting markers for partial demarcation didn't waive the fundamental breach of Britain's road-building obligation

Scholar Granados characterizes the 1931 notes as merely a "corollary" to follow up limited demarcation work, not ratification of treaty validity. Guatemala had protested the treaty's validity in 1884 and would formally declare it void in 1946, demonstrating the 1931 notes didn't represent acquiescence.

### Alternative interpretation: Why sign in 1931 if disputing the treaty?

Guatemalan scholarship explains this apparent contradiction through **selective implementation**: Guatemala was willing to place boundary markers for the limited purpose of defining "part of the boundary line" while simultaneously maintaining its position that Britain's failure on Article VII rendered the treaty fundamentally invalid. This reflects a pragmatic approach to managing the border while preserving legal claims—not acceptance of British sovereignty.

### Border incidents from Guatemala's perspective

Guatemalan academics frame border confrontations as resulting from "illegal occupation" of Guatemalan territory. When Guatemalan farmers cross into what Belize considers its territory, Guatemala views them as operating on Guatemalan land. The Sarstoon River incidents reflect Guatemala defending sovereignty over what it considers its own territory against foreign military forces.

### Quality assessment: Scholarship versus propaganda

Guatemalan academic work—particularly Herrarte's research and USAC theses—represents **serious scholarly engagement** with historical and legal questions. These works cite international law frameworks (Vienna Convention, ICJ jurisprudence), provide extensive bibliographies, and follow academic methodology. However, nationalist elements are present: emotional language about "usurpation," strong emphasis on asymmetric power relations, and limited engagement with counter-arguments or Belizean self-determination claims.

Most significantly, **there is virtually no Guatemalan academic literature critically examining weaknesses in Guatemala's own arguments**—reflecting national consensus on the issue rather than balanced scholarly debate.

## 4. Alternative Interpretations of Key Events

### The 1859 treaty signing under duress

Guatemalan historians emphasize that **Rafael Carrera's government faced existential threats** when negotiating the 1859 treaty. Filibuster William Walker threatened Central America, and Guatemala needed British arms and protection. The treaty was negotiated under **profoundly asymmetric power relations** between a regional power (Britain) and a newly independent, vulnerable state (Guatemala). Guatemalan negotiator Pedro de Aycinena has been criticized by nationalist scholars as excessively anglophile, potentially failing to protect Guatemalan interests.

This context supports Guatemala's argument that Article VII wasn't merely ancillary but **essential compensation** that made the unequal treaty palatable to Guatemala—similar to how financial compensation makes modern territorial transfers acceptable.

### Guatemala's perspective on Maya populations

While less developed in formal legal scholarship, Guatemala suggests Maya populations including Q'eqchi' and Mopan inhabited the region as part of the Spanish Capitanía General's Verapaz province. Their presence supports Guatemala's claim of territorial continuity. This represents more of a supporting cultural-historical claim rather than a primary legal argument in ICJ submissions.

### The colonial expansion beyond treaty limits

A core Guatemalan argument centers on **British territorial expansion beyond concession boundaries**. Spain's 1783 Treaty of Versailles granted timber-cutting rights between Río Hondo and Río Belice (4,802 km²). The 1786 London Convention extended this to Río Sibún (adding 1,884 km²). Both treaties **explicitly reserved Spanish sovereignty** and prohibited fortifications, settlements, or cultivation.

Guatemala contends British settlers expanded beyond Río Sibún into the Sibún-Sarstún region without authorization—**usurping approximately 4,342 square kilometers**. This territory was never legitimately acquired through any Spanish concession or formal cession, making Britain's possession based on "usurpation, not legitimate acquisition."

### The language barrier and under-representation of Guatemala's case

A crucial finding: **Guatemala's sophisticated legal arguments are systematically under-represented in English-language literature**. Factors include:

- **Volume imbalance:** Far more pro-Belize than pro-Guatemala analysis available in English
- **Belize is English-speaking; Guatemala is Spanish-speaking**
- **Commonwealth connections** create English-language sympathies  
- **Belizean government has invested heavily in English-language advocacy**
- **Guatemalan legal scholarship in Spanish not translated or disseminated internationally**

This creates a misleading impression that Guatemala's case lacks merit, when reality may be that the case is simply less accessible to English readers. No English-language sources were found presenting strong advocacy for Guatemala comparable to Belizean government sources.

### Most neutral sources identified

The **American Society of International Law (ASIL) Insight** by Montserrat Gorina-Ysern (2000) provides the most balanced English-language analysis, presenting both Guatemala's and Belize's legal positions objectively without favoring either side. Other relatively neutral sources include University of Navarra (Spain) coverage and ETH Zurich (Switzerland) analysis—European institutions without Commonwealth connections.

### What constitutes genuine neutrality versus justified assessment

Importantly, the consensus that "Belize has the stronger case" may be **objectively justified** based on ICJ jurisprudence regarding boundary stability, effective occupation, and international recognition—not merely bias. However, the **under-representation and dismissive treatment of Guatemala's arguments** in English sources creates incomplete understanding of genuine legal complexity.

## 6. Guatemala's Evidence of British Bad Faith

### Comprehensive timeline of British non-compliance

Guatemala presents detailed documentary evidence of Britain's failure to fulfill Article VII:

**1859-1863:** No action taken on road construction for several years after treaty signing, despite the commitment being central to negotiations.

**1860-1861:** Captain Wray's survey estimated £145,000 cost and four-year timeline. Survey work from Garbutt's Falls began but stopped after just 23 miles, allegedly due to "Indian activity."

**August 5, 1863:** The Additional Convention saw Britain agree to seek £50,000 from Parliament as its contribution, with both sides committed to ratify within six months. Britain ratified; Guatemala ratified in May 1865 with "clarifications" Britain rejected. The Convention lapsed.

**1869:** Britain declared itself **"free from obligations of building the road"** after Guatemala "flatly rejected" another negotiation attempt.

**1929-1934:** During boundary demarcation work, Guatemala inquired about Article VII compliance in 1933. In 1934, Britain proposed a compromise—Britain builds road from Belize City to Petén edge, Guatemala continues to its capital. Guatemala refused, demanding the original £50,000 plus interest. Britain rejected this.

### Britain's own recognition of obligation

Guatemala points to Britain's actions as evidence of acknowledged obligation:

- **Britain's willingness to negotiate the 1863 Additional Convention** proves it recognized an obligation
- **Offering £50,000** shows admission that compensation was owed  
- **Britain's 1934 compromise proposal** admits ongoing obligation even 75 years later
- **Survey work and negotiation efforts** demonstrate Britain initially took Article VII seriously

### The paradox of selective compliance

Guatemala highlights that Britain implemented some treaty provisions (boundary demarcation) while refusing others (Article VII), demonstrating **selective compliance** rather than good-faith treaty execution. The 1931 Exchange of Notes placed boundary markers while Britain continued refusing road construction or compensation—showing Britain chose which obligations to honor.

## 7. Strong Points of Guatemala's Case: Where the ICJ Might Rule Favorably

### Unlikely territorial award but possible limited victories

International legal experts—including neutral third-party scholars—believe the ICJ is **extremely unlikely to award land territory to Guatemala**. The Court strongly favors boundary stability, and Belize has exercised sovereignty since 1859/1862 with universal international recognition. The 1931 Exchange of Notes provides powerful evidence of Guatemala's treaty recognition, and modern international law emphasizes self-determination—Belize's population overwhelmingly identifies as Belizean.

However, Guatemala has legitimate arguments in several areas:

### Maritime access rights: Guatemala's best chance

Article VII arguably intended to provide Guatemala **practical access to the Caribbean Sea**. Modern international law increasingly recognizes landlocked or geographically disadvantaged states' access rights through UNCLOS (UN Convention on the Law of Sea) provisions for transit rights. The ICJ might order **negotiated maritime access arrangements** that grant Guatemala permanent passage rights through Belizean territorial waters—partially fulfilling the original treaty's spirit without territorial transfer.

### Maritime boundary delimitation

Current maritime boundaries between Guatemala and Belize remain **undefined**. Guatemala claims maritime zones adjacent to Amatique Bay. The ICJ will likely need to **delimit EEZ and territorial sea boundaries**, and Guatemala might gain more favorable maritime boundaries than currently claimed by Belize, potentially including fishing zones and offshore resource access.

### Compensation without territorial change

Even if the treaty remains valid, the ICJ might find Guatemala entitled to **damages for Britain's Article VII breach** without voiding boundary provisions. International courts can order compensation without destabilizing borders. The practical issue is that the **UK, not Belize, would be the liable party**—creating a complex remedial question.

### Legal acknowledgment of historical grievance

The ICJ might **acknowledge Guatemala had legitimate grievance** regarding Article VII, finding Britain breached treaty obligations, while concluding this doesn't invalidate boundary provisions. Such a ruling would provide Guatemala with historical vindication and moral acknowledgment while preserving territorial stability.

### Joint development arrangements

Following ICJ precedent in other cases, the Court might order **joint development zones** for disputed maritime areas, offshore resources, or cayes—allowing both countries to benefit from shared resources while avoiding territorial transfer.

### The legitimacy of uti possidetis juris

Guatemala's reliance on **uti possidetis juris** is not frivolous—it's a well-established principle in Latin American international law that the ICJ has applied in multiple cases (including Burkina Faso/Mali in 1986). While Guatemala faces challenges proving effective Spanish control and overcoming Belize's effective occupation, the legal foundation itself is sound. The ETH Zurich analysis notes uti possidetis is "even being used today in the settlement of territorial disputes at the ICJ."

### Article VII breach has factual basis

Guatemala's claim that Britain breached Article VII is **historically accurate**—the road was never built, and Britain formally refused the obligation in 1869. While modern treaty law suggests this wouldn't void boundary provisions, the breach itself is undeniable. This gives Guatemala's moral argument strength even if its legal remedy fails.

### Treaty interpretation genuinely complex

The ambiguity in Article VII—with negotiators themselves disagreeing on interpretation—represents **genuine legal complexity**, not frivolous argumentation. International law principles for treaty interpretation would need careful application to determine whether Article VII was essential or ancillary, compensatory or merely cooperative.
