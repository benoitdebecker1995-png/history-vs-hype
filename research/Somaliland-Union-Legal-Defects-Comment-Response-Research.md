## Claims Verified

### Claim Set 1: Historical Naming and Cartography

#### Claim 1: "There was never a united Somalia before the Europeans"
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "Somali history has never known a centralized political authority that has ruled the Somali region in its entirety. Prior to European colonization, Islamic and tribal sultanates were confined to specific regions."
- **Evidence:** "Somali were not subjects and they had no rulers. They were free and their clans were the physical embodiments of that freedom. The Somali bush was like the sea and the camel herders were its sailors... When a state came, it was foreign and it was immediately perceived as an imposition."
- **Sources:** Mahmoud, *Understanding the Secession of Somaliland*, page 25; Prunier, *The Country That Does Not Exist*, page 3

#### Claim 2: "Antique maps show the name 'Somali Coast' referred specifically to the North"
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "Arab geographers... refer to the Hamitic peoples (the Galla and Somali) of the north and centre by the classical name 'Berberi'... to their south [were] the Zanj."
- **Evidence:** "The name 'Somali' is first recorded... in the songs celebrating [the Abyssinian King Yeshaq's] victories over the Muslims [of the Sultanate of Adal in the North]."
- **Sources:** Lewis, *A Modern History of Somalia*, pages 20, 25
- **Note:** First written mention of "Somali" (15th century) referred to the northern sultanate of Adal. "Berberia" was the classical term for the north; "Zanj" or "Benadir" described the southern coastal regions.

#### Claim 3: "The territory known as Somalia has a distinct and different history from Somaliland"
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "For three quarters of a century before the union the two territories... had been administered by two different European states. The Northern laws and institutions were those of a British dependency; the South followed the Italian system."
- **Evidence:** "British rule in British Somaliland sought primarily to deny other empires control... Italy, however, engaged in a protracted and violent effort to establish a plantation colony in Italian Somaliland."
- **Sources:** Contini, *The Somali Republic: An Experiment in Legal Integration*, page vii; McPherson-Smith, *Better Off Alone*, page 203

---

### Claim Set 2: Paolo Contini Legal Analysis

#### Claim 4: Neither Act of Union was signed bilaterally
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "The Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law, and the Somalia Act of Union were both drafted in the form of bilateral agreements, but neither of them was signed by the representatives of the two territories."
- **Sources:** Cotran, *Legal Problems Arising out of the Formation of the Somali Republic*, page 1012; Act of Union 1960 Commentary, page 1
- **Note:** Somaliland's legislature passed their version (Law of Union) on June 27, but no joint signatures occurred.

#### Claim 5: Somalia's Act of Union was only approved "in principle"
- **Status:** ✅ SMOKING GUN
- **Evidence:** "The Somalia Act of Union was approved 'in principle' but never enacted into law."
- **Sources:** Cotran, page 1012; Contini, page 9
- **Note:** The Legislative Assembly of the Trust Territory requested a "definitive single text of the Act of Union, to be submitted to the National Assembly for approval." This text was never created.

#### Claim 6: The decree-law of July 1, 1960 lapsed (Article 63)
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "The decree law of July 1, 1960, did not come into effect since it was not converted into law in accordance with the Constitution."
- **Sources:** Cotran, page 1012; Contini, page 10
- **Note:** Article 63(3) required decree-laws to be presented to the National Assembly for conversion within five days, or they "shall cease to have effect ab initio."

#### Claim 7: Article 10 safeguards existed and were overridden
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** The Somaliland Law of Union Article 10 established a "Special Commission" with an "equal number of former members" from both assemblies to establish "uniformity between the laws, institutions, public services and social services."
- **Sources:** Act of Union 1960 Law No. 1, page 351; Cotran, page 1012
- **Note:** Somaliland's Law of Union "purported to derogate in some respects from the Constitution of the Somali Republic." The 1961 Act established constitutional supremacy, repealing inconsistent legislation.

#### Claim 8: Somalia's Constitution lacked mechanisms for amalgamation
- **Status:** ⚠️ CONTESTED/OVERSIMPLIFIED
- **Evidence:** Contini notes that on June 18, 1960, a transitional provision was added to the draft Constitution stating: "Immediately after signing the Act of Union... the new National Assembly shall elect... a Provisional President."
- **Sources:** Contini, page 8; Act of Union 1960 Commentary, page 1
- **Note:** A mechanism WAS added, but it was defective because it was predicated on "the signing of the Act of Union," which never happened.

#### Claim 9: Legal instruments lapsed, union never "legally perfected"
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "Thus when the union was formed, its precise legal effects had not been laid down in any instrument having binding force in both parts of the State."
- **Sources:** Contini, page 10; Cotran, page 1012

---

### Claim Set 3: Article 10 Special Commission (Critical Finding)

#### Claim 10: Article 10 promised 50/50 Special Commission
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "There shall be established a Special Commission... the members whereof... shall consist of an equal number of former members of the Legislative Assembly of Somalia and former members of the legislative Assembly of Somaliland."
- **Evidence:** "The Special Commission shall examine into and make recommendations... for the purpose of establishing uniformity between the laws, institutions, public services and social services."
- **Sources:** Act of Union 1960 Law No. 1, pages 350-351

#### Claim 11: Law No. 5 of 1961 nullified these safeguards
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "The 1961 retrospective Act... specifically repealed 'the provisions of the Union of Somaliland and Somalia (Law No. 1 of 1960)' except for Article 11(4)... The Act expressly provides for the supremacy of the Constitution by repealing all previous legislation inconsistent with it."
- **Sources:** Ibrahim Hashi Jama, *Act of Union Commentary*, page 2; Cotran, page 1012

#### Claim 12: The Special Commission was NEVER formed as promised
- **Status:** ✅ HIGH CONFIDENCE
- **Evidence:** "The legal formalities, as agreed, were not completed properly... the Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law did not have any legal validity in the South."
- **Evidence:** "The Act of Union formula of a Republic Legislative consisting of the Somaliland Legislative Assembly (of 33) and Somalia's Legislative Assembly (of 90) was jettisoned a few years later."
- **Sources:** Ibrahim Hashi Jama, *Act of Union Commentary*, pages 1-3
- **Note:** A "Consultative Commission for Integration" was appointed in October 1960, but it was NOT the 50/50 bilateral body promised in Article 10. The equal representation formula was "jettisoned."

### The Core Legal Debate

**Contini's conclusion:** Despite documenting all procedural failures, Contini concluded that "a full and lawful union was formed by the will of the peoples" and that Law No. 5 (January 31, 1961) "clarified" and retroactively cured the defect.

**Somaliland's position:** A retroactive law cannot cure what was void ab initio. You cannot breathe legal life into a treaty that never legally existed.

**Supporting precedent:** The 1963 Hazelwood ruling — British Judge Hazelwood acquitted northern officers, ruling "in the absence of an Act of Union, the court had no jurisdiction over Somaliland." (Prunier, page 21)

---

## Key Insight

The union failed on TWO levels:
1. **Procedural:** Unsigned agreements, "in principle" approval, lapsed decree-law
2. **Substantive:** The Article 10 safeguards (50/50 Special Commission, equal representation) were NEVER implemented

This is stronger than the procedural argument alone — it shows the promises made to secure Somaliland's participation were never fulfilled.
