## Claim 2: 1961 Military Coup Attempt

### ✅ VERIFIED - Important context not in current script

**Verified facts:**
- Date: December 10, 1961 (6 months after referendum)
- Perpetrators: At least 23 northern junior officers (British-trained)
- Objective: Restore Somaliland independence, end the union
- Outcome: Failed within hours, conspirators put on trial
- Legal verdict: British judge ACQUITTED them, ruling no legitimate Act of Union existed

**Verified quotes:**

> "The 1961 revolt in Somalia was an unsuccessful revolt and coup d'état attempt in northern regions of the Somali Republic which took place in December 1961. The coup plotters, a group of northern junior officers, intended to restore the independence of the State of Somaliland."
> — Wikipedia, 1961 revolt in Somalia

> "After the Trust Territory of Somaliland was unified with the State of Somaliland in 1960, it was discovered that the two polities had been unified under different Acts of Union. The newly unified Somali Republic's parliament promptly created a new Act of Union for all of Somalia, but this new Act was widely rejected in the former State of Somaliland."
> — Wikipedia, 1961 revolt in Somalia

> "The conspirators were put on trial, and the British judge acquitted them, reasoning that there existed no legitimate Act of Union."
> — Wikipedia, 1961 revolt in Somalia

## Claim 4: 1971 ICJ Advisory Opinion on Namibia

### ✅ VERIFIED - Establishes self-determination principle, but indirect application to Somaliland

**Case:** Legal Consequences for States of the Continued Presence of South Africa in Namibia (South West Africa) notwithstanding Security Council Resolution 276 (1970)
**Date:** June 21, 1971
**Court:** International Court of Justice

**Key holdings:**

> "The subsequent development of international law in regard to non-self-governing territories, as enshrined in the Charter of the United Nations, made the principle of self-determination applicable to all of them."
> — ICJ Namibia Advisory Opinion, 1971

> "The Court found that the continued presence of South Africa in Namibia was illegal and that South Africa was under an obligation to withdraw its administration immediately."
> — ICJ Summary

> "Developments left little doubt that the ultimate objective of the sacred trust was the self-determination and independence of the peoples concerned."
> — ICJ Namibia Advisory Opinion, 1971

**How it relates to Somaliland:**

**Pro-Somaliland argument (commenter's implication):**
- 1971 ICJ affirmed self-determination as fundamental principle for non-self-governing territories
- If Somalia is denying Somaliland's self-determination, states have obligation not to recognize that situation
- Territorial integrity cannot override self-determination

**Limitations of this argument:**
- Namibia case involved ILLEGAL colonial occupation (never recognized)
- Somaliland's 1960 union WAS internationally recognized for 31 years
- Namibia had no "voluntary union" element - pure apartheid occupation
- Not directly applicable precedent

**Script impact:**
- Could add to "precedent" section alongside Baltic States
- But note key difference: Namibia = illegal occupation, Somaliland = contested union validity
- Strengthens self-determination argument but doesn't resolve voluntary union complication
