# Fact-Checking JD Vance's Historical Claims from Ole Miss

JD Vance's October 29, 2025 speech at Ole Miss contained sweeping historical claims about Christianity's role in ending child sacrifice, abolishing slavery, and founding human rights concepts. **Academic evidence contradicts each major assertion.** While Christianity has contributed to Western civilization, Vance's claims erase pre-Christian achievements, non-Western traditions, Indigenous agency, and Christianity's own extensive record of violence and persecution. Historians, archaeologists, and scholars across disciplines have documented how these narratives misrepresent the archaeological record, ignore chronology, and perpetuate a civilizational superiority myth that contemporary scholarship rejects.

## The child sacrifice narrative: geography matters

Vance claimed that European settlers "found a very widespread child sacrifice" in the New World and that "Christian civilization ended the practice." This conflates different regions and time periods while erasing a catastrophic genocide.

**Archaeological evidence shows child sacrifice did occur in some pre-Columbian cultures**—but geography is crucial. Mesoamerican societies including the Aztec and Maya practiced ritualized child sacrifice. A 2024 Nature study documented remains of approximately 100 children over 200+ years at Chichén Itzá, while the Chimú culture's mass sacrifice at Huanchaquito-Las Llamas involved around 140 children circa 1450 CE. The Inca Capacocha ritual, documented in PNAS, involved mountaintop sacrifices. These were specific religious practices within stratified societies, not the "widespread" phenomenon Vance describes.

**English colonists who arrived in North America encountered entirely different cultures.** The Eastern Woodland peoples—Powhatan, Wampanoag, Iroquois, Algonquin, Cherokee—have no archaeological evidence of child sacrifice practices. The only documented exception was the Pawnee Morning Star Ceremony in Nebraska, practiced exclusively by the Skidi band and last recorded in 1838, well after initial English settlement. As the Cambridge World History of Violence notes, "Colonial discourse tended to construct a relatively homogeneous colonial 'other,' [but] native ritual violence differed considerably between and among the stratified and the egalitarian indigenous societies."

Vance's most bizarre claim—about excavating "ancient brothels" and finding "a lot of children who were buried with them"—appears completely fabricated. The few contested archaeological cases involve Roman or Medieval European sites, not pre-Columbian Americas. The Ashkelon bathhouse in Israel (400-500 CE) and Hambleden villa in England show infant remains, but archaeologists heavily dispute the "brothel" interpretation. Alternative theories include mother goddess shrines, midwifery sites, or natural infant mortality. A 2025 study in Archaeological and Anthropological Sciences examining a Belgian medieval site found evidence that an infant was breastfed and cared for, directly challenging "predominant narratives of abortion and infanticide with medieval sex workers."

**Christian colonizers did not morally enlighten Indigenous peoples—they brought catastrophe.** Between 1492 and 1800, an estimated 74% of the Indigenous population was wiped out. The Taíno population declined from 1.5-3 million to extinction by 1555. Bartolomé de las Casas, a Spanish priest, documented "systematic torture, rape, and mutilation that Spaniards exacted on the indigenous people." The Papal "Doctrine of Discovery" (only repudiated by the Vatican in 2023) declared non-Christians as "non-humans and without rights," providing theological justification for enslavement and land theft.

The violence extended for centuries. Indian Residential Schools and Boarding Schools, operating from the 1800s until 1997 in Canada, forcibly separated Indigenous children from their families under a "Kill the Indian, save the man" policy. Physical, sexual, and mental abuse was documented across hundreds of institutions. Multiple scholars, including David Stannard in American Holocaust, classify U.S. actions as genocidal. Where practices like the Pawnee ceremony did end, it was often through Indigenous agency—leader Petalesharo rescued a captive girl in 1816—not through Christian moral persuasion.

As Maarten Jansen and Gabina Pérez of Leiden University argue, "A critical analysis of the early colonial texts provokes serious doubts about their veracity...It is time for a re-examination of those engrained stereotypes, not only to gain a better understanding of the indigenous cultural heritage, but also to assess how western ideas about other peoples in general are still under the spell of colonial propaganda."

## Britain and slavery: neither first nor primarily Christian

Vance asserted that "It was a Christian empire, Great Britain, that abolished slavery to begin with." This is chronologically false and vastly oversimplifies the factors behind abolition.

**Britain was not first.** The Republic of Dubrovnik banned slavery in 1416—391 years before Britain's slave trade abolition. France declared slavery illegal on French soil in 1315 (though later reinstated in colonies). Japan abolished chattel slavery in 1590 under Toyotomi Hideyoshi. In the Americas, Vermont abolished slavery in 1777, Pennsylvania in 1780, and Massachusetts effectively ended it through court decisions in 1783. Most significantly, Haiti abolished slavery through successful revolution (1791-1804), establishing the first nation founded by formerly enslaved people—three decades before Britain's full abolition in 1833.

Britain's timeline reveals complexity: the 1772 Somerset case ruled slavery had no basis in English common law, the 1807 Slave Trade Act abolished the international trade, and the 1833 Slavery Abolition Act (effective 1834) ended slavery in most British territories. Full emancipation didn't occur until 1838 after an "apprenticeship" period, and slavery wasn't illegal throughout the entire British Empire until 1937.

**Christianity's role was deeply contradictory.** While Quakers and evangelical abolitionists like William Wilberforce opposed slavery, Christians extensively justified and perpetuated it. Confederate President Jefferson Davis declared slavery "was established by decree of Almighty God...it is sanctioned in the Bible, in both Testaments, from Genesis to Revelation." Baptist leader Richard Furman argued "the right of holding slaves is clearly established in the Holy Scriptures, both by precept and example." Evangelist George Whitefield campaigned to legalize slavery in Georgia in 1751. The Methodist Church split in 1844 over slavery, and the Southern Baptist Convention formed in 1845 specifically to defend it. Pope Nicholas V in 1452 authorized the Portuguese to enslave "Saracens, pagans and other enemies of Christ."

**Economic factors and enslaved people's resistance were central to abolition.** Historian Eric Williams's groundbreaking Capitalism and Slavery (1944) argued that when British capitalism found "the West Indian monopoly a nuisance, they destroyed West Indian slavery." By the 1830s, Caribbean plantations faced competition from more efficient producers, sugar prices fell below production costs, and Manchester's industrial interests viewed the slave-based system as an impediment to free trade. Williams noted that British publishers rejected his work for decades because suggesting economic rather than humanitarian motives was "contrary to the British tradition."

Crucially, enslaved people's own resistance forced change. The Haitian Revolution demonstrated that enslaved populations could defeat European powers. The Baptist War in Jamaica (1831-1832), led by Samuel Sharpe, involved 60,000 of Jamaica's 300,000 enslaved people in what was originally planned as a peaceful general strike. When white missionaries testified about the brutal colonial suppression, Parliament was outraged—directly contributing to the 1833 Abolition Act. Modern historians emphasize that enslaved people's resistance, not just white abolitionists, was central to ending slavery.

**Britain profited enormously before abolition.** British ships transported 3.4 million Africans between 1640 and 1807, with 450,000 dying during the Middle Passage. When slavery was abolished, the British government paid £20 million to slaveholders—40% of the government budget, equivalent to hundreds of billions today. The debt wasn't fully paid until 2015. Enslaved people received nothing. The Bishop of Exeter received £12,700 for 665 enslaved people; the Earl of Harewood received £26,309 for 2,554 enslaved people.

## Human rights: multiple philosophical traditions, Enlightenment foundations

Vance claimed "the very idea that human beings have rights are a Christian concept" and that "respecting every single person as an individual...that's a Christian concept." Historians and philosophers of human rights fundamentally disagree.

**Concepts of natural law and human dignity predate Christianity by centuries.** Ancient Greek philosophy articulated universal principles: Sophocles' Antigone distinguished between natural and written law, Aristotle separated "natural justice" from "legal justice" in his Nicomachean Ethics, and Stoics like Cicero (106-43 BCE) developed concepts of universal natural law, stating "true law is right reason in agreement with nature...one and the same Law, eternal and unchangeable." The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy notes these concepts had "a history spanning over two thousand years" predating Christianity.

**Non-Christian traditions developed robust human dignity concepts.** Buddhist philosophy emphasized Buddha-nature—all beings possess inherent dignity and capacity for enlightenment—alongside the Five Precepts paralleling international human rights. Buddhist scholar L.P.N. Perera asked, "if all could attain Buddhahood what greater equality in dignity and rights can there be?" Confucianism centered on ren (benevolence) and yi (justice/righteousness), with Mencius arguing that people have inherent rights and that rulers exist to serve them. In a 1947 UNESCO report, Chinese philosopher Lo Chung-Shu noted that Confucianism taught "when the ruler no longer rules for welfare of people, it is the right of the people to revolt." Islamic Golden Age scholars developed systematic philosophy emphasizing human reason and dignity through the Mu'tazila school and philosophers including Al-Kindi, Al-Farabi, Avicenna, and Averroes.

**The Enlightenment positioned itself against religious authority.** The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy is explicit: "Prior to the Enlightenment, ethical reflection begins from religious doctrines concerning God and the afterlife. During the Enlightenment, this changes." Enlightenment philosophy was characterized by "suspicion or hostility toward other forms or carriers of authority (such as tradition, superstition, prejudice, myth and miracles)" and "generally requires opposing the role of established religion in directing thought and action."

Immanuel Kant provided revolutionary justification for human rights "grounded within the authority of human reason" rather than divine command. His Categorical Imperative derived universal moral law from reason itself. As the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy notes, for Kant "any such rights originate in the formal properties of human reason, and not the will of some super-human being." This was profound because it grounded rights in "equality and moral autonomy" without requiring theological foundation.

Voltaire directed his polemic "mostly against the Catholic Church" with his famous "Écrasez l'infâme" (Crush the infamous). The violent religious wars that bloodied Europe "motivate the development of secular, this-worldly ethics," requiring Enlightenment thinkers to "confront the problem of developing ethical systems on a secular, broadly naturalistic basis."

**Christianity's historical record on individual rights was abysmal.** The Spanish Inquisition operated for 356 years (1478-1834), using torture, forced conversions, and execution against crypto-Jews, Muslims, Protestants, and alleged witches. A 2018 PNAS study found that "municipalities with stronger inquisitorial presence show lower economic performance, educational attainment, and trust today." The Fourth Lateran Council in 1215 ordered secular authorities to "exterminate heresy," creating what historian Ian Moore describes as "a persecuting society" where "deliberate and socially sanctioned violence began to be directed against groups defined by race, religion or way of life."

The Catholic Encyclopedia itself acknowledges that while early Christians advocated religious liberty, "imperial successors of Constantine soon began" persecution, and "in Medieval period" the Church pursued "positive suppression of heresy."

When the Universal Declaration of Human Rights was drafted in 1948, over 50 nations participated—not exclusively Christian. Eleanor Roosevelt recalled discussions including "pluralisms and Confucian philosophy to Thomas Aquinas." The drafting committee included Charles Malik (Lebanese Christian), P.C. Chang (Chinese Confucian scholar), and Jacques Maritain (French Catholic philosopher), reflecting multiple philosophical traditions.

## Religious freedom: ancient precedents, Enlightenment realization

Vance's claim that "freedom of religion is actually a Christian concept" ignores extensive historical evidence of religious tolerance in non-Christian civilizations and Christianity's own record of persecution.

**The Cyrus Cylinder from 539 BCE—over 500 years before Christianity—is often called the first declaration of human rights.** After conquering Babylon, Persian King Cyrus the Great issued decrees allowing freedom of worship throughout the empire, return of deported peoples to homelands, and restoration of religious sanctuaries. The cylinder's provisions parallel the first four Articles of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Neil MacGregor, former British Museum director, called it "the first attempt we know about running a society, a state with different nationalities and faiths—a new kind of statecraft." The Achaemenid Empire encompassed 44% of the world's population, with government not endorsing any particular faith.

**The Mauryan Empire under Ashoka (c. 268-232 BCE) made religious tolerance state policy.** Following remorse over the Kalinga War's 150,000+ casualties, Emperor Ashoka converted to Buddhism and inscribed edicts across India. Rock Edict 12 declared: "King Piyadasi...honors all sects and opinions...By honoring other religions one's own religion benefits, and so do other religions." Historian Romila Thapar notes Ashoka's dhamma promoted "non-violence, tolerance of all sects and opinions, obedience to parents, respect to brahmins and other religious teachers and priests, liberality toward friends, humane treatment of servants and generosity towards all."

**The Ottoman Empire's millet system allowed religious minorities to self-govern.** Christians, Jews, and Armenians maintained their own legal systems, tax structures, religious courts, and schools. Each millet's religious leaders acted as intermediaries with Ottoman authorities. Scholar Abdulaziz Sachedina regards this as "an example of pre-modern religious pluralism, as the state recognized multiple different religious groups." One source notes the system "represented a remarkably progressive approach to religious pluralism and tolerance, especially compared to contemporary European struggles with religious persecution."

The Mongol Empire under Genghis Khan decreed religious freedom for all subjects in the 13th century, exempted all religious leaders from taxation, and organized religious debates between faiths. Edward Gibbon wrote that "The Catholic inquisitors of Europe, who defended nonsense by cruelty, might have been confounded by the example of a barbarian, who anticipated the lessons of philosophy and established by his laws a system of pure theism and perfect toleration."

**Christianity's historical record shows extensive persecution, not tolerance.** The Albigensian Crusade (1209-1229) proclaimed by Pope Innocent III resulted in an estimated two million deaths. Historian Ian Moore notes that "for the first time, with the declaration of the crusade against the Cathars, the Pope was officially encouraging the killing of Christians by other Christians." The Spanish Inquisition targeted converted Jews (40,000 converted or left in 1492), Muslims (forced to convert or emigrate in 1502), and established tribunals throughout Spanish colonies. The last execution for heresy occurred in 1826.

Jews faced systematic persecution: over 50,000 murdered by mobs in Spain in 1391, expulsion in 1492, forced wearing of distinctive clothing mandated by the Fourth Lateran Council (1215), blood libel accusations leading to massacres, and ghettoization. Muslims were forcibly converted (1502 ultimatum), fought against in the War of the Alpujarras (1568-1571), and 300,000 Moriscos were expelled from Spain in 1614.

**Modern religious freedom emerged from Enlightenment philosophy, often against Christian authority.** Britannica is explicit: "The separation of church and state was one of the legacies of the American and French revolutions at the end of the 18th century. It was achieved as a result of ideas arising from opposition to the English episcopal system and the English throne as well as from the ideals of the Enlightenment."

Roger Williams, who founded Rhode Island in 1644, was the first public official to call for "a wall or hedge of separation" between "the wilderness of the world" and "the garden of the church." Thomas Jefferson, a Deist following Enlightenment-era reason-based religion, drafted Virginia's Statute of Religious Freedom and wrote in 1802 about a "wall of separation between Church & State." Enlightenment thinkers including John Locke, Voltaire, Denis Diderot, and Montesquieu argued for religious tolerance and separation as essential for preventing the religious wars that had devastated Europe.

Scholar Perez Zagorin argues that modern conceptions of religious toleration and religious freedom are "almost entirely due" to "unorthodox Protestants" and "Spinoza" in the 16th-17th centuries—movements that challenged established religious authority. While early persecuted Christians like Tertullian did articulate arguments for freedom of conscience in the 2nd-3rd centuries CE, these were arguments for their own freedom, not universal religious freedom, and were contradicted by centuries of Christian persecution once Christianity gained power.

## Christian civilization's moral record: methodological problems and historical atrocities

Vance claimed "The fruits of the Christian faith are the most moral, the most just, and the most prosperous civilization in history." This assertion presents severe methodological problems and contradicts extensive historical evidence.

**No objective standard exists for measuring civilizational "morality" or "justice."** Philosopher Anthony Pagden observes that "Civilization describes a state, social, political, cultural, aesthetic—even moral and physical—which is held to be the optimum condition for all mankind, and this involves the implicit claim that only the civilized can know what it is to be civilized." Scholar Ruth Macklin notes it's "wholly uncontroversial to hold that technological progress has taken place" but "highly controversial to assert that moral progress has occurred."

Oxford International Studies Review critiques "civilizationism"—claiming superiority for one's civilization—as presenting "characteristics of one's own group as...superior to those of others" through "exceptionalism" rather than evidence, often used to justify domination. German sociologist Norbert Elias noted that civilization "expresses the self-consciousness of the West" and "It is not too difficult to see how the harbingers of civilization might gravitate toward a (well-meaning) 'proselytising crusade.'"

**Christian civilizations were responsible for mass atrocities.** The Crusades (1095-1291) resulted in 1-3 million deaths over nearly 200 years, including 40,000 massacred in the 1099 Sack of Jerusalem. The Spanish Inquisition executed 3,000-10,000 people officially, with an additional 100,000-125,000 deaths in prison from torture and maltreatment. The witch trials across Europe (1450-1750) executed 40,000-60,000 people, 75-80% of them women. The Thirty Years' War (1618-1648), largely a religious conflict, killed 4-8 million people—approximately 20% of Europe's population.

The Atlantic slave trade forcibly transported 12.5 million Africans, with 1.8 million dying during the Middle Passage alone. Pope Nicholas V in 1455 authorized Christians "to invade, search out, capture, vanquish, and subdue all Saracens and pagans," take their possessions, and "reduce their persons to perpetual slavery" through the Doctrine of Discovery.

**Indigenous genocide in the Americas represents one of history's greatest catastrophes.** Pre-contact North American population was 10-15 million; by 1900, under 300,000 remained—a 95%+ decline. Benjamin Madley's Yale University Press book documented 16,000 California Indians killed directly between 1848-1860, with the population declining from 150,000 to 30,000. The Trail of Tears resulted in 4,000+ documented Cherokee deaths. Across the Americas, an estimated 8 million indigenous people were killed in Spanish conquest, representing 95% of all indigenous people.

Historian David Stannard concludes in American Holocaust: "In light of the U.N. language...it is impossible to know what transpired in the Americas during the sixteenth, seventeenth, eighteenth, and nineteenth centuries and not conclude that it was genocide." The Oxford Encyclopedia calls Spain's invasion "the first large-scale genocide of the modern era."

**Non-Christian civilizations achieved remarkable advances.** The Islamic Golden Age (750-1258 CE) produced algebra (Al-Khwarizmi), advanced astronomy and optics (Al-Battani, Ibn al-Haytham), revolutionary medicine (Avicenna's Canon of Medicine influenced European medicine for centuries), and preserved classical Greek knowledge that would later fuel the European Renaissance. Historian Herbert Klein notes scholars were "heavily patronized" with resources "equivalent to about twice the annual research budget of the United Kingdom's Medical Research Council."

Chinese Tang (618-907) and Song (960-1279) dynasties invented paper, gunpowder, printing (movable type by Bi Sheng, 990-1051), and the compass. Song dynasty GDP was three times larger than Europe in the 12th century. China developed paper money, mechanical clocks, advanced shipbuilding, and a merit-based civil service system. Historians note "China in 1492 was the oldest, largest, and richest civilization in the world. Its command of science and technology far exceeded that of Europe."

**Religious institutions increasingly acknowledge historical complicity.** The United Church of Christ in 2013 called to "repudiate the Doctrine of Discovery, which authorized the genocide of Native peoples and the theft of Native lands." Rev. Kelly Gallagher stated: "The dehumanizing, removal and genocide of the Indigenous communities by people who called themselves Christians laid the groundwork in the very fabric of this society for the Trail of Tears, for slavery, for Jim Crow, for alienation, oppression and continued genocide of people of color in this nation to this day."

Historian Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz argues "Genocide was the inherent overall policy of the United States from its founding." Walter Rodney observed in How Europe Underdeveloped Africa that "The Christian missionaries were as much part of the colonizing forces as were the explorers, traders and soldiers."

## Conclusion

Each of JD Vance's historical claims collapses under scholarly scrutiny. Child sacrifice was not "widespread" where English settlers arrived, the "brothel" claim appears fabricated, and Christian colonizers brought genocide rather than enlightenment. Britain was neither first to abolish slavery nor primarily motivated by Christianity; economic factors and enslaved people's own resistance were central. Human rights concepts emerged from multiple philosophical traditions—Greek, Buddhist, Confucian, Islamic—and modern human rights arose from Enlightenment philosophy that explicitly opposed religious authority. Religious freedom existed in Persian, Indian, Ottoman, and Mongol empires centuries before Christianity embraced it, while Christianity's historical record shows extensive persecution. Claims of Christian civilizational moral superiority ignore methodological problems and erase atrocities including the Crusades, Inquisition, slave trade, and indigenous genocide.

These narratives serve contemporary political purposes by constructing a civilizational hierarchy that erases non-Western achievements, Indigenous agency, and Christianity's own extensive violence. As Leiden University scholars Maarten Jansen and Gabina Pérez argue, "It is time for a re-examination of those engrained stereotypes, not only to gain a better understanding of the indigenous cultural heritage, but also to assess how western ideas about other peoples in general are still under the spell of colonial propaganda." Academic consensus across history, archaeology, anthropology, and philosophy contradicts Vance's claims, revealing them as ahistorical assertions that perpetuate harmful myths about civilizational superiority.
