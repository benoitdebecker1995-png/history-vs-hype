## Rule 19: Algorithm-Aware 4-Beat Hook Structure

**The canonical hook formula for History vs Hype.** Every hook follows this structure: Cold Fact (0:00-0:10) → Myth (0:10-0:20) → Contradiction (0:20-0:40) → Payoff Preview (0:40-1:00).

The templates below provide fill-in-the-blank versions for each video type. The existing templates (Template 1-6) remain valid as implementation examples within this structure.

---

### Territorial Hook Template

```
[COLD FACT: 0:00-0:10]
In [YEAR], [ENTITY] [SPECIFIC ACTION — published/signed/drew/surveyed] [SPECIFIC DOCUMENT/MAP] showing [SURPRISING DETAIL].

[MYTH: 0:10-0:20]
[Most maps today / The official border / Every textbook] shows [WHAT PEOPLE BELIEVE].

[CONTRADICTION: 0:20-0:40]
So I [FIRST-PERSON ACTION — read/pulled/found] the original [DOCUMENT TYPE]...
[B-ROLL: Show [SPECIFIC DOCUMENT] zooming to [SPECIFIC DETAIL]]
...and [WHAT EVIDENCE SHOWS — specific, concrete finding].

[PAYOFF: 0:40-1:00]
[MODERN STAKES — active dispute, ICJ case, economic consequence, people affected]
[PROMISE — "and that's what we're going to trace today" / "that distinction explains..." ]
```

**Example:**
```
In 1897, Mexico published an atlas showing an island at 22°33' north latitude.

Most maps today show nothing there.

So I pulled the original Mexican naval surveys...
[B-ROLL: Show 1897 atlas zooming to the island marker]
...and what they show explains a $100 billion oil dispute happening right now.

That dispute is still unresolved. And it starts with a cartographic decision made 127 years ago.
```

### Ideological Hook Template

```
[COLD FACT: 0:00-0:10]
In [YEAR], [SCHOLAR/SOURCE] [WROTE/RECORDED/DOCUMENTED] that [SURPRISING HISTORICAL FACT].

[MYTH: 0:10-0:20]
[Most people think / You've probably heard / The popular version says] [THE MYTH].

[CONTRADICTION: 0:20-0:40]
So I [read what X actually argued / checked the original sources / found the academic edition]...
[B-ROLL: Show [SOURCE — book cover, manuscript, data table]]
...and [WHAT SOURCES ACTUALLY SAY].

[PAYOFF: 0:40-1:00]
[WHY THIS MATTERS — modern belief/debate/policy this myth still fuels]
[PROMISE — "and that myth is still shaping how we think about X today"]
```

**Example:**
```
In 1492, every university in Europe taught that the earth was round.

Most people think Columbus proved the earth wasn't flat.

So I read what Columbus actually argued about — and it wasn't the shape.
[B-ROLL: Show Columbus's letter to Ferdinand and Isabella]
He argued about size. He thought the earth was 20 percent smaller than it is.

And that distinction matters because the flat earth myth is still shaping how we teach the history of science today.
```

### Untranslated Document Hook Template

```
[COLD FACT: 0:00-0:10]
[Article/Section] [NUMBER] of the [YEAR] [DOCUMENT NAME] has [never been translated / been mistranslated / been summarized incorrectly] in English.

[MYTH: 0:10-0:20]
English [summaries/textbooks/sources] say it [WHAT ENGLISH SOURCES CLAIM].

[CONTRADICTION: 0:20-0:40]
But when you read the original [LANGUAGE], it says something [more specific / completely different / much more revealing]...
[B-ROLL: Split-screen showing original text left, translation right]
...and [WHAT ORIGINAL ACTUALLY SAYS].

[PAYOFF: 0:40-1:00]
[HOW MISTRANSLATION SHAPES CURRENT UNDERSTANDING]
[PROMISE — "I'm going to read this document clause by clause in the original [LANGUAGE]"]
```

**Example:**
```
Article 3 of the 1940 Vichy Statute has never been translated into English.

English summaries say it "restricted Jewish professions."

But when you read the original French, it says something much more specific...
[B-ROLL: Split-screen — French text left, English translation right]
...it names 37 professions individually. That's not a restriction. That's a catalogue.

And that distinction changes how we understand Vichy collaboration entirely. I'm going to read this statute clause by clause in the original French.
```

### Fact-Check Hook Template

```
[COLD FACT: 0:00-0:10]
On [DATE], [PERSON] [CLAIMED/SAID/POSTED] "[SPECIFIC QUOTE]" [CONTEXT — on show/tweet/video].

[MYTH: 0:10-0:20]
[NUMBER] people [shared/believed/repeated] this because [WHY IT SOUNDED PLAUSIBLE].

[CONTRADICTION: 0:20-0:40]
So I [checked the primary source / read the actual document / found the original data]...
[B-ROLL: Show [PRIMARY SOURCE]]
...and [WHAT SOURCE ACTUALLY SHOWS].

[PAYOFF: 0:40-1:00]
[WHY TRUTH MATTERS — policy/belief/narrative this shapes]
[PROMISE — "here's what the evidence actually says"]
```

**Example:**
```
On January 6th, 2021, JD Vance told Tucker Carlson that the election results were "statistically impossible."

Millions of people shared that clip because it sounded like a specific technical claim.

So I checked the statistical analyses he was citing...
[B-ROLL: Show the original Navarro Report]
...and the methodology has been rejected by every peer-reviewed statistician who examined it.

That matters because "statistically impossible" is still circulating in 2025 political debates. Here's what the evidence actually says.
```

## Retention Trigger Checklist (Apply to Every Hook)

Before finalizing any hook, verify all three triggers are present:

- [ ] **Information Gap:** The contradiction beat creates a question. The payoff preview promises the answer WITHOUT delivering it fully. Viewer must watch to find out.
- [ ] **Visual Carrot:** A specific piece of evidence is promised or shown. Not "evidence exists" but "here's what Article 3 says in the original French."
- [ ] **Authority Signal:** First-person ownership within 60 seconds. "I read..." / "I found..." / "I pulled..." — establishes personal credibility.

## Existing Templates (Implementation Examples Within Rule 19 Structure)

The templates below predate Rule 19 but remain valid. Each maps to the 4-beat structure: the "Cold Fact" is the opening concrete detail, the "Myth" is often the "Both Extremes" framing, the "Contradiction" is "So I did some digging," and the "Payoff" is the stakes/promise.

## TEMPLATE 1: Document Reveal

**Best for:** Treaty disputes, legal claims, historical controversies

```
[VISUAL: Document on screen]

This document [did what].

[Date]. [Location if relevant]. [One key detail about it].

[ZOOM: Specific text]

[What the key text says or means].

[Beat]

But here's what [most people / the popular narrative] doesn't mention: [surprising detail].

[Context that makes it significant].

---

If you ask online about [topic], you'll get two answers.

One side says [Extreme A]. [Who believes this].

The other side says [Extreme B]. [Who believes this].

So I did some digging. Read [type of sources]. Then went to the primary sources.

Both narratives are wrong. And they're wrong because [reason].
```

**Example (Haiti):**
```
This document isn't an invoice. It's a ransom note.

April 17th, 1825. Delivered to Haiti at gunpoint. Demanding 150 million francs.

[ZOOM: Article 2]

They were paying for the "loss" of enslaved human beings.

But here's what the ransom note didn't say: the 150 million wasn't even the full demand.

The French commission valued Haiti at 1.5 billion. This was a down payment.
```

## TEMPLATE 2: Both Extremes Wrong

**Best for:** Contested history, political myths, ideological debates

```
If you ask online about [topic], you'll get two answers.

One side says [Extreme A - specific claim]. [Who believes this / where you see this].

The other side says [Extreme B - specific claim]. [Who believes this / where you see this].

[Beat]

So I did some digging. Read [books/sources about topic]. Then went to the primary sources.

Both are wrong. And they're wrong because they [miss what / start in wrong place / oversimplify what].

[Specific thing both miss].

[Why this matters for understanding the topic].
```

**Example (Iran):**
```
If you ask online about the situation in Iran, you'll get two answers.

One side says it's America's fault. The 1953 coup destroyed Iranian democracy. Everything since is blowback.

The other side says Iran is just culturally authoritarian. Nothing will change until they have their own Enlightenment.

So I did some digging. Read books about the topic. Then went to the primary sources.

Both narratives are wrong. And they're wrong because they start in the wrong place.

Everyone talks about 1953. But 1953 wasn't the beginning. It was a repetition.
```

## TEMPLATE 3: Surprising Fact

**Best for:** Myth-busting, counterintuitive history

```
This might surprise you, but [unexpected fact].

[Date/timeframe]. [Context that makes it surprising].

[Beat]

You might think [common assumption].

You'd be wrong.

[Fact that contradicts assumption].

[Beat]

You might then conclude [other common assumption].

Also wrong.

[Fact that contradicts that too].

[Beat]

The real story is [brief preview]. It's documented in [type of sources].

Here's how it actually worked.
```

**Example (Haiti):**
```
This might surprise you, but the last payment cleared in 1947.

That's 12 years before the Cuban Revolution. Within living memory.

You might think this was ancient history.

You'd be wrong.

You might then conclude France's extraction explains everything about Haiti's poverty.

Also wrong.

The Duvalier dictators stole $1.65 billion—three times what France took directly.

The real story involves ransom notes, inside men, and 122 years of extraction. It's documented in invoices and payment records.

Here's how the heist worked.
```

## QUICK CHECKLIST: First 60 Seconds

Before finalizing your opening, verify:

- [ ] Concrete detail in first 10 seconds (date, document, specific fact)
- [ ] NO forbidden phrases ("Let me show you," "Buckle up," etc.)
- [ ] Both extremes framed (if using that format)
- [ ] Stakes established (why should viewer care?)
- [ ] "I did some digging" or similar research authority
- [ ] Promise of what's coming ("Here's how it worked")
- [ ] Modern relevance mentioned or implied

- "People are still dying over this."
