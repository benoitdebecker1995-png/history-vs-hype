# Scriptwriting Debunking Framework

**Integration of public history communication research with History vs Hype scriptwriting**

*Created: 2026-01-02*
*Sources: NotebookLM Public History Playbook + Comment Response Strategy*

---

## Core Insight

Your scripts ARE public history communication. The same psychological mechanisms that cause comment section backfire also cause viewer resistance to myth-debunking videos.

**The challenge:** When you debunk nationalist myths or ideological narratives on YouTube, viewers experience **identity-protective cognition**—their brains register factual corrections as threats to group belonging.

**The solution:** Apply proven debunking strategies from cognitive psychology and public history pedagogy directly to script structure.

---

## THE BIG SEVEN DEBUNKING PRINCIPLES

### 1. Alternative Explanations Over Negation

**Research:** "Post-exposure corrections are most effective when they incorporate plausible alternative explanation." (Lewandowsky)

**The problem:** Simply negating a myth leaves a mental gap. People prefer an incorrect model to no model at all.

**Bad approach:**
> "Columbus wasn't arguing about the earth's shape. That's wrong."

**Good approach (fill the gap):**
> "Columbus wasn't arguing about shape. He was arguing about SIZE. Here's what he actually miscalculated: [detailed alternative with sources]"

**Script application:**
- Every debunking section needs a REPLACEMENT explanation
- Don't just say what DIDN'T happen, explain what DID happen and WHY
- Provide the web of causes, not just the negation

**Example from flat earth script:**
- ❌ "Medieval people didn't believe in flat earth"
- ✅ "Medieval scholars knew earth was spherical. Eratosthenes calculated it. Sacrobosco taught it for 400 years. Here's the manuscript evidence..."

---

### 2. Fact-First Headlines (Avoid Familiarity Backfire)

**Research:** "Repeating a myth to debunk it makes the myth more familiar, which brains mistake for truth over time." (Debunking Handbook)

**The problem:** The more you repeat "Columbus and the flat earth," the more that association strengthens in memory—even when you're debunking it.

**Bad structure:**
> "Many people think Columbus argued the earth was round against flat-earth priests. That's completely wrong. Actually..."

**Good structure:**
> "The 1486 Council of Salamanca debated the SIZE of the earth, not its shape. While popular culture shows Columbus defending a round earth, the evidence shows..."

**Script application:**
- Open sections with THE CORRECT FACT, not the myth
- Place myth in subordinate clause: "While some claim X, the documents show Y"
- Never make the myth the headline or section title
- Lead with evidence, not refutation

**Flat earth script example:**
- Section title: "The Real Columbus Debate" (fact-first)
- NOT: "Debunking the Flat Earth Myth" (myth-first)

---

### 3. Self-Affirmation to Reduce Identity Threat

**Research:** "Self-affirmation makes people more receptive to worldview-threatening information." (Kahan, Klein)

**The problem:** When national identity is tied to a historical myth, corrections trigger defensive motivated reasoning.

**Application - Acknowledge shared values FIRST:**

**For nationalist territorial claims:**
> "National heritage matters—it shapes who we are. That's exactly why getting the historical record right is so important. When we look at the 1899 treaty documents..."

**For ideological myths:**
> "We all want to believe our side fought for the right reasons. I get that. But if we're committed to honest history, we need to look at what the primary sources actually say..."

**Script application:**
- Start contested sections by affirming the VALUE behind the myth
- Separate person from claim: "I understand why this narrative feels important" (validate emotion)
- Then pivot: "But when we examine the documentary evidence..." (redirect to facts)
- Never mock the belief, critique the evidence

---

### 4. Toulmin Model for Explicit Argumentation

**Research:** Make logical bridges EXPLICIT. Don't leave warrants unstated.

**The model:**
1. **Claim:** Your assertion
2. **Evidence:** The data supporting it
3. **Warrant:** The logical bridge connecting evidence to claim (OFTEN UNSTATED—make it explicit!)
4. **Backing:** The principle/framework supporting your warrant

**Bad (implicit warrant):**
> "Columbus thought the earth was 20,400 miles around. The scholars at Salamanca said 24,901 miles. They were right."

**Good (explicit warrant):**
> "Columbus thought the earth was 20,400 miles around. The scholars at Salamanca calculated 24,901 miles. **The key principle here is that** sovereignty decisions in 1486 were based on navigational feasibility, not theology. If the ocean was that wide, his ships couldn't carry enough supplies. **That's why** the commission rejected him—not ignorance, but better math."

**Script application:**
- After presenting evidence, ADD: "The key principle here is..." or "That's why..." or "What this means is..."
- Never assume viewer will make the logical leap
- Spell out the "if A, then B, because C" explicitly

**Flat earth script example:**
- Claim: "Medieval scholars knew earth was spherical"
- Evidence: "Sacrobosco's textbook taught it for 400 years"
- Warrant: "If flat earth belief was mainstream, it would appear in the standard university curriculum"
- Backing: "University curricula reflect consensus knowledge of the era"

### 5. Seixas Big Six - Teaching Historical Thinking

**Research:** "Move audience from 'best story' (collective memory) to 'disciplinary approach' (evidence assessment)." (Seixas, Wineburg)

**The six concepts:**

**5a. Historical Significance**
- Don't just tell what happened—explain WHY it matters
- Apply: "This isn't just about one border. It set the legal precedent for how [consequence for many people over long time]"

**5b. Evidence (Sourcing & Corroboration)**
- WHO created the source, WHEN, and FOR WHAT PURPOSE
- Apply: "This treaty was written by [who], in [context]. Here's why their bias matters..."
- Show 2+ sources corroborating the same fact

**5c. Continuity & Change**
- What stayed the same? What changed? At what pace?
- Apply: "While the borders changed in 1960, the ethnic composition remained constant. That mismatch created..."

**5d. Cause & Consequence**
- Move beyond "great man" personalization
- Apply: "It wasn't just [leader]. Three structural factors drove this: [economic pressure], [political instability], [unintended consequence of earlier policy]"

**5e. Historical Perspective (Avoid Presentism)**
- Treat the past as a "foreign country" with its own norms
- Apply: "By 1486 standards, this wasn't controversial. The consensus was..."
- Don't judge historical actors by 2025 morality

**5f. Ethical Dimension**
- How do we make moral judgments while understanding context?
- Apply: "We can condemn the genocide while recognizing the colonial framework made it systemically possible"

**Script application:**
Use these as invisible scaffolding. Don't say "Now I'm going to explain historical significance." Just DO IT:

> "Why does this 1916 treaty still matter? Because it created borders affecting 250 million people today—from Syria to Iraq to Lebanon. That's historical significance: deep consequences for many people over long time."

---

### 6. Source Credibility Suspicion (Expose Vested Interests)

**Research:** "Arousing suspicion of the misinformer's motives further reduces myth influence." (Lewandowsky)

**Application:** When debunking, explain WHY the myth was created.

**Flat earth script example:**
- Don't just say "Irving made it up"
- Explain: "Irving needed a villain for his Columbus hero story. Draper needed ammunition against the Catholic Church after Papal Infallibility. White needed to legitimize secular education. Each had a REASON to propagate the myth."

**Script application:**
- Identify WHO benefits from the myth
- Explain WHEN and WHY they created/propagated it
- Show the myth's UTILITY (political, cultural, identity)

**Example structure:**
> "This myth persists not because the evidence is ambiguous, but because it serves a powerful function: [explain utility]. It allows [group] to claim [identity/legitimacy]. That's why simply correcting the facts doesn't work—the myth is too useful."

---

### 7. KISS Principle (Avoid Overkill Backfire)

**Research:** "Too many counter-arguments backfire. Simple myths are more 'cognitively attractive' than complex corrections." (Debunking Handbook)

**The problem:** You have 20 pieces of evidence. You're excited to share all of them. But that HURTS your persuasiveness.

**Rule: 3 KEY POINTS MAX per section.**

**Bad (overkill):**
> "Here are twelve reasons the claim is wrong: 1) Treaty violation 2) Court precedent 3) Population data 4) Economic factors 5) Border demarcation 6) Historical occupation 7) Self-determination 8) Colonial legacy 9) Modern law 10) UN Charter 11) Regional agreements 12) Diplomatic recognition."

**Good (lean and mean):**
> "Three pieces of evidence matter: 1) The 1899 treaty explicitly set the border here [show map]. 2) The 1966 independence referendum confirmed it [show results]. 3) The ICJ ruled on this exact question in 2007 [show ruling]. Everything else is noise."

**Script application:**
- Identify your 3 STRONGEST pieces of evidence
- Cut the rest (save for footnotes/description)
- Make each point crystal clear before moving to next
- Quality of evidence > quantity

## IDENTITY STAKE AWARENESS (For Scripts)

**Key insight:** Not all viewers are equally resistant. Anticipate identity stakes in your topic.

### Low Identity Stake Topics
- Ancient civilizations (Egypt, Rome, Greece)
- Medieval history in general
- Scientific discoveries

**Approach:** Teach freely. Complexity is welcomed. Viewers want to learn.

### Medium Identity Stake Topics
- Colonial history (but not "their" colony)
- Historical figures (but not national heroes)
- Economic systems in theory

**Approach:** Lead with evidence, acknowledge complexity, show multiple perspectives.

### High Identity Stake Topics
- **Territorial disputes involving viewer's nation**
- **National founding myths**
- **Religious narratives tied to identity**
- **Ideological movements viewer participates in**

**Approach:**
1. **Self-affirmation FIRST:** Acknowledge shared value
2. **Procedural fairness:** Show you examined BOTH sides' evidence
3. **Source transparency:** Make methodology visible
4. **Historiographic gaze:** Treat competing narratives as artifacts to study, not sides to pick

**Example - High stake opening:**
> "If you're from [Country X], this history is deeply personal. I get that. Your schools taught one version. [Country Y]'s schools taught another. Rather than picking a side, I'm going to show you the documents both sides cite, and walk through what they actually say. By the end, you'll have the evidence to make your own assessment."

## PRACTICAL SCRIPT APPLICATIONS

### Opening Hook Integration

**Instead of:**
> "Today we're debunking the myth that..."

**Try:**
> [Show the document] "This is what the treaty actually says. For 150 years, textbooks have claimed [myth]. Here's why that's historically inaccurate."

- Fact-first headline ✅
- Document-first hook ✅
- Avoids repeating myth unnecessarily ✅

### Mid-Script Myth Correction

**Structure:**
1. **State correct fact** (headline)
2. **Show evidence** (sources, quotes, data)
3. **Explain alternative** (fill the gap)
4. **Acknowledge myth exists** (subordinate clause)
5. **Expose why myth was created** (source credibility suspicion)

**Example template:**
> [Fact-first] The 1961 referendum showed northern rejection, not approval.
>
> [Evidence] According to Prunier, page 21: Hargeisa 72% no, Berbera 69% no.
>
> [Alternative explanation] The "91% yes" national result happened because the south had 3x the population and voted 96% yes. That's the math.
>
> [Acknowledge myth] While the national figure is often cited as proof of universal support...
>
> [Expose creation] That framing serves the unification narrative by erasing regional opposition.

### Conclusion Integration

**Apply "The Reversal" structure from flat earth script:**

Show how the myth was USEFUL:
- Who created it?
- Why was it powerful?
- What cultural need did it fulfill?
- How does it persist despite debunking?

**Template:**
> "This isn't just about getting the history right. It's about understanding how myths persist when they're politically useful. [Group X] promoted this narrative because it justified [action]. [Group Y] accepted it because it validated [identity]. Once it entered [textbooks/culture], it became nearly impossible to remove. That's the lesson: emotionally satisfying stories are impervious to evidence."

## QUALITY GATE QUESTIONS

Before finalizing any script that debunks a myth, ask:

**Debunking Quality:**
- [ ] Did I lead with the FACT, not the myth?
- [ ] Did I provide an ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATION, not just a negation?
- [ ] Did I limit to 3 KEY POINTS per section (KISS principle)?
- [ ] Did I make logical bridges EXPLICIT (Toulmin warrants)?
- [ ] Did I explain WHY the myth was created (source credibility)?

**Identity Awareness:**
- [ ] Is this high-identity-stake for some viewers?
- [ ] If yes, did I affirm shared values FIRST?
- [ ] Did I show procedural fairness (both sides' evidence examined)?
- [ ] Did I avoid mocking the belief itself?

**Historical Thinking:**
- [ ] Did I avoid presentism (judging past by present standards)?
- [ ] Did I show CAUSE & CONSEQUENCE (not just "great man" personalization)?
- [ ] Did I use EVIDENCE concept (sourcing, corroboration)?
- [ ] Did I explain HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE (why it matters)?

**Trust Building:**
- [ ] Did I demonstrate WARMTH (caring about getting it right)?
- [ ] Did I show INTEGRITY (transparent about sources and limits)?
- [ ] Did I model WILLINGNESS TO LISTEN (acknowledging valid points from other side)?

## WHEN TO USE THIS FRAMEWORK

**Always use for:**
- Territorial dispute videos (high identity stakes)
- National founding myth debunking (high identity stakes)
- Ideological narrative corrections (medium-high stakes)
- Colonial history reframing (medium stakes)

**Optional for:**
- Ancient history (low stakes)
- Scientific discoveries (low stakes)
- Medieval Europe general (low stakes unless religious)

**The rule:** If the myth is tied to GROUP IDENTITY, this framework is mandatory.

---

## SUMMARY: THE DEBUNKING CHECKLIST

**Pre-Script (Planning):**
1. Identify identity stake level (Low/Medium/High)
2. Find 3 STRONGEST pieces of evidence (KISS)
3. Craft ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATION for the myth
4. Research WHY the myth was created (vested interests)

**During Script (Structure):**
1. Fact-first headlines (avoid myth repetition)
2. Alternative explanations (fill mental gaps)
3. Explicit warrants (Toulmin model)
4. Seixas Big Six scaffolding (invisible framework)
5. Self-affirmation for high-stake sections

**Post-Script (Quality Gate):**
1. Read aloud - does it TEACH historical thinking?
2. Count key points per section - is it lean enough?
3. Check myth mentions - are they subordinate to facts?
4. Verify source transparency - is methodology visible?
5. Test identity sensitivity - does it mock or educate?

## REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE: FLAT EARTH SCRIPT

**See:** `video-projects/_IN_PRODUCTION/19-flat-earth-medieval-2025/FINAL-SCRIPT-V3.md`

**What it does right:**

1. **Fact-first opening:** Textbook contrast shows the LIE first, then traces its creation
2. **Alternative explanation:** Doesn't just say "scholars knew it was round" - shows HOW (Eratosthenes calculation, Sacrobosco textbook, royal orb)
3. **Source credibility suspicion:** Exposes Irving, Draper, White as having REASONS to propagate myth
4. **KISS principle:** Medieval evidence section = 3 key proofs (Eratosthenes, Isidore, Sacrobosco)
5. **Historical thinking:** Uses Historical Significance (150-year head start), Cause & Consequence (culture war utility)
6. **The Reversal:** Shows how myth was USEFUL for secular elites, now flipped by populists

**Result:** Debunking that TEACHES historical methodology, not just corrects facts.
