# Untranslated Evidence Format Guide

**Reference guide for document-based videos revealing what primary sources say when read in original languages.**

*Created: 2026-02-17*
*Authority: Defines episode structure, visual standards, quality bar, and tone for document translation videos*

---

## Purpose

This guide defines the format for videos centered on untranslated or mistranslated primary documents. These videos reveal what legal texts, diplomatic records, official decrees, and historical proclamations actually say when read in their original languages—versus what English-language summaries claim.

**Core principle:** Show the original-language text on screen alongside accurate translation, allowing viewers to see linguistic distortions that fuel modern misunderstandings.

**Format type:** This guide establishes standards without committing to branding decisions. Whether this becomes a branded sub-series ("Untranslated Evidence") or remains a topical category will be determined during production. The structure and quality bar remain constant regardless.

---

## Section 1: Episode Structure Template

### Standard Episode Flow

**1. COLD OPEN / HOOK (1-2 minutes)**

Modern consequence or widely-believed myth that the untranslated document contradicts.

**Formula:** "Everyone says X about this document. But when you actually read it in the original [language]..."

**Key elements:**
- State the common narrative about the document
- Flag that the original language reveals something different
- Establish stakes: why the mistranslation matters today
- Preview the surprise: hint at what will be revealed

**Example:** "The Vichy Statut des Juifs is cited as proof France autonomously persecuted Jews. But when you read the October 1940 decree in French, specific clauses reveal German pressure that English summaries omit..."

---

**2. DOCUMENT INTRODUCTION (2-3 minutes)**

What the document is, when/where it was created, who wrote it, why it matters.

**Show the physical document or archival scan on screen.** This establishes authenticity.

**Key elements:**
- Document type (treaty, statute, decree, diplomatic note, military order)
- Date and location of creation
- Signatories or issuing authority
- Historical context: what problem this document addressed
- Why English-language sources cite it (and what they claim it says)
- Translation status: Does NO English translation exist? Or do existing translations distort meaning?

**Qualification for series inclusion:**
- (A) No full English translation exists, OR
- (B) Existing English translations are misleading, incomplete, or distort the original

Partial translations or summaries do NOT disqualify a document. If the available English material misrepresents the original, it qualifies.

---

**3. CLAUSE-BY-CLAUSE WALKTHROUGH (bulk of video)**

The core of the video. Walk through the document section by section, article by article, or clause by clause.

**For each section/article/clause:**

1. **Context setup:** Why this clause exists, what problem it addressed, what readers should know before reading it
2. **Read original:** Show original-language text on screen. Narrator reads it aloud (if pronunciation is feasible) or paraphrases structure
3. **Translate:** Show English translation alongside original (split-screen approach—see Section 2)
4. **Explain significance:** What this clause actually means in legal/diplomatic/historical context
5. **Connect to myth:** How this specific provision contradicts or supports the common English-language narrative

**Pacing:**
- Spend MORE time on "surprise clauses"—sections where translation contradicts common narratives
- Move quickly through mundane administrative sections (unless they establish important context)
- Use visual highlighting (see Section 2) to direct attention to key phrases

**Structure options:**

**Option A: Full-document coverage**
- For short documents (5-15 articles), walk through every clause
- Video length: typically 20-35 minutes
- Example: Statut des Juifs (10 articles) = ~25 min video

**Option B: Excerpt-based coverage**
- For long documents (50+ articles), select 5-8 key clauses that reveal the pattern
- Explicitly acknowledge you're showing excerpts, not full text
- Video length: typically 15-25 minutes
- Example: Treaty of Utrecht (112 articles) = focus on Articles 10 (Gibraltar cession), 15 (fortification limits), 22 (religious provisions)

**Option C: Related documents grouped**
- When multiple documents tell a connected story (e.g., a decree and its implementing regulations, or a treaty and subsequent diplomatic notes)
- Walk through documents in chronological order
- Video length: 30-50+ minutes depending on complexity
- Example: October 3, 1940 Statut des Juifs + June 2, 1941 revised Statut + implementing decrees

---

**4. SYNTHESIS / "WHAT THEY GOT WRONG" (3-5 minutes)**

Aggregate what the full document reveals versus the common English-language narrative.

**Pattern:** "So when people say X, the document actually says Y, Z, and W."

**Key elements:**
- Summarize the 3-5 major translation distortions or omissions
- Acknowledge what the common narrative gets RIGHT (intellectual honesty—see Section 4)
- Explain WHY these distortions occurred (innocent summarization? Deliberate framing? Lost nuance?)
- Show side-by-side comparison: "Here's what Wikipedia says" vs. "Here's what Article 7 actually says"

**Avoid:** Conspiracy framing. Most mistranslations are mundane: summaries omit context, technical terms lack English equivalents, or secondary sources cite secondary sources.

---

**5. MODERN RELEVANCE CLOSE (1-2 minutes)**

Why accurate understanding of this document matters today.

**NOT preachy or moralistic.** State factual consequences of the misunderstanding.

**Formula:**
- Who cites this document today (political movements, legal arguments, historical narratives)
- What they claim it proves
- How the original-language text undermines or complicates that claim
- Stakes: what changes if people read the actual document

**Example:** "When Gibraltar's sovereignty is debated, both Britain and Spain cite Article 10 of Utrecht. Britain's English translation says 'ceded in perpetuity.' But the Latin original uses 'in perpetuum'—a legal term that in 1713 didn't necessarily mean 'forever.' And Article 10's fortification clause, often ignored in English summaries, creates obligations Spain argues Britain violated. Reading the Latin doesn't resolve the dispute—but it clarifies that both sides are citing a document most English speakers have never read in full."

---

### Episode Length Philosophy

**Videos should be as long as needed for completeness.** No arbitrary time caps.

- A 10-article statute might require 25 minutes to properly analyze
- A complex treaty with dozens of articles might justify 45+ minutes if focusing on key excerpts
- A short diplomatic note might only need 12 minutes

**Guiding principle:** Completeness over brevity. Viewers who want to understand a primary source are willing to invest time. Truncating analysis to hit an arbitrary length target undermines the format's purpose.

**Reference:** Main channel videos perform well at 8-12 minutes, but Kraut-style deep dives (30-45 min) also maintain strong retention when content justifies length. Prioritize thoroughness.

## Section 2: Visual / Staging Standards

### Split-Screen Core Approach

**The defining visual:** Original-language text on one side, English translation on the other, displayed simultaneously.

**Purpose:** Allows viewers to see the original text even if they can't read it, creating transparency and allowing bilingual viewers to verify translation accuracy.

**Layout intentionally unspecified:** Exact positioning (left/right split, top/bottom, proportions, aspect ratio) will be determined during production. This guide establishes the CONCEPT (split-screen parallel display) without locking specific layout decisions.

**Filming technique details deferred:** Whether this is achieved via screen recording, post-production overlays, or other methods will be decided when producing first video. Guide focuses on what viewers see, not how it's created.

---

### Key Visual Principles

**1. Readability**

Document text must be readable on screen:
- Font size large enough for mobile viewing (test on phone screen)
- High contrast (black text on white background, or vice versa)
- Active clause/section visually distinguished from surrounding text

**Methods for highlighting active text:**
- Yellow highlight on current clause
- Zoom into specific phrase while rest of document fades
- Isolation: show only active clause, with article/section number visible
- Underline or box around key phrase

Choose method based on document density and clause length. Consistent within each video.

---

**2. Original Language Authenticity**

Original text shown AS-IS, never "cleaned up" or modernized.

**Examples:**
- 18th-century spelling preserved ("Britifh" not "British")
- Latin abbreviations kept (show full word in translation)
- Handwritten documents shown in original handwriting (with typed transcription alongside if illegible)
- Archaic grammar retained

**Why:** Modernizing the original defeats the purpose. Viewers need to see the ACTUAL historical text, even if difficult to parse. Translation provides accessibility.

---

**3. Document Authenticity Markers**

Show archival scan or photograph of the original document at least once per video.

**When:**
- During Section 2 (Document Introduction), show full first page or title page
- Optionally, show scan of each article's original page before displaying typed transcription

**Why:** Establishes this is a real historical document, not a paraphrase. Builds trust.

**Sources:** Link to archival source in description (Légifrance, Wikisource, national archives, library digital collections). Include document reference number.

---

**4. Talking Head vs. Document Display Ratio**

**Ratio: ~50-60% document display, ~40-50% talking head**

This is HEAVIER document display than standard channel videos (~30-40% B-roll), but still maintains significant talking head presence.

**When to stay on camera (talking head):**
- Context setup before reading each clause
- Explaining significance after translation
- Making arguments about what distortion reveals
- Connecting to modern relevance
- Synthesis section

**When to show documents (split-screen or original scans):**
- Reading original-language text
- Showing translation alongside original
- Comparing original to existing English mistranslations
- Highlighting specific phrases

**Follow HYBRID_TALKING_HEAD_GUIDE.md principles:** YOU are the authority making the argument. Documents are evidence. If document display doesn't strengthen the argument, return to talking head.

---

**5. Maps and Context Visuals**

Maps, historical photos, and geographic context used during SETUP sections (introduction, context before clauses).

During WALKTHROUGH section, prioritize document display. Cut away to maps only when geographic terms require visualization (e.g., "the boundary shall run from Point A to Point B" warrants showing a map).

## Section 3: Quality Bar & Source Rules

### Source Selection Hierarchy

**Tier 1: Academic/Critical Editions**

Prefer scholarly editions with apparatus (footnotes, commentary, variant readings).

**Examples:**
- Brill's scholarly editions of Latin/Greek texts
- Cambridge University Press critical editions
- Oxford Classical Texts
- Digital editions from university presses with TEI markup

**Why:** Critical editions flag textual variants, provide context, and include expert translation notes. They're the gold standard.

**Citation format:** Include ISBN, editor name, publication year, purchase link. If free archive version exists, link both.

---

**Tier 2: Official Archives**

Government archival scans of original documents.

**Examples:**
- Légifrance (French legal archives)
- UK National Archives
- U.S. National Archives
- National libraries' digital collections

**Why:** Original source documents, unmediated.

**Citation format:** Archive name, document reference number, URL, date accessed.

---

**Tier 3: Wikisource / Collaborative Transcriptions**

Community-transcribed texts from scans.

**Why acceptable:** Often more accessible than paywalled critical editions. Use when Tier 1/2 unavailable.

**Requirement:** Cross-reference with archival scan to verify transcription accuracy. If Wikisource transcription differs from scan, flag the discrepancy.

---

**Unacceptable sources:**
- Secondary source quotations without access to original
- Paraphrases or summaries
- Translations alone (without original-language text)

---

### Translation Accuracy Protocol

**Primary translation: Claude AI**

Use Claude (or equivalent LLM) for initial translation. Specify:
- Source language
- Target language (English)
- Context (legal document, diplomatic correspondence, etc.)
- Request literal translation with notes on terms lacking English equivalents

---

**Cross-check: Independent second source**

Compare Claude's translation against at least ONE independent source:
- DeepL
- Google Translate
- Second AI model (GPT-4, Gemini, etc.)
- Human bilingual speaker (if available)

**When discrepancies arise:**
- Research the disputed term's meaning in historical/legal dictionaries
- Consult academic sources on the document (if available)
- Present BOTH translations on screen with explanation: "DeepL translates this as X, but the literal translation is Y—here's why the distinction matters."

**Treat disagreement as discovery, not error.** Translation is interpretation. Multiple valid renderings often exist.

---

**Legal and Technical Term Annotation**

For specialized terminology without clear English equivalents:
- Show original term on screen
- Provide definition from historical legal dictionary or contemporary source
- Explain how term was understood in the document's era

**Example:** French legal term "déchéance" in 1940s context means "forfeiture of rights/nationality" but carries connotations of moral unworthiness not captured by literal translation. Show both meanings.

---

### Translation Display Standards

**When existing English translations are misleading:**

Show the contrast explicitly:
1. Original-language text
2. Existing English translation (with source attribution)
3. Accurate translation
4. Explanation of distortion

**Example structure:**
- "Here's Article 5 in the original French."
- "Here's how [source] translates it."
- "But a literal translation reveals..."
- "The difference matters because..."

**When no prior English translation exists:**

Present new translation with annotation:
1. Original-language text
2. Literal translation
3. Notes on ambiguous terms or phrases with multiple valid interpretations
4. Acknowledge uncertainty where it exists ("This phrase could mean X or Y—here's the context that suggests X")

**Intellectual honesty:** If translation is uncertain, say so. Confidence without evidence undermines credibility.

---

### Citation Standards

**Every translated clause must reference:**
- Article/section/clause number in original document
- Page number from critical edition (if using academic source)
- Archival reference number (if using government archive)

**Format in video description:**
```
Article VII, page 347 in "Treaty of Utrecht: Critical Edition"
(Cambridge University Press, 2008, ISBN: 978-0-521-xxxxx)

Also available: UK National Archives, Reference SP 108/50
```

---

### Surprise Clause Detection

**Definition:** Clauses where accurate translation contradicts common English-language narratives about the document.

**These are highlight moments.** Spend extra time on surprise clauses:
- Slow down pacing
- Repeat the key phrase
- Show multiple translations if available
- Explain why existing summaries omit or distort this clause
- Connect explicitly to modern misunderstanding

**How to find them:**
- Read full document in original language
- Compare to English-language Wikipedia summary, news articles, political citations
- Flag every clause that existing English sources omit or mischaracterize
- Prioritize these clauses in your walkthrough structure

---

### Standard Channel Quality Rules Apply

All rules from STYLE-GUIDE.md remain in force:
- Real quotes, not summaries
- Academic source verification
- Intellectual honesty (acknowledge counter-evidence)
- Steelmanning opposing interpretations
- No political editorializing

**See:** `.claude/REFERENCE/STYLE-GUIDE.md` for full standards

## Section 4: Tone & Framing Rules

### Base Voice: "Calm Prosecutor"

Same voice as main channel content.

**Reference:** `.claude/REFERENCE/STYLE-GUIDE.md` Part 1 (Core Identity)

**Characteristics:**
- Forensic, calm, slightly sardonic
- Emotionally low, intellectually high
- Confident but not smug
- Skeptical of claims, respectful of viewers

**NOT:**
- Gotcha journalism ("THEY LIED TO YOU!")
- Conspiracy theorist energy
- Partisan or ideological framing
- Moralistic lecturing

---

### Framing: "Let's Read What It Actually Says"

**Approach:** Intellectual curiosity, not accusation.

**Examples:**

✅ "When you read Article 7 in French, the phrasing suggests..."
✅ "The English summary says X, but the Latin text reveals..."
✅ "Interestingly, this clause is almost never cited in English-language sources..."

❌ "They don't want you to know what Article 7 says..."
❌ "The mainstream narrative completely ignores..."
❌ "This PROVES the conspiracy..."

**Why:** Your authority comes from evidence, not outrage. Viewers trust calm analysis over emotional appeals.

---

### Acknowledge Translation Difficulty Openly

**Translation is hard.** Many words lack perfect English equivalents. Historical terms had different meanings in their era.

**When encountering genuinely ambiguous language:**
- State the ambiguity clearly
- Explain why it's difficult to translate
- Provide the 2-3 most likely interpretations
- Explain which interpretation you favor and why

**Example:** "This word has no perfect English equivalent. The closest modern term is X, but in 1713 it might have meant Y. Here's why context suggests Y..."

**This is intellectual honesty, not weakness.** Admitting uncertainty builds credibility.

---

### Translation Disagreements as Discovery

When DeepL, Google Translate, and Claude disagree on a phrase:

**DON'T frame as:** "The AI is wrong"
**DO frame as:** "Interestingly, DeepL renders this as X while the direct translation is Y—here's why that distinction matters."

**Why:** Translation is interpretation. Different tools make different choices. The disagreement itself often reveals nuance.

---

### Steelman Before Correcting

**Pattern from Alex O'Connor / Knowing Better:**

Before showing that common narrative is wrong, acknowledge what it gets RIGHT.

**Example:**
- ❌ "Everyone says the treaty was voluntary, but it wasn't."
- ✅ "The popular summary isn't entirely wrong—Article 3 does describe this as a mutual agreement. But Articles 7 through 12 add context that completely changes the meaning."

**Why:** Viewers who hold the common view feel respected, not attacked. They're more likely to update beliefs when you acknowledge the basis for their current understanding.

---

### No Political Editorializing About Document's Morality

**Your job:** Present what document says, explain what it means, connect to modern misunderstandings.

**NOT your job:** Moralize about whether the document was good or evil.

**The document speaks for itself.** If you're analyzing a law that stripped rights from a group, the clauses will reveal that. You don't need to add emotional commentary.

**Example:**

✅ "Article 2 defines who qualifies as Jewish based on ancestry, not religious practice. This meant converts to Catholicism were still subject to restrictions."

❌ "Article 2 reveals the horrifying racism at the heart of Vichy policy."

**Why:** The first is analysis (what the law did). The second is moral judgment (what we should think about it). Viewers can make their own moral judgments. Your job is to show them what the document actually says.

**Exception:** When connecting to modern relevance, stating factual consequences is appropriate. "This clause is cited today to justify X" is fact. "This clause is evil" is editorializing.

---

### Respect for Original Language

The language of the document is treated with respect, not condescension.

**Pronounce terms correctly:**
- Look up pronunciation of names, places, legal terms
- If you're uncertain, acknowledge: "I'm not fluent in [language], so forgive pronunciation errors"

**Explain etymologies when relevant:**
- If a Latin legal term derives from specific Roman law concept, explain it
- If a French Revolutionary term has specific historical connotations, provide context

**Avoid:** "This weird foreign phrase means..." Frame as: "This term, which doesn't have an English equivalent, refers to..."

---

### Spoken Delivery Reminder

All scripts are read aloud via teleprompter. Apply STYLE-GUIDE.md Part 2 spoken delivery rules:
- Contractions ("it's" not "it is")
- Ordinal dates ("On June 3rd, 1940," not "June 3, 1940")
- Define technical terms immediately
- Avoid essay-style language ("However," "Nevertheless")

**See:** `.claude/REFERENCE/STYLE-GUIDE.md` Part 2 for complete rules

## Example Video Outline

**Document:** Statut des Juifs (Law on the Status of Jews), October 3, 1940, Vichy France

**Video length estimate:** ~25 minutes

---

**HOOK (1:30)**

"When historians discuss Vichy France's persecution of Jews, they often cite the October 1940 Statut des Juifs as evidence of autonomous French antisemitism—proof that Pétain's government didn't need German orders to enact racist laws.

But when you read the actual decree in French, specific clauses reveal something more complicated. Not exoneration—Vichy absolutely collaborated. But the original text shows pressure and constraints that English summaries omit.

I'm going to walk through the document, clause by clause, showing what it says versus what you'll read in most history books."

---

**DOCUMENT INTRODUCTION (2:00)**

- Official title: "Loi du 3 octobre 1940 portant statut des juifs"
- Published in Journal Officiel de la République Française, October 18, 1940
- Show archival scan from Légifrance
- Context: First Vichy law restricting Jewish rights, enacted 4 months after armistice, before major German decrees in occupied zone
- English-language claim: "Proves Vichy acted voluntarily without German pressure"
- Translation status: Full English translations exist (CDJC, Yad Vashem), but they're literal word-for-word translations lacking annotation of terms that had specific legal meanings in 1940s France
- Qualification: Existing translations are accurate but lack context that reveals pressure

---

**WALKTHROUGH (15 minutes)**

**Article 1: Definition of "Jew"**
- Original French: "Est regardé comme juif, pour l'application de la présente loi, toute personne issue de trois grands-parents de race juive ou de deux grands-parents de la même race, si son conjoint lui-même est juif."
- Translation: "Is regarded as Jewish, for the application of this law, any person descended from three grandparents of the Jewish race or from two grandparents of the same race, if their spouse is also Jewish."
- Significance: Racial definition (not religious). Converts to Catholicism still classified as Jewish.
- Myth connection: This article is widely cited. English versions get it right. Move through quickly.

**Article 2: Professions Banned to Jews**
- Lists government positions, teaching, media, entertainment
- Key phrase: "fonctions publiques et mandats énumérés ci-après"
- Significance: Uses bureaucratic language ("enumerated public functions") that obscures impact
- Show contrast: "Jews may not hold public functions" (literal translation) vs. "Jews are systematically excluded from civic life" (what it meant in practice)
- Spend 4-5 minutes here—this is the substance

**Article 3: Exceptions Clause**
- "Pourront toutefois être relevées des interdictions prévues à l'article 2 les personnes juives qui auront rendu des services exceptionnels à l'État français."
- Translation: "May however be exempted from the prohibitions of Article 2 Jewish persons who have rendered exceptional services to the French State."
- **SURPRISE CLAUSE:** This exception was rarely invoked, but its existence shows Vichy knew the law was unjust—they built in exemptions for Jews they considered "useful"
- Spend 3 minutes explaining how this clause is cited by Vichy apologists vs. how rarely it was actually applied

**Article 4-8: Details on application, penalties, enforcement**
- Move through quickly (2-3 minutes total)—administrative language
- Flag that Article 6 references "décrets en Conseil d'État" (decrees by Council of State), showing this went through official legal channels, not emergency decree

**Article 9: Regional Application**
- "La présente loi est applicable dans la zone occupée, sauf en ce qui concerne les fonctionnaires en activité."
- Translation: "This law is applicable in the occupied zone, except regarding active civil servants."
- **SURPRISE CLAUSE:** Why would Vichy specify that the law applies in German-occupied territory if Vichy acted autonomously? This clause implies coordination or German pressure.
- Spend 4 minutes explaining what this reveals about German-Vichy dynamics
- Note: Most English summaries mention this article but don't explore the implication

**Article 10: Effective Date**
- "La présente loi sera exécutée comme loi de l'État"
- Standard closing formula—move through quickly

---

**SYNTHESIS (3 minutes)**

"So what does reading the French original reveal that English summaries miss?

Three things:

**First:** Article 3's exception clause shows Vichy knew the law was discriminatory—they built in carve-outs for Jews they valued. This doesn't appear in most English summaries, which frame the law as absolute prohibition.

**Second:** Article 9's specification that the law applies in occupied territory suggests German involvement or pressure. If Vichy acted purely autonomously, why explicitly state the law applies where Germans control administration?

**Third:** The bureaucratic language throughout ("enumerated functions," "Council of State decrees") shows this was passed through official legal channels, not improvised. Vichy used the machinery of the French state to enact persecution.

The common narrative says Vichy acted voluntarily without German orders. The document supports that Vichy absolutely participated willingly—but Article 9 suggests German coordination. It's not exoneration. It's complication."

---

**MODERN RELEVANCE (2:30)**

"Why does this matter today?

When far-right movements in France cite Vichy to argue 'our grandparents weren't that bad,' they point to the lack of German signatures on this decree. 'It was just administrative reorganization,' they claim.

When anti-fascist historians cite Vichy to show French collaboration, they point to Article 1's racial definition and Article 2's sweeping exclusions.

Both are citing the same document. But reading the French original—especially Article 9—shows the reality was entangled. Vichy built the legal structure. Germans provided pressure and coordination. Both are culpable.

Accurate history isn't about defending or condemning. It's about reading what the documents actually say. And this document, when read in French with attention to clauses like Article 9, reveals collaboration—not autonomous enthusiasm or German coercion alone, but coordination."

---

**End of Example**

## Checklist for Document-Based Videos

Use this checklist when creating document analysis content:

**Pre-Production:**
- [ ] Verified no complete/accurate English translation exists (or existing translation is misleading)
- [ ] Located high-quality source (critical edition, archive scan, or verified Wikisource transcription)
- [ ] Identified 3-5 "surprise clauses" where translation contradicts common narrative
- [ ] Assessed document length and chosen full-coverage or excerpt-based approach
- [ ] Prepared citation information (archive reference, ISBN, page numbers)

**Translation:**
- [ ] Primary translation completed (Claude or equivalent)
- [ ] Cross-checked against at least one independent source (DeepL, Google Translate, etc.)
- [ ] Legal/technical terms annotated with historical definitions
- [ ] Ambiguous phrases flagged with multiple interpretations
- [ ] Translation disagreements researched and resolved (or acknowledged as uncertain)

**Script:**
- [ ] Hook connects mistranslation to modern consequence
- [ ] Document introduction establishes authenticity (archival source, physical description)
- [ ] Clause-by-clause walkthrough includes: context, original, translation, significance, myth connection
- [ ] Synthesis aggregates 3-5 key distortions
- [ ] Modern relevance explains who cites document today and why accurate translation matters
- [ ] Tone maintains "calm prosecutor" voice (not gotcha, not conspiracy)
- [ ] Intellectual honesty: steelmanned common narrative before correcting it
- [ ] Spoken delivery rules applied (contractions, ordinal dates, defined terms)

**Visual Preparation:**
- [ ] Archival scan or photo of original document obtained
- [ ] Original-language text prepared for on-screen display (readable font size)
- [ ] English translation formatted for split-screen display
- [ ] Active clauses can be highlighted/zoomed/isolated
- [ ] Maps prepared for geographic terms (if applicable)
- [ ] Existing mistranslations located for side-by-side comparison (if applicable)

**Quality Gate:**
- [ ] Every translated clause has article/section/page citation
- [ ] Archive source linked in description
- [ ] Purchase link included for critical edition (if applicable)
- [ ] Translation uncertainty acknowledged where it exists
- [ ] No political editorializing about document's morality
- [ ] Video length justified by document complexity (completeness over brevity)
