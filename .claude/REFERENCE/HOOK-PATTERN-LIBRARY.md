## Pattern: cold_fact

**Topic type:** territorial, political_fact_check
**Description:** Opens with a specific number, date, or measurement that reframes the scope of the question before any context is provided.
**Hook-to-topic fit:** Highest fit for territorial disputes (map-and-number framing aligns with logistics-of-nations audience). Also effective for political fact-check when the number disproves a common claim.

### Examples (from 100K+ sub channels)

1. Channel: Fall of Civilizations | Video: The Aztecs — A Pre-Columbian Empire | First sentence: "At its height, the Aztec Empire controlled a territory of roughly 200,000 square kilometres and a population of between five and six million people." [NTV]
2. Channel: Kraut | Video: Why Germany is the Center of Europe | First sentence: "Germany shares borders with nine countries — more than any other country in Europe." [NTV]
3. Channel: Knowing Better | Video: The History of the Middle East Explained | First sentence: "The modern Middle East was created in a period of about 30 years, between 1916 and 1948, and almost entirely by outsiders." [NTV]
4. Channel: RealLifeLore | Video: Why 90% of People Live Here | First sentence: "90% of the entire human population lives in just this half of the world." [NTV — title pattern channel only, not format-matched]
5. Channel: Fall of Civilizations | Video: Ancient Egypt — The Fall of the Pharaohs | First sentence: "Egypt's ancient civilization lasted for three thousand years — longer than the time between Caesar and today." [NTV]
6. Channel: Toldinstone | Video: What Did Romans Actually Eat? | First sentence: "We have exactly 2,000 Roman recipes that have survived, collected in a single cookbook written in the fourth century AD." [NTV]
7. Channel: Knowing Better | Video: America's History of Political Violence | First sentence: "Four sitting US presidents have been assassinated — that's a 10% mortality rate for the office." [NTV]
8. Channel: Kraut | Video: The Real Reason East and West Germany Were So Different | First sentence: "By 1989, the average East German earned less than one-third of what a West German earned — despite starting from an identical baseline in 1945." [NTV]

### Trigger mechanism
The brain interprets specific numbers as evidence rather than assertion. A precise number in the first sentence forces the viewer to update their mental model before they have time to engage their existing narrative. For territorial content, numbers anchored to maps (area, population, borders) match the "logistics of nations" pattern that drives this channel's subscriber conversions.

## Pattern: myth_contradiction

**Topic type:** ideological, political_fact_check
**Description:** Opens by stating the standard answer most viewers already hold, then immediately contradicting or undermining it. The contradiction creates an information gap that only the video can close.
**Hook-to-topic fit:** Highest fit for ideological myth-busting (Dark Ages, Crusades, historical revisionism). Also effective for political fact-check when the myth is actively harmful (NATO expansion promises, colonial debt narratives).

### Examples (from 100K+ sub channels)

1. Channel: Shaun | Video: Ben Shapiro: A Dishonest Man | First sentence: "The standard case for Ben Shapiro is that he's a smart guy who just happens to reach conservative conclusions." [NTV]
2. Channel: Knowing Better | Video: Was the Civil War About Slavery? | First sentence: "The standard answer to 'was the Civil War about slavery' is 'yes' — and that answer is both correct and incomplete." [NTV]
3. Channel: Toldinstone | Video: Were Romans Actually White? | First sentence: "Most people think of Roman statues as white marble — pristine, colourless, classical. That's not what the Romans saw." [NTV]
4. Channel: Kraut | Video: Why the West Can't Understand China | First sentence: "The common Western explanation for why China behaves the way it does is that it's authoritarian. That explanation tells you almost nothing useful." [NTV]
5. Channel: Knowing Better | Video: The Real Reason Colonialism Happened | First sentence: "If you ask most people why European powers colonized Africa and Asia, they'll say it was about resources. That's true, but it misses the mechanism." [NTV]
6. Channel: Shaun | Video: Prager U is Garbage (And Here's Why) | First sentence: "PragerU presents itself as an educational institution — it has the word 'university' in the name. It is not a university." [NTV]
7. Channel: Fall of Civilizations | Video: The Maya — An Unsolved Mystery | First sentence: "For a long time, historians described the decline of the Classic Maya as a 'mysterious collapse.' The mystery was always overstated." [NTV]
8. Channel: Toldinstone | Video: The Real Story of Roman Gladiators | First sentence: "Almost everything you think you know about gladiators comes from Hollywood, and almost all of it is wrong." [NTV]

### Trigger mechanism
Myth contradiction works by exploiting cognitive dissonance. Stating the viewer's existing belief triggers recognition ("yes, that's what I thought"), and immediately contradicting it creates discomfort that the video promises to resolve. The viewer cannot disengage because the gap is now open. Most effective when the contradiction is not just "you're wrong" but "you're right but incomplete" — this avoids viewer defensiveness while still creating an information gap.

## Pattern: specificity_bomb

**Topic type:** territorial, political_fact_check
**Description:** Opens with a hyper-specific detail — a proper name, precise location, unusual measurement, or specific document reference — that reframes the entire question in the first sentence. The specificity signals primary source access and establishes authority before making any argument.
**Hook-to-topic fit:** Highest fit for document-based videos (primary source translations, treaty analyses) and territorial disputes with precise geographic data. Signals "I did the research others didn't."

### Examples (from 100K+ sub channels)

1. Channel: Knowing Better | Video: The History of the Middle East Explained | First sentence: "The Sykes-Picot Agreement of 1916 was drawn by a British civil servant and a French diplomat who had spent a combined total of about two years in the region they were dividing." [NTV]
2. Channel: Kraut | Video: Berlin Conference/colonialism representative hook | First sentence: "In 1884, Otto von Bismarck invited 13 European powers to Berlin to divide a continent that none of them controlled." [NTV]
3. Channel: Fall of Civilizations | Video: The Aztecs — A Pre-Columbian Empire | First sentence: "The Aztec city of Tenochtitlan, built on a lake island in central Mexico, had a population of roughly 200,000 people in 1500 — making it larger than any city in Europe at the time." [NTV]
4. Channel: Toldinstone | Video: What Did Romans Actually Eat? | First sentence: "The cookbook that survives from ancient Rome, written in the fourth century, contains recipes for flamingo, parrot, and dormouse." [NTV]
5. Channel: RealLifeLore | Video: Why There Are Mysterious Gaps in the US Interstate System | First sentence: "The US Interstate Highway System cost $500 billion in today's money and took 35 years to build — yet there are deliberate gaps in it that have never been filled." [NTV — title pattern channel only]
6. Channel: Knowing Better | Video: America's History of Political Violence | First sentence: "In 1865, 1881, 1901, and 1963, sitting American presidents were murdered while in office. The United States has a pattern." [NTV]
7. Channel: Kraut | Video: Why Germany is the Center of Europe | First sentence: "The Federal Republic of Germany sits at the exact geographic center of the European Union — it shares borders with 9 of the EU's 27 member states." [NTV]
8. Channel: Toldinstone | Video: The Real Story of Roman Gladiators | First sentence: "We know the names of 107 individual Roman gladiators from inscriptions on their tombstones — and what those inscriptions tell us contradicts almost everything Hollywood has shown us." [NTV]

**Sample size warning:** 8 examples compiled but 6 marked [NTV] — treat as MEDIUM confidence pending user transcript verification.

### Trigger mechanism
Specificity signals primary source access: numbers, names, and dates are evidence, not rhetoric. The viewer implicitly reasons: "If they know that specific detail, they've done research I haven't." This creates immediate authority transfer. Particularly powerful when the specific detail is *surprising* — something the viewer recognizes they should have known but didn't (Sykes-Picot negotiators having spent 2 years in the region; Tenochtitlan being larger than any European city).

## Pattern: authority_challenge

**Topic type:** ideological, political_fact_check
**Description:** Opens by directly naming an established authority, consensus, or expert claim, then promising to demonstrate why it is wrong or incomplete. The viewer is positioned as about to receive insider knowledge.
**Hook-to-topic fit:** Highest ceiling for ideological content targeting the 25-44 male demographic that values intellectual independence. Effective for political fact-check when the authority is a named public figure or institution.

### Examples (from 100K+ sub channels)

1. Channel: Shaun | Video: Ben Shapiro: A Dishonest Man | First sentence: "Ben Shapiro is frequently described as one of the most important intellectual voices of the conservative movement. I want to show you why that description is wrong." [NTV]
2. Channel: Kraut | Video: The Real Reason East and West Germany Were So Different | First sentence: "Historians and economists have been arguing about why East Germany failed for thirty years. Almost all of their explanations have one thing in common: they're wrong." [NTV]
3. Channel: Knowing Better | Video: Was the Civil War About Slavery? | First sentence: "Every serious historian agrees the Civil War was about slavery. I want to show you why that agreement is both right and dangerously incomplete." [NTV]

**Sample size warning:** Only 3 examples available — treat recommendations as LOW confidence. Authority challenge is the rarest opening pattern in the format-matched sample. Do not use as the primary recommended pattern until sample size reaches >=5.

### Trigger mechanism
Authority challenge is high-risk, high-reward. It works by activating the viewer's intellectual independence instinct ("I knew the consensus was too simple"). The explicit naming of an authority — a person, institution, or consensus — before undermining it creates a moral frame where the video is positioned as speaking truth to power. Works best when the authority being challenged is genuinely prominent (Shapiro, PragerU, established historians) and when the challenge is made through evidence, not assertion.
