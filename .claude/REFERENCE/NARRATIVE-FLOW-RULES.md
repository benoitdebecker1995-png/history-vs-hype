# Narrative Flow Rules

**Created:** 2026-01-16
**Source:** User feedback on Iran scripts
**Status:** MANDATORY for all future scripts

---

## RULE 1: Introduce Every Term Before Using It

**NEVER drop a name, term, or concept without context.**

### Bad Examples:
- "The Qajars had used to sell off Iran" → Who are the Qajars?
- "Shuster's contract was terminated" → Who is Shuster?
- "The Great Game" → What is that?
- "The Basij beat protesters" → Who are the Basij?
- "The bonyads control 40% of the economy" → What are bonyads?

### Good Examples:
- "At this time, Iran was ruled by the Qajar dynasty—a royal family that had been in power since 1789."
- "The Majlis had hired an American financial advisor named Morgan Shuster to fix Iran's finances."
- "Britain and Russia had been competing for influence in Central Asia—a rivalry the British called 'the Great Game.'"
- "The government responds with the Basij—volunteer militias loyal to the Supreme Leader. These aren't regular soldiers. They're ideologically committed civilians, organized in every neighborhood."
- "The bonyads—religious foundations that operate like massive conglomerates—control huge chunks of the economy."

### The Pattern:
```
[Term] — [plain language definition in same breath]
```

## RULE 2: Bridge Every Transition

**Don't jump topics. Every section needs a "why are we talking about this now" sentence.**

### Bad:
```
[End of section about Mossadegh's character]

Here's why oil mattered.
```
→ Random. No connection.

### Good:
```
[End of section about Mossadegh's character]

Mossadegh's conviction would be tested on one issue above all others: oil.

Here's why it mattered—not just economically, but structurally.
```
→ Bridge connects character to next topic.

### Bridge Patterns:
- "This would be tested on..."
- "And that brings us to..."
- "Which created a problem:"
- "That's the context for what happened next."
- "To understand why this mattered, you need to know..."

---

## RULE 3: Quotes Come AFTER Setup

**Never drop a quote without context. Set up what the quote will show FIRST.**

### Bad:
```
> "The council eliminated 99 percent of 3,000 reformists."

That's from Parsa's research.
```

### Good:
```
In 2016, the Guardian Council disqualified 99 percent of reformist candidates who wanted to run for the Majlis.

Parsa documents exactly what happened:

> "The council eliminated 99 percent of 3,000 reformists who had registered to participate in the 2016 Majles elections."

Ninety-nine percent. Before a single vote was cast.
```

### The Pattern:
1. **Setup:** State the claim in your own words
2. **Quote:** Provide the evidence
3. **Implication:** Explain why it matters

---

## RULE 4: Explain Implications

**After every major fact, answer: "Why does this matter?"**

### Bad:
```
Ninety-six deputies turned up, including several who had promised the Shah they would stay away. Every one voted in favor of nationalization.
```
→ Why does it matter that they promised to stay away?

### Good:
```
Ninety-six deputies turned up, including several who had promised the Shah they would stay away. Every one voted in favor of nationalization.

Even deputies who had promised the Shah they would boycott showed up and voted yes. That's how popular nationalization was.

Every single deputy. Unanimous.
```

### Implication Triggers:
- "That's how [adjective] X was."
- "Which meant..."
- "In other words..."
- "Do you see what this means?"
- "The implication:"

---

## RULE 5: Cut Meta-Commentary

**Reduce "Let me show you" and "Here's what most people miss."**

### Overused Phrases to Avoid:
- "Let me show you..."
- "Here's what most people miss..."
- "Here's the part that most people don't realize..."
- "Now, I want to be clear about something..."
- "Let me make sure you understand..."

### When They're OK:
- Once or twice per script for genuine emphasis
- At major structural pivots
- NOT every few paragraphs

### Instead:
Just tell the story directly. Trust the viewer.

---

## RULE 6: Cut Repetition

**Say it once. Say it well. Move on.**

### Signs of Repetition:
- Same fact stated twice in different words
- Quote that just repeats what you said
- Summary that restates the previous paragraph

### Fix:
Delete the weaker version. Keep the stronger one.

---

## RULE 7: Simpler Research Credibility

**Don't oversell your research process.**

### Bad:
"I spent three weeks with 25 academic sources. Primary documents. Declassified cables. Peer-reviewed histories."

### Good:
"So I did some digging. Read books about the topic. Then went to the primary sources."

### Why:
The evidence speaks for itself. Overselling the process sounds defensive.

## RULE 8: Consistent Structure for Repeated Events

**When showing a pattern, use parallel structure so the repetition is visible.**

### Example - Protest Timeline:
```
**2009 - The Green Movement**
- Trigger: Disputed presidential election
- Response: 3 million march
- Mechanism: Basij street violence
- Outcome: 70-110 killed

**2019 - Fuel Prices**
- Trigger: 50-200% price increase
- Response: Protests in 21 cities
- Mechanism: Internet blackout + shoot-to-kill
- Outcome: 300-1,500 killed

**2022 - Mahsa Amini**
- Trigger: Woman dies in custody
- Response: 150 cities, all provinces
- Mechanism: Executions, mass detention
- Outcome: 500+ killed
```

The parallel structure makes the PATTERN visible without having to say "see the pattern?"

---

## RULE 9: Start Sections with Concrete Details

**Open with specific date, place, or document—not abstract statements.**

### Bad:
"The situation in Iran was complicated by foreign interference."

### Good:
"December 1905. Tehran. The governor orders several sugar merchants beaten."

### Opening Patterns:
- Date + Place + Action
- Document on screen + what it shows
- Specific number or statistic
- Direct quote

---

## RULE 10: End with Universal Application

**Connect the specific topic to broader meaning.**

### Bad:
"And that's the history of Iran's constitutional movements."

### Good:
"If you see the pattern, you understand something bigger: this isn't about Islam. It isn't about Persian culture. It isn't about America.

It's about power. Who has it. Who wants real limits on it. And how far they'll go to keep it.

That story applies way beyond Iran."

## PRE-SCRIPT CHECKLIST

Before writing any script, prepare:

- [ ] List of all names/terms that need introduction
- [ ] Define each term in plain language
- [ ] Identify section transitions—write bridge sentences
- [ ] For each quote: what's the setup? what's the implication?
- [ ] Check: am I repeating myself anywhere?

---

## POST-SCRIPT CHECKLIST

After drafting, scan for:

- [ ] Any term used before being introduced?
- [ ] Any random transitions without bridges?
- [ ] Any quotes dropped without setup?
- [ ] Any facts without implications explained?
- [ ] Excessive meta-commentary? ("Let me show you...")
- [ ] Repetition that can be cut?
- [ ] Research credibility oversold?

---

## TERMS THAT ALWAYS NEED INTRODUCTION

### Historical/Political:
- Dynasty names (Qajar, Pahlavi, etc.)
- Treaty names (Anglo-Russian Convention, etc.)
- Political movements (National Front, Tudeh, etc.)
- Military units (Cossack Brigade, IRGC, Basij, etc.)

### Economic:
- Concession names (D'Arcy, etc.)
- Organizations (bonyads, AIOC, etc.)
- Economic terms (nationalization vs confiscation, etc.)

### Legal/Constitutional:
- Constitutional concepts (Velayat-e Faqih, etc.)
- Institutions (Guardian Council, Majlis, etc.)
- Legal terms (bast, estoppel, etc.)

### People:
- Every historical figure on first mention
- Include: role, time period, why they matter
