## Quick Reference

| Rule | Wrong | Right | Why |
|------|-------|-------|-----|
| Ordinal dates | "June 16, 2014." | "On June 16th, 2014," | Natural spoken cadence |
| Contractions | "it is" | "it's" | Sounds human |
| Term definitions | "estoppel" | "estoppel - a legal rule that..." | Viewers need context |
| Informational lists | "Britain. France. Egypt." | "Britain, France, Egypt" | Staccato only for emphasis |
| "Here's" usage | 10+ per script | 2-4 per script | Overuse sounds scripted |
| Abbreviations | "The AU" | "The African Union" | First use: full name |
| Quote introduction | "Quote: 'The treaty...'" | "The treaty actually says..." | Conversational |
| Opening | Background first | Stakes/payoff first | Prevents 1:11 dropout |
| Hedging | "This suggests..." | "The truth is..." | Confident assertions |
| Active voice | "was signed by the Shah" | "the Shah signed" | Direct and clear |
| Direct address | "One might wonder..." | "You might wonder..." | Personal connection |
| Fragments | "A judge. 1963. Invalid." (info) | "Britain never built it." (emphasis) | Rhetorical only |

## Part 1: Core Identity (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

### Channel Voice

**Who you are:** Evidence-based referee between opposing camps. Not a polemicist. Not a fence-sitter. Someone who goes to the documents and reports what they actually say.

**Your mission (in your words):**
> "I actually show the primary sources and try to bring the academic historical method to the wider audience to show people why history matters and why recognizing certain historical patterns are important."

**The Triangle: "Forensic, Calm, Slightly Sardonic"**

| Influence | What You Take |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Kraut** | Sober, analytic, deep causal chains |
| **Alex O'Connor** | Rational, conversational, intellectually honest |
| **History Matters** | Dry, understated humor |

**Your spot:** "Calm Prosecutor" - emotionally low, intellectually high. Confident but not smug. Skeptical of claims, respectful of viewers.

### Spoken Delivery (THE core constraint)

Scripts are read aloud via teleprompter. They must sound natural when spoken.

**The Stumble Test:** If a line would make a presenter stumble, pause awkwardly, or mentally translate written prose into speech, rewrite it.

**The "Would I say this?" Test:** Would you actually say this out loud explaining to a friend? If not, rewrite it.

### Forbidden Phrases

| Phrase | Why Forbidden |
|--------|---------------|
| "Let me show you something" | Sounds like YouTube cliche |
| "Here's where it gets interesting" | Overused hook pattern |
| "And that's the key insight" | Essay conclusion language |
| "Buckle up" | Clickbait energy |
| "Stay with me here" | Implies content is boring |
| "Here's the thing" | Filler phrase |
| "You won't believe..." | Clickbait |
| "SHOCKING" | Clickbait |
| "Drop your thoughts below" | Generic CTA |
| "Smash that subscribe" | YouTube cliche |
| "In this video, we will examine..." | Essay opening |
| "It's important to note that..." | Filler |
| "However," "Nevertheless," "Subsequently" | Too formal for spoken |

### Approved Phrases

| Phrase | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|
| "This might surprise you but..." | Before surprising fact |
| "The reality is..." / "The truth is..." | For conclusions/revelations |
| "On top of that..." | Adding evidence |
| "Furthermore" | Adding evidence (formal) |
| "If we are being fair..." | Before steelmanning |
| "As you can see..." | Showing documents |
| "Here's what [X] actually says." | Document reveals (2-4x per script max) |
| "But here's where it gets interesting." | Major pivot (1x per script max) |
| "So, I read/checked/found..." | Establishing research authority |
| "Look at what just happened." | Drawing attention to evidence |
| "Notice this specific phrase." | Document close-reading |
| "please subscribe" | Polite CTA |

## Part 2: Spoken Delivery Rules

### Date Formats

| Rule | Wrong | Right |
|------|-------|-------|
| Ordinal dates with "On" | "June 16, 2014. A farmer..." | "On June 16th, 2014, a farmer..." |
| Duration context | "In 1915" | "Six months later - May 1916" |
| Specific precision | "at some point" | "December 1905. Tehran." |

### Contractions

| Default | Emphasis |
|---------|----------|
| "It's not about oil" | "It is NOT about oil" |
| "They didn't care" | "They did NOT care" |
| "wasn't" not "was not" | Expand only for emphasis |

### Abbreviation Expansion

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| "The AU responded" (first use) | "The African Union responded" |
| "The ICJ ruled" (first use) | "The International Court of Justice ruled" |

**Rule:** Full name on first use, abbreviation thereafter.

### Term Definitions

**Pattern:** `[Technical term] - [plain language definition in same breath]`

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| "The principle is called estoppel." | "The principle is called estoppel - a legal rule that says you can't benefit from an agreement for decades, then suddenly claim it never existed." |
| "The Basij beat protesters." | "The government responds with the Basij - volunteer militias loyal to the Supreme Leader." |
| "The bonyads control 40%." | "The bonyads - religious foundations that operate like massive conglomerates - control huge chunks of the economy." |

**Key phrases for explanations:**
- "In simpler terms..."
- "Translation:"
- "The Court is saying:"
- "In other words..."

### List Formatting

**Informational lists:** Use flowing commas
| Wrong (choppy) | Right (flows) |
|----------------|---------------|
| "World War I. Foreign occupation. Famine." | "World War I, foreign occupation, famine." |

**Rhetorical beats:** Use staccato periods
| Purpose | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Building tension | "Not a promise. Not a transition. Independence." |
| Dramatic emphasis | "Iran's first democracy - crushed." |
| Key fact impact | "Britain never built it." |

**The test:** Would a presenter instinctively combine these while speaking? If yes, write them combined.

### "Here's" Usage

| Usage Level | Verdict |
|-------------|---------|
| 2-4 per script | Natural |
| 5-6 per script | Borderline |
| 10+ per script | Overuse - rewrite |

**Good uses:**
- "Here's what the 1859 treaty actually says." (document reveal)
- "Here's what historians actually say." (expert evidence)

**Alternatives when overused:**
| Instead of | Try |
|------------|-----|
| "Here's what they found:" | "This is what they found:" |
| "Here's the mechanism:" | "The mechanism was this:" |
| "Here's how it worked:" | "The deal worked like this:" |

### Colons vs. Periods

**Colons:** Use for introducing direct quotes or formal definitions
> "The treaty states: 'All territories south of the line...'"

**Periods:** Use for spoken pauses
| Written feel | Spoken feel |
|--------------|-------------|
| "They had a problem: they were broke." | "They had a problem. They were broke." |
| "The result: Khomeini's faction won." | "The result was that Khomeini's faction won." |

### Quote Attribution

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| "Quote: 'Sudan is defined as...'" | "The treaty says Sudan is defined as..." |
| "The AU's position: 'Firmly rejects...'" | "The African Union firmly rejected any initiative..." |

## Part 3: Voice Patterns

### Your Transition Words

| Function | Your Patterns | Avoid |
|----------|---------------|-------|
| Contrast | "But," "Still," "Yet" | "However," "Nevertheless" |
| Result | "So" (conclusions and questions) | "Subsequently" |
| Addition | "And," "Furthermore," "On top of that" | "With regard to" |
| Revelation | "The truth is...," "The reality is..." | "It should be noted" |
| Invitation | "Let's start with...," "Let's look at..." | "In this video we will" |
| Topic shift | "Now" ("Now open a Guatemalan map") | Random jumps |
| Interest hook | "But here's where it gets interesting" (1x max) | Overuse |

### High-Performance Patterns

From Belize (23K views) and Vance (42.6% retention):

**Zero/None for Impact:**
- "Were any Maya leaders consulted? No."
- "How many Maya representatives are involved? Zero."
- "Three claims checked, none held up."

**Stakes Immediate (First 30 Seconds):**
- Belize: "...whether Belize should even exist"
- Vance: "I fact-checked VP Vance's claims"
- Pattern: What's at stake RIGHT NOW, not historical background first

**First-Person Ownership Early:**
- "So, I read that treaty." (Belize, ~25 sec)
- "I fact-checked..." (Vance, ~15 sec)
- Establish research authority in first minute

**Direct Corrections:**
- "Actually, the first nation to abolish slavery was Haiti."
- "Okay, this one's interesting because he's repeating archaeology from the 1980s that has been debunked."

### Fragments for Emphasis

**USE fragments for rhetorical impact:**
- "They won."
- "Zero."
- "Same pattern. Different players."
- "Britain never built it."
- "Not 1916. Fifteen years."
- "Not 'declared.' Restored."

**DON'T USE fragments for compressed prose:**
| Wrong (compressed prose) | Right (flowing sentence) |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| "A sitting judge. 1963. No valid union." | "A sitting judge ruled in 1963 that there was no valid union." |
| "Population: 4.5 million. GDP: $2.1 billion." | "Somaliland has a population of 4.5 million and a GDP of $2.1 billion." |

### Casual Asides

**1-2 per script.** Brief moments that add personality:
- "Yeah, that Kiev, the one that Russia is bombing with missiles."
- "Yeah, that's a punishment where they whip the soles of your feet."

### Signature Phrases

**USE these (high-performing):**
- "The truth is..." / "The reality is..."
- "Let's start with..." / "Let's look at..."
- "Here's what [X] actually says." (2-4x max)
- "As you can see..."
- "If we are being fair..."
- "So, I read/checked/found..."
- "That's not my interpretation - that's [source]."
- "Reading directly from [source]:"

**AVOID these:**
- "Let me show you"
- "Buckle up"
- "Stay with me here"
- Excessive "Here's" (10+ per script)

## Part 4: Structure & Flow

### Opening Patterns

**Core principle:** Payoff in first 45-60 seconds. Don't bury the thesis.

**The 1:11 Drop-Off Problem:** Viewers leave when you drift into background context before establishing stakes.

**Rule:** No pure context before you've:
1. Named the myth
2. Stated your verdict (even if provisional)
3. Teased the key piece of evidence

**Formula A: Both-Extremes-Wrong (Your Signature)**
```
"[Topic] gets thrown around constantly. But here's the problem.
One side says [Extreme A].
The other insists [Extreme B].
The evidence reveals something more complex."
```

**Formula B: Document-First (9-10x Outlier Pattern)**
```
0:00-0:05  [SHOW: Document on screen] "This is the map they ignored."
0:05-0:10  [SHOW: Different document] "This is what they drew instead."
0:10-0:20  [Split-screen comparison] Context sentence.
0:20-0:30  [Modern conflict] "That decision created [consequence]."
```

**Formula C: "I Went to the Documents"**
```
"I went to [specific source type]. The actual [treaty/report/letters].
[Archive name] records.
[What I found]."
```

**For full templates:** See `OPENING-HOOK-TEMPLATES.md`

### Quote Integration

**3-Step Pattern:**
1. **Setup:** State the claim in your own words
2. **Quote:** Provide the evidence
3. **Implication:** Explain why it matters

**Example:**
```
SETUP: "In 2016, the Guardian Council disqualified 99 percent of reformist candidates."

QUOTE: Parsa documents exactly what happened:
"The council eliminated 99 percent of 3,000 reformists who had registered..."

IMPLICATION: "Ninety-nine percent. Before a single vote was cast."
```

**Quote Introduction Patterns:**
| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Attribution-First | "According to Chris Wickham in *Medieval Europe*, page 147..." |
| Claim-Then-Source | "The Carolingians produced ten times more manuscripts. That's Buringh and Van Zanden's analysis." |
| Block Quote Display | "Here's what [document] actually says:" [DOCUMENT on screen] |
| Historian as Witness | "As the historian John Delon described it: '[exact quote]'" |
| Reading Directly | "Reading directly from the letter: '[exact quote]'" |

### Transition Bridges

**Every section needs connection to the next.**

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| "This would be tested on..." | "Mossadegh's conviction would be tested on one issue above all others: oil." |
| "And that brings us to..." | "And that brings us to the document everyone forgets." |
| "Which created a problem:" | "Which created a problem: who actually owned the territory?" |
| "That's the context for..." | "That's the context for what happened next." |

**Kraut-style transitions:**
> "But the successor kingdoms just built the foundation. The Carolingians provided the ambition."

### Section Openings

**Start with concrete details, not abstractions.**

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| "The situation was complicated by foreign interference." | "December 1905. Tehran. The governor orders several sugar merchants beaten." |
| "Let's examine the economic factors." | "The concession document specified: 84% of profits to Britain." |

### Closing Patterns

**Return-to-Extremes Conclusion (Your Signature):**
```
"That first narrative - [Side A claim]? [How evidence complicates it].
But the second narrative - [Side B claim]? [How evidence complicates it].
[Synthesis/what the evidence actually shows]."
```

**Universal Application Ending:**
```
"If you see the pattern, you understand something bigger:
This isn't about [narrow framing]. It's about [broader principle].
That story applies way beyond [specific topic]."
```

## Part 5: Creator Techniques (Summary)

### Quick Reference

| Creator | Core Technique | Voice Marker |
|---------|----------------|--------------|
| **Kraut** | Deep causal chains | "consequently," "thereby," "which meant that" |
| **Alex O'Connor** | Intellectual honesty | "That's fair. But..." / "I'm not sure about..." |
| **Knowing Better** | Source forensics | "Where did they get X?" |
| **Shaun** | Document-first debunking | Simple story -> authority stack -> "So what's going on?" |
| **Historia Civilis** | Precise numbered details | "Using the sources, we can identify five provisions..." |
| **Fall of Civilizations** | Multi-cause framing | "It wasn't just X. It was X, Y, and Z." |

### Kraut Techniques

**Deep Causal Chains:**
Use "consequently," "thereby," "which meant that," "as a result"
> "The mongols enforced their economic order -> the centre of Russian power shifted -> these backwaters became central -> Russia developed differently from Europe."

**Comparative Analysis:**
> "While in Europe [X happened], in Russia [the opposite happened]."

**Sweep-Then-Specifics:**
> Open: "Nomadic empires were some of the most fascinating entities in human history."
> Then: "The Seljuk Turks conquered Iran and became the new Iranian empire."

### Alex O'Connor Techniques

**Intellectual Honesty:**
- Concede valid points before rebutting: "That's fair. But..."
- Admit uncertainty: "I'm not sure about this..."
- Flag expertise limits: "I'm probably not the best person to ask about..."

**Steelmanning:**
- "The strongest version of their argument goes like this: [charitable interpretation]."
- "If I'm being fair to them, here's their best case: [steelman]."

**Think-Aloud Commentary:**
> "Now I find this interesting but not particularly existential. So I'm a bit surprised to find it this low down."

### Shaun Techniques

**Quote Stacking (Authority Stack):**
```
"[Clean statement of the myth]
...
However.
This is [Authority 1] who was [credentials]... and they wrote: [QUOTE]
[Authority 2] who was [credentials] stated: [QUOTE]
So what's going on here then?"
```

**Document-as-Verdict:**
Let documents deliver moral judgment. Avoid personal pronouncements.

### Source Verification (Knowing Better)

- Trace claims to original: "Where did they get that number?"
- Expose truncation: "Notice what's missing from this quote..."
- Translation skepticism: "What does the original language say?"

**For complete patterns and examples:** See `creator-techniques.md`

### 6.1 Opening Formulas

#### Opening: Visual Contrast Hook (Belize Pattern)

**When to use:** Territorial disputes with map contradictions

**Formula:** [Action: Open map A] → [What you see] → [Action: Open opposing map B] → [Contradiction revealed] → [Stakes statement]

**Example from Belize (23K views):**
> "Open a map of Central America, you see Belize, a small country on the Caribbean coast. Now open a Guatemalan map. Belize disappears, instead you see Territorio de Belize. Guatemala claims this entire country as their own. Both sides point to an 1859 treaty to prove who's right."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "Open a map of [region], you see [entity], [brief description]. Now open a [opposing party]'s map. [Entity] disappears, instead you see [their claim name]. [Opposing party] claims [territory description] as their own. Both sides point to [document/year] to prove who's right."

#### Opening: Current Event Hook (Essequibo Pattern)

**When to use:** Active territorial disputes with military dimension

**Formula:** [Specific date/time] → [Military action with precise details] → [Quote/radio transmission] → [Context of dispute] → "To understand what's really happening..."

**Example from Essequibo (1.9K views):**
> "March 1st, 2025, 7am, a Venezuelan warship sails within 700 meters of an American oil platform, producing over 200,000 barrels daily. Over the radio, 'you are operating in Venezuelan waters,' but the warship is deep inside territory Guyana has controlled since 1899."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Specific date], [time], [military/diplomatic action with exact numbers]. [Quote or dialogue]. But [contradiction/dispute fact]. To understand what's really happening, I went to [primary source type]."

#### Opening: Fact-Check Declaration (Vance Pattern)

**When to use:** Political figure fact-checks

**Formula:** [Quote from figure] → "I fact-checked [name]'s claims with [source type]." → "Here's what [authority] actually says."

**Example from Vance (42.6% retention):**
> "What did the Christian settlers find when they came to the New World? They found a lot of civilizations that were murdering babies in weird religious rituals. I fact-checked VP Vance's claims about colonialism with original sources. Here's what historians actually say."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Quote from political figure making historical claim]. I fact-checked [name]'s claims with [academic sources/primary documents]. Here's what [historians/documents] actually say."

#### Opening: Personal Research Authority (Belize Variant)

**When to use:** Establishing documentary evidence early

**Formula:** [Dispute statement] → "So, I read that [document]." → [What one side claims] → [What document actually says] → [Stakes/current status]

**Example from Belize (~25 sec in):**
> "Both sides point to an 1859 treaty to prove who's right. So, I read that treaty. Guatemala says it's void because Britain violated it. But the treaty itself doesn't make the boundary conditional on Britain's obligations."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "Both sides cite [document/treaty] to prove their case. So, I read that [document]. [Side A] says [claim]. But the [document] actually [what it says]. [Current stakes or modern consequence]."

#### Opening: Escalation Timeline (Essequibo Variant)

**When to use:** Diplomatic crisis with recent confrontation

**Formula:** [Initial incident] → [Timeline advance: X days later] → [High-level response] → [Quote from official] → [Counter-response] → [Who lives there]

**Example from Essequibo:**
> "26 days after that warship confrontation, US Secretary of State Marco Rubio lands in Georgetown. At the joint press conference... Rubio stares into the camera. 'It would be a very bad day for the Venezuelan regime if they were to attack Guyana or Exxon Mobil.' Within hours Maduro fires back. 'There goes the imbecile Marco Rubio, threatening Venezuela from Guyana.'"

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[X days/weeks] after [incident], [high-level official] [action]. [Quote showing escalation]. [Counter-party] responds: '[escalation quote].' That confrontation made global headlines. But let me tell you who actually lives in the territory they're fighting over."

### 6.2 Transition Sequences

#### Transition: Kraut-Style Causal Chain

**When to use:** Explaining multi-step causation

**Formula:** [Event A] → [connector: consequently/thereby/which meant that] → [Result B] → [connector] → [Result C]

**Example from Belize:**
> "The 1850 Clayton-Bulwer Treaty with the United States said Britain couldn't grab new Central American territory after 1850. But Britain had already pushed past their limits. Guatemala meanwhile faced an existential threat. An American mercenary named William Walker had made himself president of Nicaragua in 1856, backed by American investors. Guatemala genuinely believed they were next. They needed British weapons and backing or they could be conquered by Walker's private army."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Event A] happened, which meant that [Result B], consequently [Result C]. This created [final outcome]."

**Connector words:** consequently, thereby, which meant that, which created, as a result

#### Transition: Temporal Jump with "Now"

**When to use:** Contrasting two perspectives or time periods

**Formula:** [Complete section] → "Now" + [new topic/time period] → [bridging sentence explaining relevance]

**Example from Belize:**
> "Open a map of Central America, you see Belize, a small country on the Caribbean coast. Now open a Guatemalan map. Belize disappears..."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Establish context]. Now [shift to contrasting view/time/location]. [Connection sentence explaining why this matters]."

#### Transition: "So how did we get here?"

**When to use:** Pivoting from modern consequence to historical chronology

**Formula:** [Present consequence described] → "So how did we get here?" → [Historical chronology begins]

**Example from Belize:**
> "Britain says the border is permanent regardless. So how did we get here? Spain claimed this territory since the 1494 Treaty of Tordesillas. Britain started logging in the 1600s..."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Modern dispute framed]. So how did we get here? [Begin historical chronology with earliest relevant event]."

#### Transition: "But here's where it gets interesting" (LIMIT: 1x per script)

**When to use:** Introducing complication that changes interpretation

**Formula:** [Expected narrative] → "But here's where it gets interesting." → [Complication/revelation]

**Example from Vance:**
> "Child sacrifice did occur in Aztec, Maya and Inca societies. Recent archaeological work confirms this. There's a 2024 DNA study in Nature about it. But here's where it gets interesting. Mexican archaeologists found 126 individuals at the Templo Mayor site, across all types of sacrifice. Spanish chronicler Diego Durán claimed over 80,000 victims in a single 1487 ceremony at that complex."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Establish initial fact that seems to support common narrative]. But here's where it gets interesting. [Introduce evidence that complicates or contradicts that interpretation]."

#### Transition: "Look at what just happened"

**When to use:** Revealing pattern or manipulation in a sequence of events

**Formula:** [Series of events described] → "Look at what just happened." → [Interpretation/implication drawn from sequence]

**Example from Belize:**
> "[Treaty details about boundaries and dates described]. Look at what just happened. The 1786 treaties had Britain's southern limit at the Sibun River. But Britain had pushed 60 miles further south to the Sarstoon River. This new treaty recognizes that unauthorized expansion."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Describe sequence of actions neutrally]. Look at what just happened. [Reveal the pattern, manipulation, or significance that wasn't obvious during the narration]."

#### Transition: "Which brings us to..." / "And that brings us to..."

**When to use:** Moving from background to key moment/document

**Formula:** [Context/background complete] → "Which brings us to [key moment/document]" → [New section begins]

**Example from Vance (structure):**
> "[Background on Spanish exaggeration pattern]. Which brings us to a question. Why does anyone in 2025 care about rehabilitating these myths?"

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Background or context established]. Which brings us to [pivotal moment/document/question]. [Begin new section that builds on previous context]."

#### Transition: Date as Section Break

**When to use:** Moving chronologically through sequence of events

**Formula:** [Narrative flow] → [DATE.] → [New event] → [Causal connection]

**Example from Belize:**
> "Still, no road. August 1863. Britain and Guatemala try again. New deal. Britain would pay 50,000 pounds..."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Previous section conclusion]. [Specific date]. [New development]. [How it connects to previous events]."

#### Transition: Contrast Pair (Competing Perspectives)

**When to use:** Showing how two sides interpret same events differently

**Formula:** [Side A perspective] → [Side B with "X saw it completely different"] → [Explanation of divergence]

**Example from Belize:**
> "From Britain's perspective, the boundary provisions in Articles 1-6 were separate from the road provision in Article 7. The road was a nice bonus that didn't work out. The boundary? That was permanent. Guatemala saw it completely different. The road was the point. Essential compensation for giving up territory. No road meant no deal."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "From [Side A]'s perspective, [their interpretation]. [Their conclusion]. [Side B] saw it completely different. [Their interpretation]. [Their conclusion]. [Why this divergence matters]."

### 6.3 Evidence Introduction Patterns

#### Evidence: Setup → Quote → Implication (3-Step)

**When to use:** Introducing academic quotes or primary sources

**Formula:** [State claim in your words] → [Source attribution] + [Quote] → [Repeat key number/phrase for emphasis] → [Why it matters]

**Example from Vance:**
> "Spanish conquistadors consistently inflated accounts of indigenous practices. Spanish chronicler Diego Durán claimed over 80,000 victims in a single 1487 ceremony at that complex. The Codex Telleriano-Remensis, written by elderly Aztec informants, reports about 4,000 victims for that ceremony. That's 20 times inflation. Still horrific, but the Spanish accounts were exaggerating four to eight times."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Context: what the debate is about]. [Authority figure] claimed [inflated/disputed number or fact]. [More reliable source with credentials] reports [actual finding]. That's [calculation of discrepancy]. [Implication: why this exaggeration/error matters]."

#### Evidence: "Notice this specific phrase"

**When to use:** Close-reading treaty/document language for hidden significance

**Formula:** [Document shown] → "Notice this specific phrase." → [Quote exact text] → [Explain why wording matters]

**Example from Belize:**
> "Notice this specific phrase in the treaty. 'Boundaries that existed previous to and on the first day of January 1850.' That date isn't random. By dating the boundary to January 1st 1850, the day before the Clayton-Bulwer treaty banned new acquisitions, Britain could tell Washington: We're not acquiring new territory. We're just recognizing boundaries that already existed."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "Notice this specific phrase. '[Exact text from document].' That [word/date/phrasing] isn't random. [Explain significance: what it reveals about intent, what it legally allows, what it conceals]."

#### Evidence: "Here's what [X] actually says" (2-4x per script max)

**When to use:** Document reveal (B-roll moment)

**Formula:** "Here's what [document/person] actually says." → [Display document/quote] → [Key phrase emphasized] → [Interpretation]

**Example from Belize:**
> "Here's what the 1859 treaty actually says. Article 7. The two parties will use their best efforts for establishing the easiest communication between the Atlantic and Pacific oceans..."

**Example from Vance:**
> "Here's what historians actually say. [Launches into archaeological evidence]"

**Copy-paste template:**
> "Here's what [document/treaty/historian] actually says. [Show evidence with key phrase]. [Emphasize critical detail]. [What this means for the competing narratives]."

#### Evidence: "Reading directly from..."

**When to use:** Formal document language needs exact quoting

**Formula:** "Reading directly from [source]:" → [Exact quote] → [Plain language translation if needed]

**Example from Belize:**
> "Article 7. The two parties will use their best efforts for establishing the easiest communication between the Atlantic and Pacific oceans. They will contribute towards the construction of a cart road. [Then explains what this meant in practice]"

**Copy-paste template:**
> "Reading directly from [source]: '[Exact legal/technical language with archaic phrasing].' In other words, [plain language version that viewers can understand]."

#### Evidence: Quote Stacking (Authority Stack)

**When to use:** Multiple sources confirm same fact, building credibility

**Formula:** [State disputed claim] → [Authority 1] + credentials + quote → [Authority 2] + credentials + quote → "So what's going on?"

**Example from Vance (debunking brothel infanticide):**
> "2014 DNA analysis of Yewden Villa. Balanced male to female ratio. All from different mothers. No trauma, proper burial, 97 infant deaths over 300 years of occupation. That's 0.32 per year. Normal infant mortality for the period. 2015 Cambridge University. 'We trust that this evidence disposes once and for all with the suggestion that infanticide was the norm in Roman Britain.' Bioarchaeologist Sian Halcrow in 2017 specifically called this out. 'Whole cemeteries of unwanted brothel babies. These are sensational research stories that capture headlines but misrepresent archaeological evidence.'"

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Claim to debunk]. [Authority 1] who was [credentials] found: '[key finding]'. [Authority 2 with different credentials] stated: '[confirming or expanding finding]'. [Authority 3] specifically called this out: '[meta-commentary on the misinterpretation]'. So what's going on?"

### 6.4 Sentence Rhythm Patterns

#### Rhythm: Long Setup + Short Punch

**Effect:** Creates emphasis through contrast, makes key fact memorable

**Example from Belize:**
> "A British surveyor estimated 157 miles through dense jungle, costing around 145 pounds, around 18 million in today's money. Britain never built it."

**Example from Belize:**
> "The Maya built this around 800, a thousand years before this treaty. When Wyke and Aycinena signed in 1859, there were two European powers carving up lands the Maya had built civilizations on. Were any Maya leaders consulted? No."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Long sentence with multiple details, commas, specific numbers building context and stakes]. [Short 2-5 word declarative sentence delivering verdict or key fact]."

#### Rhythm: Question → Zero/None Answer

**Effect:** Dramatic emphasis on exclusion, absence, or complete failure

**Example from Belize:**
> "Were any Maya leaders consulted? No."
> "How many Maya representatives are involved in the case? Zero."

**Example from Vance:**
> "Archaeological surveys of these societies shows that there's zero evidence of child sacrifice."
> "Three claims checked, none held up."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Question about inclusion/representation/evidence]? Zero. / None. / [Single emphatic word]."

#### Rhythm: Fragment for Verdict

**Effect:** Delivers moral or factual judgment with weight

**Example from Vance:**
> "[Description of Haitian revolution defeating French, British, Spanish armies]. They won."

**Example from Belize:**
> "Years went by and Britain built nothing. Colonial conflicts in India and China took priority. The road was expensive and would help Guatemala, but offer Britain nothing. So Britain just didn't do it."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Context paragraph building tension or expectation]. [2-5 word declarative sentence]. [Continue with implications]."

#### Rhythm: Contrast Pair (This vs. That)

**Effect:** Highlights contradiction or competing perspectives in parallel structure

**Example from Belize:**
> "Britain treated the boundary as settled. Guatemala treated it as theft."

**Example from Vance:**
> "Christians didn't just abolish slavery. Christians also defended slavery for centuries using the Bible."

**Copy-paste template:**
> "[Side A] [verb] it as [interpretation]. [Side B] [verb] it as [opposite interpretation]."

OR:

> "[Group] didn't just [positive action]. [Same group] also [contradictory action] for [timeframe]."

## Part 7: Quality Checklist (Unified)

### Pre-Script

- [ ] All terms/names listed for introduction
- [ ] Bridge sentences planned for each transition
- [ ] Quote setup/implication planned for each quote
- [ ] Opening formula selected (Both-Extremes, Document-First, etc.)

### Post-Script: Spoken Delivery

- [ ] **Stumble test passed:** Read entire script aloud, no awkward pauses
- [ ] **"Here's" count:** 2-4 (not 10+)
- [ ] **Contractions used** throughout (it's, they're, wasn't)
- [ ] **Dates in spoken format** (On June 16th, 2014)
- [ ] **No informational fragments** (flowing sentences for lists)
- [ ] **Rhetorical fragments preserved** for emphasis
- [ ] **Colons converted to periods** for spoken pauses (except quote intros)
- [ ] **Abbreviations expanded** on first use

### Post-Script: Voice Profile

- [ ] **No forbidden phrases** ("Let me show you," "Buckle up," etc.)
- [ ] **Approved phrases used** ("The reality is," "If we are being fair")
- [ ] **Your transitions** ("But," "So," "Now" - not "However," "Nevertheless")
- [ ] **Confident assertions** (no hedging - "The truth is..." not "This suggests...")
- [ ] **First-person ownership early** ("So, I read that treaty" in first minute)
- [ ] **Zero/None impact moment** (at least 1: "How many? Zero.")
- [ ] **Casual aside included** (1-2 per script)

### Post-Script: Structure

- [ ] **Stakes in first 30 seconds** (what's at stake NOW)
- [ ] **Every term defined** on first use
- [ ] **Bridge sentences** for every transition
- [ ] **Quotes have setup AND implication**
- [ ] **No repetition** (same fact in different words)
- [ ] **Concrete section openings** (date/place/document, not abstractions)
- [ ] **Return-to-extremes** or universal application in conclusion

### Post-Script: Intellectual Honesty

- [ ] **Steelmanning present:** Best version of opposing argument addressed
- [ ] **Valid points conceded** before rebutting ("That's fair. But...")
- [ ] **Uncertainty admitted** where evidence is ambiguous
- [ ] **Caveat inserted** after strong claims where appropriate
- [ ] **Both extremes addressed** (not one-sided)

### Post-Script: Evidence

- [ ] **Every major claim cited** with author name
- [ ] **Real quotes used** (not just summaries)
- [ ] **Primary sources displayed** as B-roll where available
- [ ] **Page numbers included** where possible
- [ ] **Documents shown, not just described**

- [ ] **Academic attributions:** Lead with story, not citations (move "According to X" to evidence display)
- [ ] **Rhetorical emphasis cut:** Remove "Do you see what this means?" phrases
