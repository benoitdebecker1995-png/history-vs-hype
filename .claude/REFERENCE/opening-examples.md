# Opening Examples Bank

**Purpose:** Pattern-matched examples of effective video openings.
**Use this for:** Direct templates when writing script hooks and "both extremes" framing.

---

## OPENING TYPE 1: Pattern → Exception (Kraut Signature)

### Template:
```
[Sweeping pattern with multiple examples]
"[Example 1]. [Example 2]. [Example 3]. [Example 4]."
"There is however one big exception: [Your subject]."
```

### Full Example (Origins of Russian Authoritarianism):
> "Nomadic empires were some of the most fascinating entities in human history... once they settled in the lands they had conquered, the nomads turned into what they had conquered. The Magyar tribes conquered Pannonia and turned into the Hungarian Kingdom. The Slavic tribes conquered Eastern Europe and turned into the Slavic kingdoms. The Seljuk Turks conquered Iran and became the new Iranian Empire. The Ottoman Turks conquered Anatolia and became the new Romans. The Chagatai Turks conquered India and became the Indians. The Mongols conquered Iran and became the new Iranian Empire. And when the Mongols conquered China they became the new Chinese Empire.
>
> **There is however one big exception** in this series of historic conquests by nomadic peoples and their consequent social restructuring: Russia."

### When to use:
- Your subject breaks an otherwise universal pattern
- You want to establish authority by showing you know the pattern before the exception
- Historical myth-busting where "everyone thinks X, but Y"

### Adaptation for History vs Hype topics:

**Territorial disputes:**
> "Colonial powers drew borders across Africa, Asia, and the Middle East. In almost every case, those borders became the permanent boundaries of new nations. Algeria kept its French-drawn borders. India kept its British-drawn partition. The Congo kept its Belgian-drawn lines.
>
> There is however one exception—a place where the colonial borders were explicitly temporary, and both sides knew it: [Subject]."

**Ideological myths:**
> "Historical myths usually fade when evidence contradicts them. The 'Flat Earth' idea collapsed with circumnavigation. The 'Geocentric universe' collapsed with telescopes. Scientific consensus typically wins.
>
> There is however one exception—a myth that has survived centuries of contradicting evidence: [Subject]."

## OPENING TYPE 2: Both Extremes Wrong

### Template:
```
"You'll hear two stories about [subject].
One side says [Extreme A].
The other side says [Extreme B].
When you examine the evidence, both oversimplify what actually happened."
```

### Full Example (Dark Ages script):
> "Here's the question: were the Dark Ages really dark?
>
> And here's what the evidence shows—both popular narratives get it wrong.
>
> One side dismisses this as Hollywood myth. The other insists the intellectual collapse after Rome's fall was real—libraries burned, literacy vanished, Europe went dark for 500 years.
>
> I went to the modern academic sources. Chris Wickham's *Medieval Europe*. Quantitative manuscript counts from Buringh and Van Zanden. Mayke de Jong's work on the Carolingians...
>
> When you examine that evidence, both extremes oversimplify what actually happened."

### When to use:
- Topic has two opposing camps (political, ideological, historical)
- You want to position yourself as above the debate
- The truth genuinely lies between the extremes

### Adaptation templates:

**Political fact-check:**
> "You'll hear two stories about [person/claim].
>
> Supporters say [defense]. Critics say [attack].
>
> When you check the actual documents, both sides are cherry-picking."

**Territorial dispute:**
> "There are two narratives about [disputed territory].
>
> [Country A] claims [their position]. [Country B] claims [their position].
>
> When you read the actual treaty, both interpretations miss what it actually says."

## OPENING TYPE 3: Anecdote → Deeper Significance (Kraut - Vodka)

### Template:
```
[Historical anecdotes establishing a pattern]
"[Example 1 from history]. [Example 2]. [Example 3]."
"But no [relationship/case] is as [significant descriptor] as [your subject]."
```

### Full Example (How Vodka Ruined Russia):
> "When you search through history books you will find many anecdotes on the role that alcohol and other addictive substances played in shaping societies throughout human history. The Pharaohs of ancient Egypt paid their farm laborers in bread and beer. Greek wine was one of the most priced commodities of the ancient world, so when the Carthaginians sold their low-quality wine abroad they falsely labeled it as Greek wine. The British notoriously forced access and control over the 19th century Chinese economy by illegally selling opium to its people.
>
> But no historic relationship between a society and an addictive substance is as fascinating, sinister, and barely talked about as the relationship between Russia and vodka."

### When to use:
- Your subject is an extreme example of a broader pattern
- You want to ground the topic in familiar context before going deeper
- The uniqueness of your subject IS the hook

## OPENING TYPE 4: Modern Hook → Historical Explanation (Channel Standard)

### Template:
```
[Specific modern event/date/person]
"On [date], [specific thing happened]."
"[Modern consequence/reaction]."
"But here's what almost nobody explained: [the historical context that makes it make sense]."
```

### Full Example (Bir Tawil script):
> "June 16, 2014. A Virginia farmer named Jeremiah Heaton planted a flag in the African desert to make his seven-year-old daughter Emily a princess.
>
> He claimed eight hundred square miles of land between Egypt and Sudan. Called it the Kingdom of North Sudan.
>
> CNN, TIME, Newsweek—hundreds of outlets covered it. Most treated it like a cute human interest story.
>
> But here's what almost nobody explained: He wasn't crazy. This is the only place on Earth—besides Antarctica—where you could even try."

### When to use:
- Topic has recent news hook
- Modern event seems bizarre without historical context
- You want to start with intrigue, then explain

## OPENING TYPE 5: "This Is More Than Just" (Stakes Escalation)

### Template:
```
[Introduce topic at surface level]
"And as much as this may sound like just [simple framing],
there is far more to it than that—[deeper implication]."
```

### Full Example (from Vodka transcript):
> "And as much as this may sound like just a mere little interesting historic anecdote on a long-gone mercantile economic system, there is far more to it than that—something sinister, the reverberations of which still echo throughout Russian society to this day."

### When to use:
- After initial hook, before diving into main content
- To signal to viewer that stakes are higher than they might think
- Transition from "interesting fact" to "this matters"

## OPENING TYPE 6: Survey/Data Hook

### Template:
```
"In [date], [credible source] asked [audience] about [topic]. Here's what they found:
[Statistic 1]. [Statistic 2]. [Surprising statistic]."
"So here's the question: [your central question]?"
```

### Full Example (Dark Ages script):
> "In March 2025, a new YouGov survey asked Americans about the Middle Ages. Here's what they found: 48% described it as 'dark.' 54% said 'violent.' And only 2% claimed they actually know much about the period.
>
> So here's the question: were the Dark Ages really dark?"

### When to use:
- You have recent data that reveals a knowledge gap
- Topic is about public misconceptions
- You want to ground the video in "what people actually think"

## QUALITY CHECKLIST FOR OPENINGS

Every opening MUST have within first 60 seconds:

- [ ] **Stakes clear:** Why should viewer care?
- [ ] **Both extremes framed:** Who says what? (if applicable)
- [ ] **Your position signaled:** What will the evidence show?
- [ ] **Evidence promise:** "I went to the documents" / "When you check the sources"
- [ ] **Modern relevance:** Why does this matter NOW?

## ANTI-PATTERNS (What NOT to Do)

### ❌ Starting with definitions
> "A border dispute is defined as..."

**Fix:** Start with specific case, define terms when needed.

### ❌ Starting with vague questions
> "Have you ever wondered about borders?"

**Fix:** Start with specific claim, event, or pattern.

### ❌ Starting with "today we're going to talk about"
> "Today we're going to explore the history of..."

**Fix:** Start with hook, let topic emerge from content.

### ❌ Taking sides in first 30 seconds
> "The truth is that [Country A] is completely right about this."

**Fix:** Frame both sides, show evidence, THEN draw conclusion.
