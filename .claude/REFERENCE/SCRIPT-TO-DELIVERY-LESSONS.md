# Script-to-Delivery Lessons

**Source:** Iran Part 1 (January 2026) - comparing SCRIPT-PART1.md to final SRT

These patterns emerged from analyzing what was cut or changed during filming/editing. Apply these rules BEFORE filming to write scripts closer to the final delivery.

---

## 1. Cut Academic Attributions in Flow

**Script wrote:**
> "According to Ervand Abrahamian, author of A History of Modern Iran..."

**Delivered:**
> "In 1905, a sugar merchant was publicly beaten..."

**Rule:** Lead with the story, not the citation. Save attributions for when showing the source on screen or for contested claims.

**When to keep attribution:** When the source's credibility IS the point (e.g., "Even the CIA's own declassified documents admit...")

---

## 2. Cut Rhetorical Emphasis Phrases

**Script wrote:**
> "Do you see what this means? Britain and Russia had just..."

**Delivered:**
> "Britain and Russia had just..."

**Rule:** Trust the audience. If the evidence is strong, it speaks for itself. Don't signal "this is important" - just deliver it.

**Phrases to cut:**
- "Do you see what this means?"
- "Think about that for a moment."
- "Let that sink in."
- "This is crucial to understand."

---

## 3. Cut Setup Questions Before Punchlines

**Script wrote:**
> "So what did Britain and Russia do? They signed..."

**Delivered:**
> "Britain and Russia signed..."

**Rule:** The question-answer format feels like stalling. Go directly to the information.

**Exception:** When the question creates genuine suspense about an unexpected answer.

---

## 4. Convert Numbered Lists to Flowing Prose

**Script wrote:**
> "First, they would bribe members of parliament. Second, they would fund street mobs. Third, they would..."

**Delivered:**
> "They bribed members of parliament, funded street mobs, and..."

**Rule:** Numbered lists work for written text but sound robotic when spoken. Convert to natural sentence flow.

---

## 5. Change Dramatic Pauses to Continuous Flow

**Script wrote:**
> "The shah fled. [PAUSE] The coup had failed. [PAUSE] Or so it seemed."

**Delivered:**
> "The shah fled. The coup had failed. Or so it seemed."

**Rule:** Written pause markers rarely translate to actual pauses. Natural delivery finds its own rhythm. Don't over-choreograph.

---

## 6. Cut Personal Reflection Framing

**Script wrote:**
> "When I first read about this, I was struck by how..."

**Delivered:**
> [Cut entirely or reframed as evidence statement]

**Rule:** Personal reactions dilute authority. Present the evidence and let viewers form their own reactions.

**Exception:** When personal discovery IS the story ("I went to the archives and found...")

---

## 7. Word Choice for Authority

| Script | Delivered | Why |
|--------|-----------|-----|
| "essentially" | [cut] | Hedging weakens |
| "basically" | [cut] | Sounds uncertain |
| "kind of" | [cut] | Undermines precision |
| "I think" | [cut or reframe] | Present facts, not opinions |
| "it seems" | "it was" | Commit to the evidence |

**Rule:** Hedging language in writing becomes weakness in delivery. If the evidence supports it, state it directly.

---

## 8. Structural Tightening

**Script pattern:** Setup → Context → Context → Point
**Delivery pattern:** Point → Evidence

**Example:**
- Script: "To understand why this matters, we need to go back to 1901 when William Knox D'Arcy first negotiated..."
- Delivery: "In 1901, Britain secured exclusive oil rights for 60 years."

**Rule:** Backstory setup can often be cut. Start with the key fact, then explain if needed.

---

## Pre-Filming Checklist

Before filming any script, do one pass applying these rules:

- [ ] Academic attributions: Lead with story, not citations
- [ ] Rhetorical emphasis: Cut "do you see what this means" phrases
- [ ] Setup questions: Go direct to information
- [ ] Numbered lists: Convert to flowing prose
- [ ] Pause markers: Remove, let delivery find rhythm
- [ ] Personal framing: Cut "when I first read this" statements
- [ ] Hedging words: Replace "essentially/basically/kind of" with direct statements
- [ ] Structure: Can any setup context be cut?

---

## The Core Principle

**Written scripts compensate for lack of voice.**
**Delivered scripts have voice as the tool.**

In writing, you need emphasis phrases, numbered lists, and setup questions because the reader can't hear your tone. In delivery, your voice carries emphasis, your pacing creates lists, and your inflection signals importance.

Write for the page, then edit for the voice.

## 9. Section Headers Are Scaffolding (NEW - Belavezha)

**Script wrote:**
```
## THE PROSECUTION (1:30-3:00)

[B-ROLL: Scale of justice tilting]

The case against the Accords is straightforward.
```

**Delivered:**
> "The case against the Accords is straightforward."

**Rule:** Section headers (`## HOOK`, `## THE PROSECUTION`, etc.) help organize writing but aren't part of the delivery. Remove them during teleprompter export or before filming read-through.

---

## 10. Add Spoken Bridges Between Sections (NEW - Belavezha)

**Script wrote:**
> [End of document section] "But was it legal?"

**Delivered:**
> [End of document section] "Was it legal? Let's look at the evidence."

**Rule:** Add explicit transition phrases between logical sections. "Let's look at the evidence" signals a shift more smoothly than jumping straight to new content.

**Useful bridges:**
- "Let's look at the evidence."
- "Here's where it gets interesting." (use sparingly)
- "The defense has answers."
- "Now watch how this actually worked."

---

## 11. Use Explicit Enumeration for Arguments (NEW - Belavezha)

**Script wrote:**
> "On top of that, the USSR had fifteen republics."

**Delivered:**
> "Third, the USSR had 15 republics."

**Rule:** When presenting multiple arguments or points, use clear enumeration (First, Second, Third) rather than connective phrases (On top of that, Also, Additionally). Enumeration helps viewers track the argument structure.

---

## 12. Simplify Metaphors for Spoken Clarity (NEW - Belavezha)

**Script wrote:**
> "Since these republics tied 'the political nuptial cord' in 1922..."

**Delivered:**
> "Since these republics tied the political knot in 1922..."

**Rule:** Written metaphors can be more elaborate. Spoken metaphors need instant comprehension. "Knot" is clearer than "nuptial cord" when heard once.

---

## 13. Cut Redundant Concluding Phrases (NEW - Belavezha)

**Script wrote:**
> "Three men. One document. No more superpower."

**Delivered:**
> "Three men, one document."

**Rule:** After a punchy conclusion, don't add more. "No more superpower" restates what's already clear. Let the impact land.

---

## 14. Always Verify Statistics Before Filming (NEW - Belavezha)

**Script wrote:**
> "77.85% of citizens—113 million people—voted to preserve the USSR."

**Delivered:**
> "76.4% of citizens, about 113 million people, voted to preserve the USSR."

**Rule:** Statistics can drift during research. Always verify numbers one more time before filming, especially percentages and dates.

---

## 15. Check for More Recent Sources (NEW - Belavezha)

**Script wrote:**
> "In 2022, former Prime Minister Sergei Stepashin..."

**Delivered:**
> "In 2025, presidential adviser Anton Kobiakov..."

**Rule:** Between script finalization and filming, newer sources may emerge. Do a quick check for more recent quotes/statements that strengthen the modern hook.

## 16. Integrate Quotes as Your Own Voice (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
```
Abrahamian describes what happened:

> "Declaring martial law, the shah appointed Colonel Liakhoff, the Russian Cossack commander, to be military governor of Tehran. Liakhoff... sent his Cossacks to occupy the telegraph office and to bombard the Majles building."
```

**Delivered:**
> "Declaring martial law, the Shah appointed Colonel Lyakhov, the Russian Cossack commander, to be military governor of Tehran. Lyakhov sent his Cossacks to occupy the telegraph office and to bombard the Majlis building."

**Rule:** ABSORB quotes into your narration. Don't frame them as "Scholar X said..." Just state the information. Keep source attribution for on-screen text or description, not spoken script.

**When to keep quote framing:**
- When the speaker's identity IS the point ("Even the CIA's own documents admit...")
- When showing self-incriminating evidence ("Roosevelt wrote in his cables...")
- When contrasting viewpoints ("Mossadegh said X, but Britain said Y")

## 17. Em-Dashes Become Commas (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
> "In August 1991—four months BEFORE the Accords—Yeltsin's press secretary..."

**Delivered:**
> "In August 1991, four months before the accords, Yeltsin's press secretary..."

**Rule:** Em-dashes (—) work in writing for visual separation. In speech, use commas. The pause is the same, but commas read more naturally on teleprompter.

---

## 18. Drop CAPS Emphasis (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
> "four months BEFORE the Accords"

**Delivered:**
> "four months before the accords"

**Rule:** Written caps signal emphasis. But in delivery, YOUR VOICE adds emphasis. Don't need visual cues. Write normally, emphasize vocally.

---

## 19. Strong Opening Lines Survive Verbatim (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
> "This document created one of the first constitutional democracies in Asia."

**Delivered:**
> "This document created one of the first constitutional democracies in Asia."

**Script wrote:**
> "And then foreign powers destroyed it. Twice."

**Delivered:**
> "And then foreign powers destroyed it. Twice."

**Rule:** Punchy opening sentences survive intact. Spend extra time crafting hooks - they're worth it.

---

## 20. Foreign Terms with Immediate Translation Work (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
> "The merchants took *bast*—sanctuary."

**Delivered:**
> "The merchants took bast—sanctuary."

**Rule:** Foreign terms followed immediately by English translation are effective. Don't over-explain. Pattern: "[foreign term]—[one-word translation]"

---

## 21. "Do You See" Questions: Keep When Insightful (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
> "Do you see what happened here?"

**Delivered:**
> "Do you see what happened here?"

**Rule:** Unlike other rhetorical questions (cut "Think about that"), "Do you see what happened here?" works because it sets up a KEY INSIGHT that reframes previous information. Keep when introducing non-obvious pattern recognition.

---

## 22. Staccato Lists Flow Together (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
> "So I did some digging. Read books about the topic. Then went to the primary sources."

**Delivered:**
> "So I did some digging, read some books about the topic and then went to the primary sources."

**Rule:** Staccato sentence fragments (period after each phrase) become flowing sentences with commas when spoken. Write as you'll say it.

---

## 23. "Here's What/Why" Gets Dropped (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
> "Here's why this matters today."

**Delivered:**
> "And this is why it matters today."

**Rule:** "Here's what..." framing is scaffolding. Change to "This is..." or "And..." for more direct statement.

---

## 24. Implicit Quote Attribution Works Better (NEW - Deep Analysis)

**Script wrote:**
```
Here's what Abrahamian found in *A History of Modern Iran*:

> "The revolution's long-term causes were rooted in the nineteenth century; its short-term ones were triggered in 1904–05..."
```

**Delivered:**
> "The revolution's long-term causes were rooted in the 19th century. Its short-term ones were triggered in 1904 and 1905. By an economic crisis brought about by government bankruptcy and spiraling inflation."

**Rule:** Drop "Here's what [Scholar] found:" entirely. Just state the content. Source goes in description/on-screen, not spoken.

## Updated Writing Style for Scripts

Based on comprehensive analysis, write scripts as:

### DO:
1. Strong opening lines (they survive)
2. Foreign term—translation format
3. "Do you see" before key insights
4. Flowing sentences with commas
5. Numbered arguments (First, Second, Third)
6. Direct statements without "Here's what"
7. Quotes integrated as your narration

### DON'T:
1. Em-dashes for parentheticals (use commas)
2. CAPS for emphasis (vocal does this)
3. "Here's what [Scholar] found:" framing
4. Staccato fragment lists
5. "On top of that" transitions
6. Dramatic concluding flourishes ("No more superpower")
7. Long quote setups
