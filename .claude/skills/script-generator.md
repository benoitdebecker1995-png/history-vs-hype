# Script Generator Skill - History vs Hype

Generate video scripts following the proven formula from top-performing videos (Essequibo, Vance Part 2).

## CHANNEL MISSION

**Core Purpose:** Bring history to the wider public by showing people the actual sources - especially when those sources are being misused or misrepresented.

**What this means for scripts:**
- Always show the primary source document on screen (not just quote it)
- Make archives and official sources accessible to general audience
- Highlight the gap between what sources actually say vs. how they're being used
- Educate viewers on how to check claims themselves (provide URLs, archive locations)
- Academic rigor, but accessible language

**The goal:** Viewers should be able to verify everything themselves and understand how to read primary sources.

## Activation

Use when user says:
- "Generate a script about [topic]"
- "Write a script for [topic]"
- "Create a History vs Hype script on [topic]"
- "Draft [topic] video"

## STEP 1: Gather Information

Ask the user these specific questions:

1. **What's the topic/myth to debunk?**
2. **What's the modern hook?** (Current event, recent news, political figure's claim)
3. **Do you have research ready?** (NotebookLM notes, sources, documents)
4. **Target length?** (Default: 10-12 minutes)
5. **Any specific "smoking gun" evidence?** (Key documents, statistics, quotes)

## STEP 2: Analyze the Proven Formula

Based on Essequibo and Vance videos, successful scripts have:

### ✅ IMMEDIATE DRAMA (First 8 seconds)
**Essequibo pattern:**
"On March 1st, 2025, a Venezuelan warship—armed with a 76-millimeter cannon—penetrated over 100 nautical miles into another country's waters. Its target: an American oil rig producing 220,000 barrels a day."

**Vance pattern:**
"So in Part 1, I fact-checked Vance on Indigenous peoples. Turns out Spanish sources inflated those sacrifice numbers by 20 times. And that brothel archaeology he cited? Roman Britain. Not the Americas. Three claims, zero held up."

**Formula:**
- Concrete action or number in first sentence
- Visual elements clear immediately
- Stakes obvious within 8 seconds
- Specificity: exact weapons, exact numbers, exact locations

### ✅ PATTERN REVELATION (Not Single Events)
**Essequibo:** Feb shooting → March warship → May illegal election → Aug deployment
**Vance:** 1832 condemnation → 1864 Syllabus → 1452-1800s slavery defense

**Formula:**
- Minimum 3 interconnected incidents/claims
- Chronological progression showing escalation
- Systematic pattern, not isolated incident
- Each adds to cumulative case

### ✅ VIRAL QUOTE INTEGRATION
**Essequibo:** Rubio's "very bad day" warning (100+ articles, most-shared quote)
**Vance:** Papal "deliramentum" (insanity) + Maduro calling Rubio "imbécil"

**Formula:**
- Find the quote that got shared widely
- Often from primary sources (papal bulls, treaties)
- Sometimes from current figures (politicians, officials)
- Build up to it, pause, deliver with emphasis

### ✅ NAMED SPECIFICS WITH PRECISION
**Essequibo:** 6 named soldiers with exact wounds, ABF Guaiquerí PO-11, 114 nautical miles, 700 meters
**Vance:** Augustine, Aquinas, Pope Gregory XVI, Mirari Vos Section 14, Syllabus Error #15/#77/#78

**Formula:**
- Name every person involved
- Exact measurements, not approximations
- Specific document sections (not just "the treaty says...")
- Times, dates, locations precise

### ✅ MODERN STAKES IN FIRST 2 MINUTES
**Essequibo:** US corporate interests, potential war, oil production at risk NOW
**Vance:** Florida curriculum, PragerU in classrooms, what kids learn TODAY

**Formula:**
- "Why does this matter in 2025?" answered by 2:00 mark
- Current consequences shown before historical causes
- Payoff-first structure
- Connect to viewer's present

### ✅ PRIMARY SOURCES ON SCREEN
**Essequibo:** ICJ orders, diplomatic statements, press conferences
**Vance:** Papal encyclicals with Latin text, Founders' writings, treaty documents

**Formula:**
- Mark where documents appear: **[DOCUMENT: Mirari Vos Section 14]**
- Zoom to exact phrases being quoted
- Show Latin + English, or original + translation
- Never just say it—SHOW it

## STEP 4: Apply Language Rules

### ✅ ALWAYS USE:
- **Precise numbers:** "114 nautical miles" not "over 100 miles"
- **Named individuals:** "Second Lieutenant Ansel Murray" not "a soldier"
- **Exact quotes:** With pause before and after for emphasis
- **Document specifics:** "Section 14" not "the document says"
- **Academic framing:** "Evidence suggests..." "Documents reveal..."
- **Investigation language:** "So I went to..." "I checked..." "I read..."

### ❌ NEVER USE:
- Vague language: "many," "some," "a lot"
- Approximations when exact numbers available
- "Historians say" without naming which historians
- Political framing ("occupation," "destruction")
- Absolutist claims ("always," "never," "all")
- Conspiracy language without documentation

### 🎯 SIGNATURE PHRASES (From Top Performers):
- "So I went to [archive/source]..."
- "I'm reading the exact quote here: [PAUSE]"
- "That's [date] - [context for why date matters]"
- "[PAUSE - let that sink in]"
- "[PAUSE] So who's telling the truth?"
- "Here's what [authority] actually said/wrote:"
- "Not [myth claim]. [PAUSE] [Reality]."

## VOICE & STYLE GUIDE (Based on 30+ Transcript Analysis)

### ✅ HOW BENOIT ACTUALLY TALKS

**Short, punchy sentences:**
- "They won." not "And then they won."
- "That's retrofitting." not "That's what we call retrofitting."
- "1452. Pope Nicholas V issues a papal bull." not "In 1452, Pope Nicholas V issues a papal bull."

**Direct transitions without buildup:**
- "So when does 'Christian human rights' language actually show up?" not "Now you might be wondering, when does..."
- "What changed?" not "So the question we have to ask is, what changed?"
- "Maybe that's just popes. What about theologians?" not "Now maybe you're thinking that's just popes, right? What about theologians?"

**Natural flow patterns:**
- Uses "So" to start investigation moments: "So I checked." "So when does..."
- Uses fragments for emphasis: "Common claim. Sounds reasonable."
- Delivers verdicts as standalone sentences: "Catholic Church was condemning these concepts in 1864."

**Evidence delivery:**
- States number/fact first, then brief context: "1844, Philadelphia. Catholic-Protestant riots..."
- Minimal setup before quotes - just attribution then quote
- Uses "Reading directly from..." or "Exact quote:" as only buildup

**Things Benoit NEVER says:**
- ❌ "Here's the thing" (only use once max per script)
- ❌ "Now - " excessively (maybe 2-3 times total, not every transition)
- ❌ "Look - " or "Listen - "
- ❌ Over-signposting ("I'm going to tell you about..." just tell them)
- ❌ Repetitive transition phrases

**Units & Measurements:**
- ✅ Use units exactly as cited in primary sources (if source says "114 nautical miles", use that)
- ✅ Otherwise use metric (Benoit is Belgian): kilometers, meters, square kilometers
- ❌ Don't convert to imperial (miles, feet, acres) unless that's what the source uses

**Pacing markers - use sparingly:**
- Don't write [PAUSE] everywhere - Benoit pauses naturally
- Only mark pauses for: dramatic quotes, letting statistics sink in, verdict moments
- Most transitions don't need pause markers

**How Benoit builds arguments:**
- Question → Evidence → Verdict (clean, direct)
- Multiple examples stacked without transition: "1844, Philadelphia. [details]. 1834, Charlestown. [details]."
- Delivers interpretation after evidence, not woven throughout

### ✅ SENTENCE STRUCTURE PATTERNS

**Opening segments:**
```
"[Myth claim in their words].

[Counter-evidence]. [Specific example].

[What I did to check]: I went to [source]."
```

**Evidence presentation:**
```
"[Date/Place]. [Event in one sentence].

[Specific detail with number].

[Second specific detail]."
```

**Verdict delivery:**
```
"Did [claim]?

[Evidence summary in one sentence].

That's not [their claim]. That's [reality]."
```

### ✅ WHAT TO CUT IN EDITING

When scripts sound too "AI", remove:
- Extra "Here's" and "Now" connectors
- Formal transition sentences ("Let me explain...", "To understand this...")
- Rhetorical setups ("You might be asking...", "The question becomes...")
- Repetitive emphasis ("very", "extremely", "significantly")
- Over-explanation of what you're about to do

Replace with:
- Just state it
- Move to next point
- Trust the evidence

### When to Mark Visuals:

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. ❌ **Generic opening** - "Today we're talking about X" → ✅ Immediate drama
2. ❌ **History-first** - Starting with 1800s context → ✅ Modern stakes first
3. ❌ **Vague attribution** - "Sources say" → ✅ Named historians, exact documents
4. ❌ **Single incident** - One treaty violation → ✅ Pattern of 3+ incidents
5. ❌ **No visual planning** - Just script text → ✅ Mark every visual cue
6. ❌ **Buried quote** - Important quote in middle of paragraph → ✅ Build up, pause, deliver
7. ❌ **Weak modern hook** - "This is interesting history" → ✅ "Florida curriculum teaches this NOW"
