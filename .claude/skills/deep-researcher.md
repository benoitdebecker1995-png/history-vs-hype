# Deep Researcher Skill - History vs Hype

**PHASE 1 of Two-Phase Research Workflow**

Conduct comprehensive preliminary research using only freely available internet sources. Map the landscape, identify claims to verify, and determine what academic sources are needed BEFORE user downloads expensive books.

## Two-Phase Research Philosophy (CRITICAL - Updated 2025-01-25)

**This skill executes Phase 1 of the channel's competitive advantage:**

### Phase 1 (This Skill):
- Use free internet sources (Wikipedia, news, Google Scholar previews)
- Identify claims that need academic verification
- Build preliminary argument structure
- Determine what academic books to download

### Phase 2 (Separate Workflow):
- Download specific academic books (university library + purchases)
- Upload to NotebookLM
- Verify claims with proper citations
- Extract smoking gun quotes for B-roll

**Why two phases?** Other history YouTubers stop at Phase 1. Your channel goes deeper with academic sources and primary documents, providing citations with page numbers and scholarly authority.

## When to Use

- User runs `/deep-research` command
- Part of `/create-video` workflow at Stage 2
- **Remember:** This is Phase 1 only. Always follow with NotebookLM Research Plan (Phase 2).

## Your Task

Perform deep-dive preliminary research to:
1. Map the topic landscape using free internet sources
2. Identify key claims (mark with ❓ for academic verification)
3. Find preliminary statistics and dates
4. Determine what academic books are needed for Phase 2
5. Validate topic is worth investing in paid sources

## What Counts as "Freely Available"

### ✅ USE THESE SOURCES:
- **Wikipedia** - Overview + mine bibliographies for source leads
- **News articles** (free access only)
- **Google Scholar** - Preview pages, free PDFs, abstracts
- **Government websites** - UN, national archives, State Department, ICJ, etc.
- **Academic repositories** - Archive.org, ResearchGate free papers, JSTOR free articles
- **Primary source databases** - Treaty databases, declassified documents, official records
- **YouTube** - Academic lectures, documentary footage for context
- **Library of Congress** - Digitized maps, documents, photos

### ❌ DON'T COUNT ON:
- Paywalled journal articles (note them for purchase recommendations later)
- Books only available for purchase
- Newspaper archives requiring subscription

## Step 2: Research Strategy

### Phase 1: Overview & Context (30 min equivalent research)

**Wikipedia Research:**
- Read main article on topic
- Check "Talk" page for disputed claims (shows controversy areas)
- Mine bibliography for authoritative sources
- Note which claims have [citation needed]
- Check related articles for context

**Preliminary web search:**
- "[Topic] primary sources"
- "[Topic] treaty text"
- "[Topic] academic research"
- "[Topic] fact check"
- "[Topic] controversy"

**Output:** 2-3 paragraph overview of the topic + key disagreements

### Phase 2: Primary Source Hunting (45 min equivalent)

**Search for freely accessible primary documents:**
- Treaties (UN treaty database, Avalon Project, etc.)
- Government archives (national archives, declassified docs)
- Court cases (ICJ, national supreme courts)
- Official statistics (census data, government reports)
- Diplomatic correspondence (if declassified)
- Historical newspapers (if digitized and free)

**For each primary source found:**
- Note exact URL
- Extract key quotes relevant to the myth
- Verify authenticity (government domain, reputable archive)
- Note what it proves/disproves

**Output:** List of 5-10 primary sources with key quotes extracted

### Phase 3: Academic Consensus (30 min equivalent)

**Google Scholar search:**
- "[Topic] historiography"
- "[Topic] academic debate"
- "Debunking [myth]"
- "[Key figure] scholarship"

**Extract from free previews/abstracts:**
- What do most historians agree on?
- What's contested?
- Who are the authoritative scholars (note for book purchases)?
- Any recent research contradicting old narratives?

**Output:** Summary of academic consensus + key scholars to follow

### Phase 4: Counterargument Research (30 min equivalent)

**CRITICAL: This is not optional. Channel credibility depends on engaging strongest opposing views.**

**Deliberately search for opposing view:**
- What's the strongest defense of the myth?
- What evidence do myth-believers cite?
- Where are the gaps in counter-narrative?
- What would a fair presentation acknowledge?
- Who are the most credible defenders of the conventional view?

**For religious/political topics:**
- If claiming "Christianity" did X, search for BOTH Catholic AND Protestant sources
- If claiming "the West" did X, check for variations by country
- If claiming X was "always" opposed, search for reform movements within that tradition

**Steel-man the opposition:**
- Don't just find weak versions of counter-arguments
- Find the most sophisticated, well-sourced defenses
- Note where they have legitimate points vs. where they fail

**Output:** 5-7 strongest counter-arguments with sources, including steel-manned versions

### Phase 5: Ambiguity & Position Changes (25 min equivalent)

**CRITICAL: Find where institutions changed positions over time or where groups were on both sides.**

**Position changes to research:**
- Did churches/institutions change their official position? When?
- Vatican II (1965) reversals on religious freedom, ecumenism, etc.
- Protestant denomination splits and reunifications
- Political party position changes
- Government policy reversals

**"Both sides" scenarios to identify:**
- Were Christians on both sides of slavery debate? (Yes - must acknowledge)
- Were Catholics on both sides of human rights? (Yes - some supported, hierarchy opposed)
- Were Americans on both sides of colonialism? (Check)
- Were [group] unified or divided on [issue]?

**Timeline precision checks:**
- Calculate actual years between dates (don't accept vague "centuries")
- If claim is "1,800 years of X," do the math: starting year to ending year
- Flag if rounded numbers obscure reality

**Search queries:**
- "[Institution] changed position on [issue]"
- "[Group] split over [issue]"
- "[Group] abolitionists" (for slavery topics)
- "[Group] reformers" (for reform movements)
- "[Issue] before and after [major event like Vatican II]"
- "When did [institution] reverse position on [issue]"

**Output:**
- List of position changes with dates
- List of "both sides" scenarios that must be acknowledged
- Timeline calculations with sources
- Reform movements within traditions

### Phase 6: Modern Connection Research (15 min equivalent)

**Search for current impact:**
- News articles connecting historical event to today
- Political speeches referencing this history
- Current policies based on this narrative
- Educational standards teaching this topic
- Social media debates about this

**Output:** 3-5 concrete examples of modern relevance

## Red Flags to Call Out

If you find these during research, explicitly warn:

### Evidence Issues
- ❌ **No accessible primary sources** - Might not be viable
- ❌ **Only one source for key claim** - Need more corroboration
- ❌ **All evidence is interpretation** - No objective documents
- ❌ **Only paywalled sources** - Expensive to verify
- ⚠️ **Controversial with little evidence** - Might not be defensible

### Credibility Issues
- ❌ **No counter-arguments found** - Either biased search or topic is one-sided
- ❌ **Position changes ignored** - Script will be attacked for omitting reversals
- ❌ **"Both sides" scenario not acknowledged** - Oversimplifies divided movements
- ❌ **Catholic sources used for "Christianity" claims** - Need Protestant sources too
- ❌ **Timeline math doesn't add up** - Vague "centuries" or inflated timeframes
- ⚠️ **Reform movements not mentioned** - Ignores internal dissenters

### Viability Issues
- ❌ **Topic overdone** - Every major channel covered it
- ❌ **Story still developing** - Facts not settled yet
