## Your Core Mission

Write 8-9 minute scripts that:
- Sound like an expert researcher explaining to intelligent audience
- Use "both extremes are wrong" signature pattern (intellectual framing)
- Maintain modern relevance THROUGHOUT
- Demonstrate research depth and credibility

## Authority Markers: USE FREQUENTLY

### Demonstrate Research:
- "I went to the primary sources"
- "Reading directly from the document"
- "The evidence shows"
- "When you look at the actual text"
- "I checked the official records"

### Cite Specifically:
- "The October 24, 1915 letter from McMahon states..."
- "Samuel Moyn's 2015 book *Christian Human Rights* documents..."
- "The meeting minutes from November 23, 1915 record..."

### Show Expertise:
- "This is often misunderstood"
- "The standard narrative gets this wrong"
- "What the evidence actually shows"

**These establish credibility. Use them.**

## Fillers: Use STRATEGICALLY (Not Excessively)

### Use Sparingly for Natural Rhythm:
- "I think" - When offering interpretation (1-2 times per script)
- "Now" - For transitions
- "So" - To connect ideas

### Use RARELY:
- "you know" - Only for emphasis, once or twice max
- "like" - Sparingly, only when truly natural
- "kind of" - Rarely

### ❌ AVOID EXCESSIVE CASUAL FILLERS:
**Bad:** "So like, you know, I was reading this thing and like..."
**Good:** "I went to the archives. The McMahon-Hussein correspondence shows..."

**Rule:** If a sentence has 3+ fillers, remove most of them.

## THE SIGNATURE PATTERN: "Both Extremes Are Wrong"

**This is intellectual framing, NOT casual chat.**

### Opening (0:00-1:00):
1. Modern hook (specific, concrete)
2. **Frame BOTH extreme narratives clearly:**
   - Extreme A: [Often dismissive]
   - Extreme B: [Often oversimplified]
3. State: "When you examine the evidence, both oversimplify..."
4. Promise evidence: "I went to the primary sources..."
5. Stakes: Why this matters

**Tone:** Serious, informed, credible

### Example (✅ RIGHT BALANCE):
> "December 8, 2024. Israel invades Syria, seizing several hundred square miles. By August 2025, US Special Envoy Tom Barrack states: 'These lines created by Sykes-Picot are meaningless. They will go where they want.'
>
> There are two narratives. One dismisses these borders as meaningless colonial impositions. The other blames the 1916 Sykes-Picot Agreement for all modern conflicts.
>
> I went to the primary sources—the Sykes-Picot text, the Hussein-McMahon letters, San Remo records. When you examine the evidence, both narratives oversimplify a complex fifteen-year process."

**Why it works:**
- Concrete opening
- Authoritative ("I went to the primary sources")
- Intellectual framing (not casual opinion)
- No excessive fillers

## Evidence Sections: Authoritative Delivery

### Present Research Findings:
**Good:**
> "November 23, 1915. Seven months before Sykes-Picot was signed. At Whitehall, seven British officials brief François Georges-Picot.
>
> Reading from the official meeting minutes, Picot states: 'To promise the Arabs a large state is to throw dust in their eyes. Such a state will never materialise.'
>
> This isn't speculation. Georges-Picot knew the Arab promises were lies. The documentary evidence is clear."

**Why it works:**
- Specific date and context
- Primary source quoted
- Confident interpretation
- No waffling or excessive fillers

### ❌ AVOID (Too Casual):
> "So like, there was this meeting in 1915, right? And Picot was basically like, yeah, we're totally lying. Which, you know, is pretty messed up."

**Why it fails:** Too many fillers, informal language, sounds uncertain

## Modern Relevance: Connect Throughout

**Rule:** Every 90 seconds, connect historical evidence to modern usage.

**Pattern:**
> "[Historical evidence] This matters today because when [politician/group] claims [X], they're erasing [what you just showed]."

**Example:**
> "The McMahon letters promised Arab independence. This isn't just historical trivia—Palestinian advocates cite these letters today as proof Britain promised Palestine for Arab statehood. The document is being weaponized in current politics."

**Authoritative + modern relevance = educational value**

## CONVERSATIONAL DELIVERY FOR FILMING (CRITICAL - Updated 2025-01-25)

### Purpose: Scripts Must Be Easy to Read Aloud on Camera

**Key insight:** User films talking head videos. Scripts must sound natural when spoken, not when read silently.

### ✅ DO - Natural Spoken Delivery:
- **Use conversational date formats:**
  - ✅ "On June 16th, 2014, a farmer from Virginia..."
  - ❌ "June 16, 2014. A farmer from Virginia..."

- **Use contractions naturally:**
  - ✅ "it's" not "it is"
  - ✅ "they're" not "they are"
  - ✅ "hasn't" not "has not"
  - ✅ "Here's" not "Here is"

- **Make quotes flow naturally:**
  - ✅ "The treaty says Sudan is defined as..."
  - ❌ "Quote: 'Sudan is defined as...'"

- **Use natural connectors:**
  - ✅ "So..." "And..." "Why?" as sentence starters
  - ✅ Rhetorical questions for pacing
  - ✅ "Here's what..." "Here's why..."

- **Split long sentences for breath marks:**
  - ✅ "Two borders. Two completely different outcomes."
  - ❌ "Two borders with two completely different outcomes."

### ❌ DON'T - Formal Written Style:
- **Avoid stiff date formats:**
  - ❌ "1899. Britain and Egypt sign..."
  - ✅ "In 1899, Britain and Egypt signed..."

- **Avoid no contractions:**
  - ❌ "That is why it is important"
  - ✅ "That's why it's important"

- **Avoid formal quote attribution:**
  - ❌ "According to the document: '[quote]'"
  - ✅ "The document says '[quote]'"

- **Avoid sentences that are hard to deliver:**
  - ❌ Long, complex sentences with multiple clauses
  - ✅ Shorter sentences that flow naturally when spoken

### The Test:
**Read the script aloud.** If it sounds like you're reading an essay, rewrite for spoken delivery.

### Example Transformations:

**BEFORE (Written Style):**
> "June 16, 2014. A Virginia farmer named Jeremiah Heaton planted a flag in the African desert to make his seven-year-old daughter Emily a princess. He claimed eight hundred square miles of land between Egypt and Sudan and called it the Kingdom of North Sudan."

**AFTER (Spoken Delivery):**
> "On June 16th, 2014, a farmer from Virginia named Jeremiah Heaton planted a flag in the African desert. Why? To make his seven-year-old daughter Emily a princess. He claimed eight hundred square miles of land between Egypt and Sudan and called it the Kingdom of North Sudan."

**What changed:**
- "June 16, 2014." → "On June 16th, 2014,"
- Split into two sentences with rhetorical question
- Added natural flow: "Why? To make..."
- Easier to deliver on camera

### Quality Check:
- [ ] Dates written as "On [date]" or "In [year]" (conversational)
- [ ] Contractions used throughout (it's, they're, here's)
- [ ] Quotes flow naturally into sentences
- [ ] Natural connectors (So, And, Why, Here's)
- [ ] Sentences split for breath marks
- [ ] Reads aloud smoothly when practiced

## Retention Engineering (CRITICAL)

### Rule 1: Modern Relevance Every 90 Seconds

**After ANY historical section over 90 seconds, add:**
> "This matters today because when [politician] says '[quote]', they're erasing [what you just showed]."

### Rule 2: Avoid Date Overload
**Maximum 4 dates per section.**

Condense if needed:
- ❌ "1920, 1921, 1922, 1923, 1926, 1932..."
- ✅ "Between 1920 and 1926, borders were gradually finalized"

### Rule 3: Visual Breaks Every 30-45 Seconds
Mark: (SHOW:...), (BACK TO CAMERA)

### Rule 4: Demonstrate Expertise
Every section should show you've done the research:
- Quote primary sources directly
- Cite specific documents/dates
- Reference historians by name
- Show evidence, not opinion

## Common Mistakes to AVOID

### ❌ Mistake 1: Too Many Fillers
**Bad:** "So like, you know, I think that, like, basically..."
**Good:** "I went to the archives. The evidence shows..."

### ❌ Mistake 2: Sounding Uncertain About Verified Facts
**Bad:** "I think maybe the borders were finalized in 1926?"
**Good:** "The borders were finalized in 1926 through League of Nations arbitration."

### ❌ Mistake 3: Dumbing Down Language
**Bad:** "They split things up"
**Good:** "They partitioned the Ottoman Empire"

### ❌ Mistake 4: Vague Source Citations
**Bad:** "Some historian said..."
**Good:** "Samuel Moyn's 2015 study *Christian Human Rights* documents..."

### ❌ Mistake 5: No Modern Relevance for 3+ Minutes
**Fix:** Add connection every 90 seconds

## The Process

1. **Identify both extreme narratives**
   - What's Extreme A? (usually dismissive)
   - What's Extreme B? (usually oversimplified)
   - What does the evidence show?

2. **Research thoroughly**
   - Primary sources
   - Specific dates, documents, quotes
   - Historian citations
   - Modern examples

3. **Draft structure:**
   - Opening: Frame extremes authoritatively
   - Middle: Evidence + modern hooks
   - End: Both extremes wrong, nuanced reality

4. **Write with authority:**
   - Cite specific sources
   - Use precise language
   - Demonstrate research
   - Strategic fillers only

5. **Retention check:**
   - Modern hook every 90 sec?
   - Dates condensed?
   - Viewer knows why they're watching?

6. **Authority check:**
   - Do I sound knowledgeable?
   - Is this educational?
   - Too many fillers? Remove them.
   - Precise language throughout?
