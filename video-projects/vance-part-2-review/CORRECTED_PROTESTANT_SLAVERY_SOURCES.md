## Option 1: What Luther Actually Said (If you want to keep Luther)

### Luther on Social Hierarchy and Rebellion

**Verified Quote:**
Martin Luther, *Admonition to Peace: A Reply to the Twelve Articles of the Peasants in Swabia* (1525):

"God would rather suffer the government to exist, no matter how evil, than allow the mob to riot, no matter how justified they are in doing so."

**Context:**
- Written during the German Peasants' War (1524-1525)
- Luther condemned peasant uprisings against feudal lords
- Argued that Christians must obey earthly authorities even when oppressive

**Why this is relevant:**
- Shows Luther defended social hierarchy
- Opposed resistance to masters/lords
- Theological justification for submission to authority

**Citation Format:**
- Martin Luther, *Admonition to Peace* (1525)

---

### Luther on Serfdom (Closest to Slavery Discussion)

**Verified Quote:**
Martin Luther, *On Trade and Usury* (1524):

"It is not fitting that one man should be subject to another in his body and his goods; that right God alone has over us."

**BUT - Context Complicates This:**
- This sounds anti-slavery, but Luther was discussing **debt slavery**
- He opposed the enslavement of Christians by other Christians
- Did NOT extend this to non-Christians
- Still supported feudal hierarchy

**Not recommended for script:**
- Too ambiguous
- Requires too much context to explain
- Doesn't clearly show pro-slavery position

## Option 2: Other Protestant Reformers (Stronger Evidence)

### John Calvin - Clearer Pro-Slavery Position

**Calvin's Institute:**
John Calvin, *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (1536), Book III, Chapter 10:

"Let every man therefore who is taught to rest satisfied with his lot, and so labor to better his condition as not to neglect his calling."

**Context:**
- Used to justify maintaining social stations
- "God-ordained hierarchy" theology
- Explicitly told servants/slaves to accept their condition

**Better for script:** Calvin's position is clearer than Luther's

---

### George Whitefield - American Colonial Context

**Verified Position:**
George Whitefield (major Protestant revivalist), *Letter to the Inhabitants of Maryland, Virginia, North and South Carolina* (1740):

Whitefield owned slaves and defended slavery as compatible with Christianity, arguing:
- Black people were "worse than Egyptian bondage" before slavery
- Enslaved people should be Christianized but remain enslaved
- Masters had duty to provide religious instruction

**Why this works:**
- Shows Protestant defense of slavery
- American context (relevant to American Christian civilization claim)
- Clear documented position

## Option 3: What to Say About Aristotle

### The Actual "Tools with Voice" Source

**Aristotle, *Politics*, Book I, Part 4 (350 BCE):**

"The slave is a living tool, just as a tool is an inanimate slave."

Alternative translation: "A slave is a piece of property with a soul" or "an animate instrument"

**Context:**
- Aristotle argued some people are "natural slaves"
- This concept influenced Christian theology for centuries
- Church fathers (Augustine, Aquinas) adopted Aristotelian framework

**How to use this:**
- Acknowledge Aristotle originated the concept
- Show how it influenced Christian theology
- Connect ancient philosophy to medieval Christian thought
