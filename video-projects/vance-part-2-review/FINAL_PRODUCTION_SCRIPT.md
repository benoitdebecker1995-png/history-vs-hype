## CLAIM 2: FOUNDERS & RELIGION (4:15-7:00)

### SETUP [4:15-4:45]

**SAY:**
"At Ole Miss, Vance said the Supreme Court made a terrible mistake separating church and state. Founders *wanted* Christianity in public life.

What did the Founders actually want?

Here's what's true: Virginia had an Anglican church. Massachusetts required Protestant leaders. Multiple states had official churches.

But the Founders completely disagreed on this."

### MADISON VS ADAMS [4:45-5:45]

(SPLIT SCREEN: Portrait of Madison / Portrait of Adams)

**SAY:**
"James Madison's fighting to end Virginia's church establishment. 1785 - Memorial and Remonstrance.

National Archives website. founders.archives.gov. You can read it.

What Madison says about 1,500 years of established Christianity:"

(SHOW: Madison quote on screen)

**SAY:**
"'During almost fifteen centuries has the legal establishment of Christianity been on trial. What have been its fruits? More or less in all places, pride and indolence in the Clergy, ignorance and servility in the laity, in both, superstition, bigotry and persecution.'

Madison's whole argument is Christianity did *better* without government support.

Meanwhile - same time - John Adams is writing to Jefferson saying Christianity's principles are 'eternal and immutable.'

*Same guy* who signs the Treaty of Tripoli in 1797."

(TEXT ON SCREEN: "The government of the United States is not in any sense founded on the Christian Religion")

**SAY:**
"Says 'the government of the United States is not in any sense founded on the Christian Religion.'

This isn't consensus. They're actively arguing."

### WHAT HAPPENED [5:45-6:30]

(SHOW: Historical images montage)
(TEXT ON SCREEN: Timeline of religious violence in early America)

**SAY:**
"What does religious freedom look like in early America?

1844, Philadelphia. Catholic-Protestant riots over which Bible version to use in schools. Over 4,000 troops deployed. At least 20 people died.

1834, Charlestown, Massachusetts - mob burns down an Ursuline convent.

Mormons expelled from Missouri in 1838. Joseph Smith murdered in Illinois in 1844. 1857 - President Buchanan sends 5,000 troops to Utah.

Historian David Sehat calls this the 'moral establishment.' States using law to enforce Protestant Christianity even after official disestablishment ended."

### VERDICT [6:30-7:00]

**SAY:**
"Did the Founders want Christianity in public life?

Some did. Some didn't. They disagreed so fundamentally that *while Adams was president*, the Senate unanimously ratified a treaty saying America isn't founded on Christianity.

Supreme Court didn't invent separation of church and state. It ended a Protestant establishment that excluded everyone else.

The big claim. Is Christian civilization the most moral in history?"
