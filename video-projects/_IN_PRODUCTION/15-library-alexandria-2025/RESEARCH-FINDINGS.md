## THE CORE MYTH

**Popular claim:** One catastrophic fire (blamed on Caesar, Christians, or Muslims) destroyed the Library of Alexandria, setting back human progress by 1,000 years.

**The correction:** The Library declined gradually over centuries. No single destruction event. Most texts had copies elsewhere. Ancient writers who visited Alexandria describe its diminished state BEFORE the famous "destruction" events.

---

## MYTH 1: CAESAR'S FIRE (48 BC)

### What People Claim
Julius Caesar accidentally burned the Library during the Alexandrian War, destroying hundreds of thousands of scrolls.

### What the Sources Actually Say

**Plutarch, *Life of Caesar* 49.6 (written ~100 AD, 150 years after events):**
> "In this war, to begin with, Caesar encountered the peril of being shut off from water, since the canals were dammed up by the enemy; in the second place, when the enemy tried to cut off his fleet, he was forced to repel the danger by using fire, and this spread from the dockyards and destroyed the great library."

**Key observations:**
- This is ONE SENTENCE. Remarkably brief for supposedly the greatest cultural catastrophe in history.
- Plutarch visited Alexandria himself (after AD 83) and says nothing more about it.
- Plutarch "mentions the Library but not the books" — no details on what was lost.

**Cassius Dio, *Roman History* XLII.38.2 (written ~220 AD):**
> "Many places were set on fire, with the result that the docks and the storehouses of grain among other buildings were burned, and also the library, whose volumes, it is said, were of the greatest number and excellence."

**Key observations:**
- The Greek word used can mean "warehouses" not just "library"
- Scholars debate whether this refers to the main Library or warehouse storage near the docks
- Luciano Canfora (*The Vanished Library*): fire destroyed manuscripts in warehouses waiting for export, not the Library itself

**Livy (59 BC – 17 AD):**
- Claims 40,000 scrolls were destroyed
- This is a fraction of the claimed 400,000-700,000 total — suggesting partial damage, not total destruction

### THE SMOKING GUN: Strabo's Visit (~24 BC)

**Strabo, *Geography* Book 17 (visiting Alexandria ~24 BC — AFTER Caesar's fire):**
- Strabo describes the Mouseion still functioning
- He mentions Eratosthenes (the Library's third director) "had a library as large as Hipparchus says it was" — implying it had since diminished
- But he also describes making comparisons between different authors, suggesting he had access to a book collection

**The problem for the "Caesar destroyed it" narrative:**
If Caesar's fire in 48 BC destroyed the Library, how did Strabo visit it 24 years later and describe it still operating?

### Verdict on Myth 1
❓ PARTIALLY TRUE — Fire damaged warehouses/docks, possibly some scrolls. The Library itself likely survived or was rebuilt. Strabo's visit proves it wasn't totally destroyed.

**Source:** [Open University - Plutarch Life of Caesar](https://www.open.edu/openlearn/history-the-arts/library-alexandria/content-section-6.3), [Bad Ancient](https://www.badancient.com/claims/julius-caesar-burn-library-of-alexandria/), [LacusCurtius - Strabo](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Strabo/17A1*.html)

## MYTH 2: CHRISTIAN DESTRUCTION OF THE SERAPEUM (391 AD)

### What People Claim
Christian mobs, incited by Bishop Theophilus, destroyed the Library when they demolished the Serapeum temple in 391 AD.

### What the Sources Actually Say

**Socrates Scholasticus, *Ecclesiastical History* V.16 (5th century):**
- Describes Theophilus soliciting an imperial order to destroy the Serapeum
- Records that pagan images were melted down to make pots and church utensils
- **Makes NO mention of books being burned**

**Sozomen, *Ecclesiastical History* (5th century):**
- Parallel account of the Serapeum's destruction
- **Also makes NO mention of books**

**Eunapius of Antioch (pagan scholar, anti-Christian):**
- Describes the destruction
- **Makes NO mention of a library** — and he had every reason to condemn book-burning if it happened

**Theodoret and Rufinus Tyrannius:**
- Also describe the event
- **Neither mentions books**

### THE SMOKING GUN: Ammianus Marcellinus (~378 AD)

**Ammianus Marcellinus, Book 22.16.12-13 (written ~378 AD — BEFORE the 391 destruction):**
> "In here have been valuable libraries and the unanimous testimony of ancient records declares that seven hundred thousand books, brought together by the unremitting energy of the Ptolemies, were burned in the Alexandrine War when the city was sacked under the dictator Caesar."

**Key observations:**
- Writing 13 years BEFORE the Serapeum was destroyed
- Uses PAST TENSE to describe the libraries ("have been")
- Suggests the library collection was already gone by 378 AD
- May be repeating Plutarch's tradition, or may reflect his own knowledge that the collection no longer existed

### The Serapeum vs. The Library

**Critical distinction:**
- The "daughter library" in the Serapeum was NOT the main Royal Library
- By 391 AD, there's no evidence the Serapeum still housed any significant book collection
- Jonathan Theodore: by 391/392 AD there was "no remaining 'Great Library' in the sense of the iconic vast, priceless collection"

### Verdict on Myth 2
❌ UNLIKELY — We have FIVE accounts of the Serapeum's destruction. None mentions books. Ammianus (writing before 391) already speaks of the library in past tense. The "daughter library" may have been empty for decades.

**Source:** [History for Atheists](https://historyforatheists.com/2017/07/the-destruction-of-the-great-library-of-alexandria/), [LacusCurtius - Serapeum](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/encyclopaedia_romana/greece/paganism/serapeum.html)

## MYTH 3: CALIPH OMAR'S BURNING (642 AD)

### What People Claim
When Muslims conquered Alexandria in 642 AD, Caliph Omar ordered the books burned, saying: "If they agree with the Quran, they are unnecessary; if they disagree, they are heretical."

### What the Sources Actually Say

**The earliest sources:**
- **al-Qifti, *History of Learned Men* (before 1248)** — First to record this story
- **Bar Hebraeus (Abu'l Faraj), *Tārīkh Mukhtasar Al-Duwal* (13th century)** — Copied from al-Qifti

**Key observations:**
- The story appears **600 YEARS** after the alleged event
- No contemporary source (Christian, Muslim, or Jewish) from the 7th century mentions it
- Bar Hebraeus's original Syriac text doesn't mention the burning — it only appears in his Arabic abstract
- The story spread to Europe through Edward Pococke's 1650 partial translation

### THE SMOKING GUN: 600-Year Gap

**Professor Bernard Lewis:**
> "Modern research has shown the story to be completely unfounded. None of the early chronicles, not even the Christian ones, make any reference to it and it is not mentioned until the 13th century."

**Why did the story appear in the 13th century?**
Bernard Lewis suggests it was politically expedient during Saladin's reign — the Sunni Ayyubids were breaking up the Shia Fatimid library collections and needed historical justification.

### Verdict on Myth 3
❌ FALSE — The story appears 600 years after the fact. No contemporary evidence. Scholars consider it apocryphal, likely invented for 13th-century political purposes.

**Source:** [Roger Pearse](https://www.roger-pearse.com/weblog/2010/09/11/bar-hebraeus-abd-al-latif-and-the-destruction-of-the-library-of-alexandria/), [History of Information](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=5023)

## THE REAL STORY: GRADUAL DECLINE

### Why Libraries Die

**Roger Bagnall, "Alexandria: Library of Dreams" (2002):**
- Papyrus scrolls were extremely fragile
- In the humid Mediterranean, scrolls had to be continuously copied every few years
- Without sustained investment, scrolls simply rotted away
- "All you had to do was not copy them and, eventually, all the manuscripts would break down"

### The Scroll Count Myth

**Traditional claims:** 400,000 to 700,000 scrolls

**Bagnall's analysis:**
- Using Callimachus's *Pinakes* (the Library's catalog), he estimates ~94,000 scrolls at most
- The Library of Pergamon (Alexandria's rival) held ~30,000 scrolls
- The Forum of Trajan library in Rome held ~20,000 scrolls
- "Six-figure estimates" are "outlandish"
- Realistic estimate: 40,000-50,000 scrolls at its height

### Why Texts Survived Anyway

**The key insight:** Books aren't saved by being in libraries. They're saved by being copied.

**The ancient library network:**
- By the end of the Hellenistic Period, almost every city in the Eastern Mediterranean had a public library
- Alexandria was one of dozens of libraries across the Mediterranean
- The Library of Pergamon rivaled Alexandria's collection
- Important texts existed in multiple copies across multiple cities

**The Oxyrhynchus Papyri (discovered 1896):**
- Over 500,000 fragments found in an ancient Egyptian rubbish dump
- Include texts by Menander, Sappho, early New Testament manuscripts
- Prove that texts circulated widely — they weren't all in one building
- 52 of 127 registered New Testament papyri come from Oxyrhynchus

**Byzantine preservation:**
- Whatever survived was copied by Byzantine scriptoriums
- Monasteries preserved classical texts through the Middle Ages
- These copies were printed during the Renaissance

### Verdict on the "Set Back Science 1,000 Years" Claim
❌ FALSE — The Library's decline was gradual, not catastrophic. Texts that mattered were copied and circulated. The Oxyrhynchus papyri prove texts survived outside Alexandria. The "1,000 years lost" narrative dramatically overstates both the Library's holdings and the uniqueness of its collection.

**Source:** [Roger Bagnall paper](https://archive.nyu.edu/handle/2451/28263), [Oxyrhynchus Papyri - Oxford](https://oxyrhynchus.web.ox.ac.uk/introduction)
