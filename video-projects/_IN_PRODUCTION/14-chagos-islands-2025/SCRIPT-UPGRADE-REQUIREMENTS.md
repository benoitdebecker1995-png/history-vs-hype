## GAPS TO FIX

### 1. NO ACADEMIC HISTORIAN QUOTES (Critical)

**Current state:** Script relies entirely on legal documents and eyewitness testimony.

**Required:** Word-for-word quotes from academic sources with page numbers.

**Key source needed:** David Vine, *Island of Shame* (Princeton University Press, 2009)

**Example of what's missing:**
> "According to David Vine in *Island of Shame*, page 47: 'The base at Diego Garcia was conceived as a place where, as one U.S. Navy official put it, "no population would [be] opposed to our operations."'"

---

### 2. WEAK CAUSAL CHAINS (Kraut-style missing)

**Current (shallow):**
> "By the 1960s, the Pentagon was watching colonies become independent nations—nations that didn't always want American bases on their soil. They needed somewhere remote."

**Required (deep causal chain):**
> "The 1959 Cuban Revolution triggered a Pentagon reassessment of base security. Cuba had been a reliable American ally—and then overnight, it wasn't. Consequently, Pentagon planners began identifying island territories where host-nation politics couldn't threaten base operations. This meant colonies on the verge of independence became valuable precisely because they could be detached before self-governance kicked in. Thereby, Britain's remaining possessions in the Indian Ocean became targets—and Chagos, remote and underdeveloped, became the ideal solution."

**Key connectors needed:** "consequently," "thereby," "which meant that," "as a result"

---

### 3. NO COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS (Kraut signature)

**Missing entirely.** Should compare Chagos to:

- **France and Mayotte:** Did France hold a referendum? What happened?
- **UK and Cyprus/Gibraltar:** How did Britain negotiate basing rights WITHOUT deportation?
- **US and Guam:** How were populations treated in other strategic territories?

**Example of what to add:**
> "While France facing a similar situation in Mayotte simply held a referendum—and the population voted to stay French—Britain never gave the Chagossians that choice. Throughout the rest of the decolonization era, Britain preserved strategic interests through negotiated basing rights—in Cyprus, in Gibraltar. But in Chagos, they created something different: a territory with no citizens to negotiate with."

---

### 4. INTELLECTUAL HONESTY SECTION WEAK (Alex O'Connor style)

**Current:** Script is one-sided against UK/US. No acknowledgment of legitimate security arguments.

**Required:** Steelman the opposing view, then address it.

**Example of what to add:**
> "And look—the security argument isn't nothing. Diego Garcia has been used for operations in Afghanistan, Iraq, and counter-piracy missions in the Indian Ocean. I'm not saying those operations weren't necessary. What I'm saying is that the security value of a base doesn't retroactively legalize how you acquired it."

---

### 5. BOTH EXTREMES NOT FRAMED IN OPENING

**Current opening:** Goes straight to the memo without framing the debate.

**Required (from style guide):**
> "There are two narratives you'll hear. One side says [X]. The other says [Y]. When you examine the evidence, both oversimplify what actually happened."

**Proposed addition to opening:**
> "You'll hear two stories about what just happened. One side says this is decolonization done right—Britain finally doing the right thing, paying reparations, returning sovereignty. The other side says it's a sham—colonialism rebranded, the military-industrial complex getting exactly what it wanted under a new flag.
>
> When you examine the documents, both narratives miss what's actually in the treaty."
