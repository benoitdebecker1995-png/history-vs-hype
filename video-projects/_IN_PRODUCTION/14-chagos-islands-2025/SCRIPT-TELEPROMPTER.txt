SCRIPT: Britain's Last Colony - The Chagos Islands Deal Nobody's Talking About

HOOK (0:00-0:50)

In May 2025, Britain signed away its last African colony. Headlines called it historic. Mauritius finally got its islands back after sixty years.

But here's what the headlines didn't mention.

The day before the meeting that started all this, a British official wrote a memo. September 22nd, 1965. His exact words: "The object is to frighten him with hope. Hope that he might get independence. Fright lest he might not unless he is sensible about the detachment of the Chagos Archipelago."

That's not interpretation. That's what they wrote down.

And here's where it gets interesting. The Prime Minister who signed the 2025 deal? His father was the man sitting across from Harold Wilson in 1965, being told to hand over those islands or forget about independence.

Sixty years later, the son gets them back. Sort of. Because the 2,000 people Britain deported to build a military base? They're still not allowed to go home.

ACT 1: THE SETUP (0:50-2:00)

So what made these islands worth threatening a country's independence over?

Diego Garcia sits right in the middle of the Indian Ocean. Within striking distance of the Persian Gulf, South Asia, East Africa. One of the best natural harbors in the world.

By the 1960s, the Pentagon was watching colonies become independent nations—nations that didn't always want American bases on their soil. They needed somewhere remote. Somewhere they could control completely.

And when I say completely, I mean it. That phrase—"exclusive control without local inhabitants"—that's from the planning documents. The goal wasn't just a military base. It was a base with no voters, no journalists, no one to complain.

There was just one problem. About 1,500 people already lived there. The Chagossians had been on those islands for generations.

So Britain decided to solve that problem. First on paper. Then in reality.

ACT 2: THE THREAT (2:00-3:30)

September 23rd, 1965. The morning after that memo was written. Harold Wilson met with Seewoosagur Ramgoolam, the Premier of Mauritius, at Lancaster House in London.

And Wilson made the situation very clear.

"The Premier and his colleagues could return to Mauritius either with independence or without it."

That's a direct quote from the meeting notes. Give us the islands, or you don't become a country.

Now think about what's happening here. Ramgoolam isn't negotiating as the leader of an independent nation. He's a colonial subject, sitting across from the government that still controls his country, being told what to do.

The ICJ would later call this out directly. They found that Britain's so-called consent from Mauritius "was not based on the free and genuine expression of the will of the people concerned."

Britain paid £3 million. And on November 8th, 1965—three years before Mauritius would even become independent—the UK carved out the Chagos Archipelago and created a brand new colony. The British Indian Ocean Territory.

The paperwork was done. Now they just had to empty the islands.

ACT 3: THE REMOVAL (3:30-5:45)

The deportation happened in stages. And it was calculated.

The only way between Chagos and Mauritius was by cargo ship. So starting in 1969, Chagossians who left the islands—for medical treatment, to visit family, to buy supplies—found out they couldn't come back. Their work contracts had "expired." The ship wouldn't take them.

Rita Bancoult was one of them. A steamship company representative told her directly: "Your island has been sold. You will never go there again."

Then the supplies started drying up. Less food arriving. Less medicine. And the message wasn't subtle. Leave, or starve.

Those who stayed got it even clearer. Chagossians reported being told they'd be "bombed or shot" if they refused to go. One elderly woman recalled an American official telling her: "If you don't leave, you won't be fed any longer."

And then there were the dogs.

Before the final deportations, British agents rounded up the Chagossians' pets—hundreds of dogs—and forced them into a sealed shed. They ran exhaust fumes from military vehicles into the building. Gassed them. Then burned the bodies.

The families waiting to be deported watched it happen.

Between 1967 and 1973, somewhere between 1,500 and 2,000 Chagossians were loaded onto cargo ships and dumped on the docks in Mauritius and the Seychelles. No housing arranged. No jobs. No support.

One survivor put it this way: "It's as if I was pulled from my paradise to put me in hell."

So the UN's highest court eventually ruled this was illegal. The General Assembly demanded Britain leave. What happened next?

ACT 4: THE RULING (5:45-7:30)

For decades, Mauritius pushed this through international law. And in 2019, they got their ruling.

I went through the ICJ opinion. All 56 pages. And the language isn't ambiguous.

Thirteen judges to one found that Britain's decolonization of Mauritius was "not lawfully completed" when they carved out Chagos.

The court's exact words: "The United Kingdom's continued administration of the Chagos Archipelago constitutes a wrongful act entailing the international responsibility of that State."

Three months later, the UN General Assembly voted on a resolution demanding Britain withdraw within six months.

116 countries in favor. 6 against. The US, UK, Australia, Israel, Hungary, and the Maldives.

British officials kept calling this a "bilateral sovereignty dispute." But when 116 countries vote against you and 6 vote with you, "bilateral" doesn't really describe it anymore.

London's response? The UK representative said Britain had "no doubt about our sovereignty" and dismissed the ruling as "merely an advisory opinion."

The six-month deadline came and went. Britain stayed. Nothing changed.

Until 2025.

ACT 5: THE DEAL (7:30-9:30)

May 22nd, 2025. Britain finally signs over sovereignty. Mauritius gets its territory back.

Decolonization complete. Right?

Not quite. Because when you actually read the treaty, it's more complicated.

Diego Garcia—the island with the base, the whole reason any of this happened—stays under British control. For 99 years. With an option to extend for another 40. And then potentially again after that.

Britain pays Mauritius about £101 million per year. Over the full term, that's roughly £3.4 billion. Not bad for a small island nation.

But here's what doesn't make the headlines.

The Chagossians—the people who were actually deported—weren't at the table. A UK spokesperson confirmed they "could not be part of the negotiations as they were conducted on a state-to-state basis."

Two governments deciding the future of islands that belonged to neither of them originally. And the people who lived there? Not invited.

And when you read the treaty, there it is in black and white. Mauritius can resettle Chagossians on the outer islands. But Diego Garcia—the island their families were removed from—is explicitly excluded.

In June 2025, UN human rights experts put out a statement saying the agreement "fails to guarantee and protect the rights of the Chagossian people, including their right to return to Diego Garcia."

There's a £40 million trust fund for the Chagossians. But as one MP pointed out, there's no guarantee that fund will actually be controlled by Chagossians.

Diego Garcia remains exactly what Washington wanted sixty years ago: a military base without an inconvenient population.

Mauritius calls this a "great victory." And for Mauritius, maybe it is. Sovereignty on paper, billions in payments.

But the Chagossians are still watching from exile.

CONCLUSION (9:30-10:30)

There's something almost poetic about the Ramgoolam story.

In 1965, Harold Wilson sat across from Seewoosagur Ramgoolam and told him: give us these islands or you won't get independence. Sixty years later, his son Navin signed the treaty that formally gets them back.

But look at what actually changed.

In 1965, Britain controlled the islands. The Chagossians couldn't go home.

In 2025, Mauritius controls the islands on paper. The Chagossians still can't go home.

The flag over Diego Garcia will change. The base stays. And until the people who were born on those islands can decide their own future, you have to ask—is this decolonization? Or is it just a rebranded lease with better PR?
