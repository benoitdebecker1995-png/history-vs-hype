# SCRIPT: Britain's Last Colony - The Chagos Islands Deal Nobody's Talking About

**Project:** 14-chagos-islands-2025
**Version:** Draft 3.0 (Steelman + Double Betrayal)
**Date:** 2025-12-12
**Tone:** Forensic, calm, slightly sardonic

---

## HOOK (0:00-0:45)

[TALKING HEAD]

In May 2025, Britain signed away its last African colony. Headlines called it historic. Mauritius finally got its islands back after sixty years.

But here's what didn't make the headlines.

[B-ROLL: "Frighten him with hope" memo on screen, slow zoom]

The day before the meeting that started all this, a British official wrote a memo. September 22nd, 1965. His exact words: "The object is to frighten him with hope. Hope that he might get independence. Fright lest he might not unless he is sensible about the detachment of the Chagos Archipelago."

[TALKING HEAD]

That's not interpretation. That's what they wrote down.

[B-ROLL: Side-by-side photos - Seewoosagur Ramgoolam 1965 / Navin Ramgoolam 2025]

The Prime Minister who signed the 2025 deal? His father was the man sitting across from Harold Wilson in 1965, being told to hand over those islands or forget about independence.

Sixty years later, the son gets them back. Sort of. Because the 2,000 people Britain deported? They're still not allowed to go home. And Mauritius agreed to keep it that way.

---

## ACT 1: THE SETUP (0:45-2:00)

[TALKING HEAD]

So what made these islands worth threatening a country's independence over?

[B-ROLL: Map showing Indian Ocean, Diego Garcia highlighted]

Diego Garcia sits in the middle of the Indian Ocean. Within striking distance of the Persian Gulf, South Asia, East Africa. One of the best natural harbors in the world.

By the 1960s, the Pentagon was watching colonies become independent nations—nations that didn't always want American bases on their soil. They needed somewhere remote. Somewhere without political complications.

[B-ROLL: Document excerpt - "exclusive control without local inhabitants"]

That phrase—"exclusive control without local inhabitants"—is from the planning documents. The goal wasn't just a military base. It was a base with no voters, no journalists, no one who might ask questions at the United Nations.

[TALKING HEAD]

There was one problem. About 1,500 people already lived there. The Chagossians had been on those islands for generations—descended from enslaved and indentured laborers, with their own distinct culture.

So Britain decided to solve that problem. First on paper. Then in reality.

---

## ACT 2: THE THREAT (2:00-3:30)

[B-ROLL: Lancaster House exterior]

September 23rd, 1965. The morning after that memo was written. Harold Wilson met with Seewoosagur Ramgoolam, the Premier of Mauritius, at Lancaster House in London.

[TALKING HEAD]

And Wilson made the situation clear.

[B-ROLL: Quote on screen - "either with independence or without it"]

"The Premier and his colleagues could return to Mauritius either with independence or without it."

That's a direct quote from the meeting notes. Give us the islands, or you don't become a country.

[TALKING HEAD]

Now think about what's happening here. Ramgoolam isn't negotiating as the leader of an independent nation. He's a colonial subject, sitting across from the government that still controls his country, being told what to do.

The ICJ would later apply what they called "heightened scrutiny" to this moment. Their conclusion: Britain's so-called consent from Mauritius "was not based on the free and genuine expression of the will of the people concerned."

[B-ROLL: Document showing £3 million payment]

Britain paid £3 million. And on November 8th, 1965—three years before Mauritius would even become independent—the UK passed the British Indian Ocean Territory Order. Using an archaic royal prerogative, no parliamentary vote required. They carved out the Chagos Archipelago and created a brand new colony.

The paperwork was done. Now they just had to empty the islands.

---

## ACT 3: THE REMOVAL (3:30-5:30)

[TALKING HEAD]

The deportation happened in stages. And it was calculated.

[B-ROLL: Map showing Mauritius and Chagos, shipping route]

The only way between Chagos and Mauritius was by cargo ship. So starting in 1969, Chagossians who left the islands—for medical treatment, to visit family—found out they couldn't come back. Their work contracts had "expired." The ship wouldn't take them.

[B-ROLL: Text on screen - Rita Bancoult quote]

Rita Bancoult was one of them. A steamship company representative told her directly: "Your island has been sold. You will never go there again."

[TALKING HEAD]

Then the supplies started drying up. Less food. Less medicine. The message wasn't subtle. Leave, or starve.

Those who stayed got it clearer. Chagossians reported being told they'd be "bombed or shot" if they refused to go.

[TALKING HEAD - slower pace]

And then there were the dogs.

[B-ROLL: Text on screen - eyewitness account]

Before the final deportations, British agents rounded up the Chagossians' pets—hundreds of dogs—herded them into a sealed shed, ran exhaust fumes from military vehicles into the building. Gassed them. Then burned the bodies.

The families waiting to be deported watched it happen.

[TALKING HEAD]

Between 1967 and 1973, somewhere between 1,500 and 2,000 Chagossians were loaded onto cargo ships and left on the docks in Mauritius and the Seychelles. No housing arranged. No jobs. No support.

[B-ROLL: Text on screen - Chagossian testimony]

One survivor put it this way: "It's as if I was pulled from my paradise to put me in hell."

---

## ACT 4: THE RULING (5:30-7:30)

[B-ROLL: ICJ building]

For decades, Mauritius pushed this through international law. And in 2019, they got their ruling.

[TALKING HEAD]

I went through the ICJ opinion. All 56 pages. The language isn't ambiguous.

[B-ROLL: Text on screen - "13 votes to 1"]

Thirteen judges to one found that Britain's decolonization of Mauritius was "not lawfully completed" when they carved out Chagos.

[B-ROLL: ICJ ruling excerpt on screen]

The court's exact words: "The United Kingdom's continued administration of the Chagos Archipelago constitutes a wrongful act entailing the international responsibility of that State."

[TALKING HEAD]

Now, to be fair to the UK position—and I want to present the strongest version of their argument.

[B-ROLL: Diego Garcia aerial / military operations]

Diego Garcia isn't just any base. B-52s flew from that atoll during the Afghanistan invasion. Prepositioned ships supplied the Gulf War. The facility supports GPS, nuclear test monitoring, counter-piracy operations. Defense experts call it "the single most important military facility" between the Mediterranean and the Pacific.

[TALKING HEAD]

And the one dissenting judge, Joan Donoghue, made a serious legal argument. She said this was a bilateral sovereignty dispute, and by ruling on it, the ICJ was circumventing Britain's right to refuse judicial settlement. In her words, the court's decision undermined "the integrity of the Court's judicial function."

[TALKING HEAD]

But here's the problem with that argument. The ICJ didn't treat this as a simple land dispute. They ruled it was fundamentally about decolonization—an obligation owed to the entire international community. And when 116 countries voted for Britain to withdraw and only 6 voted against, "bilateral dispute" doesn't quite describe it anymore.

[B-ROLL: UK representative at UN]

London's response? The UK representative said Britain had "no doubt about our sovereignty" and dismissed the ruling as "merely an advisory opinion."

The six-month deadline came and went. Britain stayed. Nothing changed.

Until 2025.

---

## ACT 5: THE DEAL (7:30-9:45)

[B-ROLL: 2025 treaty signing / headlines]

May 22nd, 2025. Britain finally signs over sovereignty. Mauritius gets its territory back.

Decolonization complete. Right?

[TALKING HEAD]

Not quite. Because when you read the treaty, it's more complicated. And this is where Mauritius deserves some scrutiny too.

[B-ROLL: Treaty document pages]

Diego Garcia—the island with the base, the whole reason any of this happened—stays under UK/US control. For 99 years. With an option to extend for another 40. And potentially again after that.

Britain pays Mauritius about £101 million per year. Over the full term, roughly £3.4 billion.

[TALKING HEAD]

But here's what doesn't make the headlines.

[B-ROLL: Text on screen - "Chagossians could not be part of the negotiations"]

The Chagossians—the people who were actually deported—weren't at the table. A UK spokesperson confirmed they "could not be part of the negotiations as they were conducted on a state-to-state basis."

Two governments deciding the future of islands that belonged to neither of them originally. And the people who lived there? Not invited.

[B-ROLL: Treaty text - Article 6]

And when you read the treaty, there it is in black and white. Mauritius can resettle Chagossians on the outer islands. But Diego Garcia—the island their families were removed from—is explicitly excluded. For 99 years.

[TALKING HEAD]

This is where the story gets uncomfortable for everyone.

The UK committed the original wrong. The deportation. The ICJ called it a wrongful act. The UK itself now admits it was "shameful and wrong."

But Mauritius agreed to this deal. They accepted the 99-year exclusion. They took the £3.4 billion. And they signed away the Chagossians' right to return to Diego Garcia for a century.

[B-ROLL: UN experts statement]

In June 2025, UN human rights experts put out a statement saying the agreement "fails to guarantee and protect the rights of the Chagossian people, including their right to return to Diego Garcia."

[TALKING HEAD]

There's a £40 million trust fund for the Chagossians. But as critics pointed out, there's no guarantee that fund will actually be controlled by Chagossians.

The Chagossian community itself is divided. Olivier Bancoult of the Chagos Refugees Group called it a "historic day." But other groups, like Chagossian Voices, oppose the deal because it doesn't include the full right of return.

[B-ROLL: Text on screen - "in substance declaratory"]

Legal analysts note the resettlement clause is "in substance declaratory." It doesn't actually secure the right to return. It just says Mauritius is "free to implement a programme." That's not the same thing.

Diego Garcia remains exactly what the planning documents called for sixty years ago: "exclusive control without local inhabitants."

---

## CONCLUSION (9:45-10:45)

[TALKING HEAD]

There's something almost poetic about the Ramgoolam story.

[B-ROLL: Side-by-side Ramgoolam photos - 1965 / 2025]

In 1965, Harold Wilson sat across from Seewoosagur Ramgoolam and told him: give us these islands or you won't get independence. Sixty years later, his son Navin signed the treaty that formally gets them back.

[TALKING HEAD]

But look at what actually changed.

In 1965, Britain controlled the islands. The Chagossians couldn't go home.

In 2025, Mauritius controls the islands on paper. Britain still controls Diego Garcia. The Chagossians still can't go home.

[B-ROLL: Chagossian community photo / protest]

The UK calls it making amends. Mauritius calls it a great victory. But neither government asked the Chagossians what they wanted.

[TALKING HEAD - direct to camera]

The flag over Diego Garcia will change eventually. The base stays. And until the people who were born on those islands can decide their own future, you have to ask—is this decolonization? Or is it just a rebranded lease with better PR?
