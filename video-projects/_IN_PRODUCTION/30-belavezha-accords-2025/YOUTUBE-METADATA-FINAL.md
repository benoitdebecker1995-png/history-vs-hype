```
In March 1991, 113 million Soviet citizens voted in a referendum. 78% said: keep the USSR.

Nine months later, three men met in a Belarusian hunting lodge and signed a document declaring the Soviet Union "no longer exists."

Was that legal?

This video examines the actual Belavezha Accords—the 1991 document Russia now uses to argue Ukraine's borders were never legitimate—and asks whether the dissolution of the Soviet Union followed its own laws.

THE EVIDENCE EXAMINED:
• The Belavezha Preamble: "as Founder States... we hereby declare..."
• Article 5: The territorial integrity clause Russia later violated
• Article 14: The provision that terminated all Soviet institutions
• The 1990 Secession Law the signers bypassed
• The March 1991 referendum they overruled
• The 1922 Treaty of Creation—and the "founder states" loophole

THE VERDICT:
Under Soviet law, almost certainly unconstitutional. But historically? The Accords formalized the death of a state that was already collapsing.

WHY THIS MATTERS NOW:
In August 1991—four months before Belavezha—Yeltsin's press secretary named two regions Russia would claim if republics left: Crimea and the Donbas.

In 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine.
In 2025, a Putin adviser declared the USSR "still legally exists."

The Belavezha question isn't academic. It's the legal argument behind a war.
```
