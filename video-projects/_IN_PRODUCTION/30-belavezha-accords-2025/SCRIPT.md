# Was the Belavezha Accords the Illegal End of the Soviet Union?

**Target length:** 6 min (~900 words)
**Angle:** Legal question - court case structure with 2022 payoff
**Gap filled:** Primary document examination + 1991→2022 connection (missing from English coverage)

## HOOK (0:00-0:30)

In 1996, the Russian parliament declared that the document ending the Soviet Union was illegal.

In 2022, former Prime Minister Sergei Stepashin went further: the Soviet Union, he said, "never legally ceased to exist."

[B-ROLL: 1996 Duma vote footage or headline]

Today we're going to examine that document—the Belavezha Accords—and ask: was the end of the Soviet Union actually illegal?

---

## THE DOCUMENT (0:30-1:30)

[B-ROLL: Full Preamble text on screen, highlight key phrases as read]

December 8th, 1991. Three leaders meet in a Belarusian hunting lodge and sign a document with this Preamble:

"We, the Republic of Belarus, the Russian Federation and Ukraine, **as founder States of the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics and signatories of the Union Treaty of 1922**, hereby declare that the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics as a subject of international law and a geopolitical reality **no longer exists**."

[B-ROLL: Article 5 text on screen]

Article 5 promised to "acknowledge and respect each other's territorial integrity and the inviolability of existing borders."

[B-ROLL: Article 14 text on screen]

And Article 14 delivered the kill shot: "The activities of organs of the former Union of Soviet Socialist Republics in the territories of the States members of the Commonwealth are hereby **terminated**."

Three men. One document. No more superpower.

But was it legal?

---

## THE PROSECUTION (1:30-3:00)

[B-ROLL: Scale of justice tilting, or "PROSECUTION" title card]

The case against the Accords is straightforward.

Soviet law already had a secession process. The 1990 Secession Law required a five-year transition, a two-thirds majority referendum, minority veto rights, and approval from the Congress of People's Deputies. The Belavezha signers ignored all of it.

[B-ROLL: 1990 law document or text summary on screen]

And just nine months earlier, the Soviet Union had held a referendum. 77.85% of citizens—113 million people—voted to preserve the USSR. Under Soviet law, that vote was binding. The three leaders overruled it in a single night.

[B-ROLL: Bar chart showing 77.85%]

On top of that, the USSR had fifteen republics. Only three were in the room. The other twelve weren't consulted.

When Mikhail Gorbachev learned what happened, he confronted Yeltsin: "The three of you got together, but **who gave you any such authorization**?"

[B-ROLL: Gorbachev archival photo with quote overlay]
[SOURCE: Plokhy, *The Last Empire*, p. 321]

Even Leonid Kravchuk—one of the signers—later admitted: "The signing of the Belavezha Accords was a **coup d'état** which was completed peacefully."

[SOURCE: Salenko, *Heidelberg Journal of International Law*, p. 158]

---

## THE DEFENSE (3:00-4:30)

[B-ROLL: Scale tilting other direction, or "DEFENSE" title card]

But the defense has answers.

The signers weren't seceding—they were rescinding the 1922 treaty that created the Soviet Union in the first place. Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus were three of the four original founders. The fourth—the Transcaucasian Republic—had been dissolved in 1936. Legal advisor Sergey Shakhrai argued that since these republics tied "the political nuptial cord" in 1922, only they could untie it.

[SOURCE: Plokhy, *The Last Empire*, p. 309]

There's also the practical reality. By December 1991, the Soviet state was—in historian Vladislav Zubok's words—"already a carcass." The August coup had failed. Inflation hit 250% per month. And just one week earlier, 90% of Ukrainians had voted for independence.

[SOURCE: Zubok, *Collapse*, p. 395]

And two weeks after Belavezha, eleven republics signed the Alma-Ata Declaration—retroactively legitimizing what had already been done.

---

## VERDICT (4:30-5:45)

[B-ROLL: Gavel, or document with "VERDICT" stamp]

So what's the verdict?

Under Soviet law as it existed in 1991, the Belavezha Accords were almost certainly unconstitutional. Three republics made a unilateral decision for fifteen. They bypassed the secession law. They ignored a binding referendum.

But historically, they formalized the death of a state that was already brain-dead.

Here's why this matters today.

[B-ROLL: Voshchanov statement text overlay]

In August 1991—four months BEFORE the Accords—Yeltsin's press secretary Pavel Voshchanov made a threat. He said Russia reserved the right to revise its boundaries with any republic that left the Union. He specifically named two regions: Crimea and the Donbas.

[SOURCE: Plokhy, *The Last Empire*; Chatham House]

Fourteen years later, Vladimir Putin called the Soviet collapse "the greatest geopolitical catastrophe of the century."

[SOURCE: Kremlin archives, April 25, 2005]

And in 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine.

The Belavezha question isn't academic. It's the legal argument Russia uses to claim Ukraine's borders—and Ukraine itself—were never legitimate.
