## Description

Did Christians steal December 25th from Sol Invictus?

The standard story: Emperor Aurelian established a sun festival on December 25th in 274 AD, and Christians later hijacked the date. It's repeated everywhere. But when you check the actual Roman calendars and church records, something strange happens.

The earliest written evidence for a Sol Invictus festival on December 25th? The Chronography of 354 AD.
The earliest written evidence for Christmas on December 25th? The same document. Same year. Same city.

That "ancient pagan festival" Christians supposedly copied? It appears in the historical record at exactly the same time as Christmas does.

In this video, I examine the Chronography of 354, the calendar anomalies historian Steven Hijmans discovered, and why scholars like C.P.E. Nothaft now call the "pagan takeover" narrative a 19th-century construct rather than ancient history.

---

**EVIDENCE EXAMINED**

• The Chronography of 354: The luxury Roman almanac containing both the pagan calendar entry ("N INVICTI CM XXX") and the Christian martyrology entry for Christ's birth
• The Aurelian Problem: Why there's no coin, decree, or document placing his Sol festival on December 25th (his games were in October)
• The Calculation Hypothesis: How early Christians derived December 25th from March 25th using theological math—documented in Augustine's De Trinitate
• The Donatist Evidence: Why a 311 AD church schism suggests Christmas existed in North Africa before Constantine
