## CLAIM 1: HUMAN RIGHTS

Vance says Christianity invented human rights and human dignity. Common claim in conservative Christian circles. But I checked church documents. In 1832. 56 years after the Declaration of Independence. Pope Gregory 16 publishes Mirari Vos.

Section 14. Exact quote:

'This shameful font of indifferentism gives rise to that absurd and erroneous proposition which claims that liberty of conscience must be maintained for everyone.'

The Pope is calling freedom of conscience absurd.

But it gets worse. Watch what the Pope said 32 years later.

1864. Pope Pius 9 publishes the Syllabus of Errors. A numbered list of 80 'modern ideas' that are condemned.

Error 15: Religious freedom. Condemned.

Error 77: Ending Catholic-only states. Condemned.

Error 78: Allowing other religions to worship publicly. Condemned.

This is 1864. The Catholic Church is officially condemning religious freedom as an error.

Maybe that's just Catholics. What about Protestants?

Martin Luther, 1520s. Says governments are ordained by God to maintain order. Individual conscience matters for salvation, but not for challenging authority. John Calvin establishes Geneva as a theocracy. 1553 - burns Michael Servetus at the stake for heresy. For 300 years after the Reformation, Protestant states enforced religious conformity just like Catholic ones.

And here's the part nobody mentions. So when does 'Christian human rights' language actually show up?

Historian at Yale, Samuel Moyn. He documented it. The phrase gets invented in the 1940s. After World War Two. A theologian named Charles Malik working at the UN starts claiming Christianity always supported human rights. Moyn shows that's retrofitting. For most of Christian history, the political model was hierarchy. Divine right of kings. Church backing monarchy.

This is where the story flips.

1965. Second Vatican Council. Declaration on Religious Freedom. The Catholic Church reverses its position. Says people have a right to religious liberty. But that's 101 years after condemning it. And almost 2,000 years after Christianity began.

Did Christianity invent human rights? Major churches were condemning these concepts in the 1800s. Changed positions in the 1900s after Enlightenment philosophy pressured them. That's not inventing rights. That's eventually accepting them.

So if churches opposed rights, what about the Founders?

## CLAIM 2: FOUNDERS & RELIGION

What did the Founders actually want? Here's what's true: Virginia had an Anglican church. Massachusetts required Protestant leaders. Multiple states had official churches. But the Founders completely disagreed on this.

James Madison's fighting to end Virginia's church establishment. 1785 - Memorial and Remonstrance.

National Archives website. founders.archives.gov. You can read it.

What Madison says about 1,500 years of established Christianity:

'During almost fifteen centuries has the legal establishment of Christianity been on trial. What have been its fruits? More or less in all places, pride and indolence in the Clergy, ignorance and servility in the laity, in both, superstition, bigotry and persecution.'

Madison's whole argument is Christianity did better without government support.

Meanwhile - same time - John Adams is writing to Jefferson saying Christianity's principles are 'eternal and immutable.' The same guy who signs the Treaty of Tripoli in 1797.

Says 'the government of the United States is not in any sense founded on the Christian Religion.'

This isn't consensus. They're actively arguing.

What does religious freedom look like in early America?

1844, Philadelphia. Catholic-Protestant riots over which Bible version to use in schools. Over 4,000 troops deployed. At least 20 people died.

1834, Charlestown, Massachusetts - mob burns down an Ursuline convent.

Mormons are expelled from Missouri in 1838. Joseph Smith murdered in Illinois in 1844. 1857 - President Buchanan sends 5,000 troops to Utah.

Historian David Sehat calls this the 'moral establishment.' States using law to enforce Protestant Christianity even after official disestablishment ended.

Did the Founders want Christianity in public life? Some did. Some didn't. They disagreed so fundamentally that while Adams was president, the Senate unanimously ratified a treaty saying America isn't founded on Christianity.

The Supreme Court didn't invent separation of church and state. It ended a Protestant establishment that excluded everyone else.

The big claim. Is Christian civilization the most moral in history?

## CLAIM 3: MORAL CIVILIZATION

1452. Pope Nicholas V issues a papal bull called Dum Diversas.

Addressed to the King of Portugal. Reading directly from the Latin translation:

The Pope grants Portugal 'full and free permission to invade, search out, capture, and subjugate the Saracens and pagans and any other unbelievers...and to reduce their persons to perpetual slavery.'

Perpetual slavery. Authorized by the Pope. 1452.

Three years later - 1455 - Romanus Pontifex. Authorizes the Portuguese slave trade.

1493 - Inter Caetera. Divides the entire 'New World' between Spain and Portugal.

Maybe that's just Catholics. What about Protestants?

Martin Luther calls slaves 'tools with voice.' Says rebellion against masters is rebellion against God.

Southern Presbyterian theologian James Henley Thornwell, 1850s, says slavery is 'the condition in which infinite wisdom has been pleased to place him.'

Catholic and Protestant theologians defended slavery for over a thousand years.

What about Christians who weren't enslaved? How did Christian authorities treat people they called heretics?

1487. Two Catholic inquisitors publish the Malleus Maleficarum. The 'Hammer of Witches.'

It's a manual. How to identify witches. How to torture them. How to execute them. Church-approved.

Between 1400 and 1782, European Christian authorities executed an estimated 40,000 to 60,000 people for witchcraft.

80% were women.

Protestant areas were just as brutal. Scotland executed 4,000. Germany executed 25,000.

Martin Luther supported witch hunts. John Calvin presided over witch trials in Geneva.

Catholic inquisitors. Protestant reformers. Same outcome.

The Southern Baptist Convention was founded in 1845 to defend slavery. Methodist Church split over it in 1844. Presbyterian Church split in 1861.

And this matters today - because this same argument is now back in school curriculums.

Florida's 2023 curriculum standards say enslaved people 'developed skills which, in some instances, could be applied for their personal benefit.'

PragerU videos - eight states including Florida and Oklahoma now allow in classrooms - claim Christianity ended slavery. Without mentioning the 1,400 years it spent defending it.

Who abolished slavery first in the Americas?

Haiti. 1804. Slave revolt. They defeat three Christian empires trying to keep them enslaved. France, Britain, Spain.

They won.

Britain abolishes slavery in 1833. But that's after Haiti. And British abolition came after decades of pressure from Enlightenment thinkers and Quaker activists.

Brazil - Catholic nation - was the last to abolish slavery in the Americas. 1888.

Christians were involved in abolition. Wilberforce, Quakers. Many abolitionists were devout Christians.

But they were fighting other Christians. Using the same Bible.

For 1,400 years, the pro-slavery Christians were the majority. The Christian abolitionists were the minority challenging centuries of church teaching.
