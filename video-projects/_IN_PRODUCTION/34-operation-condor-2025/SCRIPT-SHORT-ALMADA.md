## SCRIPT

For decades, it was dismissed as paranoid fantasy.

Six South American dictatorships secretly coordinating assassinations across borders? With CIA support? Anyone who said it out loud was written off as a conspiracy theorist.

Then a schoolteacher, tortured for four years, whose wife was killed by a phone call, walked into a police station basement and found the receipts.

[B-ROLL: Map of Paraguay, Asuncion]

His name was Martin Almada. He wasn't a journalist. He wasn't a spy.

In 1974, the Stroessner dictatorship arrested him for "intellectual terrorism." They held him for nearly four years.

While he was in prison, police called his wife. They told her he was dead. Then they played recordings of him being tortured. She died from the psychological stress.

[B-ROLL: Photo of Almada if available]

After the dictatorship fell in 1989, Almada returned to Paraguay with one goal: find his file. Understand what had been done to his family and why.

A former policeman, who wanted a job for his son, drew him a map. Documents that were supposed to have been destroyed had been moved to a police station in Lambare, a suburb of Asuncion.

[B-ROLL: Map showing Lambare suburb]

Almada took the map to a judge. Judge Jose Agustin Fernandez issued a court order. They went to the station with a television crew.

The police tried to block them. There was shouting in the hallway. Officers insisted the records had been destroyed.

The judge threatened to jail them all for contempt.

[B-ROLL: Recreation or document of discovery]

They found a padlock on a door. Someone produced a crowbar. The lock broke.

What they found inside wasn't just Almada's file.

It was 593,000 pages of documentation. The bureaucratic memory of a dictatorship that believed it would never be held accountable.

Almada named it the Archives of Terror.

[B-ROLL: Photos of the archive, mountain of documents]

Mixed in with the interrogation reports and prisoner files, they found the founding charter of Operation Condor.

[SOURCE: Show founding document on screen]

Signed November 28th, 1975. The charter bore the signatures of five intelligence chiefs from Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Paraguay, and Uruguay. Brazil joined six months later.

They found the original invitation letter from Manuel Contreras, the head of Chile's secret police. And they found the stated purpose of the alliance.

[SOURCE: Quote on screen]

The founders wanted to create, in their own words, "something similar to Interpol, but dedicated to subversion."

Translation: a continental assassination network.

The documents showed prisoner exchanges between countries, coordinated kidnappings, files explicitly labeled for distribution to "Condor."

[B-ROLL: CIA document images]

And Washington knew. Declassified CIA cables show US intelligence tracked Operation Condor from the beginning and quietly supported the regimes running it.

[SOURCE: Show declassified cable on screen]

This wasn't a conspiracy theory. It was a filing system. They kept receipts for their murders.

[B-ROLL: Courtroom footage, headlines]

Once those files were public, the denials stopped working.

Paraguay had no amnesty laws. Pastor Coronel, the torturer who ran the archive, went to prison.

But the archive's evidence reached three continents.

In Spain, Judge Baltasar Garzon used the documents to arrest Augusto Pinochet in London. The architect of Operation Condor was finally facing justice.

[B-ROLL: Pinochet arrest footage]

In Argentina, courts convicted former dictator Reynaldo Bignone to 20 years. Uruguayan Colonel Manuel Cordero got 25, the first foreign official ever extradited for human rights crimes.

In Italy, courts handed down life sentences in 2021.

[B-ROLL: Trial footage, perpetrator photos]

Almada went looking for his own file. He found proof of an entire continental assassination network. And that proof is still being used in courtrooms today.

[B-ROLL: Return to Almada photo]

Martin Almada died in April 2024. He spent his final decades as a human rights lawyer, using the archives he discovered to pursue justice for other victims.

On November 28th, 2025, Operation Condor turns fifty years old. Cases are still pending. Perpetrators are still being convicted.

For years, the Archives of Terror sat in a police station basement because everyone who mattered assumed they would never be found. The men who signed those orders died believing their secrets were safe.

So the question isn't just "How could this happen?"

It's: Who is sitting on the next archive?

[B-ROLL: Modern footage, archival shelves]

They thought the files would never be found.

They were wrong.

[END CARD]

If you want to know what was in those files, the assassinations, the death flights, the CIA cables, I'm working on a full breakdown. Subscribe so you don't miss it.
