## EDITING PHILOSOPHY

**The Golden Rule:** If the B-roll doesn't strengthen the argument, stay on camera.

B-roll serves THREE purposes in this video:
1. **GEOGRAPHIC CONTEXT** - Show WHERE things happened (Paraguay, Chile, Argentina, London)
2. **DOCUMENTARY EVIDENCE** - Display primary sources on screen (founding charter, quotes)
3. **EMOTIONAL WEIGHT** - Show the scale (mountain of documents), the tension (discovery moment)

**Stay on camera when:**
- Explaining the narrative (who, what, when, why)
- Building emotional tension (wife's death, padlock moment)
- Making interpretive claims ("Translation: assassination network")

**Use B-roll when:**
- Verifying locations (map of Paraguay, Lambaré suburb)
- Showing primary documents (founding charter, invitation letter, quotes)
- Demonstrating scale (archive photos)
- Establishing historical context (courtroom footage, trial headlines)

**Target ratio:** 65% talking head, 35% B-roll

## SHOT-BY-SHOT BREAKDOWN

### SHOT 1: TALKING HEAD - Hook Opening (0:00-0:15)
**Duration: 15 sec**

> "For decades, they said it was a conspiracy theory. Six South American dictatorships secretly coordinating assassinations across borders? With CIA support? That was the claim."

**VISUAL:** Close-up talking head, eye contact with camera, neutral/serious expression

**WHY TALKING HEAD:** Establish authority and credibility. Open with direct address to camera. The claim sounds outrageous—you need to be the one making it.

**CAMERA NOTES:** Steady cam, normal eye line, conversational tone. Slight pause before "With CIA support" for emphasis.

**PACING NOTE:** This section is the hook. Deliver slowly and with conviction. The audience needs to feel the skepticism and tension before the "found the receipts" payoff.

### SHOT 2: TALKING HEAD + B-ROLL TRANSITION - The Denial (0:15-0:45)
**Duration: 30 sec**

> "And for twenty years, anyone who said it out loud was dismissed as paranoid. Then on December 22nd, 1992, in a police station basement in Paraguay, a man found the receipts."

**VISUAL:**
- Talking head (first 20 sec): Conversational delivery
- B-ROLL (transition at "Then"): MAP OF PARAGUAY + ASUNCIÓN MARKED - Show location context
- Keep map visible for last 10 seconds while narrating discovery

**WHY B-ROLL:** Geographic grounding. Viewers need to know WHERE this happened. Paraguay is not immediately familiar, so the map orients them.

**SOURCES (B-roll):**
- Search: "Map of Paraguay Asunción location"
- Canva: Create simple map with Asunción marked
- MapChart.net: Highlight Paraguay on South American map

**CAMERA NOTES:** Tone shift at "Then"—build anticipation. Slow down the delivery of "December 22nd, 1992" (specific date = authority)

### SHOT 3: TALKING HEAD - Almada Introduction (0:45-1:15)
**Duration: 30 sec**

> "His name was Martin Almada. He wasn't a journalist. He wasn't a spy. He was a schoolteacher. In 1974, the Stroessner dictatorship arrested him on charges of 'intellectual terrorism.' They tortured him for nearly four years."

**VISUAL:** Close-up talking head, serious expression, slightly slower pacing

**WHY TALKING HEAD:** You're establishing Almada as a person, not a spy or hero, but an ordinary teacher. The viewer needs to connect emotionally. Three repetitions of "He wasn't" and "He was" creates rhythm.

**CAMERA NOTES:** Emphasize "schoolteacher"—let it land. Slight pause before "They tortured him."

### SHOT 5: TALKING HEAD - After Dictatorship (1:30-2:00)
**Duration: 30 sec**

> "After the dictatorship fell in 1989, Almada returned to Paraguay with one goal. He wanted to find his file. He wanted to understand what had been done to him and his family and why. Then he got a tip."

**WHY TALKING HEAD:** Narrative momentum. You're moving from Almada's suffering to his quest. The repetition of "He wanted" creates rhythm and determination.

**CAMERA NOTES:** Build energy incrementally. "Then he got a tip" should feel like a plot twist—lean into the camera slightly, raise eyebrows.

### SHOT 7: TALKING HEAD - The Confrontation (2:20-2:50)
**Duration: 30 sec**

> "Almada took the map to a judge. Judge Jose Agustin Fernandez issued a court order and they went to the station with a television crew. The police tried to block them. There was shouting. Tension. The officers claimed the records had been destroyed. The judge threatened contempt of court."

**VISUAL:** Talking head, building dramatic tension. Lean forward slightly at "The police tried to block them."

**WHY TALKING HEAD:** Drama is built through narration and pacing, not visuals. The tension comes from your delivery and word choice (shouting, tension, threatened).

**CAMERA NOTES:** Sentences get shorter and choppier as tension builds: "There was shouting. Tension. The officers claimed..." This verbal staccato creates pace.

**PACING NOTE:** This is the peak tension moment before the discovery. Pause briefly after "The judge threatened contempt of court"—the audience should be on the edge of their seat.

### SHOT 8: B-ROLL - The Discovery Moment (2:50-3:20)
**Duration: 30 sec**

> "They found a formidable padlock on a door. Someone produced a crowbar. The lock broke. What they found inside wasn't just Almada's file. It was rooms, plural, stacked floor to ceiling with documents. Three to four tons of paper. When microfilmed later, it came to 593,000 pages. The bureaucratic memory of a dictatorship that truly believed it would never be held accountable."

**VISUAL:**
- B-ROLL: Photos/video of the Archives of Terror (mountain of documents)
- Optional recreation: Padlock/crowbar moment (if footage unavailable, use still with dramatic lighting)
- Text overlay: "593,000 Pages" or "3-4 Tons of Documents"

**WHY B-ROLL:** Show the SCALE. Talking about "three to four tons" is abstract. Seeing a room stacked floor-to-ceiling with documents makes the number visceral. This is the visual payoff to the narrative buildup.

**SOURCES (B-roll):**
- Search: "Archives of Terror Paraguay photos" or "Archivos del Terror images"
- Wikimedia Commons: Search for archives documentation photos
- Academic sources: McSherry or Dinges books often include archive photos

**CAMERA NOTES:** Narration slows down here. Each fact lands harder. "593,000 pages" should be spoken as if you can't believe the number yourself.

**GRAPHICS NEEDED:**
- Text overlay: "593,000 Pages Microfilmed"
- Text overlay: "3-4 Tons of Documents"
- Consider: Timeline graphic showing discovery date (December 22, 1992)

**WHY TALKING HEAD:** Transition from discovery story to historical significance. You're interpreting what was found, not just reporting it. This requires your presence and authority.

**CAMERA NOTES:** "Here's what matters"—this is the pivot point from narrative to analysis. Slightly slower delivery. Make eye contact.

**PACING NOTE:** After the visual splendor of the archive room, come back to you. Re-establish authority before introducing primary documents.
