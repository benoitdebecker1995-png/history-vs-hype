### Full Description

```
A documentary investigation into Operation Condor CIA documents discovered in Paraguay.
On December 22, 1992, a schoolteacher named Martín Almada found 593,000 pages of classified files in a police station basement.
Using primary sources from the Archives of Terror and declassified U.S. documents, we trace how one man's search for his own file exposed an entire continental assassination network.

In 1974, Paraguay's Stroessner dictatorship arrested Almada on charges of "intellectual terrorism." He was tortured for nearly four years. While imprisoned, police told his wife he was dead and played recordings of his torture. She died from the psychological stress.

After the regime fell in 1989, Almada returned to Paraguay with one goal: find his file and understand what had been done to his family.

On December 22, 1992, acting on a whistleblower's tip, Almada and Judge José Agustín Fernández forced their way into a Lambaré police station with a television crew. Behind a padlock, they found rooms stacked floor-to-ceiling with documents—three to four tons of paper that, when microfilmed, totaled 593,000 pages.

They called it the Archives of Terror.

Mixed in with interrogation reports and prisoner files, they found the founding charter of Operation Condor—a secret alliance formed on November 28, 1975, by five South American intelligence agencies (Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, Paraguay, Uruguay). Brazil joined as observer six months later.

The founders stated their goal, in their own words: to create "something similar to Interpol, but dedicated to subversion."

Translation: an assassination network.

The documents revealed everything: coordinated kidnappings across borders, prisoner exchanges, operational communications explicitly labeled for distribution to "Condor." This wasn't a conspiracy theory. It was a filing system. They kept receipts for their murders.

Once the files went public, the denials stopped working.

Paraguay had no amnesty laws. Pastor Coronel, the torturer who managed the archive system, went to prison. So did General Benito Guanes Serrano, who had signed the founding charter.

In 1998, Spanish judge Baltasar Garzón used documents from the Archives of Terror to arrest Augusto Pinochet in London—the Chilean dictator who had ordered the creation of Operation Condor.

In 2016, a federal court in Buenos Aires convicted 15 officers and recognized Operation Condor as a criminal conspiracy for the first time. Former Argentine dictator Reynaldo Bignone received 20 years. Uruguayan Colonel Manuel Cordero received 25 years—the first foreign official ever extradited to Argentina for human rights crimes.

Italian courts have handed down life sentences, including as recently as October 2025.

As of 2024: 107 convictions across 45 trials. 262 victims have received some measure of justice. Eleven cases remain pending in Uruguay, Italy, Argentina, Chile, and Peru.

Martín Almada died in March 2024. He spent his final decades as a human rights lawyer, using the archives he discovered to pursue justice for other victims.

On November 28, 2025, Operation Condor turns fifty years old. Cases are still pending. Perpetrators are still being convicted.

The Archives of Terror sat in that police station basement for years. Everyone involved assumed they would never be found.

They were wrong.
```
