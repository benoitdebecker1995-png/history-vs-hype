## ACT 1: THE INVOICE (1:05-3:45)

**Visual anchor:** Primary source document (1825 Ordinance)

### Context (1:05-1:35)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

In 1804, Haiti became the first nation founded by formerly enslaved people who'd overthrown their enslavers. France refused to recognize them for 21 years. The United States waited until 1862.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing Haiti's isolation]**

When French warships arrived in 1825—14 vessels carrying 528 cannons—Haiti faced a choice: accept France's terms, or face invasion.

### The Terms (1:35-2:15)

**[B-ROLL: Royal Ordinance text on screen - FRENCH TEXT]**

Here's what the ordinance actually says:

> "Les habitans actuels de la partie française de Saint-Domingue verseront... la somme de cent cinquante millions de francs, destinée à dédommager les anciens colons."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Translation: The current inhabitants shall pay 150 million francs to compensate the former colonists.

Notice the language. "Current inhabitants." Not "the state of Haiti." They made it personal—every Haitian was responsible for this debt.

### PAYOFF: The Hard Number (2:15-3:00)

**[B-ROLL: Comparison graphic]**

That was 10 times Haiti's entire annual government revenue. To put that in perspective: imagine the US being told to pay $45 trillion tomorrow. That's the scale.

Consequently, Haiti couldn't pay in cash. And this is where the trap closes: French banks—the same country demanding payment—loaned Haiti the money to pay France. At 6% to 18% interest.

**[B-ROLL: Double debt diagram]**

This is what economists call the "double debt." Haiti owed France the indemnity AND French banks the loans to pay the indemnity. Which meant that they were paying France twice.

### Two Things Became Clear (3:00-3:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

After 1825, two things became clear:

One—Haiti would be paying this debt for generations. The first installment alone—30 million francs—was more than their entire annual budget.

Two—every school, every road, every hospital that wasn't built would be a direct consequence of this extraction.

## ACT 2: THE EXTRACTION (3:45-6:30)

**Visual anchor:** Loan contracts + financial records

### The 1838 Trap (3:30-4:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

By 1838, Haiti had paid about 30 million of the original 150 million. They were falling behind.

France renegotiated. They reduced the indemnity to 90 million francs. Sounds like a win, right?

**[B-ROLL: 1838 treaty document]**

Here's the catch: to pay this "reduced" amount, Haiti had to take out another massive loan—from French banks. The Crédit Industriel et Commercial issued a new loan in 1875.

**[B-ROLL: Timeline graphic 1825-1947]**

On paper, payments would end by 1893. In reality? 1947.

One hundred and twenty-two years. Five generations paying for their ancestors' freedom.

### The Economic Damage (4:30-5:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

The New York Times investigation had economists calculate the total extraction.

**[B-ROLL: Economic chart - $560M direct, $21-115B lost growth]**

Direct payments over 122 years: approximately $560 million in 2022 dollars.

But that's just money that left Haiti. The bigger question: what did Haiti NOT build?

**[B-ROLL: Budget breakdown graphic]**

By 1898, fully half of Haiti's government budget went to paying France and French banks. By 1914, that proportion had climbed to 80 percent.

Eighty percent. That's not an economy. That's an extraction zone.

As a result, education, healthcare, and infrastructure went practically unfunded throughout the nineteenth century.

The Times commissioned 15 economists to model what Haiti's economy might have looked like without this burden. Their estimate: between $21 billion and $115 billion in lost economic growth.

Now—counterfactual modeling isn't settled science. These are estimates. But even the low number represents massive structural damage.

**Verification Reference:** ✅ Lines 122-158, 190-200 from 01-VERIFIED-RESEARCH.md

### The Eiffel Tower Detail (5:30-6:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Here's a detail that tends to get attention.

The Crédit Industriel et Commercial—the French bank managing Haiti's debt—used that steady income to finance other projects.

**[B-ROLL: Eiffel Tower + CIC bank logo]**

At a time when typical French investments returned about 5 percent, investors in Haiti's debt cleared an average of 15 percent per year. Three times the market rate.

And some of those profits went toward financing the Eiffel Tower.

France's most famous monument, partially funded by payments from formerly enslaved people buying their freedom. That's documented financial history.

**Verification Reference:** ✅ Lines 165-172, 209-211 from 01-VERIFIED-RESEARCH.md

But was Haiti's case actually unique? The answer requires looking at how Britain handled the same problem—and it's not what you'd expect.

## ACT 3: THE UNIQUENESS (6:30-8:00)

**Visual anchor:** Comparative table (Haiti vs. Britain)

### The British Comparison (6:30-7:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

You might say: "Britain also had slavery compensation."

Yes. In 1833, Britain abolished slavery throughout its empire and paid £20 million in compensation—about 5% of the national income at the time.

**[B-ROLL: British compensation diagram - money flowing TO slaveholders]**

But here's the critical difference.

Unlike Britain—which paid £20 million to compensate slaveholders, meaning the enslaved paid nothing—France flipped the script entirely.

**[B-ROLL: Haiti comparison - money flowing FROM formerly enslaved]**

In Haiti, the people who had been enslaved were forced to pay their former enslavers for the "loss" of their own bodies. Thereby making Haiti the only country in history where the enslaved compensated their enslavers.

### The Academic Framing (7:15-7:45)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

This is why Marlene Daut—professor at the University of Virginia, co-winner of the Frederick Douglass Book Prize—called it "the greatest heist in history."

Not because of the amount. Because of who was forced to pay whom.

This debt structure—borrowing from French banks to pay French creditors—would define Haiti's relationship with international finance for over a century. And to some extent, it shapes perceptions of Haiti still to this day.

**Verification Reference:** ✅ Lines 180-195 from 01-VERIFIED-RESEARCH.md

## ACT 4: THE MODERN DEBATE (7:45-9:30)

**Visual anchor:** Modern news footage + political speeches

### Aristide's Demand (7:45-8:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

In 2003, Haitian President Jean-Bertrand Aristide made a formal demand: France should repay what Haiti was forced to pay, with interest.

His calculation: $21.68 billion.

**[B-ROLL: 2003 news headlines]**

France's response? They called it "unacceptable" and demanded an apology for raising the issue.

One year later, Aristide was overthrown in a coup. French forces were among those who escorted him out. I'm not drawing a causal connection—the politics were complicated—but the timing is notable.

**Verification Reference:** ✅ Lines 202-215 from 01-VERIFIED-RESEARCH.md

### Current Position (8:30-9:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

France's official stance has been consistent: they acknowledge the debt was unjust, but won't pay reparations.

**[B-ROLL: Hollande speech footage]**

Hollande in 2015 spoke of France's "moral debt"—but offered development projects, not cash.

**[B-ROLL: Macron Caribbean visit]**

Macron in 2023 acknowledged the historical injustice but stopped short of promising repayment.

Meanwhile, economist Thomas Piketty has argued France should pay €40 billion.

**Verification Reference:** ✅ Lines 222-235 from 01-VERIFIED-RESEARCH.md

## ACT 5: STEELMAN + CONCLUSION (9:15-12:00)

**Visual anchor:** Academic source (Lundahl book cover + key quotes)

### [ONSCREEN TEXT: "Steelmanning the 'Internal Factors' Argument"]

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Economist Mats Lundahl spent decades studying Haiti's economy. His conclusion: the indemnity was burdensome, but it wasn't the sole cause of Haiti's underdevelopment.

**[B-ROLL: Show Lundahl book or academic paper]**

His research points to internal factors: the destruction of plantation infrastructure during the revolution, the shift to subsistence farming, political instability, concentration of wealth among a small elite.

These problems would have existed regardless of French demands.

### The Synthesis (10:00-10:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

And he's right. Haiti's challenges are overdetermined—multiple causes, each sufficient to cause problems on their own.

But here's what Lundahl doesn't dispute: the debt made everything worse. It drained capital that could have gone to schools, roads, ports. It forced Haiti to prioritize French payments over domestic investment for over a century.

**Verification Reference:** ✅ Lines 245-260 from 01-VERIFIED-RESEARCH.md

### Return to Both Extremes (10:30-11:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

So. Back to our two extremes.

Extreme one: Haiti's poverty is entirely France's fault. Too simple. The Duvalier dictatorships, US interference, internal choices—all contributed.

Extreme two: the debt was paid off long ago, it's all about corruption now. That ignores 122 years of documented extraction and the fact that the last payment was in 1947—not ancient history.

**[TALKING HEAD - closer framing]**

The truth requires holding two ideas simultaneously: France did something historically unprecedented. AND Haiti's leaders made choices that compounded the damage.

Both things can be true. The documents show what France demanded. History shows what Haiti endured.

### Closing (11:00-11:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

The 1825 ordinance isn't a metaphor. It's a document. You can read it.

**[B-ROLL: Final shot of 1825 document]**

And when someone tells you Haiti's poverty is just about corruption, or when someone tells you it's entirely about colonial extraction—ask them if they've read the invoice.

Because the documents don't lie.
