## ACT 1: THE INVOICE (1:40-4:00)

**Visual anchor:** Primary source document (1825 Ordinance)

### The Defiance (1:40-2:10)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

In 1804, Haiti became the first nation founded by formerly enslaved people who'd overthrown their enslavers. They'd defeated Napoleon's army. Thirty thousand French soldiers died trying to re-enslave them.

For 21 years, France refused to recognize Haiti. The United States waited until 1862.

**[B-ROLL: Boyer proclamation text]**

By 1824, Haitian President Jean-Pierre Boyer was preparing for war. His proclamation to the army:

> "You will be determined to bury yourselves under the ruins of our country, rather than submit to the ignominious yoke of the tyrants who would claim to enslave us."

**[Short declarative]**

That was January 1824. Eighteen months later, everything changed.

### The Threat (2:10-2:30)

**[B-ROLL: Map showing French fleet approach]**

July 3rd, 1825. Fourteen French warships. 528 cannons. Baron de Mackau carrying secret instructions: if Haiti refused, announce a blockade until the island "submitted itself, without any conditions, to the supremacy of France."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

The threat wasn't just war. It was re-enslavement.

Boyer had a choice: accept France's terms, or watch everything his people had won be destroyed.

### The Terms - Article 2 (2:30-3:00)

**[B-ROLL: Royal Ordinance text on screen - FRENCH TEXT]**

Here's what Article 2 actually says:

> "Les habitans actuels de la partie française de Saint-Domingue verseront... la somme de cent cinquante millions de francs, destinée à dédommager les anciens colons."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Translation: The current inhabitants shall pay 150 million francs to compensate the former colonists.

Notice the language. "Current inhabitants." Not "the state of Haiti." They made it personal—every Haitian was responsible for this debt.

And "former colonists"? That meant slaveholders. Haiti was being billed for the "loss" of human property.

**[Short declarative]**

That was 10 times Haiti's entire annual revenue. But there was a second clause.

### The Terms - Article 1: The Hidden Knife (3:00-3:30)

**[B-ROLL: Article 1 text on screen - FRENCH TEXT]**

**[V.O.]**

Article 1:

> "Les droits perçus dans ces ports... seront égaux et uniformes pour tous les pavillons, excepté le pavillon français, en faveur duquel ces droits seront réduits de moitié."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Translation: All tariffs will be equal for every nation—except France, which gets a 50% reduction.

**[B-ROLL: Trade diagram showing money flows]**

Historian Frédérique Beauvois calls this the "double indemnity"—Haiti paid France in cash AND in trade preferences.

Before 1825, Britain had favorable trade terms with Haiti. Article 1 destroyed that. France got most-favored-nation status by force.

**[Short declarative]**

So Haiti was paying twice: in francs AND in crippled trade revenue. That's not a treaty. That's a trap.

### Boyer's Capitulation (3:30-4:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

On July 11th, 1825, Boyer accepted. But listen to how he announced it:

**[B-ROLL: Boyer's proclamation text]**

> "The French flag, in coming to salute this land of liberty, consecrates on this day the legitimacy of your emancipation."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

"Consecrates your emancipation." He framed extortion as liberation.

The details were locked away in a velvet case. Boyer never publicly shared what he'd agreed to.

**[Short declarative]**

From "bury yourselves under the ruins" to "consecrated liberty" in 18 months. That's what 528 cannons can do.

## ACT 2: THE MECHANISM (4:15-7:00)

**Visual anchor:** Loan contracts + financial records

### Mechanism Forensics Introduction (4:15-4:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Here's how France's extraction system actually worked. Not in theory—in documented practice. Step by step.

### Step 1: The Immediate Trap (4:30-4:50)

**[B-ROLL: Payment schedule graphic]**

The first installment—30 million francs—was due December 31st, 1825. That was more than Haiti's entire annual budget.

Haiti borrowed from a French bank: Ternaux Grandolphe et Cie. The bank only provided 24 million francs after fees—but required repayment of the full 30 million.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Within months of "independence," Haiti owed France the indemnity AND French banks the loans to pay the indemnity.

**[Short declarative]**

This is what economists call the "double debt." Haiti was paying France twice.

### Step 2: The Refinancing Trap (4:50-5:20)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

By 1838, Haiti had paid about 30 million of the original 150 million. They were drowning.

France renegotiated. They reduced the total to 90 million francs.

**[B-ROLL: 1838 treaty document]**

Sounds like mercy, right?

Here's the catch: to pay this "reduced" amount, Haiti had to take out another massive loan. From another French bank. The Crédit Industriel et Commercial.

**[Short declarative]**

The reduction was a refinancing. The debt continued.

### Step 3: It Gets Far Worse (5:20-5:50)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

On paper, payments would end by 1893.

In reality? 1947.

**[B-ROLL: Timeline graphic 1825-1947]**

One hundred and twenty-two years. That's longer than the United States has had income tax. Longer than most countries in the UN have existed.

From the Monroe Doctrine to the Marshall Plan.

**[Beat]**

But the payments weren't the worst part.

### Step 4: The Budget Stranglehold (5:50-6:20)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

The New York Times investigation tracked what happened to Haiti's government budget.

**[B-ROLL: Budget breakdown graphic - animated]**

By 1898: Fifty percent of Haiti's entire budget went to paying France and French banks.

By 1914: Eighty percent.

**[Short declarative]**

Eighty percent. That's not an economy. That's an extraction zone.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Every school not built. Every road not paved. Every hospital not funded. That was money going to Paris.

From 1849 to 1915, French creditors extracted an average of 5% of Haiti's entire national income. Every year. For 66 years.

### The Eiffel Tower Detail (6:20-7:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Here's a detail that tends to get attention.

The Crédit Industriel et Commercial—the bank managing Haiti's debt—used that steady income to finance other projects.

**[B-ROLL: Eiffel Tower + CIC bank logo + construction dates 1887-1889]**

At a time when typical French investments returned about 5 percent, investors in Haiti's debt cleared 15 percent per year.

Some years, the margins approached 24 percent.

**[B-ROLL: Profit comparison graphic]**

The profits from Haiti sometimes exceeded Haiti's entire public works budget.

And some of those profits helped finance the Eiffel Tower.

**[Short declarative]**

France's most famous monument. Built while Haiti was paying to exist.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

The bank made no investments in Haitian businesses. They just extracted and transferred.

That's documented financial history. Nearly all of C.I.C.'s archives from that era have been destroyed—but the Times reconstructed the payments from thousands of pages of documents across three continents.

**FACT-BASED BRIDGE:**

So was this unique? Or did other empires do the same thing?

## ACT 3: THE UNIQUENESS (7:00-8:30)

**Visual anchor:** Comparative table (Haiti vs. Britain)

### The British Comparison (7:00-7:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

You might say: "Britain also had slavery compensation."

You're right. In 1833, Britain abolished slavery throughout its empire and paid £20 million in compensation—about 5% of the national income at the time.

**[B-ROLL: British compensation diagram - money flowing TO slaveholders FROM government]**

But notice the direction of the arrows.

Britain's taxpayers paid slaveholders. The enslaved paid nothing.

**[B-ROLL: Haiti comparison - money flowing FROM formerly enslaved TO slaveholders]**

France flipped the script entirely. The people who had been enslaved paid their former enslavers for the "loss" of their own bodies.

**[Short declarative]**

Thereby making Haiti the only country in history where the enslaved compensated their enslavers.

### French Dissent (7:30-8:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Even at the time, some French voices recognized the obscenity.

**[B-ROLL: Show Schoelcher portrait or text]**

Victor Schoelcher—France's most famous abolitionist—wrote:

> "To impose an indemnity on slaves who conquered their masters is to make them pay with money what they already paid for with their blood."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

And here's the part that rarely gets mentioned.

**[B-ROLL: Dalloz Report document or text]**

In 1829, the French government commissioned its own legal experts—including Désiré Dalloz—to review the 1825 Ordinance.

Their conclusion: the ordinance was unlawful. France itself was liable for the colonists' losses, not Haiti.

**[Short declarative]**

France's own lawyers said it was illegal. France collected anyway.

### The Academic Framing (8:00-8:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

This is why Marlene Daut—professor at the University of Virginia, winner of the Frederick Douglass Book Prize—called it "the greatest heist in history."

Not because of the amount. Because of who was forced to pay whom.

This debt structure—borrowing from French banks to pay French creditors—defined Haiti's relationship with international finance for 122 years.

And to some extent, it shapes perceptions of Haiti still to this day.

**FACT-BASED BRIDGE:**

So what happened when Haiti asked for this money back?

## ACT 4: THE MODERN DEBATE (8:30-10:00)

**Visual anchor:** Modern news footage + political speeches

### Aristide's Demand (8:30-9:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

In 2003, Haitian President Jean-Bertrand Aristide made a formal demand.

France should repay what Haiti was forced to pay. With interest.

His calculation: $21.68 billion.

**[B-ROLL: 2003 news headlines]**

France's response? They called it "unacceptable" and demanded an apology for raising the issue.

When asked directly, a French Foreign Ministry spokesman said: "This case has been closed since 1885."

**[Beat]**

One year later, Aristide was overthrown in a coup. French forces were among those who escorted him out.

I'm not drawing a causal connection—the politics were complicated—but the timing is notable.

**[Short declarative]**

The new government dropped the reparations claim within a week.

### Current Position (9:15-9:45)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

France's official stance has been consistent: they acknowledge the debt was unjust. But they won't pay reparations.

**[B-ROLL: Hollande speech footage]**

Hollande in 2015: France owed Haiti a "moral debt"—but he said it was "out of the question even to consider any kind of financial or monetary reparations."

Moral debt. Not actual debt.

**[B-ROLL: Macron Caribbean visit]**

Macron acknowledged the historical injustice but stopped short of promising repayment.

Meanwhile, economist Thomas Piketty has argued France should pay €40 billion.

### The Economic Estimates (9:45-10:00)

The New York Times commissioned economists to calculate the damage.

**[B-ROLL: Economic chart]**

Direct payments over 122 years: approximately $560 million in 2022 dollars.

Lost economic growth: between $21 billion and $115 billion.

**[Short declarative]**

Counterfactual modeling isn't settled science. But even the low number represents massive structural damage.

## ACT 5: STEELMAN + CONCLUSION (10:00-13:00)

**Visual anchor:** Academic source + Duvalier footage

### Grain of Truth Acknowledgment (10:00-10:45)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Economist Mats Lundahl spent decades studying Haiti's economy. His conclusion: the indemnity was burdensome, but it wasn't the sole cause of Haiti's underdevelopment.

As with any complex historical debate, there's a grain of truth to this.

**[B-ROLL: Show Lundahl book]**

His research points to internal factors: the destruction of plantation infrastructure during the revolution, the shift to subsistence farming, political instability, concentration of wealth among a small elite.

These problems would have existed regardless of French demands.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

And then there's the Duvaliers.

**[B-ROLL: Duvalier footage]**

François and Jean-Claude Duvalier ruled Haiti from 1957 to 1986. A U.S. Department of Commerce study found that 63 percent of all recorded government revenue was being "misappropriated" each year.

**[B-ROLL: Wealth comparison graphic]**

By 1984, Jean-Claude Duvalier's personal fortune was estimated at $450 million. His mother's? $1.2 billion—more than Haiti's entire Gross National Product that year.

**[Short declarative]**

Combined: $1.65 billion. That's three times more than Haiti paid France directly over 122 years.

### Personal Interpretation (10:45-11:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Some scholars present this as an either/or. Either the debt caused Haiti's poverty, or internal factors did.

I don't see it that way.

Haiti's challenges are overdetermined—multiple causes, each sufficient to cause problems on their own.

But here's what Lundahl doesn't dispute: the debt made everything worse. It drained capital that could have gone to schools, roads, ports. It forced Haiti to prioritize French payments over domestic investment for over a century.

**[Short declarative]**

The debt didn't cause everything. But it was foundational.

And the Duvaliers? They came 130 years after the 1825 ordinance. By then, the damage was already structural.

### Return to Both Extremes (11:15-12:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

So. Back to our two extremes.

Extreme one: Haiti's poverty is entirely France's fault.

Too simple. The Duvalier dictatorships stole three times what France extracted directly. US interference, the 2010 earthquake, internal corruption—all contributed. Blaming everything on France erases Haitian agency and ignores 200 years of complex history.

Extreme two: The debt was paid off long ago. It's all about corruption now.

Also too simple. That ignores 122 years of documented extraction, $560 million in direct payments, 80% of the national budget going to Paris, and the fact that the last check cleared in 1947—within living memory.

**[TALKING HEAD - closer framing]**

The truth requires holding two ideas simultaneously.

France did something historically unprecedented—forcing the enslaved to pay their enslavers for freedom, extracting 80% of Haiti's budget for decades, making profits that exceeded Haiti's public works spending.

Even France's own lawyers said it was illegal. France collected anyway.

AND Haiti's leaders—from Boyer's capitulation to the Duvaliers' theft—made choices that compounded the damage.

Both things can be true. The documents show what France demanded. History shows what Haiti endured.

The 1825 ordinance isn't a metaphor. It's a document. You can read it.

**[B-ROLL: Final shot of 1825 document, slow zoom to Article 2]**

One hundred fifty million francs. Payable in five installments. To compensate the former colonists.

And Article 1: Half-price tariffs for France. Forever.

**[B-ROLL: Boyer's two proclamations side by side - 1824 defiance vs 1825 acceptance]**

Jean-Pierre Boyer went from "bury yourselves under the ruins" to "consecrated liberty" in 18 months.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

And when someone tells you Haiti's poverty is just about corruption—ask them if they've read the invoice.

When someone tells you it's entirely about colonial extraction—ask them about the Duvaliers.

The real story isn't either extreme. It's in the documents.
