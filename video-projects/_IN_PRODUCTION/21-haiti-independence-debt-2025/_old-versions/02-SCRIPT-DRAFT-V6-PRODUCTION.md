### The Defiance (1:45-2:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

In 1804, Haiti became the first nation founded by formerly enslaved people who'd overthrown their enslavers. They'd defeated Napoleon's army. Thirty thousand French soldiers died trying to re-enslave them.

For 21 years, France refused to recognize Haiti. The United States waited until 1862.

**[B-ROLL: Boyer proclamation text]**

By 1824, Haitian President Jean-Pierre Boyer was preparing for war. His proclamation to the army:

> "You will be determined to bury yourselves under the ruins of our country, rather than submit to the ignominious yoke of the tyrants who would claim to enslave us."

**[Short declarative]**

That was January 1824. Eighteen months later, everything changed.

### The Secret Instructions (2:15-3:00)

**[B-ROLL: Map showing French fleet approach + document overlay]**

July 3rd, 1825. Fourteen French warships. 528 cannons. Baron de Mackau carrying secret instructions from the Ministry of the Marine.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

I'm going to show you what those instructions actually said.

**[B-ROLL: Text on screen - FRENCH then ENGLISH]**

Mackau wrote to his superiors:

> "My mission to the people of our former colony is not a negotiation... I am limited to explaining to them on what conditions His Majesty consents to grant their independence. I lack the powers to make the slightest modification."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

This wasn't diplomacy. This was delivery of an ultimatum.

**[B-ROLL: Next section of instructions]**

And if Haiti refused?

> "Announce to the leaders of that government that henceforth they will be treated as enemies of France."

The instructions specified a blockade that would continue until the island had—and this is the exact wording—"submitted itself, without any conditions, to the supremacy of France."

**[Short declarative]**

Not recognition. Submission.

**[B-ROLL: Payment schedule graphic]**

The first installment—30 million francs—was due December 31st, 1825. That was more than Haiti's entire annual budget.

Haiti borrowed from a French bank: Ternaux Grandolphe et Cie. The bank only provided 24 million francs after fees—but required repayment of the full 30 million.

**[B-ROLL: Quote on screen]**

**[Short declarative]**

**[TALKING HEAD]**

**[B-ROLL: Show Farnham / National City Bank logo]**

Farnham told Secretary of State William Jennings Bryan that Haiti's political system "constitutes a certain form of slavery for the masses." His solution? American control of Haitian finances.
