## ACT 1: THE SETUP (1:30-5:30)

**[ON SCREEN TEXT: "THE SETUP"]**

### The Target (1:30-2:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

In 1804, Haiti became the first nation founded by people who'd overthrown their enslavers. They'd beaten Napoleon's army. Thirty thousand French soldiers died trying.

For 21 years, France refused to accept it. Neither did the United States.

**[B-ROLL: Boyer proclamation text]**

By 1824, Haitian President Jean-Pierre Boyer was preparing for war. His proclamation to the army:

> "You will be determined to bury yourselves under the ruins of our country, rather than submit to the ignominious yoke of the tyrants."

**[Short declarative]**

That was January 1824. Eighteen months later, Boyer folded.

What changed? Fourteen warships showed up.

### The Enforcers (2:00-2:45)

**[B-ROLL: Map showing French fleet approach + document overlay]**

**[ON SCREEN: "JULY 3, 1825 - THE ENFORCERS ARRIVE"]**

July 3rd, 1825. Fourteen French warships. 528 cannons. Baron de Mackau carrying sealed orders from Paris.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Here's what those orders actually said.

**[B-ROLL: Text on screen - FRENCH then ENGLISH]**

Mackau wrote to his superiors:

> "My mission to the people of our former colony is not a negotiation... I am limited to explaining to them on what conditions His Majesty consents to grant their independence."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

This wasn't diplomacy. Mackau was a bagman delivering an ultimatum.

**[B-ROLL: Next section of instructions]**

And if Haiti refused?

> "Announce to the leaders of that government that henceforth they will be treated as enemies of France."

The instructions specified a blockade until the island had—exact wording—"submitted itself, without any conditions, to the supremacy of France."

**[Short declarative]**

Not recognition. Submission.

### The Commission Says No (2:45-3:05)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Boyer appointed a commission of advisers to review the terms. Their response was immediate.

**[B-ROLL: Commission document or text]**

According to historian Laurent Dubois, "The members of the commission, shocked by what they read, rejected the document immediately and categorically."

**[Short declarative]**

The experts said no. Boyer said yes anyway.

That's what makes him the inside man.

### The Inside Man (3:05-4:00)

**[ON SCREEN TEXT: "THE INSIDE MAN"]**

**[TALKING HEAD]**

On July 7th, Boyer convened a secret council—senators and generals. But here's the critical detail.

**[B-ROLL: Show Boyer's questions on screen - translated]**

He never showed them the actual French document.

Instead, he presented three questions he'd written himself:

**[B-ROLL: Question 1 on screen]**

First: "Can the King of France reserve half-tariffs for French commerce?"

**[B-ROLL: Question 2 on screen]**

Second: "Could France use this against future trade deals?"

**[B-ROLL: Question 3 on screen]**

Third: "Since independence is finally being recognized, wouldn't rejecting this risk losing the only chance to conclude this great affair?"

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Notice how Question 3 is framed. Not "should we accept extortion?" but "can we afford to miss this opportunity?"

According to Beaubrun Ardouin—a 19th-century Haitian historian who had access to participants—Boyer "was able to induce them to think" the terms were similar to earlier negotiations.

He manipulated his own council.

**[Short declarative]**

Boyer was the inside man. He didn't share the terms. He manufactured consent.

### The Terms (4:00-4:45)

**[B-ROLL: Royal Ordinance text on screen - FRENCH TEXT]**

**[ON SCREEN: "THE RANSOM"]**

Here's what Article 2 actually says:

> "Les habitans actuels de la partie française de Saint-Domingue verseront... la somme de cent cinquante millions de francs."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Translation: 150 million francs to compensate the former colonists.

**[B-ROLL: Claims statistics graphic + MONEY TRAIL ARROW: Haiti → French colonists]**

France established a commission to process the claims. They approved 25,838 colonists or their descendants.

**[B-ROLL: Text showing "biens immeubles"]**

And the basis for calculating payouts? The formerly enslaved were classified as *biens immeubles*—immovable property. Like land. Like buildings.

**[Short declarative]**

France itemized human beings to calculate the "loss."

### The Handoff (4:45-5:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

On July 11th, Boyer accepted. The Haitian Senate heard the terms read aloud—then the document was locked in a velvet case.

**[B-ROLL: Boyer's proclamation text]**

Boyer's public spin:

> "The French flag, in coming to salute this land of liberty, consecrates on this day the legitimacy of your emancipation."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

It took months for Haitians to learn the truth—when newspapers from France finally arrived describing the terms.

**[B-ROLL: American Monitor quote on screen]**

The American Monitor—a newspaper at the time—offered a devastating assessment:

> "By purchasing the acknowledgment of what was before an undisputed fact, [Boyer] has afforded room to doubt that Haiti had the means of keeping that which she had begged herself to buy."

**[Short declarative]**

From "bury yourselves under the ruins" to "consecrated liberty" in 18 months. That's what 528 cannons and one inside man can do.

**[Beat]**

Some historians argue Boyer had no choice—that France would have destroyed Haiti's economy with a blockade. Others say he could have called France's bluff. The documents show what he chose.

### The Toast (5:15-5:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

At the celebration banquet, a Haitian Senator proposed a toast to Abbé Grégoire—a French bishop who had been one of the few voices in France defending Haiti's independence for decades.

The French officers set down their glasses with "visible disapproval." They wouldn't toast a man who had opposed the Bourbon monarchy—the royal family that had been restored to power after Napoleon's defeat.

**[Short declarative]**

The Haitians drank with "noisy enthusiasm" anyway.

Even in surrender, they knew who their real friends were.

## ACT 2: THE TAKE (6:45-10:00)

**[ON SCREEN TEXT: "THE TAKE"]**

### How the Extraction Worked (6:45-7:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Here's how France's system actually worked. Not the theory—the documented mechanics. Step by step.

### Step 1: The Trap (7:00-7:25)

**[B-ROLL: Payment schedule graphic + MONEY TRAIL ARROWS]**

**[ON SCREEN: "STEP 1: THE TRAP"]**

The first installment—30 million francs—was due December 31st, 1825. That was more than Haiti's entire annual budget.

Haiti didn't have the cash. So they borrowed from Ternaux Grandolphe—a French bank. The bank provided only 24 million after fees—but required repayment of the full 30 million.

**[B-ROLL: Quote on screen + MONEY TRAIL: Haiti → French Bank → French Treasury]**

As historian Anthony Phillips documents: "The remaining 24,000,000 francs made its way from the bank's vaults to the French treasury—a short trip across Paris that began Haiti's long spiral into impoverishment."

**[Short declarative]**

Within months of "independence," Haiti owed the ransom AND the loans to pay the ransom. Economists call this the "double debt."

### Step 2: The Refinancing (7:25-7:50)

**[ON SCREEN: "STEP 2: THE REFINANCING"]**

**[TALKING HEAD]**

By 1838, Haiti had paid about 30 million of the original 150 million. They were drowning.

France renegotiated. Reduced the total to 90 million francs.

**[B-ROLL: 1838 treaty document]**

Sounds like mercy, right?

Here's the catch: to pay the "reduced" amount, Haiti had to take out another massive loan. From another French bank—the Crédit Industriel et Commercial.

**[Short declarative]**

The reduction was a refinancing. The extraction continued.

### Step 3: The Squeeze (7:50-8:20)

**[ON SCREEN: "STEP 3: THE SQUEEZE"]**

**[TALKING HEAD]**

On paper, payments would end by 1893.

In reality? 1947.

**[B-ROLL: Timeline graphic 1825-1947]**

One hundred and twenty-two years. From the Monroe Doctrine—America's 1823 declaration that the Western Hemisphere was off-limits to European powers—to the Marshall Plan—America's post-WWII reconstruction of Europe.

**[B-ROLL: Budget percentage graphic - animated]**

By 1914: Eighty percent.

**[Short declarative]**

Eighty percent. That's not an economy. That's an extraction zone.

And then the Americans showed up.

The USS Machias dropped anchor in Port-au-Prince harbor. US Marines walked into the Banque Nationale d'Haïti, removed $500,000 in gold from the vaults—$11 million today—and shipped it to New York.

And here's the inside man who made this possible.

**[B-ROLL: Show Farnham / National City Bank logo]**

**[ON SCREEN: "THE INSIDE MAN - ROGER FARNHAM"]**

Roger Farnham was simultaneously vice president of National City Bank in New York—the predecessor to Citibank—AND vice president of the Banque Nationale d'Haïti.

**[B-ROLL: MONEY TRAIL: Haiti → Banque Nationale → National City Bank (same guy on both ends)]**

The gold seizure was a transfer between his two institutions.

**[B-ROLL: Quote on screen]**

Farnham told Secretary of State William Jennings Bryan that Haiti's political system "constitutes a certain form of slavery for the masses." His solution? American control of Haitian finances.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

By 1922, National City Bank had acquired all of Haiti's outstanding debts to French banks.

The extraction system stayed the same. Only the beneficiary changed.

**[B-ROLL: Smedley Butler quote on screen]**

That's not my interpretation. That's his confession.
