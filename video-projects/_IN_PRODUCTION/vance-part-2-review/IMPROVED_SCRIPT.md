### SYLLABUS [1:45-2:30]

**SHOW:** Syllabus document

**SAY:**
"32 years later - 1864 - Pope Pius IX goes further.

Syllabus of Errors. A numbered list of 80 'modern ideas' that are condemned.

Error 15: 'Every man is free to embrace and profess that religion which, guided by the light of reason, he shall consider true.' Condemned.

Error 77: 'In the present day it is no longer expedient that the Catholic religion should be held as the only religion of the State, to the exclusion of all other forms of worship.' Condemned.

Error 78: 'Hence it has been wisely decided by law, in some Catholic countries, that persons coming to reside therein shall enjoy the public exercise of their own peculiar worship.' Condemned.

1864. One year before the Civil War ends. The Catholic Church is officially condemning religious freedom as an error."

For 300 years after the Reformation, Protestant states enforced religious conformity just like Catholic ones."

## "MOST MORAL CIVILIZATION" (7:15-12:00)

### CATHOLIC BULLS [7:15-8:00]

**SAY:**
"1452. Pope Nicholas V issues a papal bull - official decree - called Dum Diversas.

Addressed to the King of Portugal. Reading directly from the Latin translation:

The Pope grants Portugal 'full and free permission to invade, search out, capture, and subjugate the Saracens and pagans and any other unbelievers...and to reduce their persons to perpetual slavery.'

Perpetual slavery. Authorized by the Pope. 1452."

**SHOW:** Document - zoom on "perpetual slavery"

**SAY:**
"Three years later - 1455 - Pope Nicholas V issues Romanus Pontifex. Specifically authorizes the Portuguese slave trade along the West African coast.

1493 - one year after Columbus - Pope Alexander VI issues Inter Caetera. Divides the entire 'New World' between Spain and Portugal. Authorizes conquest and 'reduction to the faith.'

These aren't obscure documents. They're the foundation of European colonialism. And they explicitly authorize slavery."

**SHOW:** Timeline - 1452, 1455, 1493, 1804, 1888

### PROTESTANT POSITIONS [8:00-8:45]

**SAY:**
"Maybe that's just Catholics. What about Protestants?

Martin Luther. 1520s. Says serfdom and slavery are part of God's order. Calls slaves 'tools with voice.' Says rebellion against masters is rebellion against God.

John Calvin. Institutes of the Christian Religion. Doesn't condemn slavery. Says social hierarchy is divinely ordained.

Southern Presbyterian theologian James Henley Thornwell. 1850s. Says slavery is 'the condition in which infinite wisdom has been pleased to place him' - meaning enslaved people.

For 1,400 years - from Augustine to the American Civil War - Catholic and Protestant theologians defended slavery using Christian doctrine."

**SHOW:** Split screen - Luther quote / Thornwell quote

### THEOLOGY [8:45-9:30]

**SAY:**
"These weren't fringe figures.

Augustine. City of God, Book XIX, Chapter 15. Argues slavery is divine punishment for sin. Says it's part of preserving God's natural order.

Thomas Aquinas. Summa Theologica. Says slavery wasn't part of God's original plan. But then argues it was 'devised by human reason for the benefit of human life.'

Different reasoning. Same conclusion: slavery is justified.

These are the two most influential theologians in Christian history. Texts at newadvent.org - linked below.

Southern Baptist Convention? Founded in 1845 specifically to defend slavery. Methodist Church split over it in 1844. Presbyterian Church split in 1861."

### WHO ABOLISHED FIRST [9:30-10:30]

**SHOW:** Map - Haiti

**SAY:**
"Who abolished slavery first in the Americas?

Haiti. 1804.

Slave revolt. Led by Toussaint Louverture and Jean-Jacques Dessalines. They defeat the French army. Declare independence. Immediately abolish slavery. Forever.

First nation in the Western Hemisphere to do it. Only nation in history founded by a successful slave revolt.

They did it by *defeating* three Christian empires. France, Britain, Spain. Who were trying to keep them enslaved.

Britain abolishes slavery in 1833. But that's *after* Haiti. And British abolition came after decades of pressure from Enlightenment thinkers and Quaker activists.

Brazil - Catholic nation - was the last to abolish slavery in the Americas. 1888."

### CHRISTIAN ABOLITIONISTS [10:30-11:30]

**SAY:**
"Christians were involved in abolition. Wilberforce in Britain. Quakers in America. Many abolitionists were devout Christians.

But here's what matters: they were fighting *other Christians*. Using the *same Bible*.

Southern Baptist Convention defended slavery using Scripture. Methodist Church defended slavery using Scripture. Presbyterian Church defended slavery using Scripture.

They split their denominations over it. These weren't small debates. Millions of Christians on each side.

And here's the thing - for 1,400 years, the pro-slavery Christians were the majority. The Christian abolitionists were the minority challenging centuries of church teaching.

When abolition finally came, it came through Enlightenment philosophy, slave revolts, and economic pressure. Not from dominant Christian theology changing its mind.

After the Civil War, many of those same churches spent another century defending segregation. Using the same theological arguments about natural hierarchy and divine order."

### VERDICT [11:30-12:00]

**SAY:**
"Is Christian civilization the most moral in history? By what metric?

1,400 years of theological defense of slavery. Papal bulls authorizing it. Protestant reformers defending it. Major American denominations splitting to defend it in the 1840s.

Christian individuals fought slavery. But they were a minority fighting the dominant Christian position for over a millennium.

Why does any of this matter in 2025?"
