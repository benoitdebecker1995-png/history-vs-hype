**Visual:** B-roll - Communist Manifesto and Das Kapital excerpts on screen

**Script:**
"Let's start with what Karl Marx actually defined as communism.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Communist Manifesto, 1848]

In the Communist Manifesto, Marx describes communism as the end stage of historical development. The working class overthrows the capitalist class, establishes a temporary 'dictatorship of the proletariat' to reorganize society, and then—critically—the state withers away.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Das Kapital, Vol. 1]

The final stage is a stateless, classless society where workers collectively own the means of production. No government. No ruling class. No private ownership of factories or land.

Marx called this inevitable. He believed capitalism would collapse under its own contradictions, workers would revolt, and this communist society would emerge.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Key characteristics]

So according to Marx: No state. No classes. Collective ownership. Governed by the principle 'from each according to ability, to each according to need.'

That's the theory Marx laid out in the 1840s-1860s. Now here's what the Soviet Union actually built."

### SECTION 4: PRIMARY SOURCE 2 - USSR Implementation (3:30-4:45) [~135 words]

**Visual:** B-roll - Soviet Constitution, economic data, maps

**Script:**
"The Soviet Union called itself communist. But look at what it actually implemented.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Soviet Constitution, 1936 (Stalin Constitution)]

The 1936 Soviet Constitution established a centralized state under the Communist Party. The party controlled all economic planning, all political decisions, all media. This wasn't temporary—it was the permanent structure.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Economic structure]

Instead of workers owning the means of production, the state owned everything: factories, farms, resources. Workers had no control over what they produced or how. Central planners in Moscow made those decisions.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Comparison]

Marx's vision: Stateless society, workers control production.
USSR reality: Totalitarian state, party controls production.

The Soviet Union built the exact opposite of what Marx described as the final stage. They stopped at the 'dictatorship of the proletariat' and never moved beyond it."

### SECTION 5: Common Misconceptions (4:45-6:00) [~135 words]

**Visual:** Talking head → B-roll (text overlays, graphics)

**Script:**
"So this creates five common misconceptions about communism.

Misconception 1: 'Communism means big government.' Marx's final stage had no government.

Misconception 2: 'The USSR was exactly what Marx wanted.' Marx wanted statelessness; the USSR built a totalitarian state.

Misconception 3: 'Real communism has never been tried.' Technically true for Marx's final stage, but ignores why—centralized power doesn't voluntarily dissolve.

Misconception 4: 'Communism and socialism are the same thing.' Marx saw socialism as a transitional stage, communism as the end goal.

Misconception 5: 'Modern democratic socialists are communists.' They're advocating for regulated capitalism with welfare programs, not abolishing private property or the state.

These aren't just semantic arguments. The definitions matter for understanding history and modern politics."

**Visual:** Talking head

Understanding what Marx actually wrote, what the USSR actually did, and how modern democratic socialism differs from both—that's how you have informed debates instead of shouting past each other."
