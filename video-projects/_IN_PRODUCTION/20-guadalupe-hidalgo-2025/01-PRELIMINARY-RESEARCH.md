## The Core Story

### The Myth
"The US-Mexico border was settled once and for all by the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo in 1848."

### The Reality
The treaty referenced a map (Disturnell's 1847 map) that had MAJOR errors. When surveyors tried to implement the treaty, they discovered the border couldn't be drawn as described. This led to a territorial dispute that nearly caused a second war, ultimately requiring the Gadsden Purchase (1853) to "fix" the border.

## Key Facts (To Be Verified in Phase 2)

### The Disturnell Map Errors

| Error | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| El Paso placed ~30 miles too far NORTH | Placed it at 32°15'N instead of actual 31°45'N |
| Rio Grande drawn ~2 degrees too far WEST | Placed it at 104°38'W instead of actual 106°29'W |

**Source:** [History Colorado](https://www.historycolorado.org/story/2024/09/20/mistaken-map), [TSHA Online](https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/bartlett-garcia-conde-compromise)

**Why it matters:** These errors meant the boundary described in Article V of the treaty didn't match actual geography. The "southern boundary of New Mexico" referenced in the treaty was in the wrong place.

### The Discovery (1850)

- US and Mexican boundary commissioners met in El Paso del Norte in 1850
- Astronomical observations revealed the Disturnell map errors
- Mexican commissioner García Conde knew about the errors before the survey
- He argued the line should follow the map's coordinates, not the actual geography
- US commissioner John Bartlett initially agreed to a compromise (Bartlett-García Conde Compromise)
- This compromise favored Mexico and was rejected by the US Congress

**Source:** [National Archives](https://www.archives.gov/publications/prologue/2009/winter/gilman-map.html)

### The Mesilla Valley Dispute

- The disputed area included the Mesilla Valley (present-day southern New Mexico)
- 5,000-6,000 square miles at stake
- In 1853, Mexico kicked Americans out of the territory
- New Mexico Governor William Lane responded by claiming the Mesilla Valley for the US
- President Pierce had to recall Lane and replace him with David Meriwether
- Tensions nearly escalated to armed conflict

**Source:** [Office of the Historian](https://history.state.gov/milestones/1830-1860/gadsden-purchase)

### The Gadsden Purchase (1853)

**Why Mexico sold:**
- Santa Anna needed money to fund an army against domestic rebellions
- Mexico's finances were devastated after the Mexican-American War
- Fear of further US aggression if he refused
- He needed "as much money for as little territory as possible"

**What the US wanted:**
- The Mesilla Valley to settle the boundary dispute
- A southern railroad route to the Pacific (required flat land south of the Gila River)

**The deal:**
- Original proposal: $15 million for 45,000 square miles
- Final deal: $10 million for 29,670 square miles
- Signed December 30, 1853
- Ratified 1854

**Aftermath:**
- Santa Anna pocketed much of the $10 million
- He squandered the first $7 million
- This contributed to his loss of the presidency and exile
- Mexico never forgot the humiliation

**Source:** [Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/today-in-history/december-30), [UT Austin Exhibits](https://exhibits.lib.utexas.edu/spotlight/santa-anna-in-life-and-legend/feature/legacy-in-loss-the-shrinking-of-mexico-s-territory)

## Article V of the Treaty (Boundary Description)

**Full text available at:** [Yale Avalon Project](https://avalon.law.yale.edu/19th_century/guadhida.asp)

**Key passage:**
> "The boundary line between the two Republics shall commence in the Gulf of Mexico, three leagues from land, opposite the mouth of the Rio Grande... from thence up the middle of that river, following the deepest channel... to the point where it strikes the **southern boundary of New Mexico**; thence, westwardly, along the whole **southern boundary of New Mexico**..."

**The problem:** The treaty references "the southern boundary of New Mexico" multiple times, but this boundary was defined by the Disturnell map — which had El Paso 30 miles north of its actual location.

**The irony:** The treaty states the boundary "shall be religiously respected by each of the two republics, and no change shall ever be made therein, except by the express and free consent of both nations." — Yet the boundary had to be changed just 5 years later.
