## COMPREHENSIVE 2025 QUOTES (From Grok Research)

Nicholas Joseph Fuentes has emerged as a polarizing figure in American far-right politics, known for his livestreamed commentary that blends Christian nationalism, white supremacy, and revisionist historical interpretations. Born on August 18, 1998, Fuentes first gained attention as a student at Boston University, where he dropped out after attending the 2017 Unite the Right rally in Charlottesville, Virginia—a white supremacist event that resulted in violence and one death. He described the rally as part of a "tidal wave of white identity" and expressed pride in his participation, despite receiving death threats afterward. Since then, Fuentes has built a following through his "America First" platform, which promotes misogyny, anti-LGBTQ sentiments, and antisemitism, often veiled in irony to evade accountability. Organizations such as the Anti-Defamation League (ADL) and Southern Poverty Law Center (SPLC) classify him as a white supremacist and Holocaust denier.

Fuentes' historical claims often center on 20th-century events, particularly World War II and the Holocaust, which he frames through an antisemitic lens. He has questioned the Holocaust's established narrative, including the figure of six million Jewish deaths, in a monologue that he later dismissed as parody. In posts on X (formerly Twitter), Fuentes has mocked Holocaust remembrance, such as criticizing Senate recognition of a survivor or comparing unrelated events like economic issues to Holocaust Remembrance Day. He argues that fear of questioning WWII narratives about Adolf Hitler stems from Jewish influence, noting that discussions of other theaters like the Pacific or Italy face no such restrictions. Fuentes has expressed admiration for Hitler, giggling in praise during speeches and stating "I love you, and I love Hitler" in a 2023 rant to fellow white supremacists. In Wikiquote entries, he references Hitler positively in self-deprecating contexts, such as noting Hitler's height to boost his own confidence. During a 2022 AFPAC conference, he praised Russia's invasion of Ukraine by favorably comparing Vladimir Putin to Hitler, leading chants of "Putin! Putin!"

Beyond WWII, Fuentes idealizes pre-modern history, identifying as a "12th century man" and advocating for Catholic autocracy over democracy. He praises the Crusades and Inquisition as "pretty good stuff," contrasting them with modern issues like obesity, divorce, and liberalism attributed to democracy. In interviews, he told Louis Theroux he supports these historical actions as part of a reactionary Christian theocracy. Fuentes extends this to contemporary analogies, praising the Taliban's 2021 takeover of Afghanistan as a "positive development" for its conservative religious force against U.S. liberalism. He has expressed support for Russia's invasion of Ukraine as liberation from the "evil empire" of the United States and even advocated for China to invade Taiwan to humiliate America.

His views on Jews intertwine historical and modern conspiracies. Fuentes has called for a "holy war" against "Talmudic Jews," claimed Jews control society, and blamed them for obstructing policies like overturning Roe v. Wade. In a 2022 interview with Kanye West (now Ye) and Alex Jones, he agreed with West's Holocaust denial and praise for Hitler, asserting Hitler's reputation was "crafted by Jews." On X, he has defended songs praising Hitler and criticized bans on them as proof of Jewish control. He frames U.S. support for Israel as ethnically biased, claiming the October 7, 2023, attacks were staged.

Fuentes' involvement in U.S. political events reflects his historical revisionism. At the 2019 "Groyper War," his followers posed provocative questions on Israel and immigration, rooted in antisemitic theories. Before January 6, 2021, he spoke at rallies, suggesting force to reclaim the country and rhetorically discussing killing legislators, though he clarified against violence. He was subpoenaed by the January 6 Committee but did not enter the Capitol.

Criticism of Fuentes' claims is extensive, particularly regarding Holocaust denial. The ADL highlights his antisemitic rants, including Holocaust revisionism. Jonathan Greenblatt of the ADL described a 2021 video where Fuentes denied Holocaust atrocities with a smile. Conservative figures and organizations, including the Heritage Foundation, faced backlash in 2025 for defending engagements with him, leading to rifts over antisemitism. A congressional document from 2022 noted his Holocaust revisionism during a dinner with Donald Trump and Kanye West. Counterarguments emphasize historical evidence: the Holocaust's documentation through trials, survivor testimonies, and archives contradicts denial. Critics argue his praise for Hitler ignores the regime's atrocities, including the deaths of millions beyond Jews. Some supporters, like in X posts, frame his views as challenging "demythologization" of WWII, but mainstream sources view them as hate speech.

Fuentes' rhetoric extends to modern analogies, such as comparing Israeli actions in Gaza to historical conflicts or mocking "Never Again" slogans in Epstein discussions. He has praised authoritarian figures like Francisco Franco and Yahya Sinwar indirectly through edits and comments. In a broader societal critique, he claims a "global conspiracy" controls key sectors, implying Jewish involvement.
