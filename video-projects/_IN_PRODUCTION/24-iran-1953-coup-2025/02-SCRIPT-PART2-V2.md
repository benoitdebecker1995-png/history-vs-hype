## ACT 1: THE DOCUMENT HOOK [2.5 min]

[VISUAL: 1979 Constitution, Article 110 on screen]

This is Article 110 of Iran's constitution. The Supreme Leader's powers.

Commander of the armed forces. Appoints the head of the judiciary. Appoints half the Guardian Council. Confirms presidential election results. Can dismiss the president.

Eleven powers. One person. Appointed for life.

And here's what most people don't realize: the first draft of this constitution didn't include any of this.

---

1979. The Iranian Revolution is in full swing. Millions in the streets. Liberals, nationalists, communists, Islamists—all united against the Shah.

And the early revolutionary documents promised democracy.

The first draft of the constitution—written by moderates, lawyers, academics, secular-leaning nationalists—looked like a standard parliamentary democracy. Strong parliament. Elected president. Checks and balances.

No Supreme Leader. No Guardian Council veto. No concept called Velayat-e Faqih—which means "guardianship of the jurist," the idea that one Islamic scholar should hold ultimate political power.

That stuff all got added later.

How did a revolution promising freedom end up building this?

---

If you watched Part 1, you already know Iran has a 120-year history of people trying to build constitutional government—only to have it smashed, first by foreign powers in 1907-1911, then by the CIA in 1953.

In 1953, foreign powers destroyed Iranian democracy.

In 1979, Iranians actually won. The Shah fled. The revolution succeeded.

And then Iranians built a new system that turned out to be even harder to challenge than the one they overthrew.

So the real question isn't just "what did America do to Iran." It's "what did Iran do to itself?"

## ACT 2: THE BAIT AND SWITCH [4 min]

### The First Draft

[VISUAL: 1979 revolutionary assembly]

June 1979. Four months after the revolution. A draft constitution gets published.

It was written by a committee of moderates—lawyers, academics, people who wanted a secular republic. And it looked normal. Elected president. Powerful parliament. Independent judiciary. Clerics would have religious authority, but not political supremacy.

No mention of Velayat-e Faqih—no Supreme Leader with ultimate power over everything.

So what happened?

### Khomeini's Rejection

[VISUAL: Khomeini]

Khomeini rejected it. He said no, we need an elected Assembly of Experts to write a new constitution.

"Elected assembly" sounds democratic. But here's how it actually worked.

The clerical faction—Khomeini's supporters—controlled how the election rules were set up. Those rules heavily favored clerical candidates.

The result: Khomeini's faction won 55 out of 73 seats.

Secular groups like the National Front saw what was happening. They boycotted the whole process because they considered it rigged.

The Assembly then wrote Velayat-e Faqih into the new constitution—creating the position of Supreme Leader with ultimate authority.

The referendum to approve this constitution happened in December 1979. And here's the context that matters: December 1979 was right in the middle of the hostage crisis. Iranian students had seized the American embassy. Revolutionary fervor was at its peak. Voting against the constitution meant voting against the revolution.

The form was democratic: an elected assembly, a public referendum.

The substance was the opposite: the process was designed to produce exactly the outcome Khomeini wanted.

### The Guardian Council

[VISUAL: Guardian Council structure diagram]

And here's the mechanism they created—the reason Iranian elections don't work the way you think they do.

The Guardian Council. Twelve people with two powers: they can veto any law, and they can disqualify any candidate before voters ever see them on the ballot.

Six of them are clerics, appointed directly by the Supreme Leader.

The other six are jurists—legal experts. They're nominated by the head of the judiciary. But the head of the judiciary is also appointed by the Supreme Leader. Then the Majlis—the parliament—confirms them. But the Majlis members were already vetted by the Guardian Council in the previous election.

Do you see the closed loop?

The Supreme Leader appoints the clerics. He appoints the judiciary chief who picks the jurists. The Majlis is supposed to confirm—but the Majlis members were already approved by the same Guardian Council.

Everyone in power was selected by someone who was selected by the Supreme Leader.

The Shah had to send tanks to shut down parliament. The Islamic Republic just makes sure the "right" people get elected in the first place.

## ACT 3: DEBUNKING "JUST A THEOCRACY" [4.5 min]

### Where It's Right

[VISUAL: Guardian Council statistics]

Now let's be clear. These control mechanisms are real.

In 2016, the Guardian Council disqualified 99 percent of reformist candidates who wanted to run for the Majlis.

> "The council eliminated 99 percent of 3,000 reformists who had registered to participate in the 2016 Majles elections."

Ninety-nine percent. Before a single vote was cast.

They've disqualified former President Rafsanjani. They've disqualified Hassan Khomeini—Ayatollah Khomeini's own grandson.

And economic control reinforces political control. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps—the IRGC—is both a military force and a business empire. They and the bonyads—religious foundations that operate like massive conglomerates—control huge chunks of the Iranian economy. Estimates range from 20 to 40 percent.

> "Foundations (Bonyads)... dominated some 40 per cent of the Iranian economy."

If you oppose the regime, you might also be opposing your own employer. Your own paycheck.

So the "oppressive theocracy" story is accurate about how the system actually works.

Where does it go wrong?

### Where It's Wrong

[VISUAL: Comparative timeline]

It goes wrong when people treat Iranian authoritarianism as some inevitable cultural thing. As if Iranians are just naturally suited to theocracy.

But look at the history.

Iran had a constitutional democracy in 1906—demanded by Iranians themselves, not handed down by colonizers.

While Egypt's parliament was set up under British supervision, Iran's was built against foreign opposition.

While Iraq's 1925 constitution was basically written by British mandate authorities, Iran's 1906 constitution was written by Iranian revolutionaries.

The protests happening right now aren't some weird outlier. They're the continuation of a 120-year fight.

### The Evidence of Continuity

[VISUAL: Protest timeline]

1906: Fourteen thousand people camped in the British Embassy garden demanding a parliament.

1951: Unanimous Majlis vote to nationalize oil.

2009: Three million in Tehran chanting "Where is my vote?"

2022: Protests in 150 cities across all 30 provinces—"Woman, Life, Freedom."

2025-2026: Bazaar merchants—the traditional middle class, people who were supposed to be regime supporters—closing their shops in protest.

And the language has changed. Abrahamian tracked the vocabulary shift:

> "By the end of the century, the key terms were demokrasi, pluralism... hoquq-e beshar (human rights), jam'eh-e madani (civil society)..."

People stopped talking about themselves as ri'yat—subjects. They started using shahrvandi—citizen.

They talk about demokrasi, pluralism, human rights, civil society.

That's not cultural failure. That's Iranian political culture succeeding despite 120 years of suppression.

## ACT 4: THE CYCLE REPEATS [4.5 min]

### The Protest Mechanism

[VISUAL: Protest comparison]

Here's how the pattern keeps repeating—not just what happened, but how.

**2009 - The Green Movement**

There's a disputed presidential election. The official results say Ahmadinejad won. Millions believe the election was stolen.

Three million march in Tehran chanting "Where is my vote?"

The government responds with the Basij—volunteer militias loyal to the Supreme Leader. These aren't regular soldiers. They're ideologically committed civilians, organized in every neighborhood, every workplace, every university. When the regime needs to suppress protests without using the regular army, they call the Basij.

The Basij beat people in the streets. Mass arrests follow.

70 to 110 killed. The leaders of the Green Movement—Mousavi and Karroubi—were put under house arrest. They're still there today.

**2019 - Fuel Prices**

The government announces fuel prices will jump 50 to 200 percent overnight.

Protests explode in 21 cities within hours.

The government shuts down the internet completely—one of the most comprehensive internet blackouts any country has ever imposed. Then they issue shoot-to-kill orders.

The Supreme Leader reportedly said the Islamic Republic itself was in danger. Stop the protests by any means necessary.

> "The Islamic Republic is in danger."

Amnesty International documented 304 to 321 deaths by name. Government insiders told Reuters the real number was closer to 1,500.

**2022 - Mahsa Amini**

A 22-year-old woman named Mahsa Amini dies in morality police custody. Her crime: wearing her hijab improperly.

Protests erupt in 150 cities, all 30 provinces.

The government responds with executions, mass detention. The UN later calls it crimes against humanity.

Over 500 killed. 22,000 detained. At least 7 executed.

### The 2025 Uprising

[VISUAL: 2025 protest footage]

And now, 2025-2026. Economic collapse.

The Iranian rial has lost half its value. Food prices up 72 percent. Inflation around 40 percent.

Protests in all 31 provinces.

And here's what's different this time: it started with bazaar merchants shutting their shops. The bazaaris. The traditional middle class. People the regime always counted on for support.

When the bazaaris turn, something fundamental has shifted.

### Why the Cycle Continues

[VISUAL: Cycle diagram]

Every protest: demands for fair elections, press freedom, economic accountability, women's rights.

Every response: the same toolkit.

Russian Cossacks bombarding parliament in 1908. Basij militias with iron rods and handguns in 2009. Internet blackouts and sniper fire in 2019.

The tools change. The pattern doesn't: constitutional demands met with violent suppression.

## ACT 5: THE ACTUAL TRUTH [3 min]

### What the Evidence Shows

[VISUAL: Full timeline]

So what do the sources actually show?

Not "it's all America's fault"—that turns Iranians into passive victims and ignores the internal collaborators, the elite power struggles, the control mechanisms Iranians built to control other Iranians.

Not "Iran is just hopelessly theocratic"—that erases 120 years of people fighting for constitutional limits and pretends authoritarianism is some deep cultural destiny.

The evidence shows a repeating cycle:

1. Iranians demand constitutional government
2. Those demands threaten whoever holds power
3. Internal elites collaborate to destroy the institutions—sometimes with foreign help, sometimes without
4. Violent suppression follows
5. The trauma radicalizes the next generation

In 1907 and 1953: foreign powers pulled the trigger.

In 1979-1981: Iranian revolutionaries did it to themselves.

The common thread isn't "foreign meddling" or "Islamic culture." It's the refusal of people in power to accept any real limits on that power.

### Why Both Narratives Are Comforting

[VISUAL: Split screen]

Both big narratives are comforting. That's why they stick around.

"It's all America's fault" says the problem is external. Fix Washington and Iran will magically be free. Satisfying—but it means Iranians just wait for outsiders to save them.

"Iran is just a theocracy" says the problem is baked into the culture. Nothing changes until Iranians become different people. Satisfying for outsiders—it means the West has no responsibility and nothing needs to change.

The truth is messier: external intervention and internal mechanisms created a self-reinforcing cycle that Iranians keep trying—and dying—to break.

That's not a clean blame story. It's not an easy fix.

But it's what the documents actually show.

### The Question

Both the regime and the opposition keep circling back to 1953. The regime uses it to say "foreign hands are everywhere—suppress everything." The opposition uses it to say "we were robbed from the start."

If everyone is trapped in the same betrayal story, how do you ever break the cycle?
