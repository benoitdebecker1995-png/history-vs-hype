## ACT 2: THE FIRST DEMOCRACY [5.5 min]

### The Trigger - Causal Chain [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Map of Qajar Iran, 1905]

December 1905. Tehran. The governor orders several sugar merchants beaten with the bastinado—they whip the soles of your feet—because they refused to lower prices during an economic crisis.

Now, this seems like a small thing. A local dispute. But here's what the sources reveal about why it exploded.

Abrahamian, in *A History of Modern Iran*, explains the underlying conditions:

> "The revolution's long-term causes were rooted in the nineteenth century; its short-term ones were triggered in 1904–05 by an economic crisis brought about by government bankruptcy and spiraling inflation."

[SOURCE: Abrahamian, p.42]

Government bankruptcy. Spiraling inflation. Which meant the Shah couldn't pay his debts. Consequently, he'd been selling off pieces of Iran to foreign powers for decades—telegraph rights, banking rights, tobacco rights, oil rights. Each sale made him more dependent on foreigners and less responsive to Iranians.

So when the governor beat those merchants, he wasn't just enforcing price controls. He was defending a system that had sold the country piece by piece while ordinary people starved.

And that's when something unprecedented happened.

### The Bast - Comparative Framing [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: British Legation building, historical photo]

The merchants took *bast*—sanctuary. First in mosques. Then in the British Embassy garden.

Now, here's what makes this different from other Middle Eastern protests of that era.

[VISUAL: Comparison graphic]

While in Egypt, the British were dissolving parliaments, in Iran, protesters were DEMANDING one—and using British territory to do it.

While in the Ottoman Empire, the 1876 constitution had been granted from above by the Sultan, in Iran, the constitution was being demanded from below by merchants, intellectuals, and dissident clerics working together.

By August 1906, there were 14,000 people camped in the British Legation garden. Here's the British report:

> "Their numbers gradually increased, and soon there were 14,000 persons in the Legation garden."

[SOURCE: British Correspondence, Vol I, p.4 - SHOW ON SCREEN]

Fourteen thousand. Not a metaphor. Not an estimate. The British counted them.

And here's what they demanded: A Majlis. A parliament. Constitutional limits on the Shah's power.

They won.

### The 1906 Constitution - The Mechanism [2 min]

[VISUAL: 1906 Constitution scan - key articles highlighted]

On October 7th, 1906, the first Majlis opened. On December 30th, the Shah signed the constitution.

But here's what most people miss: this wasn't just a symbolic document. It was an operational manual for limiting royal power. Let me show you how it actually worked.

[VISUAL: Flowchart of 1906 constitutional structure]

**The Mechanism:**

1. **The Majlis** — elected representatives from across Iran
2. **Required approval** for all budgets, treaties, loans, and concessions
3. **Could question ministers** and force them to resign
4. **Controlled the court budget** — the Shah couldn't spend without parliamentary approval

Abrahamian summarizes:

> "The Majles was designed to be the central piece of the new constitution. Described as 'the representative of the whole people,' it had final say over all 'laws, decrees, budgets, treaties, loans, monopolies, and concessions.' It even controlled the court budget."

[SOURCE: Abrahamian, p.49]

Do you see what they built? The Shah couldn't sell another concession without Majlis approval. He couldn't take another loan without Majlis approval. Every mechanism the Qajars had used to sell off Iran was now under parliamentary control.

The constitution was modeled on Belgium's—itself derived from British constitutional principles. Iran had created, on paper, a genuine constitutional monarchy.

And here's the part that matters: it wasn't imposed by foreigners. It grew from internal pressure. Iranians built this.

Which is exactly why foreign powers decided to destroy it.

## ACT 3: THE FIRST DESTRUCTION [3 min]

### The Anglo-Russian Convention - Causal Chain [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Map showing 1907 Anglo-Russian Convention zones]

One year later, 1907. Britain faced a problem that had nothing to do with Iran.

Germany was rising. The Royal Navy needed allies. And the only power that could counterbalance Germany on land was Russia.

But Britain and Russia had been rivals in Central Asia for decades—the "Great Game." To get Russia as an ally against Germany, Britain needed to settle their Asian disputes.

Consequently, they signed the Anglo-Russian Convention. Britain got southern Iran. Russia got northern Iran. A "neutral zone" in between.

Here's the critical detail:

> "Iran was not consulted but was simply informed of this arrangement after the treaty was signed in St. Petersburg."

[SOURCE: Kinzer, p.38 - SHOW MAP ON SCREEN]

Iran wasn't at the table. They were on the menu.

And this created a fundamental problem: the constitutional government that Iranians had just built depended on controlling concessions. But Britain and Russia had just divided up the right to grant concessions between themselves.

The constitution was designed to limit foreign influence. The Anglo-Russian Convention was designed to guarantee it.

Something had to give.

### The Bombardment - Quote Stack [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Mohammad Ali Shah photo]

The new Shah, Mohammad Ali Shah, saw his opportunity. The constitution limited his power. Russia and Britain had just shown they didn't care about the constitution. So in 1908, he moved to destroy it.

And here's the mechanism he used: Russian officers commanding Iranian troops.

Abrahamian:

> "Declaring martial law, the shah appointed Colonel Liakhoff, the Russian Cossack commander, to be military governor of Tehran. Liakhoff... sent his Cossacks to occupy the telegraph office and to bombard the Majles building."

[SOURCE: Abrahamian, p.52]

Let me make sure you understand what happened. The Iranian parliament was bombarded by Iranian troops commanded by a Russian officer, appointed by an Iranian Shah, with the tacit approval of Britain and Russia who had just divided Iran between themselves.

Foreign intervention AND domestic authoritarianism. Working together.

About 250 people died. The Majlis building was destroyed.

The constitutional forces fought back. They retook Tehran. They restored the Majlis. But in 1911, Russia delivered an ultimatum: dismiss the American financial advisor, or face occupation.

The Majlis refused. So Russia occupied.

> "The second Majlis was closed in December 1911, and Shuster's contract was abruptly terminated a few weeks later. With the closure of the Majlis, the first phase of the revolution effectively came to a stuttering halt."

[SOURCE: Ansari, p.111]

Iran's first democracy—crushed.

## ACT 5: MOSSADEGH [6 min]

### The Man - Think-Aloud [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Mossadegh photos, footage]

I'll be honest—before I did this research, I had a simple picture of Mossadegh. Nationalist hero. Overthrown by CIA. Victim.

The sources complicate that picture. Not in the way you might think—the CIA still overthrew him. But understanding WHO he was explains both why he succeeded and why he failed.

Mossadegh wasn't a young revolutionary. He was 69. He'd been fighting for constitutional government since 1906. He was an aristocrat—educated in Europe, wealthy, connected. And he had one absolute conviction: Iran must control its own resources.

Kinzer describes his entry into politics:

> "In his speech he [Mossadegh] called two of the incoming ministers traitors for their role in negotiating the Anglo-Persian Agreement."

[SOURCE: Kinzer, p.59]

The 1919 agreement would have made Iran a British protectorate. Mossadegh called its negotiators traitors—publicly, in parliament.

This wasn't a man who compromised with foreign powers.

### The Oil Mechanism [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: AIOC refinery at Abadan - historical footage]

Here's why oil mattered. Not just economically—structurally.

Since 1901, British interests had controlled Iranian oil through the D'Arcy Concession. Let me show you how the mechanism worked.

[VISUAL: Flowchart of oil profit distribution]

**The D'Arcy Concession (1901):**
- Duration: 60 years exclusive rights
- Iranian share: 16% of net profits
- British control: All drilling, refining, export operations
- British government ownership: 51% of Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (by 1914)

But here's what I found surprising. Iran's share wasn't even the core issue.

Abrahamian, citing Foreign Office records:

> "British Petroleum (BP) archives and Foreign Office records show that the core of the dispute was not profit-sharing but control. A March 1951 memo stated: 'We can be flexible in profits, administration, or partnership, but not in the issue of control.'"

[SOURCE: Abrahamian, The Coup, p.48]

Control. Not profits. Control.

The British would share money. They wouldn't share power.

### The Nationalization [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Majlis chamber, 1951]

So when Mossadegh proposed nationalization—not confiscation, nationalization with compensation—it wasn't radical by global standards.

Saudi Arabia had just negotiated a 50/50 split with Aramco. Venezuela had done the same. Iran was asking for what others had already achieved.

But there was a difference. Saudi Arabia and Venezuela negotiated from within the system. Mossadegh wanted to change the system itself.

On March 15th, 1951, the Majlis voted to nationalize oil.

> "The Majlis met on March 15 to cast its historic vote. Ninety-six deputies turned up, including several who had promised the Shah they would stay away. Every one voted in favor of nationalization."

[SOURCE: Kinzer, p.78]

Every single deputy. Unanimous.

This wasn't Mossadegh imposing his will. This was the Majlis—the constitutional institution Iranians had been fighting for since 1906—exercising its authority.

And that's precisely why Britain couldn't accept it.

### The British Response - Quote Stack [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Sir William Fraser / British Parliament]

Britain's response? Absolute refusal.

Here's AIOC Chairman Sir William Fraser:

> "There will be no further concessions."

[SOURCE: Wilber, cited in Abrahamian, The Coup, p.86 - SHOW ON SCREEN]

And here's what I find most revealing. Christopher Woodhouse—the MI6 officer who would help plan the coup—admitted how they sold it to the Americans:

> "I decided to emphasize the Communist threat to Iran rather than the need to recover control of the oil industry."

[SOURCE: Woodhouse, cited in Kinzer, p.3 - SHOW ON SCREEN]

Three quotes. Same picture: Britain wanted control, not just profits. When they couldn't get control through negotiation, they reframed the issue as communism to get American help.

And here's where the causal chain matters: Britain couldn't overthrow Mossadegh alone. They needed American power. And the only way to get American power was to make Iran about the Cold War, not about oil.

Consequently, a 69-year-old constitutional nationalist became, in American eyes, a communist threat.

## ACT 6: THE COUP [4.5 min]

### The Mechanism [2 min]

[VISUAL: CIA/MI6 organizational chart]

Let me show you how the coup actually worked. Not the narrative—the mechanism.

[VISUAL: Operation TPAJAX organizational structure]

**Operation TPAJAX/BOOT - The Mechanism:**

**Funding:**
- $150,000 budgeted for propaganda
- Additional funds for bribing Majlis deputies
- Weekly payments to newspapers, clerics, street organizers

**Tactics:**
1. Bribe newspapers to print anti-Mossadegh stories
2. Pay clerics to preach against "communist" influence
3. Create fake communist leaflets threatening religious leaders
4. Purchase Majlis deputies for no-confidence vote
5. Organize street mobs (paid demonstrators)
6. Military action to complete the overthrow

Rahnema documents the funding:

> "A sum of $150,000 was budgeted for conducting 'a massive propaganda campaign against Mosaddeq.' ... Money was also allocated to the 'purchase' of deputies in the Majles."

[SOURCE: Rahnema, p.61 - SHOW ON SCREEN]

And here's the part that most people miss. The CIA didn't just organize a coup. They created a fake communist threat to justify the real coup.

> "CIA agents gave serious attention to alarming the religious leaders at Tehran by issuing black propaganda in the name of the [Communist] Tudeh party, threatening these leaders with savage punishment if they opposed Mosaddegh."

[SOURCE: CIA Report, cited in Kinzer, p.97]

They printed fake communist leaflets threatening clerics—to make clerics oppose Mossadegh—to create the appearance of a communist threat—to justify removing him.

### August 1953 [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Timeline August 15-19, 1953]

August 15th, 1953. The first coup attempt failed. A leak. The Shah fled to Rome.

Washington sent a cable ordering the operation discontinued:

> "In view foregoing and in absence strong contrary recommendations from you and Ambassador Henderson, operations against Mossadegh should be discontinued."

[SOURCE: FRUS 1951-1954 Iran, Document 278 - SHOW ACTUAL CABLE]

Roosevelt ignored it.

August 19th. Paid mobs flooded the streets. Military units moved.

Richard Cottam, who was on CIA staff, later admitted:

> "That mob that came into north Tehran and was decisive in the overthrow was a mercenary mob... it had no ideology, and that mob was paid with American dollars."

[SOURCE: Cottam, cited in Kinzer, p.230]

By evening, Mossadegh was under arrest.

The CIA's own assessment, declassified in 2013:

> "The military coup that overthrew Mosadeq and his National Front cabinet was carried out under CIA direction as an act of US foreign policy, conceived and approved at the highest level of government."

[SOURCE: CIA history, cited in Rahnema, p.4 - SHOW ON SCREEN]

### Mossadegh's Words [1 min]

[VISUAL: Mossadegh at trial]

At his trial, Mossadegh said:

> "My only crime is that I nationalized the Iranian oil industry and removed from this land the network of colonialism and the political and economic influence of the greatest empire on earth."

[SOURCE: Kinzer, p.268 - SHOW ON SCREEN]

He was sentenced to three years in prison, then house arrest for life. He died in 1967, still under house arrest. No public funeral was permitted.

Iran's second democracy—crushed.

## ACT 7: THE UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH [4 min]

### Where "America's Fault" Is Right [1 min]

[VISUAL: "It's All America's Fault" graphic]

Now, if you've been following the evidence, you might think the "America's fault" narrative sounds pretty accurate.

The CIA did overthrow a democratic government. The documents prove it. The CIA admitted it.

And the trauma this created is real. Abrahamian identifies what he calls the "paranoid style" in Iranian politics:

> "Political polemics in Iran are replete with such terms as tuteah (plot), jasouz (spy), khianat (treason)... This vocabulary treats Iranian politics as a puppet show in which the real players are 'hidden hands.'"

[SOURCE: Abrahamian, Khomeinism, p.909]

Every regime since 1953 has used the coup as justification. Every failure gets blamed on foreign conspiracies.

So where does this narrative go wrong?

### Where It's Wrong - Quote Stack [2 min]

[VISUAL: Academic sources stacked]

Here's where I had to revise my own thinking.

The coup was foreign-planned. But it was Iranian-executed. And here's where multiple scholars converge on the same point.

**Rahnema** (Iranian scholar):
> "The events were steered, executed and operationalized by Iranians who were directly and indirectly carrying out modified British–US plans."

[SOURCE: Rahnema, p.294]

**Gasiorowski** (American scholar):
> "Mosaddeq and his colleagues made a number of key strategic and tactical mistakes. Most importantly, they failed to create an organization capable of mobilizing broad support for their movement."

[SOURCE: Gasiorowski, p.275]

Three scholars. Three countries. Same conclusion: foreign powers provided the plan and the money, but Iranians provided the execution.

And Mossadegh made mistakes. The referendum with separate ballot boxes for yes and no—that's not democracy. Emergency powers that alienated allies. Knowing Zahedi was plotting but not acting forcefully.

Now—does this justify the coup? No. But it explains why the coup succeeded. The CIA didn't overthrow a unified government. They exploited divisions that already existed.

### What This Narrative Misses [1 min]

And here's the deeper problem: treating 1953 as the beginning.

But as we've seen, the pattern started in 1907. Foreign powers destroying Iranian constitutional government. The 1953 coup wasn't a rupture. It was a repetition.

The same pattern. Different players.

And treating 1953 as the original sin means waiting for America to fix what America broke. That's not a political program. That's learned helplessness.
