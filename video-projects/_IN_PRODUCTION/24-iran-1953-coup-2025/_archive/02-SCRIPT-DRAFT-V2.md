## ACT 2: THE FIRST DEMOCRACY - MECHANISM [6 min]

### The Trigger - Causal Chain [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: Map of Qajar Iran, 1905]

December 1905. Tehran. The governor orders several sugar merchants beaten with the bastinado—they whip the soles of your feet—because they refused to lower prices during an economic crisis.

Now, this seems like a small thing. A local dispute. But here's what the sources reveal about why it exploded.

Abrahamian, in *A History of Modern Iran*, explains the underlying conditions:

> "The revolution's long-term causes were rooted in the nineteenth century; its short-term ones were triggered in 1904–05 by an economic crisis brought about by government bankruptcy and spiraling inflation."

[SOURCE: Abrahamian, p.42]

Government bankruptcy. Spiraling inflation. Which meant the Shah couldn't pay his debts. Consequently, he'd been selling off pieces of Iran to foreign powers for decades—telegraph rights, banking rights, tobacco rights, oil rights. Each sale made him more dependent on foreigners and less responsive to Iranians.

So when the governor beat those merchants, he wasn't just enforcing price controls. He was defending a system that had sold the country piece by piece while ordinary people starved.

And that's when something unprecedented happened.

### The Bast - Comparative Framing [1.5 min]

[VISUAL: British Legation building, historical photo]

The merchants took *bast*—sanctuary. First in mosques. Then in the British Embassy garden.

Now, here's what makes this different from other Middle Eastern protests of that era.

[VISUAL: Comparison graphic]

While in Egypt, the British were dissolving parliaments, in Iran, protesters were DEMANDING one—and using British territory to do it.

While in the Ottoman Empire, the 1876 constitution had been granted from above by the Sultan, in Iran, the constitution was being demanded from below by merchants, intellectuals, and dissident clerics working together.

By August 1906, there were 14,000 people camped in the British Legation garden. Here's the British report:

> "Their numbers gradually increased, and soon there were 14,000 persons in the Legation garden."

[SOURCE: British Correspondence, Vol I, p.4 - SHOW ON SCREEN]

Fourteen thousand. Not a metaphor. Not an estimate. The British counted them.

And here's what they demanded: A Majlis. A parliament. Constitutional limits on the Shah's power.

They won.

### The 1906 Constitution - The Mechanism [2 min]

[VISUAL: 1906 Constitution scan - key articles highlighted]

On October 7th, 1906, the first Majlis opened. On December 30th, the Shah signed the constitution.

But here's what most people miss: this wasn't just a symbolic document. It was an operational manual for limiting royal power. Let me show you how it actually worked.

[VISUAL: Flowchart of 1906 constitutional structure]

**The Mechanism:**

1. **The Majlis** — elected representatives from across Iran
2. **Required approval** for all budgets, treaties, loans, and concessions
3. **Could question ministers** and force them to resign
4. **Controlled the court budget** — the Shah couldn't spend without parliamentary approval

Abrahamian summarizes:

> "The Majles was designed to be the central piece of the new constitution. Described as 'the representative of the whole people,' it had final say over all 'laws, decrees, budgets, treaties, loans, monopolies, and concessions.' It even controlled the court budget."

[SOURCE: Abrahamian, p.49]

Do you see what they built? The Shah couldn't sell another concession without Majlis approval. He couldn't take another loan without Majlis approval. Every mechanism the Qajars had used to sell off Iran was now under parliamentary control.

The constitution was modeled on Belgium's—itself derived from British constitutional principles. Iran had created, on paper, a genuine constitutional monarchy.

And here's the part that matters for understanding the cycle: it wasn't imposed by foreigners. It grew from internal pressure. Iranians built this.

Which is exactly why foreign powers decided to destroy it.

### The Destruction - Deep Causal Chain [1 min]

[VISUAL: Map showing 1907 Anglo-Russian Convention zones]

One year later, 1907. Britain faced a problem that had nothing to do with Iran.

Germany was rising. The Royal Navy needed allies. And the only power that could counterbalance Germany on land was Russia.

But Britain and Russia had been rivals in Central Asia for decades—the "Great Game." To get Russia as an ally against Germany, Britain needed to settle their Asian disputes.

Consequently, they signed the Anglo-Russian Convention. Britain got southern Iran. Russia got northern Iran. A "neutral zone" in between.

Here's the critical detail:

> "Iran was not consulted but was simply informed of this arrangement after the treaty was signed in St. Petersburg."

[SOURCE: Kinzer, p.38 - SHOW MAP ON SCREEN]

Iran wasn't at the table. They were on the menu.

And this created a fundamental problem: the constitutional government that Iranians had just built depended on controlling concessions. But Britain and Russia had just divided up the right to grant concessions between themselves.

The constitution was designed to limit foreign influence. The Anglo-Russian Convention was designed to guarantee it.

Something had to give.

[VISUAL: AIOC refinery at Abadan - historical footage]

[VISUAL: Flowchart of oil profit distribution]

**The D'Arcy Concession (1901):**
- Duration: 60 years exclusive rights
- Iranian share: 16% of net profits
- British control: All drilling, refining, export operations
- British government ownership: 51% of Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (by 1914)

By the 1940s, AIOC was one of Britain's most valuable assets. The sources indicate Britain received about $100 million annually in foreign exchange from AIOC operations—in 1951 money.

But here's what I found surprising. Iran's share—that 16%, later raised to about 20%—wasn't even the core issue.

Abrahamian, citing Foreign Office records:

> "British Petroleum (BP) archives and Foreign Office records show that the core of the dispute was not profit-sharing but control. A March 1951 memo stated: 'We can be flexible in profits, administration, or partnership, but not in the issue of control.'"

Control. Not profits. Control.

On March 15th, 1951, the Majlis voted to nationalize oil.

> "The Majlis met on March 15 to cast its historic vote. Ninety-six deputies turned up, including several who had promised the Shah they would stay away. Every one voted in favor of nationalization."

[SOURCE: Kinzer, p.78]

Every single deputy. Unanimous.

This wasn't Mossadegh imposing his will. This was the Majlis—the constitutional institution Iranians had been fighting for since 1906—exercising its authority.
