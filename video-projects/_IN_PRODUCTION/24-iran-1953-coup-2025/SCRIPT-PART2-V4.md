## ACT 1: THE BAIT AND SWITCH [1:45-6:00]

### The Promise

[VISUAL: 1979 revolutionary crowds]

February 1979. Millions in the streets. The Shah is gone.

And the coalition that overthrew him was everyone. Liberals, nationalists, communists, Islamists—united against the monarchy.

The early revolutionary documents promised what they'd all been fighting for: democracy.

[VISUAL: First draft constitution text]

The first draft of the constitution was written by moderates—lawyers, academics, secular nationalists. People who wanted an Iranian republic without clerical rule.

And their draft looked like a normal parliamentary democracy.

Strong Majlis—that's the parliament. Elected president with real power. Independent courts. The clerics would have religious authority, but not political supremacy.

No mention of Velayat-e Faqih. That's the concept that one Islamic scholar should hold ultimate political power—the idea that became the Supreme Leader.

That wasn't in the first draft. It got added later.

---

### The Switch

[TALKING HEAD]

Khomeini rejected it.

He said no, we need an elected Assembly of Experts to write a proper Islamic constitution.

"Elected assembly" sounds democratic. But watch how it actually worked.

[VISUAL: Assembly of Experts composition]

Khomeini's faction controlled how the election rules were set up. The rules heavily favored clerical candidates.

Result: Khomeini's supporters won 55 out of 73 seats.

The secular groups—the National Front, the people who'd written the first draft—saw what was happening. They boycotted the whole process. They called it rigged.

The Assembly then wrote Velayat-e Faqih into the new constitution. They created the position of Supreme Leader with ultimate authority over everything.

---

### The Referendum Trap

[TALKING HEAD]

And then came the referendum. December 1979.

The timing matters. December 1979 was the middle of the hostage crisis. Iranian students had seized the American embassy. Revolutionary fervor was at its peak.

The question on the ballot wasn't "Do you approve this specific constitution?" It was framed as: Are you for the Islamic Republic or against it?

Voting no meant voting against the revolution itself.

[VISUAL: Referendum results]

The form was democratic. Elected assembly. Public referendum.

The substance was a hijacking. The process was designed to produce exactly the outcome Khomeini wanted.

**The first draft promised democracy. The final draft was designed to make sure democracy could never happen.**

And this is where I need to show you the mechanism—because once you see it, you'll understand why every Iranian election since 1979 has been theater.

## ACT 2: THE DEMOCRACY-PROOF MACHINE [6:00-10:30]

### The Guardian Council

[VISUAL: Guardian Council structure diagram]

This is the Guardian Council. Twelve people. Two powers.

Power one: They can veto any law passed by parliament if they decide it violates Islam or the constitution.

Power two: They can disqualify any candidate before voters ever see them on a ballot.

Any candidate. For any office. President, parliament, local councils. If the Guardian Council says no, you don't run.

Now watch how they're selected.

[VISUAL: Selection flow diagram - animated]

Six members are clerics. Appointed directly by the Supreme Leader.

The other six are jurists—legal experts. They're nominated by the head of the judiciary. But the head of the judiciary is also appointed by the Supreme Leader.

Then parliament confirms them. But the parliament members were already vetted by the Guardian Council in the previous election.

Do you see the closed loop?

**Everyone who holds power was selected by someone who was selected by the Supreme Leader.**

The Shah had to send tanks to shut down parliament. The Islamic Republic doesn't need tanks. They just make sure the "right" people get elected in the first place.

---

### The Numbers

[VISUAL: Disqualification statistics]

This isn't theoretical. Here are the numbers.

In 2016, the Guardian Council disqualified 99 percent of reformist candidates who wanted to run for parliament.

Let me say that again. Three thousand reformists registered to run. The Guardian Council approved thirty.

> "The council eliminated 99 percent of 3,000 reformists who had registered to participate in the 2016 Majles elections."

Ninety-nine percent eliminated. Before a single vote was cast.

They've disqualified former President Rafsanjani—one of the founders of the Islamic Republic.

They've disqualified Hassan Khomeini—Ayatollah Khomeini's own grandson.

If the founder's grandson isn't approved, what chance does anyone else have?

---

### Economic Lock-In

[TALKING HEAD]

But political control is only half the machine. The other half is economic.

[VISUAL: IRGC economic empire diagram]

The Revolutionary Guards—the IRGC—started as a military force. Now they're also a business empire.

They and the bonyads—religious foundations that operate like massive conglomerates—control somewhere between 20 and 40 percent of Iran's entire economy.

> "Foundations (Bonyads)... dominated some 40 per cent of the Iranian economy."

Construction. Oil and gas. Telecommunications. Banking.

What this means: If you oppose the regime, you might also be opposing your own employer. Your own paycheck. Your family's livelihood.

**The system doesn't just control who can run for office. It controls who can eat.**

### Why This Isn't "Just a Theocracy"

[TALKING HEAD]

So the "oppressive theocracy" narrative is accurate about the mechanisms. The Guardian Council is real. The economic control is real. The suppression is real.

Where does it go wrong?

It goes wrong when people treat this as some inevitable cultural thing. As if Iranians are naturally suited to theocracy. As if they've never wanted anything else.

But look at the actual history.

[VISUAL: Comparative timeline]

Iran had a constitutional democracy in 1906—demanded by Iranians themselves. Not handed down by colonizers.

While Egypt's parliament was set up under British supervision, Iran's was built against foreign opposition.

While Iraq's 1925 constitution was drafted by British mandate authorities, Iran's 1906 constitution was written by Iranian revolutionaries who took sanctuary in the British embassy to demand it.

The protests happening right now aren't some weird outlier. They're the continuation of a 120-year fight.

## ACT 3: THE EVIDENCE OF CONTINUITY [10:30-13:00]

### The Vocabulary Shift

[VISUAL: Persian terms on screen with translations]

Here's something most Western coverage misses.

Historian Ervand Abrahamian tracked how Iranians talk about themselves:

> "By the end of the century, the key terms were demokrasi, pluralism... hoquq-e beshar (human rights), jam'eh-e madani (civil society)..."

People stopped calling themselves ri'yat—subjects. They started using shahrvandi—citizen.

They talk about demokrasi. Pluralism. Human rights. Civil society.

That vocabulary doesn't come from foreign NGOs. It comes from 120 years of Iranians demanding the same thing their great-grandparents demanded in 1906.

**That's not cultural failure. That's Iranian political culture succeeding despite 120 years of suppression.**

## ACT 4: THE MECHANISM OF SUPPRESSION [13:00-17:30]

### 2009 - The Green Movement

[VISUAL: Green Movement footage]

June 2009. Presidential election. The official results say Ahmadinejad won by a landslide.

Millions believe the election was stolen. Three million march in Tehran. "Where is my vote?"

The government's response reveals the machine.

[VISUAL: Basij explanation]

The Basij. Volunteer militias loyal to the Supreme Leader. These aren't regular soldiers. They're ideologically committed civilians, organized in every neighborhood, every workplace, every university.

When the regime needs to suppress protests without using the regular army, they call the Basij.

> "Images of basijis, many in plain clothes, beating protesters with iron rods, bats, and clubs were prolifically shared on social media."

Between 70 and 110 killed, depending on whose count you trust. The leaders of the Green Movement—Mousavi and Karroubi—were put under house arrest.

They're still there today. Sixteen years later.

---

### 2019 - Bloody November

[VISUAL: 2019 protest footage]

November 2019. The government announces fuel prices will jump 50 to 200 percent overnight.

Protests explode in 21 cities within hours.

The government's response was the most violent since 1979.

First, they shut down the internet completely. One of the most comprehensive blackouts any country has ever imposed.

Then they issued shoot-to-kill orders.

[VISUAL: Death toll statistics]

Amnesty International documented 304 to 321 deaths by name.

Government insiders told Reuters the real number was closer to 1,500.

The Supreme Leader reportedly told advisers: "The Islamic Republic is in danger. Stop the protests by any means necessary."

**By any means necessary.**

### 2022 - Woman, Life, Freedom

[VISUAL: Mahsa Amini photo]

September 2022. A 22-year-old woman named Mahsa Amini dies in morality police custody.

Her crime: wearing her hijab improperly.

[VISUAL: 2022 protest montage]

Protests erupt in 150 cities. All 30 provinces. The slogan: "Woman, Life, Freedom."

Over 500 killed. 22,000 detained. At least 7 publicly executed—including one man hung from a crane in the street.

The United Nations called it crimes against humanity.

And here's what most coverage missed: the women leading these protests weren't just fighting about hijab.

They were using the same constitutional language their grandparents used. The same demand for limits on arbitrary power. The same fight that started in 1906.

**Hijab was the spark. The fuel was 120 years old.**
