## PRIMARY SOURCES (Tier 1 - Must Have)

### 1. Alice de Bryene's Household Account Book (1412-1413)
**Source Type:** Medieval estate records
**Priority:** 🔴 CRITICAL - This is your strongest "she owned property" example
**Publication:** *The Household Account Book of Alice de Bryene, 1412-1413*
**Editor:** Vincent Redstone & Marion Dale
**Date:** Originally 1412-1413, published 1931

**Where to Find:**
- Archive.org: https://archive.org/details/householdaccount00bryeuoft
- Google Books preview
- British Library digitized manuscripts

**What to Extract:**
- Evidence of Alice managing estates after widowhood
- Property ownership details
- Business transactions (grain sales, livestock)
- Proof of legal autonomy for widows

**Why Critical:**
- Concrete example of wealthy widow owning/managing property
- Primary source you can show on screen
- Counters "women owned nothing" myth

---

### 2. London Silkwomen's Guild Records (1300s-1400s)
**Source Type:** Guild charters and regulations
**Priority:** 🔴 CRITICAL
**Key Document:** Petition of London Silkwomen (1368)

**Where to Find:**
- *Women and Work in Preindustrial Europe* - Maryanne Kowaleski (anthology with primary source excerpts)
- British History Online: London guild records
- *Medieval London Widows, 1300-1500* - Caroline Barron

**What to Extract:**
- Names of specific silkwomen (Rose de Burford, etc.)
- Guild charter granting trade rights
- Evidence of women running independent businesses
- Apprenticeship records

**Why Critical:**
- Shows women in regulated, taxable professions
- Named individuals (not just "some women")
- Guild recognition = legal business status

---

### 3. Paris Tax Rolls (1292)
**Source Type:** Medieval tax records
**Priority:** 🔴 CRITICAL
**Original Title:** *Le Livre des métiers d'Étienne Boileau*

**Where to Find:**
- *Working Women in Renaissance Germany* - Merry Wiesner (has excerpts)
- *Women and Work in Medieval Europe* - Sharon Farmer
- Excerpts in various medieval history anthologies

**What to Extract:**
- List of women's occupations (200+ trades)
- Specific professions: brewers, bakers, metalworkers, barber-surgeons
- Tax amounts (proves these were substantial businesses)

**Why Critical:**
- Comprehensive list showing scope of women's work
- Tax records = documented, legal professions
- Counters "women stayed home" completely

---

### 4. Decretum Gratiani - Canon Law on Marriage (c. 1140)
**Source Type:** Medieval Catholic canon law
**Priority:** 🟡 HIGH
**Full Title:** *Concordia discordantium canonum* (Harmony of Discordant Canons)

**Where to Find:**
- Excerpts in *Medieval Canon Law* collections
- *Women and Marriage in Medieval England* - Frederik Pedersen (has relevant excerpts)
- Translation: *The Treatise on Laws (Decretum DD. 1-20)* - Augustine Thompson, O.P.

**What to Extract:**
- Requirements for valid marriage (mutual consent)
- Legal definition of consent
- Age requirements (12 girls, 14 boys)

**Why Important:**
- Shows marriage required consent (not just purchase/property)
- BUT reveals very young ages considered acceptable
- Demonstrates complexity: some protections, major limitations

---

### 5. English Common Law - Coverture Doctrine
**Source Type:** Legal treatise
**Priority:** 🟡 HIGH
**Key Text:** Blackstone's *Commentaries on the Laws of England* (1765) - references medieval precedent

**Where to Find:**
- *Blackstone's Commentaries* Book I, Chapter XV (online, public domain)
- *Women and Property in Early Modern England* - Amy Erickson (explains medieval origins)

**What to Extract:**
- Definition of coverture (married woman's legal status merged with husband's)
- How this affected property ownership
- Exception for unmarried women and widows (feme sole)

**Why Important:**
- Explains why marital status mattered so much
- Shows married women lost legal rights
- But unmarried/widows retained them

## SOURCE VERIFICATION NOTES

**Red Flags to Watch For:**
- ⚠️ Sources claiming "medieval women had equal rights" (FALSE - oversimplification)
- ⚠️ Sources claiming "all women were oppressed equally" (FALSE - class/marital status mattered hugely)
- ⚠️ Cherry-picked examples without acknowledging limitations

**Green Flags (Trustworthy):**
- ✅ Primary sources (wills, guild charters, court records)
- ✅ Academic books with extensive citations
- ✅ Sources that acknowledge regional/class variations
- ✅ Sources that note both rights AND restrictions
