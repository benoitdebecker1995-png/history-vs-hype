### SECTION 3: PRIMARY SOURCE 1 - Property Rights (1:30-3:00) [~165 words]

**THIS IS KEY - SHOW ACTUAL DOCUMENTS**

**Visual:** B-roll - Medieval legal codes and property records on screen

**Script:**
"Let's start with property. Could medieval women own land and property?

[TEXT ON SCREEN - English Common Law, 13th century]

Under English common law, married women operated under 'coverture'—their legal identity merged with their husband's. A married woman couldn't own property separately.

But—and this is what both sides miss—unmarried women and widows absolutely could.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Will of Alice de Bryene, 1435]

This is the will of Alice de Bryene, a wealthy English widow who died in 1435. She owned multiple estates, managed farms, employed dozens of workers, and left detailed instructions for distributing her property.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Court rolls from London, 1300s]

London court records show hundreds of women—widows and single women—buying, selling, and inheriting property throughout the 1300s and 1400s.

The law said: if you're a married woman, your husband controls property. If you're unmarried or widowed? You're a legal person with full property rights.

That's not 'zero rights.' But it's also not the romanticized version where marriage gave women protection and status. Marriage actually reduced their legal rights."

### SECTION 4: PRIMARY SOURCE 2 - Businesswomen (3:00-4:30) [~165 words]

**Visual:** B-roll - Guild regulations and tax records

**Script:**
"What about work? The myth says women stayed home. The documents say otherwise.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Silkwomen's Guild Charter, London 1368]

In 1368, the Silkwomen's Guild in London was officially recognized. These were women who imported silk, employed apprentices, and ran their own businesses. The guild charter names specific women—Rose de Burford, Johanna Rippelyngham—who petitioned the king for trade rights.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Paris Tax Rolls, 1292]

Paris tax rolls from 1292 list over 200 occupations for women: brewers, bakers, metalworkers, barber-surgeons, money changers. These weren't side hustles. These were taxable, regulated professions.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Ale-wife regulations, England 1400s]

Brewing ale was dominated by women in medieval England. Town regulations show 'ale-wives' had to be licensed, follow quality standards, and pay taxes just like male craftsmen.

Again—this wasn't universal freedom. Guild rules often restricted women to certain trades. Married women often needed husband's permission. But 'women didn't work' is completely false.

The reality: work was stratified by marital status and class, not completely forbidden."

### SECTION 5: PRIMARY SOURCE 3 - Marriage Laws (4:30-5:45) [~135 words]

**Visual:** B-roll - Canon law texts and marriage records

**Script:**
"What about marriage? Was it romantic partnership or legal ownership?

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Canon Law, Decretum Gratiani, 12th century]

Medieval Catholic canon law required both parties to consent to marriage. No consent, no valid marriage. This is from the Decretum Gratiani, compiled around 1140.

[TEXT ON SCREEN - Marriage annulment cases, Church courts]

Church court records show women successfully arguing for annulments based on lack of consent, impotence, or abuse. In 1460s York, a woman named Alice Milner got her marriage annulled because she was coerced.

But—and this matters—the age of consent was 12 for girls, 14 for boys. Arranged marriages were common among nobility. Domestic abuse wasn't grounds for divorce, only annulment.

So: women weren't chattel with zero say. But church law and social practice gave them far less power than men, especially once married."

### SECTION 6: The Queens & Power (5:45-6:45) [~110 words]

**Visual:** Talking head → B-roll (portraits, seals)

**Script:**
"And then there were queens who actually ruled.

Eleanor of Aquitaine controlled more territory than the King of France. Matilda of Tuscany commanded armies. Jadwiga of Poland was crowned 'king'—not queen—because she ruled in her own right.

These weren't exceptions that prove the rule. They show that medieval people understood women could wield power when circumstances allowed.

But only elite women. Only women with inherited titles or wealth. A peasant woman had no path to power, same as a peasant man.

Medieval Europe wasn't feminist. But it also wasn't the 'natural order' tradwives imagine. It was a complex legal system that treated women differently based on class and marital status."

## FACT-CHECKING REQUIREMENTS

**Every claim must be verified with:**

1. **English Common Law - Coverture**
   - Source: Blackstone's Commentaries (references medieval precedent)
   - Academic: "Women and the Law in Medieval England" - Frederik Pedersen

2. **Alice de Bryene's Will (1435)**
   - Source: British Library, Estate records
   - Academic: "The Household Account Book of Alice de Bryene" (ed. Vincent Redstone)

3. **Silkwomen's Guild (1368)**
   - Source: London guildhall records
   - Academic: "Women and Work in Medieval London" - Maryanne Kowaleski

4. **Paris Tax Rolls (1292)**
   - Source: *Le Livre des métiers d'Étienne Boileau*
   - Academic: "Working Women in Medieval Paris" - Sharon Farmer

5. **Decretum Gratiani - Consent**
   - Source: Canon law text, c. 1140
   - Academic: "Marriage in Medieval England" - Conor McCarthy

6. **Church Court Annulments**
   - Source: York consistory court records
   - Academic: "Women and Marriage in Medieval England" - Frederik Pedersen

**Red Lines:**
- ❌ DO NOT say "medieval women had equal rights" (false oversimplification)
- ❌ DO NOT romanticize medieval marriage (arranged, young ages, abuse not grounds for divorce)
- ✅ DO show complexity: some rights, many restrictions, stratified by class/marital status
