## EDITING PHILOSOPHY

**The Golden Rule:** If the B-roll doesn't make your argument stronger, stay on camera.

**B-roll is EVIDENCE, not decoration.**

Use B-roll when:
- Showing statistics/data (YouGov, manuscript counts)
- Displaying maps (Visigothic Spain, Lombard Italy, diplomatic reach)
- Presenting primary sources (manuscripts, jury tickets, legal codes)
- Making visual comparisons (6th-7th vs 8th-9th century output)

Stay on camera when:
- Making arguments and interpretations
- Explaining causation ("What does that tell us?")
- Delivering key thesis points ("both extremes oversimplify")
- Rhetorical questions ("how high were we to begin with?")

**Target Ratio:** 65% talking head, 35% B-roll

## TROUBLESHOOTING

**"This segment feels too long on camera"**
→ Check if it's analytical/interpretive. If yes, stay on camera. Only cut to B-roll if there's EVIDENCE to show.

**"I have great B-roll but it's not in the guide"**
→ Ask: Does this EVIDENCE prove a claim, show geography, or display a primary source? If no = decoration. Don't use.

**"The map sequence is confusing"**
→ Slow down. Add text overlays. Give audience 5+ seconds per complex map.

**"The 915% reveal doesn't land"**
→ Add more beats. Let numbers hang. Consider bar graph animation. This is your smoking gun - spend time here.
