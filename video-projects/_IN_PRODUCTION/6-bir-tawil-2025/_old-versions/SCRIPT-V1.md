**[VISUAL: Split screen - Egypt map left, Sudan map right]**

Bir Tawil: eight hundred square miles of landlocked desert. No water. No resources. Worthless.

**[VISUAL: Highlight Hala'ib Triangle]**

Hala'ib Triangle: eight thousand square miles. Ten times bigger. Red Sea coastline. Oil potential. Seven hundred thousand tons of manganese.

**[HOOK 1:00 - PATTERN INTERRUPT]**

And here's the problem. Because of how the British drew the borders, you can't claim both.

**[VISUAL: 1899 map overlay]**

1899. Britain and Egypt sign the Anglo-Egyptian Condominium Agreement. Quote:

**[VISUAL: Treaty text on screen]**

"Sudan is defined as territories south of the 22nd parallel of latitude."

**[VISUAL: Show straight line across map at 22° N]**

Simple. Straight line. Under this border, Egypt gets Hala'ib. Sudan gets Bir Tawil.

**[HOOK 1:25]**

But then in 1902, the British changed their minds.

**[VISUAL: 1902 administrative boundary - irregular line]**

November 4, 1902. New administrative boundary. Not based on a straight line. Based on which tribes lived where.

**[VISUAL: Tribal territories highlighted]**

The Ababda tribe used Bir Tawil for grazing. Tied to Egypt. So the British assigned it to Egyptian administration.

The Beja and Bisharin tribes in Hala'ib were culturally closer to Khartoum. Assigned to Sudanese administration.

**[HOOK 1:50 - CRITICAL REVEAL]**

**[VISUAL: Both boundaries side by side]**

Now watch what happens.

If you use the 1899 border: Egypt gets Hala'ib, Sudan gets Bir Tawil.

If you use the 1902 border: Sudan gets Hala'ib, Egypt gets Bir Tawil.

**[VISUAL: Animated switch between the two]**

Both countries want Hala'ib. So Egypt claims the 1899 line - and rejects Bir Tawil.

Sudan claims the 1902 line - and also rejects Bir Tawil.

**[HOOK 2:10]**

Two colonial borders. One paradox. The only unclaimed land on Earth.
