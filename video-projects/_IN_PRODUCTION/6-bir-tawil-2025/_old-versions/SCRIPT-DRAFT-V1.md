## THE SCRIPT

### HOOK (0:00-0:30) [~55 words]

**[ON CAMERA]**

This desert between Egypt and Sudan is called the only unclaimed land on Earth.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing Bir Tawil location]**

In 2014, an American dad traveled here to plant a flag and claim it—to make his seven-year-old daughter a princess.

**[B-ROLL: Heaton news headlines - TIME, Washington Post]**

But here's the thing: it's not really unclaimed. And you definitely can't just claim it.

Let me show you why.

---

### SETUP: THE HEATON STORY (0:30-1:30) [~130 words]

**[B-ROLL: Heaton photo, "Kingdom of North Sudan" flag]**

His name was Jeremiah Heaton. He was a farmer from Virginia. And when his daughter Emily asked if she could be a real princess, he took it literally.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing journey to Bir Tawil]**

On June 16, 2014—Emily's seventh birthday—Heaton drove through the desert, planted a blue flag he'd designed, and declared the area the Kingdom of North Sudan.

**[B-ROLL: News coverage montage]**

The story went viral. TIME Magazine covered it. Disney was reportedly interested in a movie deal.

**[ON CAMERA]**

But no government recognized his claim. Not Egypt. Not Sudan. Not anyone.

Why?

Because Bir Tawil isn't unclaimed because nobody noticed it. It's unclaimed because both countries specifically reject it.

And that rejection? It goes back to 1899.

---

### HISTORY: THE COLONIAL TRAP (1:30-4:00) [~315 words]

**[B-ROLL: Map of Africa, zoom to Egypt-Sudan region]**

In 1899, the British Empire and Egypt signed an agreement establishing joint control over Sudan. They called it the Anglo-Egyptian Condominium.

**[B-ROLL: 1899 treaty text on screen OR historical document]**

The agreement defined Sudan's northern border at the 22nd parallel of latitude. A straight line across the map.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing 22nd parallel as straight red line]**

Clean. Simple. Wrong.

**[ON CAMERA]**

Because here's what the British quickly realized: that straight line didn't match how people actually lived.

**[B-ROLL: Historical photo or illustration of Ababda tribe]**

The Ababda tribe—based near Aswan in Egypt—used this area for grazing. That's Bir Tawil.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing Hala'ib Triangle to the northeast]**

Meanwhile, the tribes living in the Hala'ib Triangle further northeast—the Beja and Bisharin—were culturally closer to Sudan.

**[B-ROLL: 1902 administrative boundary map]**

So in 1902, the British issued a new decree. They drew a second border—an administrative boundary based on where tribes actually lived.

**[B-ROLL: Animated map showing both lines]**

Under the 1902 line, Bir Tawil went to Egypt. And Hala'ib went to Sudan.

But the 1899 line was never officially repealed.

**[ON CAMERA - KEY MOMENT]**

So now there were two borders. And here's the trap:

**[B-ROLL: Split-screen animation showing both claims]**

Egypt claims the 1899 border. Under that line, Hala'ib is Egyptian territory. But Bir Tawil belongs to Sudan.

Sudan claims the 1902 border. Under that line, Hala'ib is Sudanese territory. But Bir Tawil belongs to Egypt.

**[ON CAMERA]**

Both countries want Hala'ib. So both countries claim the border that gives them Hala'ib. And both countries reject Bir Tawil—because claiming it would mean accepting the border that costs them Hala'ib.

They're stuck. And Bir Tawil is the leftover.

---

### STAKES: WHY HALA'IB MATTERS (4:00-5:30) [~180 words]

**[B-ROLL: Satellite image of Hala'ib Triangle showing coastline]**

So why does Hala'ib matter so much?

**[B-ROLL: Size comparison graphic]**

First, size. The Hala'ib Triangle is 20,580 square kilometers. That's more than ten times bigger than Bir Tawil.

**[B-ROLL: Red Sea map showing shipping lanes]**

Second, location. Hala'ib has 250 kilometers of Red Sea coastline. That's access to one of the world's busiest shipping lanes, right near the approach to the Suez Canal.

**[B-ROLL: Resource graphics/maps]**

Third, resources. The area has an estimated 700,000 tons of manganese deposits. There's also iron, phosphate, and gold. And a 2024 Egyptian Geological Survey estimated potential offshore oil reserves at 1.2 billion barrels.

**[B-ROLL: Egyptian military presence or Berenice naval base satellite image]**

In 1994, Egypt sent in troops and took military control. They built infrastructure. In 2020, they opened a naval base there.

**[ON CAMERA]**

Sudan still claims it diplomatically. But Egypt controls it. And that control is worth far more than an empty desert with no coastline and no resources.

That's Bir Tawil.

---

### MYTH-BUST: WHY CLAIMS DON'T WORK (5:30-6:45) [~140 words]

**[ON CAMERA]**

So when Jeremiah Heaton planted his flag in 2014, why didn't it work?

**[B-ROLL: International law graphics or UN building]**

Because under international law, creating a sovereign state requires four things: a permanent population, a defined territory, a functioning government, and the capacity to enter into relations with other states.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing Bir Tawil empty]**

Bir Tawil has no permanent population. Heaton didn't establish a functioning government. And most importantly—no other country recognized his claim.

**[ON CAMERA]**

Not Egypt. Not Sudan. Not anyone. Because even though neither country claims Bir Tawil, both of them reject anyone else claiming it.

You can't just plant a flag and declare sovereignty. That's not how international law works. It never has been.

**[B-ROLL: Antarctica map]**

Even Antarctica—governed by international treaty—isn't claimable by individuals.

Bir Tawil isn't a legal loophole. It's a diplomatic trap.

---

### CONCLUSION (6:45-7:15) [~50 words]

**[ON CAMERA - FINAL POINT]**

So is Bir Tawil really unclaimed?

Technically, yes. Practically, no. Both Egypt and Sudan reject it because the British drew two different borders in 1899 and 1902, then left without clarifying which one was real.

**[B-ROLL: Final map showing both borders, Bir Tawil highlighted]**

This is what empire leaves behind: a line drawn wrong, redrawn three years later, and then abandoned for successor states to fight over.

**[ON CAMERA - CTA]**

Bir Tawil isn't the only place where colonial borders created modern problems. If you want to see how French colonial borders in Lebanon are fueling conflict today, check out this video next.

**[END SCREEN]**
