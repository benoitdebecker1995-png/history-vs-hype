# Columbus's Geographical Calculation Error (For Spoken Delivery)

**Research Date:** 2025-12-31
**Purpose:** Explain Columbus's error in simple terms that work for talking head video

---

## The Core Error: Columbus Was WRONG, His Critics Were RIGHT

### What Columbus Thought

**His Estimate:**
- Earth's circumference: **~21,000 miles** (way too small)
- Distance from Spain to Asia: **2,500 miles** (massively underestimated)
- Time to reach Japan: A few weeks

**What He Actually Got:**
- True Earth circumference: **24,901 miles**
- True distance Spain to Japan: **12,500 miles** (5x what he thought!)
- Result: He hit the Caribbean and thought it was the East Indies

---

## The Two Mistakes That Compounded

### Mistake #1: The Mile Confusion

**The Problem:**
Columbus used measurements from **Alfraganus** (medieval Persian geographer), who said:
- 1 degree of Earth's circumference = **56⅔ miles**

**Columbus's Error:**
He thought Alfraganus meant the **Roman mile** (4,856 feet)
But Alfraganus actually meant the **Arabic mile** (7,091 feet)

**Result:** Columbus's estimate was about **25-30% too small**

**For spoken delivery:**
"Columbus made a basic conversion error. He read an Arab geographer who measured one degree as 56 miles—but he used the wrong kind of mile. It's like if you read a British recipe calling for a gallon of milk, but used an American gallon instead. The numbers look the same, but you're off by 20 percent."

### Mistake #2: Cherry-Picking the Wrong Source

**What Most Scholars Used:**
- Ptolemy's estimate: Earth's circumference = **180,000 stades**
- This was closer to correct

**What Columbus Preferred:**
- Alfraganus and Pierre d'Ailly: Much smaller estimates
- Why? Because it made his voyage seem feasible

**For spoken delivery:**
"Columbus basically Googled until he found the answer he wanted. Most scholars used Ptolemy's measurements. Columbus preferred a medieval Persian source that gave him a smaller number—not because it was more accurate, but because it made his trip to Asia look possible."

## What the Salamanca Scholars Actually Said

### The Real Debate (1486)

**NOT DISPUTED:** Earth's sphericity (both sides agreed it was round)
**ACTUALLY DISPUTED:** How far Columbus would have to sail

### The Commission's Correct Arguments:

1. **"The earth is too large"**
   - Based on Ptolemy: 180,000 stades circumference
   - Columbus's 21,000 miles was wrong
   - Reality: 24,901 miles (scholars were closer)

2. **"The ocean is too wide to cross"**
   - Distance to Asia: Not 2,500 miles, but ~12,500 miles
   - Ships couldn't carry enough food and water for 12,500 miles
   - This was a **logistical objection**, not a scientific one

3. **"Sailing down the curve would be impossible to return from"**
   - Some argued it would be "like sailing a ship to the top of a mountain"
   - This shows they understood the earth WAS curved
   - They worried about the distance, not falling off an edge

### The Irony

**The scholars at Salamanca were RIGHT to reject Columbus's plan.**

If the Americas hadn't existed exactly where they did, **Columbus's crew would have starved to death** in the middle of the Pacific Ocean.

## The Commission's Composition (To Debunk "Ignorant Monks" Myth)

### Who Was Actually There:

**Chairman:** Hernando de Talavera
- Queen Isabella's confessor
- Later became Archbishop of Granada

**Members:**
- Professors of **astronomy**
- Professors of **geography**
- Professors of **mathematics**
- **Rabbi Abraham Zacuto** (famous Jewish astronomer)

**What They Were:**
Ad hoc committee of experts, NOT a formal ecclesiastical council

**What They Weren't:**
- Not "shaved monks" citing Bible
- Not ignorant clerics afraid of heresy
- Not the Inquisition

## For Spoken Delivery: The Key Talking Points

### Opening (Set up the irony):

"Here's what actually happened at Salamanca in 1486. Columbus stood before a committee of geography professors, math professors, and astronomers—including a famous Jewish astronomer named Abraham Zacuto. And they told him his numbers were wrong.

Not that the earth was flat. That the earth was too **big**."

### The Calculation Error:

"Columbus thought the earth's circumference was about 21,000 miles. The actual number? Almost 25,000. He was off by about 4,000 miles—the distance from New York to London.

How did he mess this up? He used the wrong kind of mile. Medieval Arab astronomers had calculated that one degree of the earth's circle equals about 56 miles. Columbus read that number and used the Roman mile—which is about 30 percent shorter than the Arabic mile they were actually using.

It's like reading a British recipe that calls for a gallon of milk, but using an American gallon. The numbers look the same, but you're way off."

### Why the Scholars Rejected Him:

"The scholars at Salamanca did the math. If Columbus was right and Asia was 2,500 miles away, great—go for it. But if Ptolemy was right and it was actually 12,500 miles away, Columbus's ships couldn't carry enough food and water to make it.

They weren't afraid of heresy. They were afraid his crew would starve to death in the middle of the ocean.

Which is exactly what would have happened—if the Americas hadn't been sitting right where Columbus thought Asia was."

### The Punchline:

"Columbus was saved by being wrong in the right direction. He made a massive geographical error, hit a continent he didn't know existed, and then insisted until his dying day that he'd reached the East Indies.

The scholars who rejected his plan? They were doing their job. They were right."

## Specific Numbers for Script (With Sources)

### Columbus's Estimate:
- **Earth's circumference:** ~21,000 miles (25% too small)
- **Distance to Japan:** 2,500 miles (off by 10,000 miles!)
- **Based on:** Alfraganus (56⅔ miles per degree)
- **Error:** Used 4,856-foot Roman mile instead of 7,091-foot Arabic mile

### Actual Numbers:
- **Earth's circumference:** 24,901 miles at equator
- **Distance Spain to Japan:** ~12,500 miles going west
- **What Columbus hit:** Caribbean (~4,000 miles from Spain)

### Ptolemy's Estimate (Used by Salamanca scholars):
- **Circumference:** 180,000 stades
- **More accurate than Columbus** (though still not perfect)

### The Stakes:
- A voyage of 12,500 miles with 15th-century ships = **impossible without resupply**
- Salamanca scholars: "No ship can carry that much food"
- **They were RIGHT**
