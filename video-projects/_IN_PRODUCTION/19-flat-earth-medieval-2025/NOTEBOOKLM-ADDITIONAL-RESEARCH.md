## 1. Nuance in the "Handmaiden" Framework

To avoid "Whiggish" claims—the error of judging the past by its anticipation of modern science—medieval natural philosophy must be presented as an activity that was not "failed science," but rather a successful pursuit of its own specific goals.

### Non-Autonomy
Medieval natural philosophy was not intended to be autonomous. It was "not to be loved, but to be used" [When Science and Christianity Meet, p. 1541]. It served as a "way station" for developing the intellectual virtues required for theology [The Territories of Science and Religion, p. 981].

### Cognitive Formation
The study of nature was viewed as "cognitive formation"—a mental training to prepare the mind for direct theological insight [The Territories of Science and Religion, p. 992].

### Avoidance of Progressivism
One must avoid the claim that medieval thinkers were "scientists manqués." Their primary identity was as "philosophers" whose goal was "personal transformation" and "the building up of love" through the interpretation of "God's two books" (Nature and Scripture) [The Territories of Science and Religion, p. 989; The Foundations of Modern Science in the Middle Ages, p. 830].

## 2. Saint Augustine's Primary Source Warning

The exact passage regarding "talking nonsense" comes from Augustine's *De Genesi ad litteram* (*Literal Commentary on Genesis*), Book I, Chapter 19:

> "Usually, even a non-Christian knows something about the earth, the heavens, and the other elements of this world, about the motion and orbit of the stars and even their size and relative positions... now it is a disgraceful and dangerous thing for an infidel [non-Christian] to hear a Christian, presumably giving the meaning of Holy Scripture, talking nonsense on these topics; and we should take all means to prevent such an embarrassing situation, in which people show up vast ignorance in a Christian and laugh it to scorn."

[When Science and Christianity Meet, p. 550]

## 3. The Salamanca Commission (1486–1490)

The commission was an *ad hoc* committee, not a formal ecclesiastical council [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 624].

### Leadership and Members
- Headed by **Hernando de Talavera**, Queen Isabella's confessor and later Archbishop of Granada [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 624]
- Included professors of astronomy, geography, and mathematics
- Featured the famous Jewish astronomer **Rabbi Abraham Zacuto** [Flat-earth: the history of an infamous idea, p. 469; Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 672]

### Specific Measurement Objections
The commissioners correctly used the earth's sphericity to argue *against* Columbus. Based on Ptolemy's measurements, they argued:

1. The earth's circumference was far too large (Ptolemy's estimate was 180,000 stades) [The Beginnings of Western Science, p. 725]
2. The ocean sea was too wide to cross before supplies ran out [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 626]
3. A ship "sailing down" the curve of the globe would find it "impossible to return," as it would be like "sailing a ship to the top of a mountain" [Flat Earth or round sphere, p. 448; Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 668]

## 4. Visual Evidence Descriptions for B-roll

### The Ebstorf Map (13th Century)
The entire world is depicted as the **body of Christ**. Viewers should see:
- Christ's head at the top (East/Paradise)
- His hands extending to the North and South
- His feet at the bottom (West/Gibraltar)

[Flat Earth or round sphere, p. 456]

### Sacrobosco's "Ship and Mast" (Modena MS, 13th Century)
A diagram showing the **curvature of the sea**. A ship sails toward a coastal tower; the signal on the tower is visible to a man on the masthead, but invisible to a man on the deck because of the "bulge of the water" [Early Physics and Astronomy, p. 391; The Sphere of Sacrobosco and Its Commentators, p. 1414].

### Hildegard of Bingen's Cosmos (Wiesbaden MS B)
A striking **egg-shaped universe**. The earth is a small sphere at the very center, surrounded by layers of:
- "Pure ether"
- "Dark fire" (ignis niger)
- A shell of "bright fire" containing the outer planets

[Planets, stars, and orbs, p. 1052]

### The Globus Cruciger (11th Century Regalia)
Royal portraits of the Holy Roman Emperors. The monarch holds a **golden ball** (the globe) surmounted by a cross, indicating that Christ—and by extension the king—rules over a spherical earth [Flat-earth: the history of an infamous idea, p. 470; Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 651].

### Nicole Oresme (Paris MS fr. 565)
A miniature of Oresme in his study, sitting next to a large **armillary sphere** (a 3D skeletal model of the celestial spheres), proving he worked within a spherical framework [Medieval and Early Modern Science Vol. 2, p. 882].
