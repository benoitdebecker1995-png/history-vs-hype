## Description (First 3 Lines = Hook)

```
You probably learned in school that medieval people thought the earth was flat until Columbus proved them wrong.

That story appears in textbooks, Hollywood movies, and modern conspiracy theories. And it's completely fabricated.

I went through the medieval manuscripts—from Bede in 725 AD to university textbooks from 1230—and traced the lie to three specific men who invented it between 1828 and 1896.

---

THE MEDIEVAL EVIDENCE

The documents are clear. Medieval scholars explicitly described a spherical earth:

• Bede (725 AD) wrote the earth "resembles more a ball, being equally round in all directions" in The Reckoning of Time
• Isidore of Seville (630 AD) defined day as "the sun over the earth" and night as "the sun under the earth"—impossible on a flat disc
• Johannes de Sacrobosco's Tractatus de Sphaera (c. 1230) taught spherical earth astronomy as the standard university curriculum for 400 years, surviving in hundreds of manuscripts
• The globus cruciger—a royal orb showing a cross atop a sphere—appeared in European art from the 11th century onward

These weren't fringe opinions. This was mainstream medieval knowledge.

---

THE FABRICATION TIMELINE

So how did the flat earth myth enter our textbooks? Three men, three books, forty years:

1. Washington Irving (1828): A novelist—not a historian—invented a dramatic scene in his biography of Columbus. He portrayed Columbus facing "ignorant monks" at Salamanca who cited the Bible to prove the earth was flat. This scene never happened. The actual Salamanca commission included astronomers, mathematicians, and a famous Jewish scholar. They debated the SIZE of the earth, not the shape.

2. John William Draper (1874): Published "History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science" during the culture wars after Darwin's Origin of Species. Draper turned Irving's fiction into anti-Catholic propaganda, claiming medieval Christianity suppressed scientific knowledge. The book went through 50 printings in 50 years and was translated into 10+ languages.

3. Andrew Dickson White (1896): Co-founder of Cornell University, published a two-volume "History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom." White buried the flat earth myth under a mountain of footnotes, creating what historians call an "illusion of meticulous scholarship."

Between 1870 and 1920, this fabrication entered American and European textbooks. Jeffrey Burton Russell's survey of history textbooks found almost none mentioned the flat earth myth before 1870. After 1880, it appeared in nearly all of them.

---

WHY IT PERSISTS

Despite historians debunking this myth since the 1990s, it's still taught in schools and repeated in documentaries. Why?

• Presentism: We assume the past was more ignorant than it was
• Utility: The myth serves modern agendas (both secular and religious)
• The "Dark Ages" stereotype: Reinforces the idea that medieval Europe was backward

But there's a supreme irony here. The medieval flat earth myth was invented by 19th-century anticlerical rationalists to make religion look stupid. Today, it's primarily used by religious conspiracy theorists to argue that "experts have always lied to us." Modern flat earthers are attacking science using anticlerical propaganda—their enemies' weapon.

---

WHAT'S AT STAKE

This isn't just about correcting a historical error. Between 2020 and 2023, trust in science declined 15% in the United States. Today, 27% of Americans have little to no confidence in scientists.

Research on conspiracy radicalization shows that belief in flat earth rarely comes first. It comes after someone starts questioning mainstream narratives. And one of the first questions is often: "If they lied about medieval people believing the earth was flat, what else are they lying about?"

The medieval flat earth myth becomes a gateway—evidence that institutions deceive the public. And ironically, they're right about the deception. Just not in the way they think.

When we get medieval history this catastrophically wrong, we don't just misunderstand the past. We undermine trust in scholarship, education, and expertise itself.
```
