## DESCRIPTION (VidIQ Optimized)

```
The flat earth myth was invented in 1828. Here's who did it—and why schools still teach it.

You probably learned that medieval people thought the earth was flat until Columbus proved them wrong. That story appears in textbooks, Hollywood movies, and modern conspiracy theories.

It's completely fabricated.

I went through the medieval manuscripts—Bede (725 AD), Sacrobosco (1230), the royal orb held by kings—and traced this lie to three specific men who invented it between 1828 and 1896.

---

THE MEDIEVAL EVIDENCE

Medieval scholars explicitly described a spherical earth:

• Bede (725 AD): "The earth resembles more a ball, being equally round in all directions"
• Isidore of Seville (630 AD): Defined night as "the sun under the earth"—impossible on a flat disc
• Johannes de Sacrobosco's Tractatus de Sphaera (c. 1230): Standard university astronomy textbook for 400 years, surviving in hundreds of manuscripts
• The globus cruciger—a royal orb showing a cross atop a sphere—appeared in European coronation ceremonies from the 11th century

This wasn't fringe. This was mainstream medieval knowledge.

---

THE FABRICATION TIMELINE (1828-1896)

Three men turned fiction into textbook fact:

1. Washington Irving (1828): A novelist—not a historian—invented the Salamanca scene in his Columbus biography. He fabricated "ignorant monks" citing the Bible to prove the earth was flat. That scene never happened.

2. John William Draper (1874): Published "History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science" during the culture wars after Darwin. Turned Irving's fiction into anti-Catholic propaganda. 50+ printings.

3. Andrew Dickson White (1896): Cornell's co-founder buried the lie under a mountain of footnotes, giving it academic respectability.

Between 1870 and 1920, this fabrication entered every textbook.

---

WHY IT PERSISTS

Historian Jeffrey Burton Russell debunked this in 1991. It didn't matter.

The lie persists because:
• It's simple, dramatic, emotionally satisfying
• It serves the "Dark Ages vs Enlightenment" narrative
• Modern flat earthers now use it as evidence that "experts always lie"

The supreme irony: 19th-century anticlerical academics invented this myth to build up science's authority. Today, populist movements use it to tear science down.
```
