### AUDIO OVERVIEW 1: The Evidence (Act 1 Focus)

**Settings:**
- **Format:** Select "Deep Dive"
- **Length:** Select "Long"

**COPY-PASTE THIS INTO "What should the AI hosts focus on?" BOX:**

```
Create an engaging audio discussion about the PRIMARY SOURCES proving medieval scholars knew Earth was spherical. The goal is to SHOW evidence viewers have never seen, not just state the debunking.

FOCUS ON:
• Bede's 725 CE manuscript: What exact phrase did he use? ("like a ball, not a shield")
• Sacrobosco's De Sphaera: How widely used? Give specific manuscript/edition statistics
• Medieval university curriculum: Which universities taught this? What was standard?
• Isidore's Etymologies: What did he write about Earth's shape in 630 CE?
• Visual diagrams: Describe specific medieval cosmological diagrams that show spherical Earth

TONE:
Make it visual and concrete. Describe what manuscripts look like, what they say word-for-word, why this evidence is undeniable. Act like you're showing a viewer physical documents they've never encountered.

EMPHASIS:
• Focus on 8th-9th century sources (earlier = more impressive)
• Get exact quotes with book/chapter references
• Explain WHY this matters (these weren't secret - they were taught in every university)
• Provide statistics (how many manuscript copies, how many universities, how many years used)

EXCLUDE:
• Modern scholarship meta-discussion
• Don't spend time on "why the myth exists" (that's for later)
• Keep focus on SHOWING the evidence
```

**After it generates, use Interactive Mode to ask:**
- "Which piece of evidence would be most surprising to a modern viewer?"
- "What's the earliest clear statement of Earth's sphericity?"
- "Give me exact page numbers for the best quotes"
- "How can I explain Sacrobosco's 400-year influence visually?"

### AUDIO OVERVIEW 2: Myth Invention (Act 2 Focus)

**Settings:**
- **Format:** Select "Deep Dive"
- **Length:** Select "Long"

**COPY-PASTE THIS INTO "What should the AI hosts focus on?" BOX:**

```
Create an audio discussion analyzing how Washington Irving, John Draper, and Andrew Dickson White fabricated the flat-earth myth in the 19th century. Treat this as investigative journalism.

FOCUS ON:

EMPHASIS:

• Trace propagation: Book → textbooks → "common knowledge"
• Explain WHY they did it (anti-Catholic agenda, conflict thesis ideology)

PROVIDE:
• Page numbers for all quotes
• Publication dates and edition counts
• Names of historians who later debunked them
```

**After it generates, use Interactive Mode to ask:**

### AUDIO OVERVIEW 3: Why It Persists (Act 3 Focus)

**Settings:**
- **Format:** Select "Debate" (or "Critique" - either works for meta-analysis)
- **Length:** Select "Long"

**COPY-PASTE THIS INTO "What should the AI hosts focus on?" BOX:**

```
Create an audio discussion exploring the META-question: Why does the medieval flat-earth myth persist 30+ years after scholarly debunking (Russell 1991)?

This is the intellectual payoff. Go deeper than "the myth is false" to ask "why do myths persist?" Connect to modern conspiracy thinking and information ecology.

FOCUS ON:
• Psychology of "common knowledge": Why is false information sticky?
• Cognitive factors: Confirmation bias, availability heuristic, narrative appeal
• The myth's utility: How does it serve BOTH anti-religious AND anti-medieval narratives?
• Educational inertia: Why hasn't Russell's 1991 debunking reached textbooks?
• Lag between academic knowledge and public education (20-50 years)
• Modern flat-earth movement: How do conspiracists ironically cite this historical myth?
• The irony: Flat-earthers claim "they've been lying to you about history" while citing a 19th-century lie
• Connection to misinformation: What does this case study teach about fighting false history?

TONE:
Intellectual and analytical. This should feel relevant to 2025. Make connections to:
• How misinformation spreads today
• Why "debunking" often fails
• The relationship between conspiracy thinking and distrust of expertise

PROVIDE:
• Specific examples of the myth persisting (textbooks, media, popular culture)
• Analysis of WHY it's hard to correct
• Connections to modern information ecosystem
```

**After it generates, use Interactive Mode to ask:**
- "What's the psychological mechanism making this myth resistant to correction?"
- "Are there other historical myths with similar persistence patterns?"
- "How can educators effectively counter false 'common knowledge'?"
