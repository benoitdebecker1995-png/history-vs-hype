## ACT 2: THE MEDIEVAL EVIDENCE (1:15-5:00)

### [1:15-2:15] Greek Foundation - Eratosthenes

**[SHOW: Diagram of Eratosthenes experiment]**

The knowledge that the earth is a sphere didn't start in the Middle Ages. It started with the ancient Greeks—and they didn't just theorize. They measured it.

In 240 BC, Eratosthenes of Cyrene used sticks and shadows to calculate the earth's circumference. We know this from later Greek writers like Cleomedes and Strabo, who preserved his method.

At noon on the summer solstice in Syene—modern Aswan—a vertical stick cast no shadow. The sun was directly overhead.

But in Alexandria, at the exact same time, a stick cast a shadow at seven point two degrees.

**[SHOW: Calculation graphic]**

If the sun's rays are parallel, the only reason for different shadow angles is the curvature of the earth's surface. Seven point two degrees is one-fiftieth of a circle.

So the distance between the two cities—about 5,000 stadia—is one-fiftieth of the earth's circumference.

Total circumference: 250,000 stadia. Roughly 29,000 miles.

The actual value? 24,901 miles.

In 240 BC, using sticks and shadows, he got within 15 percent.

This wasn't secret knowledge. This was standard geography taught throughout the classical world—and inherited by medieval scholars.

### [2:15-3:00] Isidore's Embedded Assumption (630 AD)

**[SHOW: Manuscript page from Etymologies]**

Jump to the year 630. The "Dark Ages" have supposedly begun.

Isidore of Seville writes the *Etymologies*—the encyclopedia of the Middle Ages. If the flat earth myth were true, this is where it would be written down.

But when you actually read it, you find something different.

Isidore defines day and night in Book III, Chapter 51:

**[ON SCREEN: "When the sun rises, it creates the day, and when it sets it brings on the night, for day is the sun over the earth, and night is the sun under the earth." — Etymologies III.51, p. 102]**

The sun *under* the earth.

If the earth were a flat disk, how would you explain night? The sun goes behind a mountain? Gets extinguished?

It doesn't go *under* the ground you're standing on.

For the sun to pass under the earth, the earth has to be three-dimensional. It requires a globe.

Isidore uses this definition without explanation—suggesting it was so basic, so universally understood, that it didn't need defending.

### [3:00-4:15] Sacrobosco's University Textbook (1230)

**[SHOW: 13th-century manuscript of De Sphaera]**

Now jump to 1230. Johannes de Sacrobosco writes *Tractatus de Sphaera*—Treatise on the Sphere.

Here's the critical thing: This was the standard astronomy textbook for four hundred years.

Required reading at every university in Europe—Paris, Oxford, Bologna, Salamanca.

How many students studied this? Hundreds of thousands. In the German universities alone, a quarter million from 1350 onward.

The text survives in hundreds of manuscripts. After the printing press, it was printed 30 times in Latin before 1500 alone.

What did it teach?

Chapter 1 provides sensory proofs for the globe. The most famous: the ship and mast observation.

**[SHOW: Medieval diagram of ship approaching shore]**

Sacrobosco writes:

**[ON SCREEN: "When a ship approaches land, a signal on shore is visible from the masthead after it has disappeared from the sight of someone at the foot of the mast. And there is no other explanation of this thing than the bulge of the water." — Tractatus de Sphaera, Chapter 1, p. 123]**

Medieval students weren't debating whether the earth was round. They were learning spherical geometry—how latitude changes which stars you see, how the celestial equator intersects the horizon differently in Cairo versus London.

You cannot explain these observations without a precise three-dimensional model of a spherical earth.

### [4:15-5:00] The Royal Orb & The Outliers

**[SHOW: Medieval coronation portrait with globus cruciger]**

But maybe you're thinking—okay, scholars knew. What about regular people?

It was public. Literally in the king's hand.

This is the *globus cruciger*—the royal orb. A golden sphere surmounted by a cross. Medieval kings held this in coronation ceremonies from the 11th century onward.

What did it represent? Isidore of Seville explained: "Augustus is said to have established the orb as a standard... so that he might the more display the figure of the globe."

The sphere represented the earth. The cross represented Christ's sovereignty over it.

**[BRIEF MENTION]**

Were there any medieval writers who thought the earth was flat?

Yes. Two marginal figures: Lactantius in the 4th century and Cosmas Indicopleustes in the 6th.

As historian Jeffrey Burton Russell put it: Cosmas had "no followers whatsoever."

They were outliers. Ignored by the mainstream.

The consensus was clear: spherical earth.

## ACT 3: THE REAL COLUMBUS DEBATE (5:00-7:30)

### [5:00-5:45] Setting Up the Myth

**[SHOW: Popular culture depiction - Columbus defending round earth]**

Most people have heard some version of the same story.

Columbus, standing in front of churchmen who insist the earth is flat, arguing that he won't sail off the edge of the world.

That scene is usually presented as medieval ignorance versus modern thinking.

What almost nobody is told is that this scene is supposed to have a specific setting.

A meeting in 1486, known as the Council of Salamanca.

And once you know that, an obvious question follows.

If medieval scholars already knew the earth was round—which we just proved they did—what was Columbus actually arguing about at Salamanca?

### [5:45-6:30] Columbus's Calculation Error

**[SHOW: Map comparing Columbus's world vs reality]**

Not shape. Size.

Columbus made a catastrophic double error—and we know this from his own writings and navigational logs that survive.

**Error #1: He thought the earth was smaller.**
- Columbus's estimate: 20,400 miles circumference
- Actual: 24,901 miles
- He was off by about 4,500 miles—farther than the distance from New York to London

How? He used measurements from a medieval Persian geographer named Alfraganus, who said one degree equals 56⅔ miles.

But Columbus thought Alfraganus meant the Roman mile. Alfraganus actually meant the Arabic mile—which was nearly 50 percent longer.

This compounded his error. Columbus estimated the voyage at about 20 percent of its actual length.

**Error #2: He thought Asia was bigger.**

He wildly overestimated how far Asia extended east.

Consequently, Columbus thought the distance from Spain to Japan was maybe 2,500 miles.

The scholars at Salamanca correctly calculated it was closer to 12,500 miles.

### [6:30-7:30] The Scholars Were Right - And Our Sources

**[SHOW: Portrait of Hernando de Talavera]**

The royal commission at Salamanca wasn't a panel of ignorant monks citing the Bible.

It was headed by Hernando de Talavera, Queen Isabella's confessor. The commission included professors of astronomy, geography, mathematics—including the Jewish astronomer Rabbi Abraham Zacuto.

How do we know what they argued?

We know from two main sources: Bartolomé de Las Casas—a Spanish historian and Dominican friar who had access to Columbus's papers and interviewed people who were there—and Ferdinand Columbus, Christopher's own son, who wrote a biography of his father.

According to these accounts, the commission told Columbus his numbers were wrong. Not that the earth was flat. That it was too *big*.

They correctly argued that if the ocean was 12,500 miles wide, Columbus's ships couldn't carry enough food and water. His crew would starve.

They were right.

Columbus was saved by dumb luck. If the Americas hadn't been sitting exactly where he thought Asia was, his voyage would have been a complete disaster.

But history doesn't like stories about optimistic failures who get lucky.

History likes heroes battling ignorance.

### [7:30-7:45] Irving's Fabrication

**[CRITICAL]**

Washington Irving gave history that hero. In 1828, he invented an entire scene—Columbus at the Council of Salamanca, arguing with foolish churchmen who insisted the earth must be flat, pounding tables, citing scripture.

A scene that never happened.

Historian Samuel Eliot Morison later debunked it: "This is pure moonshine. Washington Irving, scenting his opportunity for a picturesque and moving scene, took a fictitious account... and let his imagination go completely."

But it was a bestseller. The dramatic scene burned into the public imagination as historical fact.

Irving gave the world a simple story: Enlightened Columbus versus flat-earth religious zealots.

It was fiction. But it was useful fiction.

And that brings us to the perpetrators—and the question: Why was this lie so powerful?

## ACT 4: THE PERPETRATORS & THE POLITICAL WEAPON (7:45-10:45)

### [7:45-8:45] Perpetrator #1: Washington Irving (1828) - The Romanticizer

**[SHOW: Title page of Irving's Columbus, 1828]**

Washington Irving was a novelist. Not a historian.

In 1828, he published *A History of the Life and Voyages of Christopher Columbus*. He was writing a romantic epic for a young American nation looking for heroic founding figures.

But every hero needs a villain. And a dry logistical debate about the earth's circumference? Not very dramatic.

So he fabricated the Salamanca confrontation. Columbus forced to argue against ignorant clerics insisting the earth was flat.

It sold 10,000 copies immediately.

Irving created the story. But why did it have such staying power?

Because it was waiting for someone to weaponize it.

### [8:45-10:00] Perpetrator #2: John William Draper (1874) - The Ideological Enforcer

**[SHOW: Title page of Draper's *Conflict*, 1874]**

Forty-six years later, John William Draper takes Irving's fiction and turns it into a weapon.

In 1874, Draper publishes *History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science*. The title says it all. This book was designed to prove that science and religion are fundamentally incompatible—locked in perpetual war.

And to prove this massive thesis, Draper needed historical evidence. Smoking guns showing religion actively suppressing scientific truth.

Irving's fabricated scene was sitting there, waiting.

**[CRITICAL - POLITICAL CONTEXT]**

Draper was writing with a very specific target: the Roman Catholic Church.

His book came out just four years after the Vatican declared Papal Infallibility in 1870—a decision that caused massive backlash among liberal intellectuals.

Draper seized the moment. He used the flat earth myth as Exhibit A. The Church had systematically opposed scientific progress throughout history. The Council of Salamanca was his star witness.

The book was a bestseller. Went through over fifty printings. It armed a whole generation of secular thinkers with what they thought was undeniable historical ammunition.

Irving created the story. Draper provided the ideological engine.

### [10:00-10:45] Perpetrator #3: Andrew Dickson White (1896) - The Propagandist

**[SHOW: Title page of White's *Warfare*, 1896]**

Twenty-two years later, Andrew Dickson White provides the final piece: institutional backing.

White's massive two-volume work, *A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom*, came out in 1896.

Modern historians call it "more propaganda than history."

White was the co-founder and first president of Cornell University—a major force in the movement for secular higher education in America.

His technique? Sheer volume.

He buried the flat earth story in an avalanche of footnotes and historical anecdotes—many cherry-picked, misleading, or just wrong. He created an impression of overwhelming scholarly evidence.

By citing Irving and Draper, White gave the fiction an air of academic respectability.

And thereby, the lie flows from popular book to scholarly framework to textbook to common knowledge.

**[NEW SECTION - WHY THIS MYTH WAS SO USEFUL]**

### [10:45-11:15] The Cultural Weapon - What Need Did This Fulfill?

**[SHOW: Timeline - 1859 Darwin, 1870 Papal Infallibility, 1874 Draper, 1896 White]**

Why did this specific myth become orthodoxy? Why did it beat out seventy years of solid historical evidence to the contrary?

Because it fulfilled a powerful cultural need.

In the late 1800s, intellectual elites were fighting a culture war. Darwin's *Origin of Species* came out in 1859. The Catholic Church declared Papal Infallibility in 1870.

Draper and White weren't neutral historians. They were aggressive participants. They used the flat earth myth to prove a dethronement narrative—that brave science overcomes backward religion.

The flat earth story was the perfect weapon. It was simple. Dramatic. Emotionally satisfying.

It served a secular "story of redemption"—contrasting the "Dark Ages" with the "sunlit uplands" of modern reason.

And once it entered textbooks, it became nearly impossible to remove.

Which brings us to the question: If historians debunked this thirty years ago, why is it still being taught?
