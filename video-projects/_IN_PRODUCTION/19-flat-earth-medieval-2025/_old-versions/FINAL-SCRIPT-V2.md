## ACT 2: THE VICTIM'S ALIBI - Medieval Scholars Were Innocent (4-5 minutes)

### [2:00-3:00] Greek Foundation - Eratosthenes and the Sticks

The knowledge that the earth is a sphere didn't start in the Middle Ages. It started with the ancient Greeks—and they didn't just theorize about it. They measured it.

[SHOW: Diagram of Eratosthenes experiment]

In 240 BC, Eratosthenes of Cyrene, chief librarian at the Library of Alexandria, heard a report. At noon on the summer solstice in the town of Syene—modern Aswan—a vertical stick cast no shadow. The sun was directly overhead.

But in Alexandria, at the exact same time, a stick did cast a shadow.

Eratosthenes made a brilliant assumption: The sun is so far away that its rays are essentially parallel. So if the rays are parallel, the only reason for different shadow angles is the curvature of the earth's surface.

He measured the shadow angle in Alexandria. Seven point two degrees. That's exactly one-fiftieth of a full circle.

[SHOW: Calculation on screen]

So if the angle is one-fiftieth of a circle, the distance between the two cities must be one-fiftieth of the earth's total circumference.

The distance was about 5,000 stadia. Multiply by fifty. Total circumference: 250,000 stadia—roughly 29,000 miles.

The actual value today? 24,860 miles.

In 240 BC, using sticks and shadows, Eratosthenes calculated the earth's circumference to within 15 percent.

This wasn't secret knowledge. This was standard geography taught throughout the classical world—in Greece, in Rome, and inherited by medieval scholars.

### [3:00-4:00] Medieval Evidence Part 1 - Isidore's Embedded Assumption

[SHOW: Manuscript page from Etymologies]

Let's jump to the year 630. The "Dark Ages" have supposedly begun. The Western Roman Empire has collapsed.

This is when Isidore of Seville writes the *Etymologies*—the encyclopedia of the Middle Ages. If the flat earth myth were true, this is the book where it would be written down and taught for centuries.

But when you actually read it, you find something different.

Isidore defines day and night in Book III, Chapter 51. Here's the quote:

> "Day is the sun over the earth, and night is the sun under the earth."

[Pause]

The sun *under* the earth.

If the earth were a flat disk, how would you explain night? In flat earth models, the sun either goes behind a mountain, shrinks away, or gets extinguished.

But it doesn't go *under* the ground you're standing on.

For the sun to pass under the earth, the earth has to be a three-dimensional object with a lower hemisphere for the sun to travel through. It requires a globe.

Isidore uses this definition without any explanation—which suggests it was so basic, so universally understood among educated people, that it didn't need defending.

### [4:00-5:30] Medieval Evidence Part 2 - Sacrobosco's University Textbook

[SHOW: 13th-century manuscript of De Sphaera]

Now jump forward to 1230. We're in the High Middle Ages—the era of universities.

This is Johannes de Sacrobosco's *Tractatus de Sphaera*—Treatise on the Sphere. Written around 1230.

And here's the critical thing: This was the standard astronomy textbook for four hundred years. Required reading at every university in Europe—Paris, Oxford, Bologna, Salamanca.

How many students studied this? Between 1350 and 1500 alone, about 750,000 students went through European universities. All of them studied Sacrobosco.

The text survives in hundreds of manuscripts. After the printing press was invented, it went through eighty different editions.

And what did it teach?

Chapter 1 provides sensory proofs for the globe. The most famous: the ship and mast observation.

[SHOW: Medieval diagram of ship approaching shore]

Sacrobosco writes:

> "When a ship approaches land, a signal on shore is visible from the masthead after it has disappeared from the sight of someone at the foot of the mast. Of this there is no other cause than the bulge of the earth."

This is direct observational evidence. Medieval students weren't debating whether the earth was round. They were learning the complex mathematics of spherical geometry—how latitude changes which stars you see, how the celestial equator intersects the horizon differently in Cairo versus London.

Sacrobosco discusses the *rectosphere*—the "right sphere" you experience standing at the equator, where stars rise perpendicular to the horizon. And the *oblique sphere*—what you see in Europe, where stars rise at an angle.

You cannot explain these observations without a precise three-dimensional model of a spherical earth inside spherical heavens.

### [5:30-6:30] The Royal Orb - Public Symbol

[SHOW: Medieval coronation portraits with globus cruciger]

But maybe you're thinking—okay, monks and university scholars knew. But what about regular people? Wasn't it kept secret?

No. It was public. Literally in the king's hand.

[SHOW: 11th-century Holy Roman Emperor holding orb]

This is the *globus cruciger*—the royal orb. A golden sphere surmounted by a cross. Medieval kings held this in coronation ceremonies from the 11th century onward.

What did it represent?

Isidore of Seville explained in his *Etymologies*:

> "Augustus established the orb as a standard... to display the figure of the globe."

The sphere represented the earth. The cross represented Christ's sovereignty over it. This was a public symbol that assumed everyone understood the earth was a ball.

So we have:
- 240 BC: Greeks measure it with sticks and shadows
- 630 AD: Isidore embeds spherical assumptions in basic definitions
- 1230 AD: University textbook teaches spherical geometry to hundreds of thousands
- 11th century onward: Kings hold a globe in public ceremonies

The spherical earth wasn't controversial. It wasn't secret. It was the consensus.

## ACT 3: THE REAL COLUMBUS DEBATE - They Were Right, He Was Wrong (2-3 minutes)

### [6:30-7:30] Columbus's Catastrophic Double Error

[SHOW: Map comparing Columbus's imagined world vs reality]

So if the earth's shape was settled, what was Columbus arguing about at Salamanca in 1486?

Not shape. Size.

Columbus made a catastrophic double error.

**Error #1: He thought the earth was smaller.**
- Columbus's estimate: ~21,000 miles circumference
- Actual: 24,901 miles
- He was off by about 4,000 miles—the distance from New York to London

How did he mess this up? He used measurements from a medieval Persian geographer named Alfraganus, who said one degree of the earth's circumference equals 56⅔ miles.

But Columbus thought Alfraganus meant the Roman mile—4,856 feet. Alfraganus actually meant the Arabic mile—7,091 feet.

It's like reading a British recipe that calls for a gallon of milk, but using an American gallon. The numbers look the same, but you're off by 25 percent.

**Error #2: He thought Asia was bigger.**
He wildly overestimated how far Asia extended east, which meant the ocean between Europe and Asia looked much smaller.

Consequently, Columbus thought the distance from Spain to Japan was maybe 2,500 miles. The scholars at Salamanca correctly calculated it was closer to 12,500 miles.

### [7:30-8:30] The Scholars Were Right

[SHOW: Portrait of commission members]

The royal commission at Salamanca wasn't a panel of ignorant monks citing the Bible.

It was headed by Hernando de Talavera, Queen Isabella's confessor and later Archbishop of Granada. The commission included professors of astronomy, geography, and mathematics—including a famous Jewish astronomer named Rabbi Abraham Zacuto.

And they told Columbus his numbers were wrong.

Not that the earth was flat. That the earth was too *big*.

They correctly argued that if the ocean was 12,500 miles wide, Columbus's ships couldn't carry enough food and water to make it. His crew would starve to death in the middle of the ocean.

They were doing their job. They were right.

Columbus was saved by being wrong in the right direction. If the Americas hadn't been sitting exactly where he thought Asia was, his voyage would have been a complete disaster—just like the experts predicted.

But history doesn't like stories about optimistic failures who get lucky. History likes heroes battling the forces of darkness.

And that need for a hero leads us directly to Perpetrator #1.

## ACT 4: THE PERPETRATORS - How the Lie Was Built (3-4 minutes)

### [8:30-9:30] Perpetrator #1: Washington Irving (1828) - The Romanticizer

[SHOW: Title page of Irving's Columbus biography, 1828]

Washington Irving was a novelist. Not a historian.

In 1828, he published *A History of the Life and Voyages of Christopher Columbus*. He wasn't trying to write dry, factual history. He was writing a romantic epic for a young American nation looking for heroic founding figures.

But every hero needs a villain. And a dry logistical debate about the earth's circumference? Not very dramatic.

So Irving invented an entire scene out of thin air.

He fabricated a confrontation at the Council of Salamanca where Columbus is forced to argue against ignorant, dogmatic medieval clerics pounding the table, insisting the earth must be flat, citing scripture to prove it.

Historian Samuel Eliot Morison later called Irving's account "pure moonshine."

But it was a massive bestseller. It sold 10,000 copies immediately. The dramatic scene burned into the public imagination as historical fact.

Irving gave the world a simple, black-and-white story: Enlightened Columbus versus flat-earth religious zealots.

It was fiction. But it was useful fiction.

### [9:30-10:30] Perpetrator #2: John William Draper (1874) - The Ideological Enforcer

[SHOW: Title page of Draper's *Conflict*, 1874]

Forty-six years later, John William Draper takes Irving's fiction and weaponizes it.

In 1874, Draper publishes *History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science*. The title says it all. This was designed to prove that science and religion are fundamentally incompatible—locked in perpetual war.

And to prove this massive thesis, Draper needed evidence. He needed historical smoking guns showing religion actively suppressing scientific truth.

Irving's story was sitting there, waiting for him.

Draper was writing with a specific target: the Roman Catholic Church. His book came out just four years after the Vatican declared Papal Infallibility in 1870—a decision that caused massive backlash among liberal intellectuals.

Draper seized the moment. He used the flat earth myth as Exhibit A, claiming the Church had systematically opposed scientific progress throughout history, with the Council of Salamanca as his star witness.

The book was a bestseller. Went through dozens of editions. It armed a whole generation of secular thinkers with what they thought was undeniable historical ammunition.

Irving created the story. Draper provided the ideological engine that gave it speed and a destination.

### [10:30-11:30] Perpetrator #3: Andrew Dickson White (1896) - The Propagandist

[SHOW: Title page of White's *Warfare*, 1896]

Twenty-two years later, Andrew Dickson White provides the final piece: institutional backing.

White's massive two-volume work, *A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom*, came out in 1896. Modern historians call it "more propaganda than history."

White was the co-founder and first president of Cornell University—a major force in the movement for secular higher education in America. His work had a clear political aim: undermine the authority of religious institutions that had dominated universities for centuries.

His technique? Sheer volume.

He took the flat earth story and buried it in an avalanche of footnotes and historical anecdotes—many cherry-picked, misleading, or just wrong. He created an impression of overwhelming scholarly evidence.

A reader would be so overwhelmed by the size and authoritative tone, they'd feel compelled to accept the thesis.

By citing Irving and Draper, White gave the fiction an air of academic respectability.

And thereby, the lie flows from popular book to scholarly framework to textbook to common knowledge.

## ACT 5: HOW THE CRIME SUCCEEDED - Textbook Takeover (2 minutes)

### [11:30-12:30] The 1870-1920 Timeline

[SHOW: Chart - Pre-1870 vs Post-1880 textbooks]

Historian Jeffrey Burton Russell surveyed 19th-century American history textbooks. Here's what he found:

Before 1870: Very few mentioned the flat earth myth.

After 1880: Almost all of them featured the legend.

What changed between 1870 and 1880?

- 1859: Darwin publishes *Origin of Species* (culture wars begin)
- 1870: Papal Infallibility declared (anti-Catholic backlash)
- 1874: Draper's *Conflict* becomes a bestseller
- 1896: White's *Warfare* cements the narrative

The flat earth myth became useful. It served a secular "story of redemption"—contrasting the "Dark Ages" with the "sunlit uplands" of modern reason.

It fulfilled a cultural need to believe in the "superiority of the present" by making our ancestors seem stupid enough to think the world was flat.

And once it entered textbooks, it became nearly impossible to remove.

### [12:30-13:00] Why It Persists - Presentism and Utility

The myth persists because it's useful.

For 19th-century secularists, it proved religion opposes science.

For modern conspiracy theorists, it proves experts lie.

For textbook publishers, it's a simple, dramatic story that's easy to teach.

Everyone gets something from the myth. Consequently, it's impervious to evidence.

Historians have been debunking this since the 1990s. Jeffrey Burton Russell's *Inventing the Flat Earth* came out in 1991. That's over 30 years of correction.

The lie is still being taught.
