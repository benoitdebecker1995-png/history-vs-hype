## ACT 1: THE HOOK - Both Extremes Wrong (2-3 minutes)

### [0:00-0:45] Opening - The Textbook Lie

You probably learned in school that Christopher Columbus had to prove the earth was round to ignorant medieval priests. That story appeared in almost every American textbook after 1880.

And it's completely made up.

Here's the question: Did medieval people believe the earth was flat?

And here's what you'll hear:

One side says yes—medieval Christians were so ignorant and superstitious that they thought the world was a flat disc. The Church suppressed science. The "Dark Ages" meant a thousand years of stupidity.

The other side dismisses this as pure Hollywood myth. They'll claim medieval scholars were basically modern scientists, doing experiments and making discoveries that anticipated the Scientific Revolution.

I went to the academic sources. Jeffrey Burton Russell's *Inventing the Flat Earth*. Chris Wickham's *Medieval Europe*. Bede's *The Reckoning of Time* from the 8th century. The actual manuscripts of Johannes de Sacrobosco—the standard university astronomy textbook for four hundred years.

When you examine that evidence, both extremes oversimplify what actually happened.

### [0:45-1:30] The Real Story Setup

Medieval people—or at least everyone with an education—knew the earth was a sphere. They had geometric proofs. They had sensory observations. They had it literally in the king's hand as a symbol of royal power.

But they weren't modern scientists. They studied the natural world as preparation for theology, not to overthrow authority or discover new facts through experiments.

The flat earth myth? That was invented in the 1800s. By novelists and polemicists who wanted to win culture wars after Darwin. And it worked so well that we're still teaching it in schools 150 years later.

Here's how they did it. Here's why it persists. And here's what's actually at stake when we get medieval history this catastrophically wrong.

### [1:30-2:30] The Fabrication Timeline Preview

Between 1870 and 1920, something dramatic happened to American history textbooks.

Historian Jeffrey Burton Russell surveyed nineteenth-century textbooks and found that very few mentioned the flat earth myth before 1870. After 1880? Almost all of them featured the legend.

[SHOW: Timeline graphic - Pre-1870 vs Post-1880]

What changed?

Three things:

First, Washington Irving published a fictionalized biography of Columbus in 1828 that invented a dramatic scene—Columbus facing bigoted monks who cited the Bible to prove the earth was flat.

Second, Darwin published *Origin of Species* in 1859, which triggered culture wars between science and religion.

Third, two polemicists—John William Draper in 1874 and Andrew Dickson White in 1896—weaponized Irving's fiction to create what historians call the "Conflict Thesis"—the idea that science and religion are in perpetual warfare.

The flat earth myth became their primary example. Their "canonical homily," as one historian put it.

But first, let me show you what medieval people actually believed—using their own words, from the 8th, 12th, and 13th centuries.

## ACT 2: MEDIEVAL EVIDENCE - The Spherical Consensus (3-4 minutes)

### [2:30-3:30] Bede - The Playing Ball (8th Century)

[SHOW: 9th-century manuscript page, St. Gall MS 250]

This is a 9th-century manuscript of Bede's *The Reckoning of Time*—written in 725 AD. Here's what it says about the earth's shape, Chapter 32:

[SHOW: Latin text on screen, then English translation]

> "The earth is not merely circular like a shield... but resembles more a ball, being equally round in all directions."

The Latin word is *pilae*—a playing ball. Bede explicitly rejected the flat disc model in favor of a three-dimensional sphere.

And this wasn't some fringe view. Bede was the most influential scientist of the early Middle Ages. His work was copied hundreds of times and taught in monastery schools across Europe for centuries.

So if anyone tells you "Dark Ages Europeans thought the earth was flat," show them this. From the year 725.

### [3:30-5:00] Sacrobosco - The University Standard (13th Century)

[SHOW: 13th-century manuscript, Modena Biblioteca Estense]

Now let's jump forward to the High Middle Ages—the era of universities.

This is Johannes de Sacrobosco's *Tractatus de Sphaera*—Treatise on the Sphere. Written around 1230. And here's the critical thing: this was the standard astronomy textbook for four hundred years. It was required reading at every university in Europe.

How many students studied this? Between 1350 and 1500 alone, about 750,000 students went through European universities. All of them studied Sacrobosco.

And here's what Sacrobosco taught—Chapter 1:

> "That the earth is round is shown thus... when a ship approaches land, a signal on shore is visible from the masthead after it has disappeared from the sight of someone at the foot of the mast. Of this there is no other cause than the bulge of the earth."

[SHOW: Ship and mast diagram from medieval manuscript]

This is a sensory proof. Not abstract philosophy. Medieval students were taught to observe the curvature of the ocean with their own eyes.

The text survives in hundreds of manuscripts. Not one or two rare copies—hundreds. This was mainstream medieval education.

### [5:00-6:15] The Globus Cruciger - Royal Symbolism

[SHOW: Medieval royal portraits with orb]

But maybe you're thinking—okay, monks and university scholars knew. But what about regular people? Wasn't it kept secret from the masses?

No. It was public. Literally in the king's hand.

[SHOW: 11th-century Holy Roman Emperor holding orb]

This is the *globus cruciger*—the royal orb. A golden sphere surmounted by a cross. Medieval kings held this in coronation portraits and official ceremonies from the 11th century onward.

What did it represent? Isidore of Seville explained in his *Etymologies*, Book 18, Chapter 3:

> "Augustus established the orb as a standard... to display the figure of the globe."

The sphere represented the earth. The cross represented Christ's sovereignty over it. This was a public symbol of royal power that assumed—required—that everyone understood the earth was a ball.

So we have:
- 8th-century monastic teaching (Bede)
- 13th-century university curriculum (Sacrobosco)
- 11th-century royal symbolism (globus cruciger)

All of them assume a spherical earth. This wasn't controversial. It wasn't debated. It was the consensus.

### [6:15-7:00] What About Columbus and Salamanca?

[SHOW: Portrait of Columbus]

So what about the famous story—Columbus facing the Church at Salamanca, proving the earth was round?

The story is fiction. Written by Washington Irving in 1828.

Here's what actually happened at Salamanca in 1486.

Columbus stood before a royal commission—not monks citing the Bible, but professors of astronomy, geography, and mathematics. Including a famous Jewish astronomer named Rabbi Abraham Zacuto.

And they told Columbus his numbers were wrong.

Not that the earth was flat. That the earth was too *big*.

Columbus thought the earth's circumference was about 21,000 miles. The actual number is almost 25,000. He was off by about 4,000 miles—the distance from New York to London.

Consequently, Columbus thought he could sail to Japan in a few weeks. The scholars at Salamanca correctly calculated it would take months—longer than any ship could carry supplies.

The scholars were doing their job. They were right. Columbus made a massive geographical error, hit a continent he didn't know existed, and insisted until his dying day that he'd reached the East Indies.

He was saved by being wrong in the right direction.

## ACT 3: THE FABRICATION - How the Myth Was Built (3-4 minutes)

### [7:00-8:00] Washington Irving (1828) - The Novelist

[SHOW: Title page of Irving's Columbus biography, 1828]

So if medieval people knew the earth was round, where did the flat earth myth come from?

It starts with Washington Irving. A fiction writer—not a historian.

In 1828, Irving published *A History of the Life and Voyages of Christopher Columbus*. And he invented the scene at Salamanca—Columbus facing "monastic bigotry" from "shaved monks" who cited the Bible to prove the earth was flat.

Historian Samuel Eliot Morison later called Irving's account "pure moonshine."

But it was compelling. Dramatic. Irving sold 10,000 copies immediately. The story spread.

And thereby, a novelist's invention became accepted as historical fact.

### [8:00-9:30] Draper and White (1874-1896) - The Weaponizers

[SHOW: Title pages of Draper's *Conflict* and White's *Warfare*]

But Irving was just telling a good story. The real weaponization came later—after Darwin.

In 1874, John William Draper published *History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science*. He was angry about the Catholic Church's 1870 declaration of Papal Infallibility. So he used Irving's flat earth fiction as "proof" that the Church always opposed science.

The book went through 50 printings in 50 years. Translated into ten languages. It created what historians call the "Conflict Thesis"—the idea that science and religion are in perpetual warfare.

Then in 1896, Andrew Dickson White—founder of Cornell, the first secular American university—published *A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom*.

White was under attack from religious critics for making Cornell secular. So he decided, in his own words, to "smite his enemies with history."

He used the flat earth myth as his primary example. He falsely claimed that the "great majority" of Church Fathers "took fright" at the spherical earth and used the Bible to "crush" the concept.

This was completely wrong. Augustine explicitly warned Christians in the 4th century NOT to use the Bible as a science textbook. Here's what Augustine wrote in *De Genesi ad litteram*, Book 1, Chapter 19:

[SHOW: Quote on screen]

> "It is a disgraceful and dangerous thing for an infidel to hear a Christian, presumably giving the meaning of Holy Scripture, talking nonsense on these topics."

Augustine said that in the year 400. Not 1400. Not during the "Renaissance." In late antiquity, before the "Dark Ages" supposedly began.

But White ignored this. He had hundreds of footnotes—creating what one historian called an "illusion of meticulous scholarship"—but the footnotes didn't actually support his claims.

### [9:30-10:30] The Textbook Takeover (1870-1920)

[SHOW: Chart of textbook adoption]

And it worked.

Historian Jeffrey Burton Russell surveyed American history textbooks. Before 1870, very few mentioned the flat earth myth. After 1880, almost all of them did.

One 1898 textbook by John J. Anderson directly paraphrased Irving's fiction, claiming medieval scholars believed "the earth was flat like a plate."

Which meant that by 1900, every American schoolchild was learning a fabricated story from the 1820s, weaponized in the 1870s, to win culture wars about Darwin.

And we're still teaching it today.

## ACT 4: WHY IT PERSISTS - The Utility of Myths (2-3 minutes)

### [10:30-11:30] Presentism and Narrative Utility

So historians have been debunking this since the 1990s. Jeffrey Burton Russell's *Inventing the Flat Earth* came out in 1991. That's over 30 years of correction.

Why does the myth survive?

First: Presentism. Historian Russell defines this as "the tendency of a culture to congratulate itself on its superiority" by inventing an ignorant past.

By believing our ancestors were stupid enough to think the world was flat, we feel smarter. More advanced. We get to pat ourselves on the back for being born in the right century.

Second: Narrative utility. The myth is useful.

For New Atheist authors like Sam Harris and Christopher Hitchens, the flat earth myth proves religion is "an engine of repression." For conspiracy theorists, it proves experts lie and "they" suppress truth. For textbook publishers, it's a simple, dramatic story that "puts the phenomena first."

Everyone gets something from the myth. Consequently, it's nearly impervious to evidence.

### [11:30-12:30] The Cycle of Myths

Third: The flat earth myth is part of what historians call a "cycle of myths."

It reinforces the "Dark Ages" taxonomy—the idea that a thousand years were lost to "ecclesiastical darkness." Which reinforces the "Conflict Thesis"—that science and religion are always at war. Which reinforces the flat earth myth. Which reinforces the Dark Ages narrative.

Once these myths interlock, they form what one historian calls a "social imaginary"—a shared false vision of the past that shapes how we think about the present.

Stephen Jay Gould called the human mind a "dichotomizing machine." We want clear categories. Good vs. evil. Reason vs. superstition. Progress vs. regression.

The flat earth myth gives us that. It makes history simple. Dramatic. Satisfying.

The actual history—medieval scholars studying Aristotle and Ptolemy, using spherical earth proofs to calculate church calendars, holding rational debates about whether the earth rotated—is more complex. Less satisfying.

But it's true.

## ACT 5: MODERN CONSEQUENCES - What's At Stake (2-3 minutes)

### [12:30-13:30] The Trust Crisis and Conspiracy Pathways

So why does this matter? It's just medieval history, right?

Wrong.

Between 2020 and 2023, trust in science dropped 15 percent. Today, 27 percent of Americans have little to no confidence in scientists.

And one of the tools undermining that trust is this exact myth.

Modern flat earthers—and yes, they exist on TikTok right now—claim "the Church used to teach the earth was flat" as proof that experts suppress truth. They use this to justify rejecting climate science, vaccine research, evolution.

Here's the irony: The medieval flat earth myth was invented by 19th-century scientific rationalists to make religion look stupid. Modern conspiracy theorists are using that anticlerical propaganda to attack science.

They're falling for the exact myth designed to discredit their worldview.

Research on conspiracy theory communities shows a "gateway effect." Belief in one historical myth—medieval flat earth—makes it easier to believe contemporary conspiracies. The pattern: distrust what you learned in school, do your own research, trust community consensus over expert consensus.

Once you believe historians fabricated the flat earth story, it's easier to believe scientists are fabricating climate data. Or vaccine efficacy. Or evolution evidence.

### [13:30-14:30] Epistemological Stakes

This is about more than medieval history. It's about epistemology—how we know what's true.

If mainstream historians and educators are wrong or lying about something as basic as medieval cosmology, how do we determine truth?

The dangerous answer: Trust what feels right. Do your own research. Community intuition over academic rigor.

That's the path from "they lied about the flat earth" to "they're lying about everything."

The alternative is to actually check sources. Read the primary documents. Understand that academic correction takes time, but the correction exists.

Historians *have* debunked the flat earth myth. For decades. The problem isn't that experts are lying. The problem is that the myth serves too many ideological purposes to die easily.

### [14:30-15:30] Conclusion - Why Accurate History Matters

Here's what's actually true:

Medieval people—at least the educated ones—knew the earth was a sphere. They had Bede's "playing ball" in the 8th century. They had Sacrobosco's sensory proofs in the 13th century. They had the king holding a globe as a symbol of earthly power.

The flat earth myth was invented by Washington Irving in 1828, weaponized by Draper and White in the 1870s-1890s, and embedded in textbooks between 1880 and 1920.

It persists because of presentism, narrative utility, and the cycle of myths that make it nearly impervious to correction.

And it matters because teaching false history undermines trust in expertise, fuels conspiracy thinking, and makes it harder to distinguish between credible and incredible claims—not just about the past, but about the present.

This isn't about defending medieval Christianity or attacking New Atheism. It's about defending the principle that truth matters. That primary sources matter. That when you actually read what medieval people wrote—in their own words, from their own time—you discover they were neither stupid flat-earthers nor modern scientists in robes.

They were sophisticated thinkers working within a different framework. Understanding that framework on its own terms—not through 19th-century propaganda or 21st-century assumptions—is how we learn from history instead of just congratulating ourselves for not living in it.

Check the sources. Question the narratives. Demand evidence.

That's the lesson. Not just for medieval history, but for everything.
