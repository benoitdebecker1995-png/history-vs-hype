## Fabricating the Flat Earth: The Anatomy of a Myth

The flat-earth myth became embedded in educational materials during the late nineteenth century as a "primary homily" used to support the "Conflict Thesis"—the false historical narrative that science and religion are in a state of perpetual warfare [1, 2]. While medieval scholars were actually "united in their conviction that it [the Earth] was a sphere," 19th-century polemicists fabricated the myth of a "Great Interruption" of geographical knowledge to portray the Church as a "stumbling-block in the intellectual advancement of Europe" [3-5].

### Timeline of Textbook Appearance

Historian Jeffrey Burton Russell pinpointed the systematic entry of the myth into the educational canon between 1870 and 1920 [6, 7].

• Pre-1870: Russell's survey of nineteenth-century history texts for secondary schools found that "very few mentioned the flat-earth myth before 1870" [8].
• Post-1880: The transition was rapid and nearly universal; "almost all texts after 1880 featured the legend" [8, 9].
• Catalyst: The shift was driven by the popularity of John William Draper's History of the Conflict between Religion and Science (1874) and Andrew Dickson White's A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom (1896) [10, 11].

### The Role of Washington Irving

The myth's most "dramatic" entry into the classroom came from the fictionalized 1828 biography of Columbus by Washington Irving [9]. Irving invented a scene at the Council of Salamanca where Columbus faced "monastic bigotry" from "shaved monks" who cited the Bible to prove the Earth was flat [12, 13]. Though Samuel Eliot Morison dismissed Irving's account as "pure moonshine," the story was so compelling that textbooks adopted it as fact [13, 14]. For example, a 1898 textbook by John J. Anderson used a direct paraphrase of Irving to claim that medieval scholars believed "the earth was flat like a plate" [15, 16].

### Why the Myth Persists (Academic-Pedagogical Lag)

Despite being thoroughly debunked by scholars like Russell (1991) and Cormack (1994), the "Flat Error" remains a "perennial mold in our collective mental cupboards" for several reasons [17, 18]:

• Narrative Utility: The myth serves a secular "story of redemption," contrasting the "stinking alleyways of the Middle Ages" with the "sunlit uplands" of modern reason [1, 19].
• Presentism: It fulfills a cultural need to believe in the "superiority of the present" by making our ancestors seem "stupid enough to think the world flat" [20, 21].
• The "Cycle of Myths": The Flat Error is part of a self-reinforcing loop that includes other false categories like the "Dark Ages," creating a worldview that is "impervious to evidence" [22, 23].
• Pedagogical Expediency: Textbooks rely on "pedagogical reconstructions"—oversimplified stories that "put the phenomena first" and portray science as a linear accumulation of truth [24, 25].

## The Flat Error: Narrative Utility of a Medieval Myth

The persistence of the medieval flat-earth myth, categorized as the "Flat Error" by historian Jeffrey Burton Russell, is not a failure of evidence but a result of the myth's profound narrative utility [1, 2]. Despite being debunked by medievalists and historians of science for decades, the legend survives because it functions as a "canonical homily" for the perceived triumph of progress, secularism, and modernity over a caricatured past of ecclesiastical ignorance [3-5].

### Psychological and Social Resistance to Correction

Several cognitive and social mechanisms make this false "common knowledge" nearly impervious to correction:

• The Dichotomizing Machine: As Stephen Jay Gould observes, the human mind operates as a "dichotomizing machine," partitioning history into polar dualities such as "nature vs. culture" or "reason vs. superstition" [6, 7]. This mental habit obscures the actual continuity of history, preferring a dramatic break between a "Dark Age" and a "Scientific Revolution" [8-10].
• Presentism: Russell identifies "presentism"—the tendency of a culture to congratulate itself on its superiority—as a primary driver [11, 12]. By believing our ancestors were "stupid enough to think the world flat," we reinforce our own sense of intellectual security [13, 14].
• The Cycle of Myths: The Flat Error is part of a self-reinforcing "cycle of myths" that includes the "Dark Ages," "The Inquisition," and the "Conflict Thesis" [15]. Once these myths are interlinked, they form a "social imaginary" that shapes how individuals relate to the past, making the myth impervious to contradictory data [14, 16].
• Textbook Inertia: Russell's survey found that very few texts mentioned the flat-earth myth before 1870, but it became "standard fare" after 1880 [17, 18]. This shift coincided with the "warfare" narratives of John William Draper (1874) and Andrew Dickson White (1896), whose work bedazzled generations of readers with a "horde of footnotes" that created an "illusion of meticulous scholarship" [19-21].

### Narrative Utility: Anti-Religious vs. Anti-Intellectual

The myth serves distinct, often opposing, ideological agendas:

• Anti-Religious Camps: For nineteenth-century polemicists like Draper and White, the myth was a weapon used to characterize the Church as a "stumbling-block in the intellectual advancement of Europe" [3, 22]. They used the isolated figures of Lactantius (c. 245–325) and Cosmas Indicopleustes (fl. 540s) as scapegoats to suggest a "dominant theology" of flatness that never actually existed among the medieval establishment [23-25].
• Anti-Intellectual and Conspiracy Camps: Conversely, the modern flat-earth movement, founded by Samuel Birley Rowbotham ("Parallax") in 1849, used the myth to attack "expert" elites [26, 27]. Rowbotham's "Zetetic" philosophy argued that "common sense" and immediate sensory perception were superior to the "imaginary foundations" of Newtonian astronomy [28, 29]. In this narrative, the "globalist" view is the deception, and modern flat-earthers claim to be reclaiming a "biblical truth" that they mistakenly believe was the medieval consensus until suppressed by a scientific cabal [30-33].

### Russell on the Difficulty of Correction

Jeffrey Burton Russell argues that correcting the Flat Error is uniquely difficult because it challenges a foundational piece of modern identity:

• The Terror of Meaninglessness: Russell eloquently states: "The terror of meaninglessness, of falling off the edge of knowledge, is greater than the imagined fear of falling off the edge of the earth. And so we prefer to believe a familiar error than to search, unceasingly, the darkness" [34, 35].
• The Allure of the Hero Narrative: The myth provides an inspirational "schoolchild version" where a lone genius (Columbus) confounds "shaved monks" with simple common sense [36-38]. Because this "storyhouse romance" is so dramatic, people are loath to trade it for the more complex reality that Columbus was actually wrong about the earth's size and his opponents were right [37, 39, 40].

## The Modern Flat Earth Revival and the Zetetic Myth

The modern flat Earth movement is not a survival of medieval tradition but a reactionary phenomenon that emerged in the mid-nineteenth century, fundamentally built upon a misunderstanding of both historical reality and scientific method [1, 2]. While modern conspiracy theorists often claim to be returning to an "ancient" or "biblical" truth, they are actually reinforcing a historical fabrication—the "Flat Error"—invented by nineteenth-century rationalists to disparage the medieval Church [3, 4].

### Emergence and Key Figures

The modern revival was initiated by Samuel Birley Rowbotham (1816–1884), writing under the pseudonym "Parallax" [1].

• Key Date: In 1849, Rowbotham published the pamphlet Zetetic Astronomy: A Description of Several Experiments which Prove that the Surface of the Sea is a Perfect Plane and that the Earth is not a Globe [5, 6].
• Institutional Evolution: The movement transitioned from Rowbotham's Zetetic Society to the Universal Zetetic Society (1893) under Lady Elizabeth Blount, then to the International Flat Earth Research Society (1956) founded by Samuel Shenton, and finally to the International Flat Earth Research Society of America (1972) led by Charles K. Johnson [7-9].

### Key Claims of the Modern Movement

Modern flat-earthism relies on "Zetetic" philosophy, which prioritizes immediate sensory perception over "expert" scientific theory [10, 11].

• The Disc Model: The Earth is a flat disc with the North Pole at its center and a 150-foot-high wall of ice (the "South Pole") encircling the perimeter to contain the oceans [12, 13].
• Celestial Mechanics: The Sun and Moon are small (roughly 32 miles in diameter) and move in circles approximately 3,000 miles above the Earth [14-16].
• Denial of Gravity: Gravity is dismissed as "hocus pocus" or "a myth"; objects simply fall because of their inherent weight or density relative to the air [17, 18].
• The "Global Conspiracy": Flat-earthers claim that the spherical model is a "Satanic spinning globe hoax" designed to replace religion with science and hide God [19, 20].

### Historical Misunderstanding and the "Narrative of Lies"

Modern flat-earthers fundamentally misunderstand history by claiming that their view was the standard medieval consensus until a scientific "cabal" suppressed it [21, 22]. In reality:

• Medieval Globalism: Educated people in the Middle Ages knew the Earth was spherical. The Venerable Bede (673–735) explicitly taught that the Earth resembles "more a ball, being equally round in all directions ... no matter what way it is turned" [23, 24].
• The Salamanca Myth: Flat-earthers often cite the fictional version of the Council of Salamanca (1486) invented by Washington Irving in 1828, where Columbus supposedly faced "monkish bigotry" for believing in a round Earth [25, 26]. In reality, the council assumed sphericity but correctly argued that the Earth was far larger than Columbus estimated [27, 28].
• Inversion of the Myth: Modern flat-earthers like John Hampden (1870) used a "they lied" narrative, claiming the globe idea itself was a "superstitious delusion of the dark ages" rather than the flat-earth idea [29].

### The Irony of the Conspiracy

The greatest irony of the modern movement is that it cites the "medieval flat-earth myth" to support its claims, yet that very myth was invented by the nineteenth-century scientific rationalists—the "enemies" of the flat-earthers [30, 31].

• Rationalist Propaganda: Historians such as John William Draper (1874) and Andrew Dickson White (1896) created the myth of the medieval flat Earth to frame history as a "warfare" where science eventually defeated "religious bigotry" [32-34].
• Self-Defeating Logic: By asserting that "the Church used to teach the Earth was flat," modern conspiracy theorists are actually falling for an anticlerical, pro-science propaganda tool designed to make traditional believers look "ignorant" and "stupid" [4, 35].

## The Handmaiden Consensus: Medieval Cosmology and the Spherical Earth

To construct a historically rigorous framing for the video, we must reject two equally unscholarly extremes: Extreme A (The Flat Error), which posits a millennium of ecclesiastical ignorance, and Extreme B (Whiggish Overclaiming), which falsely characterizes medieval natural philosophy as a precursor to modern experimental science. The evidence supports a nuanced middle position defined by "The Handmaiden Consensus," where medieval scholars utilized inherited Greek proofs of sphericity to support a rational, theological worldview.

### Extreme A: The Popular Misconception (The "Flat Error")

Extreme A asserts that from roughly AD 300 to 1492, Christian dogma suppressed classical knowledge, leading to a "Great Interruption" where educated Europeans believed the Earth was a flat disc [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 4; Flat Earth or round sphere, p. 363-364].

• The Claim: Columbus had to defy "shaved monks" at Salamanca who cited the Bible to prove the Earth was flat [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 52-54].
• The Evidence Against: In the 8th century, the Venerable Bede explicitly taught that the Earth was "not merely circular like a shield... but resembles more a ball, being equally round in all directions" [Bede: The Reckoning of Time, p. 91].
• Visual Refutation: Every medieval king's regalia included the globus cruciger (the royal orb surmounted by a cross), a public symbol of terra rotunda [God's Philosophers, p. 240; Flat-earth: the history of an infamous idea, p. 157].

### Extreme B: The Scholarly Overclaim (Whiggish Progressivism)

Extreme B involves selecting isolated medieval ideas that "sound modern" to claim that the Scientific Revolution was merely a continuation of medieval trends [Foundations of Modern Science, p. xii].

• The Claim: That medieval figures like Thomas Aquinas or Roger Bacon were revolutionary "scientists" in the modern sense, working in laboratories to overturn authority [When Science and Christianity Meet, p. 16; Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 35].
• The Evidence Against: Medieval natural philosophy was almost entirely book-based, not experimental. Sarah Waterlow notes that the Aristotelian concept of "substance" actually made controlled experiments seem superfluous or obstructive to understanding a thing's "inner principle" [Foundations of Modern Science, p. 159-160].
• Statistics of Context: While the University of Paris required astronomy, it was taught as part of the quadrivium, where it served a "handmaiden" role to theology, specifically for calculating the calendar (computus) [When Science and Christianity Meet, p. 23; Bede: The Reckoning of Time, p. xxiv].

### The Nuanced Middle Position: The Handmaiden Consensus

The historical reality is that medieval scholars were "united in their conviction that it [the Earth] was a sphere" but operated within a "sacramental" framework that prioritized spiritual utility over technical progress [Flat Earth or round sphere, p. 363; When Science and Christianity Meet, p. 16].

• Rational Sphericity: The standard university textbook, Sacrobosco's De Sphaera (c. 1230), provided sensory proofs for the globe, noting that "the mast of a departing ship disappears from sight after the hull" [The Sphere of Sacrobosco, p. 120; Early Physics and Astronomy, p. 93].
• Theological Integration: Scholars like Nicole Oresme (14th century) could argue for the possibility of Earth's rotation while ultimately choosing immobility based on a "figurative" reading of the Bible and a lack of demonstrative physical proof [Foundations of Modern Science, p. 116; When Science and Christianity Meet, p. 132].
• Visual Evidence (The T-O Map): Often cited as "proof" of flat-earth belief, the T-O map (as seen in Isidore of Seville's 1472 edition) was actually an iconographic diagram representing only the habitable quarter (oikoumene) of a spherical world [God's Philosophers, p. 235; Flat-earth: the history of an infamous idea, p. 172].

### Dangers of Both Extremes

1. Presentism (Danger of A): This extreme encourages us to congratulate ourselves on our "modernity" by inventing an ignorant past, leading to a "hall of mirrors" that obscures actual historical development [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 6, 76; Flat-earth: the history of an infamous idea, p. 163].
2. Incommensurability (Danger of B): By projecting modern values onto the past, we fail to understand medieval people on their own terms. ## Evidence of Medieval Belief in a Spherical Earth

The following primary source citations provide irrefutable evidence that medieval scholars and educators understood the Earth to be a three-dimensional sphere. These quotes are selected for their linguistic precision and their presence in the standard educational curriculum of the era.

### 1. The Venerable Bede (c. 725 AD)

• Original Text: "Causa autem in-aequalitatis eorundem dierum terrae rotunditas est... non in latitudinis solum giro quasi instar scuti rotundus sed instar potius pilae undique versum aequali rotunditate persimilis." [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 87]
• Source: De temporum ratione (The Reckoning of Time), Chapter 32 [Bede: The Reckoning of Time, p. 91]
• Translation: "The cause of the unequal length of the days is the globular shape of the earth... [it is] not merely circular like a shield, but resembles more a ball, being equally round in all directions." [Flat Earth or round sphere, p. 365]
• Debunking Effectiveness: This quote is the most effective tool for debunking the "Dark Ages" myth. Written in the 8th century, it explicitly rejects the "shield" (flat disk) model in favor of the "playing ball" (sphere) model.
• Visual Evidence: A 9th-century manuscript of this text (e.g., St. Gall MS 250) shows this passage in clear Carolingian minuscule, proving the concept was taught in monastic schools 1,300 years ago. [Bede: The Reckoning of Time, p. 91]

### 2. Johannes de Sacrobosco (c. 1230 AD)

• Original Text: "Quod terra etiam sit rotunda sic patet... huius nulla alia causa est nisi tumor terre." [The Sphere of Sacrobosco and Its Commentators, p. 81-82]
• Source: Tractatus de Sphaera (Treatise on the Sphere), Chapter 1 [The Sphere of Sacrobosco and Its Commentators, p. 120]
• Translation: "That the earth, too, is round is shown thus... of this there is no other cause than the bulge of the earth." [The Sphere of Sacrobosco and Its Commentators, p. 120]
• Debunking Effectiveness: This was the standard astronomy textbook for all university students from the 13th to the 17th centuries. It survives in hundreds of manuscripts, indicating that every educated person in the High Middle Ages was required to study the physical proofs of Earth's sphericity. [Early Physics and Astronomy, p. 251]
• Visual Evidence: 13th-century manuscripts of Sacrobosco regularly feature the "ship and mast" diagram (see Fig. 5.9 in Pedersen), showing a ship sailing over the curve of the Earth's "bulge." [Early Physics and Astronomy, p. 93]

### 3. Martianus Capella (c. 420 AD)

• Original Text: "...non planam... neque concavam... sed rotundam, globosam etiam." [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 86]
• Source: De nuptiis Philologiae et Mercurii (The Marriage of Philology and Mercury), Book 6 [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 20]
• Translation: "[The Earth is] not flat... nor concave... but round, even a globe." [Inventing the Flat Earth, p. 20]
• Debunking Effectiveness: Martianus Capella was a cornerstone of the early medieval liberal arts curriculum. His explicit use of "not flat" (non planam) removes any ambiguity regarding early medieval terminology. [Flat Earth or round sphere, p. 365]
• Visual Evidence: Show the text alongside the globus cruciger, the golden orb surmounted by a cross held by medieval monarchs in portraits since the 11th century, representing Christ's sovereignty over a spherical world. [Flat-earth: the history of an infamous idea, p. 157]

### 4. Isidore of Seville (d. 636 AD)

• Original Text: "Sphaera caeli species est in rotundum formata, cuius centrum terra est, ex omnibus partibus aequaliter conclusa." [The Etymologies of Isidore of Seville, p. 100]
• Source: Etymologiae, Book III, Chapter 32 [The Etymologies of Isidore of Seville, p. 100]
• Translation: "The sphere of the sky is a certain shape that is round in form, and its center is the earth, which is equally enclosed on all sides." [The Etymologies of Isidore of Seville, p. 100]
• Debunking Effectiveness: Isidore was the most widely read encyclopedist of the Middle Ages. Even his "T-O Maps," often misinterpreted today as flat-earth evidence, were actually intended as iconographic diagrams of the habitable quarter of a spherical globe. [God's Philosophers, p. 235]
• Visual Evidence: A T-O map from a 1472 printed edition of Etymologies placed next to Isidore's text describing the Earth as "enclosed on all sides" by the celestial sphere. [God's Philosophers, p. 235]

### 5. Sir John Mandeville (c. 1370 AD)

• Original Text: "And therefore I say sickerly that a man myght go all the world about, both above and beneath, and come again to his own country..." [Flat Earth or round sphere, p. 366]

## The Medieval Globalist Consensus: Debunking the Flat Earth Myth

The following pieces of evidence provide a robust academic debunking of the flat-earth myth, prioritizing primary medieval texts, institutional records, and visual artifacts that demonstrate a continuous globalist consensus.

### 1. The Disappearance of a Ship's Hull (Visual Proof)

• Claim Debunked: The belief that medieval people lacked sensory evidence or scientific observation for a round earth.
• Evidence: Johannes de Sacrobosco's Tractatus de sphaera (ca. 1230), the standard university textbook for four centuries, provided explicit sensory proofs. He used the observation of a ship approaching land: a signal on the shore is visible from the masthead after it has disappeared from the sight of someone at the foot of the mast [1, 2].
• Quotable/Statistic: "The clearest, most elementary, and most used textbook in astronomy and cosmography from the thirteenth to the seventeenth century" survives today in "hundreds of manuscripts" [3, 4].

### 2. The "Playground Ball" Analogy (Linguistic Precision)

• Claim Debunked: The idea that early medieval scholars (AD 500–1000) were "benighted" and lost Greek knowledge of sphericity.
• Evidence: The Venerable Bede (673–735), in his pedagogical work The Reckoning of Time, explicitly rejected the idea of a disk-shaped earth. He distinguished between being "circular like a shield" and being truly spherical [5, 6].
• Quotable Passage: Bede wrote that the earth "resembles more a ball, being equally round in all directions ... no matter what way it is turned" [5, 7, 8].

### 3. The University Science Requirement (Institutional Proof)

• Claim Debunked: That the medieval Church suppressed scientific inquiry and forced a flat-earth dogma.
• Evidence: In the mid-13th century, the University of Paris (supported by the papacy) made natural philosophy and the "mathematical quadrivium" (including astronomy) mandatory [9, 10]. Hundreds of thousands of students were required to study the Greco-Arabic tradition of a globular earth [9].
• Quotable/Statistic: "About 30 percent of the medieval university curriculum covered subjects and texts concerned with the natural world" [9, 11].

### 4. The Misinterpretation of T-O Maps (Cartographic Evidence)

• Claim Debunked: That medieval mappaemundi (world maps) depict a flat disk because they are drawn in a circular "T-O" format.
• Evidence: These maps were not intended as literal geographical projections but as iconographic diagrams of the habitable quarter (the oikoumene) of a spherical globe [12-14]. They appeared in manuscripts alongside texts that explicitly stated the earth was a sphere [15, 16].
• Quotable Passage: These maps were "only intended to map the quarter of the earth's surface that medieval people believed to be inhabited" [17].

### 5. The Royal Globus Cruciger (Visual Regalia)

• Claim Debunked: That the spherical earth was a secret known only to an academic elite and hidden from the public.
• Evidence: Since at least the 11th century, portraits and regalia of European kings featured the globus cruciger—a golden sphere surmounted by a cross [18, 19]. This public symbol represented Christ's (and by extension the king's) sovereignty over a spherical world [18].
• Quotable Description: "A golden ball representing the earth ... indicating Christ's sovereignty over the earth" [18].

### 6. The True Nature of the Salamanca Dispute (Historical Context)

• Claim Debunked: Washington Irving's 1828 claim that Columbus had to defend a round earth against "bigoted friars" at the University of Salamanca who used the Bible to prove a flat earth [20, 21].
• Evidence: The 1486 royal commission at Salamanca accepted the earth's sphericity but used it against Columbus. They argued that the earth's circumference was too large (citing Ptolemy) for his ships to survive the distance to Asia [22-24].
• Quotable Passage: "The sphericity of the globe was not in question. The issue was the width of the ocean; and therein the opposition was right" [24].

### 7. The Isolation of Cosmas Indicopleustes (Scholarly Isolation)

• Claim Debunked: That the 6th-century flat-earth monk Cosmas Indicopleustes was a typical or influential medieval representative.
• Evidence: Cosmas's Christian Topography was written in Greek and remained completely untranslated into Latin until 1706 [25, 26]. Only two full medieval manuscripts exist, and no medieval Western scholar is known to have read his work [27, 28].
• Quotable/Statistic: "Cosmas is, in fact, the only medieval European known to have defended a flat earth cosmology" [29].
