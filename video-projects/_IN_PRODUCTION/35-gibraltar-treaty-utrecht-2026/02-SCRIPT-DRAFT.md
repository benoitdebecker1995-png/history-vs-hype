## SCRIPT

### OPENING (0:00-1:30)

**[B-ROLL: Footage of the Gibraltar-La Línea border crossing — cars queuing, pedestrians, the fence]**

Every day, about 15,000 people cross the border between Spain and Gibraltar. Most of them are Spanish workers heading to jobs on the Rock. The town on the Spanish side, La Línea de la Concepción, has 35% unemployment. Gibraltar has about 2.5%. They need each other.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

But until very recently, crossing that border meant going through a fence, passport controls, and vehicle checks — all squeezed into a space smaller than a football pitch. The queue could take hours.

In June 2025, Britain and Spain agreed to tear that fence down. The joint declaration says, and I'm reading directly: "All physical barriers, checks and controls on people and goods moving between Spain and Gibraltar will be removed."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

That sounds like the end of a very long argument. But if you look at the fine print of that 2025 deal, you'll find a phrase: "without prejudice to sovereignty." In plain English, that means both sides still disagree about who actually owns the Rock. The fence is coming down, but the argument that built it hasn't been resolved.

That argument goes back to a single document from 1713. And in that document is a clause that has effectively blocked 34,000 people from deciding their own future for over three centuries.

One side says Gibraltar is British. They've voted 99% to stay. The other side says it's stolen land that belongs to Spain. They're both making real legal arguments. And that's exactly the problem.

---

### ACT 1: THE ACCIDENTAL CONQUEST (1:30-3:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

So how did Britain end up with a 6.8 square kilometer rock at the bottom of Spain?

**[B-ROLL: Map of War of Spanish Succession, 1704 fleet positions]**

It wasn't the plan. In 1704, Britain was fighting the War of Spanish Succession — a massive European conflict over who would sit on the Spanish throne. An Anglo-Dutch fleet of about 62 warships was failing. Their attack on the city of Cadiz had fallen apart because the British troops, and this is from the reports, "disintegrated in an orgy of drunkenness." An attempted uprising in Barcelona produced nothing.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

The commanders needed a win. A council of war chose Gibraltar because it was, in their words, "indifferently garrisoned." That's 18th-century language for barely defended — somewhere between 80 and 150 regular soldiers, depending on the source, plus a few hundred militia.

**[B-ROLL: Battle illustration or map of 1704 attack]**

On August 1st, 1704, about 2,000 marines landed on the isthmus. Three days of bombardment, roughly 15,000 rounds fired. On August 4th, the Spanish governor surrendered.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

But Britain didn't plan to keep it. Peter Gold, one of the leading scholars on Gibraltar, writes that "initially no interest was shown from London in accepting responsibility for the administration of Gibraltar." British ministers considered giving it back at least seven times in the following fifteen years.

They stumbled into it. And then the diplomats made sure they could never leave.

---

### ACT 2: ARTICLE X — THE CONTRACT (3:15-5:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

On July 13th, 1713, Spain and Britain signed the Treaty of Utrecht, ending the war. The British negotiators had realized that controlling the entrance to the Mediterranean was too valuable to hand back. So they wrote Article X.

Here's what it actually says. It's written in what scholars call "dog Latin" — a clunky, simplified version of Latin that 18th-century diplomats used for contracts. It sounds authoritative, but it's full of ambiguity.

**[B-ROLL: Treaty text on screen, cession clause highlighted]**

"The Catholic King does hereby, for Himself, His heirs and successors, yield to the Crown of Great Britain the full and intire propriety of the Town and Castle of Gibraltar, together with the port, fortifications, and forts thereunto belonging."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

That's the cession. Spain gave Britain the Rock. But notice this specific phrase: "the abovenamed propriety be yielded to Great Britain without any territorial jurisdiction, and without any open communication by land with the country round about."

No territorial jurisdiction. No land connection. Britain got the Rock and the walls around it. Not one meter beyond.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing what was ceded in 1713 vs. current boundary including isthmus and airport]**

And this is where the border dispute actually starts. That flat strip of land connecting the Rock to Spain — the isthmus — wasn't part of the deal. In 1713, it was "neutral ground." Nobody was supposed to build on it.

But in 1814, a wave of Yellow Fever hit Gibraltar. People were dying in the cramped city, so Britain asked Spain for permission to move civilians onto that empty flat land to build temporary hospital huts.

Spain agreed. It was a health emergency.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Britain never left. They replaced the huts with permanent buildings. Eventually, they built the airport runway right across it. To Spain, that's not just a border dispute. That's 200 years of squatting on land that was never part of the original treaty.

But the isthmus isn't the real problem. The real problem is what comes next in Article X.

**[B-ROLL: Treaty text, reversionary clause highlighted]**

"And in case it shall hereafter seem meet to the Crown of Great Britain to grant, sell, or by any means to alienate... the preference of having the same shall always be given to the Crown of Spain before any others."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

In plain English: if Britain ever gives up Gibraltar or changes its status, they have to offer it to Spain first.

Think about what that means for the people living there. In most places, a population can eventually push for the right to govern themselves. But for Gibraltar, any move toward self-governance risks being interpreted as Britain "alienating" the territory. And the moment that happens, the clause says the Rock goes to Spain.

The UK government has confirmed this in writing: "Under the Treaty of Utrecht, independence is not an option, unless Spain is prepared to agree."

34,000 people, locked into a choice between British and Spanish, by a sentence written in dog Latin over 300 years ago.

### ACT 3: WHY SPAIN NEVER STOPPED TRYING (5:30-7:15)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Spain didn't just sign the treaty and move on. They tried to take Gibraltar back. Fourteen times.

The biggest attempt was the Great Siege — 1779 to 1783. Three years, seven months, twelve days. It's the longest siege in British military history. Spain and France threw 40,000 troops, 246 guns, and ten specially designed floating batteries at about 7,000 British defenders under General Eliott.

**[B-ROLL: Great Siege illustration, floating batteries]**

The British heated cannonballs in iron furnaces until they were cherry-red and fired them into the floating batteries. All ten were destroyed. The bay lit up with burning ships.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing how France/Spain diverted resources from American Revolution]**

This siege mattered beyond Gibraltar. France and Spain committed enormous resources here — troops, ships, money — that could have gone to the American Revolution. The British garrison held, and after the siege failed, George III privately admitted he wished to be "rid of Gibraltar." But the garrison's success made that politically impossible.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

"Strong as the Rock of Gibraltar" entered the English language. Mozart wrote a commemorative piece. After 1783, giving back the Rock wasn't just a bad idea. It was unthinkable.

The Great Siege turned Gibraltar into a British symbol. But it was a 20th-century dictator who turned the people living on it into a nation.

---

### ACT 4: THE BLOCKADE THAT BUILT A NATION (7:15-9:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Because the people living on that rock weren't originally British. They were Genoese, Maltese, Sephardic Jewish, Moroccan, Portuguese, and Spanish. They spoke a language called Llanito — a mix of old Andalusian Spanish and English. Until the late 1950s, 85% of children started school speaking only Spanish. Main Street had bullfighting posters.

So what changed?

**[B-ROLL: 1960s UN footage, Francisco Franco]**

In the 1960s, the United Nations was pushing for decolonization — pressuring European empires to give up their overseas territories. The Spanish dictator Francisco Franco expected the UN to force Britain's hand and deliver Gibraltar back to Spain.

But in 1967, Gibraltar held a referendum. The question was clear: pass under Spanish sovereignty, or retain the link with Britain? 12,138 voted to stay British. 44 voted for Spain. That's 99.64%.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Franco was furious. On June 8th, 1969, he shut the border. Completely. Land, sea, telephone lines — all cut. Sixteen years of total isolation.

**[B-ROLL: Historical photos of La Línea border, families at fence]**

Overnight, Gibraltar lost a third of its workforce — about 13,500 Spanish workers who crossed daily. Britain flew in Moroccan replacements within two weeks. Gibraltarian women entered the workforce. The military baked the bread and collected the rubbish.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

And on Sundays, families would stand at the closed border and scream news across the neutral ground. Parents held up newborn babies for grandparents in Spain to see through binoculars. One woman stood at the fence in her wedding dress so her grandmother on the Spanish side could watch from across the border.

Franco thought he'd starve them into surrendering. Instead, he did the opposite. English became a marker of loyalty. Being "not Spanish" became the core of a new identity. By the time the border reopened in 1985, the people behind it weren't just occupants of a British rock. They were Gibraltarians.

---

### ACT 5: THE VOTES THAT CHANGED NOTHING (9:00-11:00)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Since then, the people of Gibraltar have voted three times on their future. And every time, it didn't matter.

1967 — 99.64% voted to stay British. Three months later, the UN General Assembly passed Resolution 2353, calling the referendum a "contravention" of decolonization principles. The reasoning was that Gibraltar's colonial situation "destroys the national unity and territorial integrity" of Spain. The vote didn't count.

**[B-ROLL: Referendum results on screen]**

2002 — Britain and Spain proposed shared sovereignty. 98.97% voted against. The UK Foreign Secretary called the referendum "eccentric" and said the results wouldn't be recognized.

2016 — Brexit. Gibraltar voted 95.91% to remain in the EU. The strongest Remain vote anywhere in Britain. They were pulled out anyway.

Three votes. Near-unanimous each time. Overruled, dismissed, and ignored.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Now, if we are being fair to Spain, their argument isn't just a leftover from Franco. This is a position held by every Spanish government — left and right — going all the way back to Queen Isabella's will in 1504, where she wrote: "I ask and require of the Kings, my successors, that they may hold and retain the said City for themselves and in their own possession."

Spain's legal case rests on territorial integrity — the idea that a colonial power shouldn't be allowed to carve out a permanent chunk of another country's land. Legal scholars like Jamie Trinidad at Cambridge point out that the UN distinguishes between self-determination and territorial integrity, and in Gibraltar's case, those two principles directly conflict.

Spain argues the current population was brought in by Britain after the original Spanish residents were expelled. So in their view, the votes of 34,000 people shouldn't override the integrity of the Spanish map.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

It's a serious legal argument. But Spain has a contradiction.

**[B-ROLL: Map showing Ceuta and Melilla on the Moroccan coast]**

Spain holds two cities on the coast of Africa — Ceuta and Melilla. Ceuta has been under Spanish or Portuguese control since 1415. Melilla since 1497. Morocco claims them both using the exact same arguments Spain uses for Gibraltar: geographic proximity, territorial integrity, colonial legacy.

There are real differences. Ceuta and Melilla aren't on the UN's list of Non-Self-Governing Territories. Their residents have full Spanish citizenship and representation in the Spanish parliament, which Gibraltarians don't have in Westminster. Spain argues they're integral parts of the Spanish state, not colonies.

**[TALKING HEAD]**

But the parallel is hard to ignore. King Hassan II of Morocco once predicted that the day Britain leaves Gibraltar, international pressure will force Spain out of its African cities. Spain knows this. And it constrains everything they do.

---

### CLOSING (11:00-12:30)

**[TALKING HEAD]**

Which brings us back to that 2025 treaty.

The fence is coming down. Spanish workers won't have to queue for hours anymore. Frontex — the EU border agency — will handle Schengen checks at the airport and port. For the 15,000 people who cross that border daily, life gets easier.

**[B-ROLL: Side by side — 1713 treaty text and 2025 joint declaration]**

But the 2025 agreement includes its own version of the same old phrase: "without prejudice to sovereignty." The fence comes down. The question of who owns the Rock does not get answered.

And Article X is still there. "The preference of having the same shall always be given to the Crown of Spain before any others."

**[TALKING HEAD]**

The people of Gibraltar are a modern, democratic society that still can't fully govern themselves because of a sentence written in dog Latin over three hundred years ago. They can't be Spanish — 99% of them have said so, repeatedly. They can't be fully self-governing — the reversionary clause blocks that path. And they're increasingly frustrated with being governed from London by a country that just pulled them out of the EU against their will.

The 2025 treaty makes the border easier to cross. It doesn't change the contract that built it.
