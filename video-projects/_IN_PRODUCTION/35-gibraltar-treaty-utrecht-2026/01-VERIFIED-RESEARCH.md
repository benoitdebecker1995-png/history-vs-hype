## ACT 1: THE 1704 CAPTURE

### Fleet Composition and Command

✅ **VERIFIED** - Anglo-Dutch fleet:
- 52 English + 10 Dutch ships of the line (~50-60 ships total)
- Landing force: just under 2,000 men (4 English + 4 Dutch marine regiments + Catalan volunteers)
- Commander: Admiral George Rooke + Prince George of Hesse-Darmstadt

### Spanish Defense Forces

✅ **VERIFIED** - Spanish garrison (sources vary):
- Governor: Diego de Salinas
- **Garrison strength varies by source:**
  - Version 1: 80 regulars + 350 French/militia (~430 total)
  - Version 2: Fewer than 100 regulars
  - Version 3: 150 regulars + 250 armed citizens (~400 total)
- **Artillery:** 112 guns (80 iron, 32 brass) according to one source; another says only 50 guns with 6 crews
- **Note for script:** Present range and acknowledge source variance

### Timeline of Attack

✅ **VERIFIED** - Dates: August 1-4, 1704 (New Style/Gregorian calendar). Old Style: July 21-24.

- **August 1:** Fleet enters bay, 2,000 marines land on isthmus
- **August 2:** Heavy winds delay positioning
- **August 3:** Six-hour bombardment (~15,000 rounds), landing at New Mole
- **August 4:** Surrender accepted

### Casualties

✅ **VERIFIED** - Battle losses:
- **Attackers:** 60 killed, 216 wounded (many from powder magazine explosion at New Mole)
- **Defenders:** ~60 men

### Surrender Terms

✅ **VERIFIED** - Seven-article capitulation:

- **Article I:** Officers/soldiers march out with baggage; gentlemen keep horses
- **Article II:** Garrison takes 3 brass cannon + 12 rounds each
- **Article III:** Attackers provide 6 days rations
- **Article IV:** 3 days to depart, no trunk searches
- **Article V:** Inhabitants who stay get same privileges + religious freedoms under oath to Charles III
- **Article VI:** All powder/arms magazines yielded
- **Article VII:** French subjects excluded — remain prisoners of war

### Strategic Context

✅ **VERIFIED** - Original objectives had FAILED:
- Cadiz expedition 1702: troops "disintegrated in an orgy of drunkenness"
- Barcelona 1704: no uprising materialized
- Council of war off Tetuan chose Gibraltar as "indifferently garrisoned"

✅ **VERIFIED** - Strategic value:
- Control Strait of Gibraltar
- Divide French fleets (Brest/Toulon)
- Protect Levant trade
- Observe all shipping

✅ **VERIFIED** - NOT intended as permanent annexation:
- Meant as base for Charles III to advance into Andalusia
- "Initially no interest was shown from London in accepting responsibility for the administration of Gibraltar" (Peter Gold, p. 5)
- British "stumbled into annexation" after interior advance failed
- British ministers "contemplated withdrawing from Gibraltar in return for territorial compensation elsewhere" at least 7 times in following 15 years

### Immediate Counter-Attack ("Twelfth Siege")

✅ **VERIFIED** - First siege attempt:
- Began October 1704 — Philip V ordered immediate recapture
- Marquis of Villadarias reached with 4,000 men in September
- By late October: 7,000 troops (Spanish + French) on isthmus
- Goatherd's path assault nearly succeeded but was not followed up
- Siege failed early 1705, solidified Rock as "impregnable fortress"

## ACT 2: THE TREATY OF UTRECHT (1713)

### Negotiators and Signatories

✅ **VERIFIED** - British plenipotentiaries who signed the Spain-Britain treaty:
- **John Robinson** — Bishop of Bristol, Keeper of the Privy Seal, Dean of Windsor
- **Thomas Wentworth, Earl of Strafford** — First Commissioner of the Admiralty

✅ **VERIFIED** - Spanish plenipotentiaries:
- **Francis Maria de Paula, Duke of Ossuna** — High Chamberlain
- **Isidore Cazado de Azevedo de Rosalez, Marquis of Monteleone**

✅ **VERIFIED** - Political architects behind the negotiations (London):
- **Henry St John, Lord Bolingbroke** — Secretary of State; visited Versailles in 1712 to finalize terms with France
- **Robert Harley, Earl of Oxford** — described as relishing "machinations" and "keeping people in the dark"

✅ **VERIFIED** - Key intermediaries:
- **Lord Lexington** — British Ambassador to Madrid during war's final years; instructed to reaffirm commercial treaties and secure confirmation of preliminary articles with Spanish court
- **Manoel Manasses Gilligan** — Roman Catholic Irishman; expert specialist for commercial and Asiento (slave trade) negotiations, working with Lexington in Madrid
- **Nicolas Mesnager** — French director of the Compagnie de l'Assiento; sent to London by Louis XIV to negotiate commercial concessions that formed basis of the preliminaries

✅ **VERIFIED** - Modern treaty negotiators (2020-2026):
- **Pedro Sánchez government** (since 2018): pragmatic approach, moved from co-sovereignty demands to cross-border cooperation focus
- **Forum of Dialogue on Gibraltar** (est. 2004): gave Government of Gibraltar its own voice and effective "veto" over agreements
- **Jack Straw** (UK Foreign Secretary) and **Josep Piqué** (Spanish Foreign Minister): attempted shared sovereignty deal 2001-2002, rejected by Gibraltarian referendum

### Treaty Date

✅ **VERIFIED** - **CRITICAL CORRECTION:**
- Spain-Britain treaty signed: **July 13, 1713**
- NOT April 11 (that was the France treaties)

### Article X Text

✅ **VERIFIED** - Original language described as "dog Latin"

✅ **VERIFIED** - Exact text (cession clause):
> "The Catholic King does hereby, for Himself, His heirs and successors, yield to the Crown of Great Britain the full and intire propriety of the Town and Castle of Gibraltar, together with the port, fortifications, and forts thereunto belonging..."

✅ **VERIFIED** - Exact text (restrictions clause):
> "...the abovenamed propriety be yielded to Great Britain without any territorial jurisdiction, and without any open communication by land with the country round about."

### Reversionary Clause ("Right of First Refusal")

✅ **VERIFIED** - Exact text:
> "And in case it shall hereafter seem meet to the Crown of Great Britain to grant, sell, or by any means to alienate... the preference of having the same shall always be given to the Crown of Spain before any others"

### Jews and Moors Exclusion

✅ **VERIFIED** - Exact text:
> "no leave shall be given under any pretence whatsoever, either to Jews or Moors, to reside or have their dwellings in the said town of Gibraltar"

✅ **VERIFIED** - Quickly subverted in practice:
- Jewish merchants from Morocco provided essential food
- Britain signed treaties with Emperor of Morocco (1721, 1729) contradicting Utrecht

### What Was Ceded

✅ **VERIFIED** - Territorial scope:
- "Town and Castle of Gibraltar, together with the port, fortifications, and forts thereunto belonging"
- Ceded "without any territorial jurisdiction" and "without any open communication by land"

### The "Independence Trap"

✅ **VERIFIED** - Reversionary clause consequences:
- Described as "monstrous veto" — prevents independent statehood
- If Britain leaves, territory reverts to Spain
- UK government confirmed: "under the treaty of Utrecht independence is not an option, unless Spain is prepared to agree"
- Gibraltarians limited to binary choice: British or Spanish

### The Isthmus Dispute

✅ **VERIFIED** - Core disagreement:
- Treaty ONLY ceded "town and castle" — isthmus NOT mentioned
- **UK argument:** "Cannon-shot principle" + "fortifications thereunto belonging" included isthmus fortifications
- **Spain argument:** Isthmus is Spanish territory occupied through "encroachment"

✅ **VERIFIED** - Historical timeline:
- 1814: Yellow fever — British moved civilians onto isthmus with Spanish permission — never left
- 1854: More substantial buildings built during another epidemic
- 1908-1909: British built fence (not for defense — to save money on sentries stopping tobacco smuggling)
- Spain argues continuous diplomatic protests prevent UK from claiming "prescriptive acquisition"

## ACT 3: THE GREAT SIEGE (1779-1783)

### Duration and Dates

✅ **VERIFIED** - Timeline:
- **Started:** June 21, 1779 (communications cut)
- **Ended:** February 3, 1783 (peace preliminaries)
- **Treaty of Versailles:** September 3, 1783
- **Total duration:** 3 years, 7 months, 12 days

### Attacking Forces

✅ **VERIFIED** - Spain + France combined:
- 40,000+ men at peak
- 246 guns and mortars for September 1782 bombardment
- 47 ships of the line during Grand Attack
- 10 specially designed floating batteries by French engineer D'Arcon

### British Defense

✅ **VERIFIED** - Under General Eliott:
- **Initial strength:** 5,382 officers and men + 1,500 dependents
- **Total strength:** ~7,000 men (5 British + 3 Hanoverian regiments + Corsican volunteers)
- Eliott personally lived on 4 ounces of rice per day during shortages

### Red-Hot Shot ("Hot Potatoes")

✅ **VERIFIED** - Decisive weapon:
- Iron furnaces heated cannonballs cherry-red
- September 13, 1782: Switched to red-hot shot after conventional bombardment failed
- Heated balls overwhelmed internal fire-suppression systems of floating batteries
- All 10 floating batteries destroyed or blown up
- Bay "lit up by burning ships"

### Relief Convoys

✅ **VERIFIED** - Three critical resupply missions:
- **First (January 1780):** Admiral Rodney, defeated Spanish at Cape St. Vincent en route
- **Second (April 1781):** Admiral Darby, brought flour/food under heavy bombardment
- **Third (October 1782):** Lord Howe, restocked after Grand Attack failure

### Casualties

✅ **VERIFIED** - Losses:
- **British:** 1,231 total
  - 333 killed/died of wounds
  - 536 died of sickness (scurvy, smallpox)
- **Spanish/French:** ~2,000 lost during floating battery attack alone (out of 5,000 manning them)

### Treaty of Versailles Outcome

✅ **VERIFIED** - Final settlement:
- Britain retained Gibraltar
- Spain received East and West Florida + Minorca as compensation
- George III privately wished to be "rid of Gibraltar" but garrison's success made it politically impossible
- Gibraltar became "brightest Jewel of the British Crown"
- Mozart composed commemorative piece
- "Strong as the Rock of Gibraltar" entered English language

✅ **VERIFIED** - 14 historical sieges + "15th":

2. **1315:** Moorish attempt (failed)
3. **1333:** Merinid forces (successful, 4 months)

5. **1349-1350:** Alfonso XI dies of Black Death (failed, 8 months)
6. **1411:** Kingdom of Granada takes from Fez (successful, short)

## ACT 4: GIBRALTARIAN IDENTITY

### Population and Geography

✅ **VERIFIED** - Basic statistics:
- **Land area:** 6.8 km² (2.6 sq mi) — includes reclamation and isthmus. Older figures of 5.8 km² exclude reclamation
- **Population:** ~34,000 residents (2019). 2001 census: 27,495 permanent + transients = 31,623. ~23,000-24,000 are "Gibraltarians"
- **Rock height:** 1,398 ft (426 m)
- **Isthmus length:** 800 meters

### Ethnic Composition

✅ **VERIFIED** - Diverse origins:
- Mixed: Genoese, Maltese, Sephardic Jewish, Moroccan, Portuguese, Spanish (Andalusian), British
- NOT ethnically homogeneous
- Genoese were more than 1/3 of population in first half of 18th century

### Llanito Language

✅ **VERIFIED** - Linguistic hybrid:
- **Base:** Old Andalusian Spanish + British English
- **Influences:** Genoese, Maltese, Portuguese, Haketia (Judeo-Spanish)
- **Examples:** beicbins (baked beans), carne combí (corned beef), panquequi (pancakes), chingas (chewing gum)
- Younger generation increasingly English-dominant, less Spanish fluent

### Identity Formation Process

✅ **VERIFIED** - Constructed but authentic:
- Dr. H.W. Howes (1946/1951) deliberately constructed "melting pot" narrative to distance from Spain
- As late as 1950s: 85% of children started school speaking only Spanish
- "Simulacrum of Britishness": red telephone boxes, bobbies in custodian helmets — preserved in Gibraltar but disappearing in UK
- 1940s Main Street had Spanish bullfighting posters
- Separate toilets at Royal Naval Dockyard until 1960s (UK British, Gibraltarians, Spaniards)

### The "Manufactured" But Authentic Identity

✅ **VERIFIED** - Unique colonial dynamic:
- "More British than the British" — WANTED stronger UK ties (unlike most colonies)
- Defined more by being "not Spanish" than by traditional Englishness
- Genoese/Maltese roots emphasized, Spanish heritage downplayed
- Franco's blockade was the catalyst that cemented this identity

## ACT 5: REFERENDUMS AND INTERNATIONAL LAW

### 1967 Referendum

✅ **VERIFIED** - Details:
- **Date:** September 10, 1967
- **Organized by:** UK government (Judith Hart, Minister of State for Commonwealth Affairs)

✅ **VERIFIED** - Exact ballot question:
- **Option A:** "To pass under Spanish sovereignty in accordance with the terms proposed by the Spanish Government to Her Majesty's Government on 18 May 1966."
- **Option B:** "Voluntarily to retain their link with Britain, with democratic local institutions and with Britain retaining its present responsibilities."

✅ **VERIFIED** - Results:
- **For Britain:** 12,138 (99.64%)
- **For Spain:** 44 (0.36%)
- **Invalid ballots:** 55
- **Turnout:** 95.8% (12,237 out of 12,762 registered)

✅ **VERIFIED** - Aftermath:
- Spain condemned results, lobbied UN

### UN Resolution 2353

✅ **VERIFIED** - International response:
- **Passed:** December 19, 1967 (3 months after referendum)
- **Key language:** "Declares the holding of the referendum of 10 September 1967 by the administering Power to be a contravention of the provisions of General Assembly resolution 2231 (XXI)"
- **Also stated:** colonial situation "destroys the national unity and territorial integrity of a country"
- Lord Caradon (UK representative) rejected as "unworthy of the UN"

### 2002 Referendum

✅ **VERIFIED** - Shared sovereignty rejected:
- **Date:** November 7, 2002
- **Question:** Shared sovereignty proposal
- **Results:**
  - **Against:** 17,900 (98.97%)
  - **For:** 192 (1.03%)
- **Turnout:** 87.9%
- UK Foreign Secretary Jack Straw called it "eccentric"
- UK Foreign Office said wouldn't recognize results

### ICJ Case Law

✅ **VERIFIED** - No direct Gibraltar case (Spain rejected 1966 UK proposal for ICJ referral)

✅ **VERIFIED** - Relevant advisory opinions and cases:

**Western Sahara Advisory Opinion (1975):**
- "Need to pay regard to the freely expressed will of peoples" — weakened irredentist argument
- Judge Dillard concurrence: "It is for the people to determine the destiny of the territory and not the territory the destiny of the people"

**Chagos Islands Advisory Opinion (2019):**
- Fragmentation of Non-Self-Governing Territory by administering power generally illegal

**Right of Passage (1960):**
- Portugal's title to Indian enclaves recognized but India's surrounding authority also acknowledged

**East Timor (1995):**
- Self-determination is right erga omnes (owed to all)

**Trinidad analysis (Cambridge, 2018):**
- No coherent legal doctrine allowing annexation of enclaves against people's wishes
- Gibraltarians qualify as "people" under territorial definition regardless of origin
- Spain's treaty-based sovereignty argument "legally weak"

### Self-Determination vs Territorial Integrity

✅ **VERIFIED** - Core legal debate:

**UK/Gibraltar position:**
- UN Charter supports self-determination
- Gibraltarians consistently voted British
- They are a distinct people

**Spain position:**
- UN Resolution 2353 supports territorial integrity
- Gibraltar historically Spanish
- Decolonization should restore pre-colonial borders
- Population is "imported" colonists

**"Fourth option" proposed:**
- Crown Dependency model (like Isle of Man)
- Enshrines self-determination while remaining "Her Majesty's Dominions"
- Potentially avoids triggering reversionary clause

## ACT 6: FRANCO'S BLOCKADE (1969-1985)

### Border Closure

✅ **VERIFIED** - Details:
- **Date:** June 8, 1969
- **Trigger:** 1967 referendum + 1969 Gibraltar Constitution Order
- **Total closure:** Land, sea, telephone communications cut
- **Duration:** 16 years

### Impact on Gibraltar

✅ **VERIFIED** - Economic effects:
- Lost ~13,500 daily Spanish workers (one-third of labor force)
- Economy "stagnated, but did not fail" — grew in real terms with British support
- UK committed £4 million ($7.2M) in development aid over 3 years
- ~£2 million per annum for housing and infrastructure after that
- National income (1970 prices): rose from £10.8M (1963) to £13.46M (1970)

### Labor Replacement

✅ **VERIFIED** - Workforce transformation:
- 1,000 Moroccan workers arrived within 2 weeks, eventually ~3,000
- Moroccan-born residents: 39 (1961) → 2,045 (1970) → 2,894 males by August 1970
- Gibraltarian women entered workforce en masse
- British military ran essential services (rubbish, bread baking)

### Impact on La Línea

✅ **VERIFIED** - Spanish side suffered:
- Population fell from 70,000 to 50,000
- Unemployment reached 20-25% by 1979, 38% by 1982

### Algeciras Paradox

✅ **VERIFIED** - Neighboring city prospered:
- Algeciras PROSPERED — became exclusive cross-Strait hub
- Port became one of most active container complexes in Spain
- Spain invested £22.5 million/year for 5 years in Campo development
- Petrochemical plant, steel rolling mill built nearby

### Identity Transformation

✅ **VERIFIED** - Cultural shift:
- English adopted as "devotional" marker of loyalty
- "Siege mentality" + internal solidarity from family separation
- "Melting pot" narrative emphasized (Genoese, Maltese roots) while Spanish heritage downplayed
- Successful strikes for UK wage parity
- Created modern "British Gibraltarian" identity defined by being "not Spanish"

### Human Stories

✅ **VERIFIED** - Personal impacts:
- "Screams on Sundays" — families shouted news across neutral ground
- "Binocular babies" — parents held up newborns for grandparents to see through binoculars
- Fence bride — woman stood in wedding dress at closed fence for grandmother in Spain
- "Wall of Shame" — original frontier gates saved as memorial

### Reopening

✅ **VERIFIED** - Gradual normalization:

**December 15, 1982:** Pedestrians only
- 7,000-8,000 crossed in first 24 hours

**February 5, 1985:** Full reopening (vehicles + all persons)
- Midnight February 4/5
- Tourism income doubled: £10M → £20M within one year
- Land arrivals: 2,411,655 in 1985
- 400-650 new jobs created immediately
- Spanish exports to Gibraltar: £20-30M ($36-54M) in first year
