## PRIMARY SOURCES (Tier 1 - Must Have)

### 1. British Parliamentary Sadler Committee Report (1832)
**Source Type:** Primary government document
**Priority:** 🔴 CRITICAL - This is your evidence for child labor conditions
**Official Title:** Report from the Select Committee on the Bill to Regulate the Labour of Children in Mills and Factories

**Where to Find:**
- British History Online: http://www.british-history.ac.uk/
- House of Commons Parliamentary Papers database
- Excerpts in labor history anthologies

**What to Extract:**
- Testimony on child ages (5-6 years old working)
- Working hours (14-16 hour days documented)
- Conditions (machinery accidents, physical deformities)
- Specific quotes from testimony

**Why Critical:**
- Primary source documenting child labor
- Can display excerpts on screen
- Counters "child labor wasn't that bad" narrative

---

### 2. Real Wage Data (1760-1850)
**Source Type:** Economic data / statistical evidence
**Priority:** 🔴 CRITICAL

**Key Sources:**
- Gregory Clark, *A Farewell to Alms* (2007) - wage series data
- Robert Allen, *The British Industrial Revolution in Global Perspective* (2009) - wage comparisons
- Academic databases (e.g., MeasuringWorth.com)

**Where to Find:**
- University libraries
- Google Books preview (data tables)
- Academic economics papers (JSTOR, NBER)

**What to Extract:**
- Real wage trends 1760-1850 (adjusted for inflation)
- When wages stagnated (1760-1820) vs when they rose (1820-1850)
- Percentage increase by 1850
- Which workers benefited when

**Why Critical:**
- Quantifies "did workers benefit?" question
- Shows timeline complexity (stagnation then growth)
- Central to "both sides right" argument

---

### 3. Productivity Data - Textile Industry (1770-1840)
**Source Type:** Economic/technological data
**Priority:** 🔴 CRITICAL

**Key Source:** Robert Allen, *The British Industrial Revolution in Global Perspective*

**Where to Find:**
- Allen's book (Chapter on textiles)
- Economic history papers on textile productivity

**What to Extract:**
- Spinning productivity: hand vs. machine (50,000 hours → 135 hours per 100 lbs cotton)
- Power loom vs. hand weaving productivity
- When specific technologies were adopted
- Overall productivity multipliers

**Why Critical:**
- Shows scale of productivity explosion
- Evidence for "progress" narrative
- Specific numbers to display on screen

---

### 4. Life Expectancy Statistics (1750-1900)
**Source Type:** Demographic data
**Priority:** 🟡 HIGH

**Key Sources:**
- Maddison Project Database (economic history)
- Simon Szreter, *Fertility, Class and Gender in Britain* (demographic data)
- Robert Woods, *The Demography of Victorian England and Wales*

**Where to Find:**
- Maddison Project: https://www.rug.nl/ggdc/historicaldevelopment/maddison/
- Academic demographic papers

**What to Extract:**
- Life expectancy 1750 (baseline)
- Life expectancy 1800 (early industrial, actually lower in cities)
- Life expectancy 1850, 1900 (eventual rise)
- Urban vs. rural differences

**Why Important:**
- Shows initial decline, then eventual dramatic improvement
- Supports "timeline complexity" argument
- Long-term vs. short-term outcomes

---

### 5. Urban Mortality and Living Conditions (1820s-1840s)
**Source Type:** Primary reports and statistical evidence
**Priority:** 🟡 HIGH

**Key Sources:**
- Friedrich Engels, *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845)
- Edwin Chadwick, *Report on the Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population* (1842)

**Where to Find:**
- Both available free online (public domain)
- Marxists.org has Engels text
- British government archives have Chadwick report

**What to Extract:**
- Infant mortality rates (Manchester 57% in working-class districts)
- Housing conditions (back-to-back housing, overcrowding)
- Disease rates (cholera, typhoid, tuberculosis)
- Specific examples from Manchester, London, Birmingham

**Why Important:**
- Documents urban squalor
- Supports "exploitation" narrative
- Shows costs of industrialization

## SOURCE VERIFICATION NOTES

**Red Flags to Watch For:**
- ⚠️ Sources claiming "Industrial Revolution made everyone better off immediately" (FALSE - wages stagnated until 1820)
- ⚠️ Sources claiming "no one benefited" (FALSE - eventual widespread gains)
- ⚠️ Cherry-picking data (either only early period or only late period)
- ⚠️ Ignoring timeline complexity (who benefited when matters)

**Green Flags (Trustworthy):**
- ✅ Academic sources with comprehensive wage/productivity data
- ✅ Primary parliamentary documents (unbiased factual records)
- ✅ Sources acknowledging both gains AND suffering
- ✅ Timeline-aware analysis (short-term vs. long-term)
- ✅ Class-stratified analysis (who benefited when)
