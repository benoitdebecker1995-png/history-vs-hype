## [OPENING] (0:00-0:45) - 85 words

Will AI take your job?

Amazon just announced plans to automate another 100,000 warehouse positions. Google's laying off workers while rolling out AI assistants. And you keep hearing the same promise: "It'll create more jobs than it destroys."

We've heard that promise before—200 years ago during the first Industrial Revolution.

On one side, you've got charts of GDP growth, life expectancy, "hockey stick" progress. On the other, child labor statistics, urban misery, novels about human suffering.

Both of these stories are cherry-picking the same 100-year period.

Here's what the evidence actually shows.

## [SECTION 1: The Stagnation Nobody Talks About] (0:45-2:30) - 200 words

The traditional narrative is a hockey stick. 1760 hits, steam power arrives, boom—explosive growth for everyone.

Modern economic historians have revised that story dramatically.

Real output per capita grew at 0.3 percent per year between 1760 and 1830. That's not a revolution. That's a crawl.

And for workers? Historians now identify something called "Engels' Pause"—a span of decades when technological progress actually weakened workers' incomes rather than raising them.

Here's the data: Real wages stagnated from the 1790s through the 1840s. Family incomes fell an estimated 14 percent between the 1790s and 1816.

And it gets worse when you look at the kids.

Historian Jane Humphries analyzed working-class autobiographies and found that "the classic era of industrialization, 1790–1850, apparently saw an upsurge in child labour." By 1851, at least 36 percent of children aged 10 to 14 were working—and that's probably an underestimate.

Robert Collyer, born in 1823, recalled starting factory work at age seven or eight, standing at spinning frames with other children: "13 hours a day five days a week and eleven on Saturday."

Urban mortality tells the same story. Best estimates put life expectancy in industrial Manchester at about 25 years in 1841. Rural Surrey? Around 45 years. A 20-year penalty for living in an industrial city.

For over half a century, technological progress did not translate into better lives for the people doing the work.

## [SECTION 2: Why It Was Still Essential] (2:30-4:00) - 160 words

Because the alternative was worse.

Britain's population was growing at 1.5 percent per year. Under the old economic rules—where wealth comes from land, and land is fixed—that kind of population boom should have been catastrophic.

Economist Joel Mokyr calculated that if the old pre-industrial rules had still applied, real income per person would have fallen 8 to 18 percent between 1760 and 1830.

The Industrial Revolution prevented that disaster. It broke what historians call the "iron link between population and living standards."

But here's the critical nuance: it prevented catastrophe without immediately creating prosperity.

Historian Charles Feinstein summarizes the evidence: "For the majority of the working class the historical reality was that they had to endure almost a century of hard toil with little or no advance from a low base before they really began to share in any of the benefits."

The gains did come. Wages increased by about 32 percent between 1810 and 1850, then accelerated dramatically. By the 1870s, real wages were growing continuously at roughly 1 percent per year.

And here's the surprising conclusion from modern research: unskilled workers—the poorest segment—benefited most in the long run.

So the Industrial Revolution was essential. But that doesn't mean the suffering was inevitable or that the delay was unavoidable.

## [SECTION 3: What Finally Changed] (4:00-5:30) - 170 words

Imagine a 9-year-old in 1830 working 13-hour days in a textile mill.

The 1833 Factory Act comes along. It bars children under 9 and limits hours for older kids. Sounds like reform, right?

Here's what happened: that same child, now "too young" for the regulated textile mill, gets reclassified into an unregulated workshop or mine. On paper, reform. In practice, nothing changed.

The proportion of working children aged 9 to 15 actually rose through the 1860s.

What finally ended child labor wasn't voluntary reform or factory regulation. It was compulsory education in the 1870s—forcing children out of the labor market entirely.

The same pattern held for other gains. The welfare state. Unionization. Mass public education. These weren't optional additions to industrialization. They were necessary mechanisms for converting technological potential into shared prosperity.

The first steam engines start running in the 1780s. It's not until the 1870s—nearly a century later—that compulsory education starts pulling kids out of factories for good.

Right now, we're trying to manage 21st-century AI with mid-20th-century labor laws and education systems. That's exactly the kind of institutional lag that made the first Industrial Revolution so painful for so long.

## [SECTION 4: The Pattern] (5:30-6:15) - 90 words

Three findings from the historical record.

First: technological disruption does not automatically create widespread prosperity. Engels' Pause lasted half a century. Gains can be delayed for generations while a small class captures most benefits.

Second: policy intervention isn't optional. Education infrastructure, welfare systems, worker protections—these weren't feel-good additions to the Industrial Revolution. They were the mechanisms that finally distributed the gains.

Third: institutional change takes time. The question isn't whether AI will transform the economy. It will. The question is how long we'll let the transition cost accumulate before building the new institutions to manage it.

The first Industrial Revolution forced societies to invent police forces, central banks, compulsory education. Two centuries later, we're repeating the experiment with AI. The question isn't whether we need new institutions—it's which ones, and whether we'll wait a century to build them.
