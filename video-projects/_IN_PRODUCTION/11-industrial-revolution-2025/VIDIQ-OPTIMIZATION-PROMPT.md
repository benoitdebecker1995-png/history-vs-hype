## VIDEO CONCEPT

I'm creating an educational history video debunking myths about the Industrial Revolution's impact on workers, with direct modern parallels to AI/automation debates.

**Core Thesis:** Both the "technology always creates prosperity" and "industrialization only brought suffering" narratives are wrong. The IR involved decades of brutal conditions (Engels' Pause: wage stagnation 1790s-1840s, child labor surge, urban mortality crisis) before substantial gains materialized post-1850. The lesson: technological disruption requires proactive policy intervention to distribute benefits—highly relevant to modern AI/automation anxiety.

**Target Audience:**
- Males 25-44 (primary)
- Interested in economics, history, technology debates
- Likely searching for: AI job displacement, automation impact, Industrial Revolution living standards, economic history, wage stagnation explanations
- Education level: College-educated or intellectually curious
- Political lean: Mixed (appeals to both pro-capitalism and progressive audiences by challenging both narratives)

**Video Structure:**
1. **Opening (0:00-0:45):** Modern automation anxiety hook → "we've been here before"
2. **Section 1 (0:45-2:45):** Debunk "instant prosperity" myth (Engels' Pause, child labor data, urban mortality)
3. **Section 2 (2:45-4:45):** Why IR was essential (Malthusian escape, long-term gains)
4. **Section 3 (4:45-6:30):** The policy lag lesson (took 50-70 years for institutions to catch up)
5. **Section 4 (6:30-7:30):** Modern implications → What new institutions do we need for AI era?

**Key Evidence/Data Points:**
- Real wages stagnated 1790s-1840s (Engels' Pause)
- Child labor surged 1790-1850 (36% of 10-14 year-olds working by 1851)
- Manchester life expectancy: 25.3 years (1841) vs. Rural Surrey: 45.1 years
- Growth was slow: 0.3% per capita (1760-1830)
- But essential: without IR, population boom would have caused 8-18% income decline
- Post-1850: substantial wage gains, life expectancy rose to 78+ years by 2009
- Modern parallel: wage stagnation + record profits = repeating Engels' Pause pattern

**Sources Cited:**

- Joel Mokyr (technological change expert)
- Primary sources: Robert Collyer testimony, 1851 Census, Sadler Committee
