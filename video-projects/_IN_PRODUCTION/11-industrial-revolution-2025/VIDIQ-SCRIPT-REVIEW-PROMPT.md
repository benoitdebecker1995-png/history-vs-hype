# VidIQ Script Review Prompt - Industrial Revolution

I have a 7:30 script on the Industrial Revolution debunking both the "technology always creates prosperity" and "industrialization only brought suffering" narratives. Target audience: males 25-44 interested in economics/history/tech. Please review and tell me if anything needs to change.

---

## VIDEO CONCEPT

**Thesis:** Both extremes are wrong. The Industrial Revolution involved 50+ years of brutal conditions (Engels' Pause: wage stagnation 1790s-1840s, child labor surge, urban mortality crisis) before substantial gains materialized post-1850s. Lesson: technological disruption requires proactive policy intervention—directly relevant to modern AI/automation debates.

**Target:** 6-8 minutes, evidence-based myth-busting with modern parallels

---

## SCRIPT STRUCTURE (825 words, 7:30 runtime)

### Opening (0:00-0:45) - 75 words
- Hook: Modern automation anxiety (AI, wage stagnation + record profits)
- "We've been here before" → Industrial Revolution had identical debates
- Preview: Both optimists and pessimists cherry-pick history

### Section 1: The Myth of Instant Prosperity (0:45-2:45) - 220 words
**Debunks "hockey stick" growth narrative:**
- Real growth was **0.3% per capita** (1760-1830)—not explosive
- **Engels' Pause:** Wages stagnated/fell 1790s-1840s
- Family incomes **fell 14%** (1790s to 1816/20)
- **Child labor surged** (not declined)—36% of 10-14 year-olds working by 1851
- Urban mortality crisis: Manchester **25.3 years** life expectancy vs. Rural Surrey **45.1 years**
- **Primary source:** Robert Collyer testimony—"13 hours a day five days a week" starting at age 7-8

**Retention Trigger (2:45):** "So if it was this brutal, why didn't everyone just starve?"

### Section 2: Why IR Was Essential (2:45-4:45) - 220 words
**Debunks "only brought suffering" narrative:**
- Population growing **1.5%/year**—under old rules, should have caused mass starvation
- **Malthusian counterfactual:** Without IR, real income would have fallen **8-18%** (Mokyr estimate)
- IR broke "iron link between population and living standards"
- **But gains delayed:** Feinstein quote—"almost a century of hard toil" before workers benefited
- **Long-term success:** Wages rose **32%** (1810-1850), accelerated post-1850s
- Life expectancy: **40 years** (1820s) → **78+ years** (2009)
- **Unskilled workers benefited most**—narrowed inequality

**Retention Trigger (4:45):** "Did it HAVE to take almost a century? Or was the delay a policy failure?"

### Section 3: The Policy Lag (4:45-6:30) - 230 words
**The real lesson:**
- Factory Acts **porous**—1833 Act barred children under 9, but they just moved to unregulated sectors
- **Compulsory education (1870s)** was decisive—not voluntary reform
- **50-70 years** from IR start to effective policy intervention
- Same institutions needed: welfare state, unionization, mass education
- **Modern parallel:** Current wage stagnation + record profits = Engels' Pause repeating
- Policy lesson: **"Don't wait until society has become completely horrible"**
- Problem: AI/automation accelerating, but we're using mid-20th-century institutions

**Retention Trigger (6:30):** "So what does 200 years of evidence tell us?"

### Section 4: Modern Implications (6:30-7:30) - 110 words
**Three findings:**
1. **Tech disruption ≠ automatic prosperity**—Engels' Pause lasted 50+ years
2. **Policy intervention essential**—education/welfare/protections weren't optional
3. **Nature of work matters**—IR replaced muscles, AI replaces minds (harder adjustment?)

**Provocative close:**
- First IR required new institutions (police, central banks, compulsory education)
- **Question:** What new institutions do WE need for AI era?

### Closing (7:30-7:45) - 55 words
- Full sources (Feinstein, Humphries, Allen, Mokyr with page numbers)
- Subscribe CTA
