## THE PRIMARY SOURCE: GRÁGÁS (GREY GOOSE LAWS)

### What It Is
- **Name:** Grágás (Old Norse: "Grey Goose")
- **Type:** Legal code of medieval Iceland
- **Origin:** Oral tradition from Viking Age, written down c. 1117-1118 AD
- **Language:** Old Norse/Old Icelandic
- **Survival:** Two main manuscripts (13th century copies)
  - Konungsbók (GKS 1157 fol.) - Royal Library, Copenhagen
  - Staðarhólsbók (AM 334 fol.) - Árni Magnússon Institute, Reykjavík

### Why "Grey Goose"?
- Origin of name unclear
- Theories: Grey goose quill used to write it, grey binding, or metaphorical
- Not important for video - acknowledge mystery and move on

### Historical Context
- Iceland settled c. 870-930 AD by Norse (Viking) settlers
- Althing established 930 AD - annual legal assembly
- Laws memorized and recited by Lawspeaker (lögsögumaður)
- Christianization 1000 AD
- Laws written down c. 1117-1118 under bishop's patronage
- Manuscripts we have are 13th century copies

## THE INSULT LAWS (NÍÐ) - VIRAL CONTENT

### What Is Níð?
- Old Norse concept of defamation/insult
- Attacked a person's honor and reputation
- Could be verbal or through actions
- Particularly serious: accusations of cowardice or sexual passivity

### Types of Insults (to verify specific provisions)
1. **Verbal insults (tunguníð)**
   - Calling someone a coward
   - Calling a man "beardless" (ragr/argr implications?)
   - Accusations of sexual passivity (argr, ragr, stroðinn)

2. **Composed insults (níðvísur)**
   - Insulting poetry
   - Particularly serious offense
   - Could result in outlawry

3. **Symbolic insults (tréníð)**
   - Níðstöng - "scorn pole" with horse head
   - Physical symbols of insult

## THE ALTHING - OLDEST PARLIAMENT

### Basic Facts
- **Established:** 930 AD (traditional date)
- **Location:** Þingvellir (Thingvellir), southwest Iceland
- **Frequency:** Annual, two weeks in summer (around summer solstice)
- **Attendees:** All free men (goðar - chieftains, and their followers)
- **Modern status:** Icelandic parliament still called Althing

### How It Worked
- **Lögretta** - Legislative council (chieftains + bishops after Christianization)
- **Lögsögumaður** - Lawspeaker, recited laws from memory
- **Quarter Courts** - Regional judicial bodies
- **Fifth Court** - Supreme court (established c. 1004)

### "Oldest Parliament" Claim
- ⏳ VERIFY: Is it actually the oldest?
- Competitors:
  - Isle of Man Tynwald (claimed 979 AD, but uncertain)
  - Faroese Løgting (possibly earlier)
- Althing claim: Oldest with continuous institutional history to present

### The Principle
- Injuries compensated with wergild (manngjöld) - "man payment"
- Amount varied by injury severity and victim's status
- Alternative to blood feud

## OUTLAWRY (ÚTLÆGÐ)

### Types
1. **Full outlawry (skóggangr)**
   - "Forest-going" - expelled from society
   - Could be killed by anyone without penalty
   - Property confiscated
   - Permanent (unless pardoned)

2. **Lesser outlawry (fjörbaugsgarðr)**
   - Three-year exile
   - Could return after period
   - Property preserved

### Famous Examples from Sagas
- ⏳ Erik the Red - outlawed, discovered Greenland
- ⏳ Grettir the Strong - Grettis saga
- ⏳ Characters in Njáls saga

## POTENTIAL COUNTERARGUMENTS

### "Grágás was written after the Viking Age"
**Response:** True - written down c. 1117. But it records oral tradition that predates writing. Scholars agree it reflects earlier practices. The Lawspeaker's role was to memorize and recite these laws for generations before they were written.

### "This is just Iceland, not all Vikings"
**Response:** Correct. Grágás is specifically Icelandic law. However, similar legal traditions existed across Scandinavia (Thing assemblies, compensation systems). Iceland is our best-documented example because they wrote it down.

### "Vikings still did lots of violence"
**Response:** Absolutely. The law code regulated violence, it didn't eliminate it. Blood feuds happened. Raids happened. But the existence of this complex legal system shows they weren't simply "lawless barbarians."
