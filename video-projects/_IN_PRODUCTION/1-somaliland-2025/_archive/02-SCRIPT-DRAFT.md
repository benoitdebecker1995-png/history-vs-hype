## OPENING HOOK (0:00-0:45)

Yesterday, Israel became the first country to recognize Somaliland.

And if you've followed this story at all, you've probably heard one of two narratives.

The first says Somaliland is a separatist region—it's been part of Somalia for 60 years, it has no legal right to independence, and Israel's recognition is basically an attack on Somali sovereignty.

The second says Somaliland was never legally part of Somalia at all—the treaty was void from day one, and this is just the world finally catching up.

I think both of these are oversimplified. Here's why.

**[DOCUMENT: "The Union of Somaliland and Somalia was never ratified" - AU Fact-Finding Mission, 2005]**

In 2005, the African Union itself sent a fact-finding mission to Somaliland. And here's what they found:

**[DOCUMENT: AU Report, page 4]**

"The fact that the union between Somaliland and Somalia was never ratified, and also malfunctioned when it went into action from 1960 to 1990, makes Somaliland's search for recognition **historically unique and self-justified** in African political history."

That's not my interpretation. That's the African Union's own investigation.

So why did the same African Union reject Israel's recognition yesterday? Why has no one followed their own recommendation in twenty years?

I'm going to show you the documents: the 1960 treaty that was never signed, the 1963 court ruling, and the AU report they've been ignoring. By the end, you'll understand the legal paradox—and why Israel just made it impossible to ignore.

## ACT 1: THE FIVE DAYS (0:45-3:30)

### The British Exit

Let's start with what actually happened in 1960.

**[MAP: British Somaliland Protectorate boundaries]**

Britain had controlled the Somaliland Protectorate since 1884. By 1960, decolonization was sweeping across Africa, and Britain was preparing to leave.

**[DOCUMENT: British Somaliland Independence Act 1960, page 1279]**

On June 26, 1960, the Somaliland Order in Council came into effect. Here's the exact language:

"Whereas it is intended that the territories known as the Somaliland Protectorate shall become an independent country on the 26th day of June, 1960."

That's not a promise. That's not a transition. That's independence.

### The Recognition Wave

**[MAP: Countries that recognized Somaliland in 1960]**

Within hours, the telegrams started arriving.

**[DOCUMENT: Chen Yi telegram to PM Egal, June 25, 1960]**

China's Foreign Minister Chen Yi sent congratulations before independence even took effect—June 25th. US Secretary of State Christian Herter followed on June 26th. The Soviet Union, Britain, France, Egypt, Ethiopia, Ghana, Israel—

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Within days, Somaliland exchanged diplomatic recognition with more than 35 states" — Somaliland Is Not De Facto, page 2]**

More than 35 states extended recognition. That's not informal acknowledgment. That's bilateral agreements. Formal recognition. De jure statehood.

### The Caveat

Now, I should be clear about something. One source notes this figure is "reportedly 35" and some UN archive records from this period were destroyed during later fighting. So I'm presenting this as the historical record—widely accepted, but worth noting the caveat.

What's not disputed: Somaliland existed as an independent state, recognized by major powers, for five days.

Then came July 1st.

## ACT 2: THE UNION THAT WASN'T (3:30-6:30)

### The Merger

**[MAP: British Somaliland + Italian Somalia → Somali Republic]**

The plan was always unification. British Somaliland in the north, Italian Somalia in the south—two former colonies, same ethnic group, merging into a single Somali Republic.

On July 1, 1960, Italian Somalia gained independence. The same day, both territories declared themselves unified.

But here's where the legal problem begins.

### The Documents

**[DOCUMENT: Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law, June 27, 1960]**

Somaliland's legislature passed the "Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law" on June 27, 1960.

**[DOCUMENT: Atto di Unione, June 30, 1960]**

Somalia's legislature passed the "Atto di Unione"—the Act of Union—on June 30, 1960.

Sounds straightforward. Two parliaments, two laws, one union.

Except they weren't the same law.

### The Legal Problem

This is the heart of the case—and I want to let the documents speak for themselves.

Eugene Cotran was a legal scholar who studied this in the 1960s. Here's what he found:

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, Legal Problems Arising out of the Formation of the Somali Republic, page 1012]**

"The Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law, and the Somalia Act of Union were both drafted in the form of bilateral agreements, but neither of them was signed by the representatives of the two territories."

Neither was signed by both sides.

It gets worse:

"The Somalia Act of Union was approved 'in principle' but never enacted into law. The decree law of July 1, 1960, did not come into effect since it was not converted into law in accordance with the Constitution."

So what does that mean? Somalia's parliament approved the union "in principle"—meaning provisionally, pending a final version. They even passed a resolution requesting:

**[DOCUMENT: Act of Union 1960 text, page 1]**

"the Government of Somalia to establish with the Government of Somaliland a definitive single text of the Act of Union, to be submitted to the National Assembly for approval."

That definitive single text? Never created.

### The Judicial Precedent

Now, this isn't just an academic argument. It was tested in court.

**[DOCUMENT: Prunier, The Country That Does Not Exist, page 21]**

In 1963, British Judge Hazelwood was presiding over a case involving northern officers. His ruling:

"In the absence of an Act of Union, the court had no jurisdiction over Somaliland."

A sitting judge ruled there was no valid union. In 1963.

### The Attempted Fix

Now, to be fair, Somalia did try to fix this. On January 31, 1961, the new Somali Republic passed Law No. 5—

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, page 1013]**

—which attempted to retroactively validate the union. Cotran notes:

"The position has now therefore been clarified... the two important instruments setting up the union and the new Somali State are the Act of Union, deemed to have come into effect on July 1, 1960, and the Constitution of the Somali Republic of July 1, 1960."

"Deemed to have come into effect." Retroactive legislation, curing a void agreement.

Whether you can retroactively fix a defect this fundamental—that's where legal scholars disagree. But the defect itself isn't disputed.

## ACT 3: THE COUNTER-ARGUMENT (6:30-8:00)

### Somalia's Case

Now, let me present Somalia's side fairly, because the strongest version of this argument matters.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Somalia's Legal Arguments"]**

**First:** The union was voluntary.

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, Understanding the Secession of Somaliland, page 14]**

"Northerners can in no way claim that the 1960 merger was a shotgun wedding—by all accounts unification was wildly popular."

And that's true. There were celebrations. Pan-Somali nationalism was the dominant political force. The merger wasn't imposed by force—people wanted it.

**Second:** The international community recognized the union.

Cotran again:

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, page 1012]**

"It is clear from the above account that a full union had in fact been effected by the will of the peoples of Somaliland and Somalia through their duly elected representatives and that such union was recognised by the international community."

The UN admitted the Somali Republic on September 20, 1960. Every nation accepted the unified state.

**Third:** 31 years of practice. Even if the paperwork was defective, the Somali Republic functioned as a single country from 1960 to 1991.

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 23]**

"The procedures to unify the two regions were completed, and the state continued to exist as a single national entity for more than two decades, regardless of any legal shortcomings associated with the initial steps of the merger."

These are legitimate arguments. The question is whether they outweigh the documentary evidence of a never-ratified treaty.

## ACT 4: THE 1991 RESTORATION (8:00-9:30)

### The Collapse

**[MAP: Somali Civil War, 1991]**

The Somali Republic didn't just fail—it imploded.

By 1991, the Siad Barre regime had collapsed. The south descended into warlord-controlled chaos. But the north—the former British Somaliland—took a different path.

### The Burao Conference

**[DOCUMENT: 1991 Burao Conference declaration]**

On May 18, 1991, elders and political leaders gathered in Burao. And here's what gets me: their declaration was very carefully worded.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Original Somali + Translation]**

"Dalkii hore logu yiqiin Somaliland ee Xorriyaddiisii ka qaatay Boqortooyada Ingriiska iyo Waqooyiiga Ireland 26kii Juun 1960 ayaa dib loogu soo celiyey Madaxbanaanidii laga bilaabo 18/05/91."

Translation: "The country previously known as Somaliland which obtained its independence from the United Kingdom and Northern Ireland on 26 June 1960 has **restored** its independence with effect from 18/05/91."

Do you see what they did? Not "declared." **Restored.**

### The Legal Distinction

This framing matters enormously in international law.

**[DOCUMENT: Crawford, The Creation of States in International Law, page 375]**

James Crawford—probably the most cited scholar on statehood—defines secession as "the creation of a State by the use or threat of force without the consent of the former sovereign."

Somaliland's argument is that they're not seceding. They're restoring sovereignty that existed on June 26, 1960—sovereignty from a union that was never legally completed.

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 22]**

"Somaliland has demanded secession based on several alternative historical, legal, and political justifications... [One] is based on a region's right to revive the colonized peoples' right to self-determination or restore their lost sovereignty, in a process dubbed 'restorative secession'."

Restoration. Not secession.

## ACT 5: THE BALTIC PRECEDENT (9:30-10:30)

### The Parallel

**[MAP: Baltic States 1940 → 1991]**

There's a precedent for this: Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania.

In 1940, the Soviet Union annexed the Baltic states. For 50 years, they existed as Soviet republics.

In 1990-91, they declared independence. But they didn't call it independence—they called it restoration.

**[DOCUMENT: Crawford, pages 394, 416]**

Crawford again: "The independence of the Baltic States was restored peacefully... the essential rationale was the recovery of independence forcibly suppressed in 1940."

**[DOCUMENT: Somaliland Is Not De Facto — It Is De Jure, page 4]**

"Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania were recognized as continuing states after decades of illegal annexation."

The international community accepted this framing. The Baltic states were recognized not as new countries, but as restored states.

### The Key Difference

But here's the crucial distinction—and this is where Somaliland's case gets complicated.

The international community never recognized the 1940 Soviet annexation as legal. It was always considered occupation.

The 1960 Somali union? Whatever its documentary defects, it was recognized by the entire international community for 31 years.

That's the gap Somaliland has to bridge. I'm not super convinced the Baltic precedent fully applies here—but I also don't think you can dismiss it entirely. Let me know what you think in the comments.

## ACT 6: THE AU PARADOX (10:30-12:00)

### The 2005 Mission

**[DOCUMENT: AU Fact-Finding Mission to Somaliland Resumé, 2005]**

Remember that AU report I mentioned at the beginning?

In April-May 2005, the African Union sent a fact-finding mission to Somaliland. Deputy Chairperson Patrick Mazimhaka led a team that spent five days investigating.

Their conclusion was remarkable.

### The Findings

**[DOCUMENT: AU Report, page 4 - displayed in full]**

"The fact that the 'union between Somaliland and Somalia was never ratified' and also malfunctioned when it went into action from 1960 to 1990, makes Somaliland's search for recognition historically unique and self-justified in African political history."

Read that again. The African Union's own investigation found the case "unique and self-justified."

But there's more.

"Objectively viewed, the case should not be linked to the notion of 'opening a Pandora's box'."

So the AU was directly addressing the precedent concern—the fear that recognizing Somaliland would trigger a wave of secessionist claims across Africa.

**[DOCUMENT: AU Report, page 3]**

"That process was anchored, and remains so, on the recognition by the Somalilanders of the inherited colonial borders at the time of independence from Britain in June 1960."

The AU distinguished Somaliland from other secessionist movements. Unlike Biafra or Katanga—regions trying to break from their original colonial unit—Somaliland is a complete colonial unit leaving a failed merger with a different colonial unit.

Their recommendation:

"The AU should find a special method of dealing with this outstanding case."

### The Irony

**[DOCUMENT: Somaliland Is Not De Facto — It Is De Jure, page 5]**

Here's the irony. The AU's refusal to recognize Somaliland is based on uti possidetis juris—basically, the principle that colonial borders at independence must be preserved.

But Somaliland's borders ARE the colonial borders at independence—June 26, 1960.

"The borders received by Somaliland at the moment of independence were those of the British Somaliland Protectorate, not the Somali Republic. As such, an independent Somaliland could be considered as satisfying the requirement of uti possidetis juris."

By refusing to recognize Somaliland's colonial borders, the AU is arguably violating its own doctrine. Which, yeah, puts things into perspective.

### Twenty Years of Nothing

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: 2005 → 2025: No AU Action]**

The AU's recommendation? "Find a special method."

Twenty years later, no method has been found. No action has been taken. The report has been effectively shelved.

**[DOCUMENT: Prunier, page 204]**

The historian Gérard Prunier put it pretty bluntly:

"The ironic paradox in the summer of 2002 was that, while a government did not actually exist in Mogadishu, it was recognized and disingenuously promoted by the U.N.; in contrast, the functioning and democratically elected Somaliland government, that owed virtually everything to its own efforts, remained unrecognized. This absurd anomaly did nothing to assist southern Somalia's recovery."

## ACT 7: THE RECOGNITION CASCADE (12:00-13:30)

### Ethiopia Moves First

**[MAP: Ethiopia, landlocked since Eritrea 1993]**

Ethiopia broke the logjam in January 2024.

Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed had been calling Red Sea access an "existential question" for his landlocked country. Ethiopia's reliance on Djibouti's port was expensive and geopolitically risky.

**[DOCUMENT: MOU announcement, January 2024]**

The Memorandum of Understanding: Ethiopia would lease a port facility at Berbera for 50 years. In exchange, Ethiopia agreed to conduct—quote—an "in-depth assessment" toward formally recognizing Somaliland. Not a guarantee. But the clearest signal yet from a major African power.

**[DOCUMENT: Khan/Idle, Exploring the Implications, page 902]**

"Ethiopia's economic expansion and military strategy are heavily dependent on access to maritime routes. The agreement provides Ethiopia with a strategic maritime outlet, enhancing its logistical capabilities and economic resilience."

Somalia was furious. The deal hasn't been finalized. But the precedent was set.

### Israel's Move

**[NEWS: December 26, 2025 headlines]**

Then, yesterday, Israel went further.

Prime Minister Netanyahu announced full diplomatic recognition. Not a port deal. Not a MOU. Full relations—ambassadors, embassies, the works.

**[QUOTE ON SCREEN: "In the spirit of the Abraham Accords" — Netanyahu]**

Foreign Minister Gideon Saar: "We will work together to promote the relations between our countries and nations, regional stability and economic prosperity."

Israel is the first UN member state to formally recognize Somaliland in 34 years.

### The Reaction

**[NEWS: Somalia's response]**

Somalia called it an "attack" on sovereignty. An "unlawful action." They vowed to pursue "all necessary diplomatic, political, and legal measures."

**[DOCUMENT: AU statement, December 26, 2025]**

The AU responded within hours. Chairperson Mahmoud Ali Youssouf:

"Firmly rejects any initiative or action aimed at recognizing Somaliland as an independent entity. Somaliland remains an integral part of the Federal Republic of Somalia."

The same AU that called Somaliland's case "unique and self-justified" in 2005 is now calling recognition a "dangerous precedent."

## CONCLUSION (13:30-14:30)

### The Legal Question

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "The Country That Already Existed"]**

So what's actually happening here?

Somaliland isn't trying to become independent. It's arguing that it already was—for five days in 1960, recognized by 35 nations, before signing a union treaty that was never properly ratified.

The documents support this. The 1963 court ruling supports this. The AU's own 2005 investigation called it "unique and self-justified."

### Returning to Both Extremes

So that first narrative—Somaliland is just a separatist region with no legal basis? The 1960 independence, the 35 recognitions, the never-ratified treaty, the 2005 AU report—that undercuts it pretty significantly.

But the second narrative—that Somaliland was never part of Somalia at all? The 31 years of unified practice, the international recognition of that union, the voluntary nature of the merger—that complicates it.

The truth is somewhere in between. The legal case for Somaliland is stronger than most people realize. But the political obstacles are real.

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 25]**

"The AU fears that the recognition of Somaliland would spur the emergence of more secessionist cases on the continent."

South Sudan is the only successful modern African secession—and that required parent state consent. Somaliland doesn't have that.

Somalia will never consent. Which means the international community has to decide: Does documentary evidence trump political reality? Does a never-ratified treaty from 1960 matter more than 31 years of unified statehood?

### The Paradox

**[DOCUMENT: Somaliland Is Not De Facto — It Is De Jure, pages 1-2]**

"Somaliland is not merely de facto. It is de jure—by virtue of its independence on June 26, 1960. The failure to acknowledge this reality is not a matter of ambiguity or unresolved history; it is a political choice that contradicts the very legal principles the international system claims to uphold."

Israel just made that choice impossible to ignore.

The documents exist. The precedents exist. The AU's own investigation supports the case.

The question now is whether anyone will act on them.
