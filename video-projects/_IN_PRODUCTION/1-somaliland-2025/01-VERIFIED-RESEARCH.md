## SECTION 2: The Act of Union Failure

### ✅ CLAIM 4: The Act of Union Was Never Properly Ratified

**Verified via:** Cotran, Act of Union text, NotebookLM

**Verified facts:**
- Two DIFFERENT documents were passed by the two legislatures
- Somaliland passed: "Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law" (June 27, 1960)
- Somalia passed: "Atto di Unione" (June 30, 1960) - approved "in principle" only
- Neither was signed by representatives of both territories
- Somalia's legislature requested a "definitive single text" — never created

**Verified quotes:**

> "The Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law, and the Somalia Act of Union were both drafted in the form of bilateral agreements, but neither of them was signed by the representatives of the two territories. . . . The Somalia Act of Union was approved 'in principle' but never enacted into law. . . . The decree law of July 1, 1960, did not come into effect since it was not converted into law in accordance with the Constitution."
> — Cotran, Legal Problems Arising out of the Formation of the Somali Republic, page 1012

> "The Assembly [of Somalia] requested the 'Government of Somalia to establish with the Government of Somaliland a definitive single text of the Act of Union, to be submitted to the National Assembly for approval'."
> — Act of Union 1960 text, page 1

### ✅ CLAIM 8: Legal Distinction Between Restoration and Secession

**Verified via:** Crawford, Mahmoud, NotebookLM

**Verified definitions:**
- **Secession:** "The creation of a State by the use or threat of force without the consent of the former sovereign." (Crawford, page 375)
- **Restoration/Restorative secession:** Reclamation of previously held and recognized sovereignty

**Verified quote:**
> "Somaliland has demanded secession based on several alternative historical, legal, and political justifications. . . . [One] is based on a region's right to revive the colonized peoples' right to self-determination or restore their lost sovereignty, in a process dubbed 'restorative secession'."
> — Mahmoud, Understanding the Secession of Somaliland, page 22

## SECTION 9: The Paradox (Narrative Summary)

### The Core Paradox

> "The ironic paradox in the summer of 2002 was that, while a government did not actually exist in Mogadishu, it was recognized and disingenuously promoted by the U.N.; in contrast, the functioning and democratically elected Somaliland government, that owed virtually everything to its own efforts, remained unrecognized. This absurd anomaly did nothing to assist southern Somalia's recovery."
> — Prunier, The Country That Does Not Exist, page 204

### The Uti Possidetis Contradiction

> "Somaliland's borders are exactly those — the borders it inherited at independence in 1960. Ironically, refusing to recognize Somaliland undermines the AU's own legal doctrine."
> — Somaliland Is Not De Facto — It Is De Jure, page 5

> "The borders received by Somaliland at the moment of independence were those of the British Somaliland Protectorate, not the Somali Republic. As such, an independent Somaliland could be considered as satisfying the requirement of uti possidetis juris."
> — ICG Report 110, page 15

### The System

> "Somaliland is not merely de facto. It is de jure — by virtue of its independence on June 26, 1960... The failure to acknowledge this reality is not a matter of ambiguity or unresolved history; it is a political choice that contradicts the very legal principles the international system claims to uphold."
> — Somaliland Is Not De Facto — It Is De Jure, pages 1-2
