**[MAP: Horn of Africa → Zoom to Somaliland]**

Thirty-five countries recognized Somaliland in 1960.

Then they all forgot.

**[MAP: Gulf of Aden trade corridor]**

This is where 12% of global trade passes—and where five powers are now competing for control. Ethiopia—120 million people—is landlocked and desperate for port access. China, the UAE, Turkey, Israel—all want in.

**[ON-CAMERA]**

And right in the middle: a functioning democracy with its own government, currency, and borders—that the world pretends doesn't exist.

**[STAKES - 0:25]**

In December 2025, Israel became the first UN member to formally recognize Somaliland. Somalia called it an attack. The AU rejected it within hours.

**[AUTHORITY MARKER - 0:30]**

I went to the documents. The 1960 treaty that was never signed. The 1963 court ruling. The AU's own investigation they've been ignoring for twenty years.

**[PAYOFF - 0:35]**

Here's what they found—and why both popular narratives get this wrong.

## ACT 1: THE FIVE DAYS (0:45-3:15)

**[MAP: British Somaliland Protectorate, 1960]**

**[VO]** Britain controlled the Somaliland Protectorate from 1884—a separate colony with its own borders. By 1960, decolonization was sweeping Africa.

---

**[DOCUMENT DISPLAY: British Somaliland Independence Act 1960]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

On June 26, 1960, the Somaliland Order in Council came into effect.

**[PRIMARY SOURCE READOUT]**

Reading directly from the Act, page 1279:

"The territories known as the Somaliland Protectorate shall become an independent country on the 26th day of June, 1960."

Not a promise. Not a transition. Independence.

---

**[MAP: Countries recognizing Somaliland - animated flags appearing]**

**[VO]** Within hours, the telegrams arrived.

**[DOCUMENT: Chen Yi telegram]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

China's Foreign Minister sent congratulations before independence even took effect—June 25th. The US followed June 26th. Soviet Union. Britain. France. Egypt. Ethiopia. Ghana. Israel.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "35+ states extended formal recognition"]**

More than 35 states extended formal recognition through bilateral agreements, establishing de jure statehood.

---

**[ON-CAMERA]**

**[CAVEAT]**

Now—one caveat. Some sources note this figure is "reportedly 35." Certain UN archives from this period were destroyed. I'm presenting the historical record as widely accepted.

What's not disputed: Somaliland existed as an independent, recognized state for five days.

Then came July 1st.

---

## ACT 2: THE UNION THAT WASN'T (3:15-6:00)

**[MAP: British Somaliland + Italian Somalia → Somali Republic]**

**[VO]** The plan was unification. British Somaliland in the north. Italian Somalia in the south. Same ethnic group, two colonies, one new republic.

---

**[ON-CAMERA]**

On July 1, 1960, Italian Somalia gained independence. The same day, both territories declared themselves unified.

But here's where the legal problem begins.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law]**

Somaliland's legislature passed the "Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law" on June 27th.

**[DOCUMENT: Atto di Unione]**

Somalia's legislature passed the "Atto di Unione" on June 30th.

Two parliaments. Two laws. One union.

Except they weren't the same law.

---

**[DOCUMENT DISPLAY: Cotran, Legal Problems, page 1012]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Eugene Cotran was a legal scholar who examined the union documents while they were still fresh.

**[PRIMARY SOURCE READOUT]**

Reading from his analysis:

"The Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law, and the Somalia Act of Union were both drafted in the form of bilateral agreements, but neither of them was signed by the representatives of the two territories."

Neither signed by both sides.

It gets worse.

"The Somalia Act of Union was approved 'in principle' but never enacted into law."

---

**[DOCUMENT: Act of Union 1960, page 1]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Somalia's parliament even passed a resolution requesting—quote—"a definitive single text of the Act of Union, to be submitted to the National Assembly for approval."

That text was never created.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Prunier, page 21]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

This isn't academic. It was tested in court.

**[PRIMARY SOURCE READOUT]**

1963. British Judge Hazelwood ruled:

"In the absence of an Act of Union, the court had no jurisdiction over Somaliland."

A sitting judge ruled in 1963 that there was no valid union.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, page 1013]**

**[VO]** Somalia did try to fix this. January 31, 1961—Law No. 5 attempted to retroactively validate the union.

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Cotran's analysis: "The Act of Union, deemed to have come into effect on July 1, 1960."

"Deemed." Retroactive legislation curing a void agreement.

Whether you can retroactively fix a defect this fundamental—scholars disagree. But the defect itself isn't disputed.

So how does Somalia defend a union built on paperwork that doesn't exist?

---

## ACT 3: SOMALIA'S CASE (6:00-7:15)

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Somalia's Legal Arguments]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Let me present Somalia's side fairly. The strongest version of this argument matters.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 14]**

**First:** The union was voluntary.

**[PRIMARY SOURCE READOUT]**

"Northerners can in no way claim that the 1960 merger was a shotgun wedding—by all accounts unification was wildly popular."

That's true. There were celebrations. Pan-Somali nationalism was the dominant force. This wasn't conquest.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, page 1012]**

**Second:** The international community recognized the union.

"A full union had in fact been effected by the will of the peoples... and such union was recognised by the international community."

The UN admitted the Somali Republic on September 20, 1960. Every nation accepted the unified state.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 23]**

**Third:** Thirty-one years of practice.

"The state continued to exist as a single national entity for more than two decades, regardless of any legal shortcomings."

---

**[ON-CAMERA]**

These are legitimate arguments—and they're why this case is harder than either side admits. The question is whether practice and recognition can cure a treaty defect this fundamental.

---

## ACT 4: THE RESTORATION (7:15-8:30)

**[MAP: Somalia 1991 - collapse]**

**[VO]** The Somali Republic didn't just fail. It imploded.

**[ON-CAMERA]**

By 1991, the Siad Barre regime had collapsed. The south descended into warlord chaos. The north—former British Somaliland—took a different path.

---

**[DOCUMENT: 1991 Burao Declaration]**

On May 18, 1991, elders gathered in Burao. Their declaration was carefully worded.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Original Somali + Translation]**

**[PRIMARY SOURCE READOUT]**

"Dalkii hore logu yiqiin Somaliland... ayaa dib loogu soo celiyey Madaxbanaanidii."

Translation: "The country previously known as Somaliland... has restored its independence."

---

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Not "declared." Restored.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Crawford, page 375]**

That framing matters. James Crawford—the most cited scholar on statehood—defines secession as "the creation of a State by the use or threat of force without the consent of the former sovereign."

Somaliland's argument: they're not seceding. They're restoring sovereignty that existed on June 26, 1960—from a union that was never legally completed.

---

## ACT 5: THE PRECEDENT (8:30-9:00)

**[MAP: Baltic States 1940 → 1991]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

There's a precedent: Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania.

In 1990-91, the Baltic states didn't declare independence—they restored it. Recovery of sovereignty suppressed in 1940.

The international community accepted that framing—because they'd never recognized the suppression as legal.

**[CAVEAT]**

But there's a key difference. The world never recognized the 1940 Soviet annexation as legal. The 1960 Somali union was recognized for 31 years.

Whether that difference is fatal depends on who's deciding. And for twenty years, the AU has been the one deciding.

---

## ACT 6: THE AU PARADOX (9:00-10:45)

**[DOCUMENT: AU Fact-Finding Mission 2005]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Remember the AU investigation I mentioned?

April 2005. The African Union sent a fact-finding mission to Somaliland. Deputy Chairperson Patrick Mazimhaka. Five days investigating.

---

**[DOCUMENT DISPLAY: AU Report, page 4 - FULL SCREEN]**

**[PRIMARY SOURCE READOUT - KEY MOMENT]**

Their conclusion:

"The fact that the union between Somaliland and Somalia was never ratified... makes Somaliland's search for recognition historically unique and self-justified in African political history."

That's not my interpretation. That's the AU's own fact-finding mission.

---

**[ON-CAMERA]**

But there's more.

"Objectively viewed, the case should not be linked to the notion of opening a Pandora's box."

---

**[DOCUMENT: AU Report, page 3]**

The AU distinguished Somaliland from other secessionist movements. Unlike Biafra or Katanga—regions breaking from their original colonial unit—Somaliland is a complete colonial unit leaving a failed merger.

Their recommendation: "The AU should find a special method of dealing with this outstanding case."

---

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: 2005 → 2025]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

That was twenty years ago. No special method was ever found. No action was ever taken.

---

**[DOCUMENT: De Jure essay, page 5]**

Here's the irony. The AU refuses recognition based on uti possidetis juris—colonial borders at independence must be preserved.

But Somaliland's borders ARE the colonial borders. June 26, 1960.

By refusing to recognize those borders, the AU is arguably violating its own doctrine.

---

## ACT 7: THE CASCADE (10:45-12:00)

**[MAP: Ethiopia - landlocked]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Ethiopia broke the logjam in January 2024.

Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed called Red Sea access an "existential question." Reliance on Djibouti's port was expensive and risky.

---

**[DOCUMENT: MOU, January 2024]**

The deal: Ethiopia leases a port at Berbera for 50 years. In exchange, Ethiopia agreed to an "in-depth assessment" toward recognition.

Not a guarantee. But the clearest signal yet from a major African power.

---

**[NEWS FOOTAGE: December 2025]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Then Israel went further.

Prime Minister Netanyahu announced full diplomatic recognition, including the exchange of ambassadors and the opening of embassies.

**[QUOTE ON SCREEN: "In the spirit of the Abraham Accords"]**

Israel—the first UN member to formally recognize Somaliland in 34 years.

---

**[NEWS: Somalia response]**

Somalia called it an "attack" on sovereignty.

**[DOCUMENT: AU statement, December 2025]**

Within hours, the African Union responded by firmly rejecting any initiative aimed at recognizing Somaliland.

The same AU that called the case "unique and self-justified" now calls recognition a "dangerous precedent."

---

## CONCLUSION (12:00-13:00)

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: The Country That Already Existed]**

**[ON-CAMERA]**

So what's happening here?

Somaliland isn't trying to become independent. They're arguing they already were—for five days in 1960, recognized by 35 nations, before a union that was never properly ratified.

The documents support this. The 1963 ruling supports this. The AU's own investigation called it "unique and self-justified."

---

**[RETURN TO EXTREMES]**

That first narrative—Somaliland is just a separatist region with no legal basis? The 1960 independence, the 35 recognitions, the never-ratified treaty, the 2005 AU report—that undercuts it.

But the second narrative—Somaliland was never part of Somalia? The 31 years of practice, the international recognition, the voluntary merger—that complicates it.

The legal case is stronger than most people realize. The political obstacles are real.

---

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 25]**

"The AU fears that recognition would spur more secessionist cases."

South Sudan is the only modern African secession—and that required parent state consent. Somalia will never consent.

---

**[ON-CAMERA]**

Which means the international community has to decide: Does documentary evidence trump political convenience?

**[DOCUMENT: De Jure essay, pages 1-2]**

"Somaliland is not merely de facto. It is de jure—by virtue of its independence on June 26, 1960. The failure to acknowledge this is not ambiguity. It is a political choice."

Israel just made that choice impossible to ignore.

The documents exist. The precedents exist. The AU's own investigation called it "unique and self-justified."

The only thing missing is the political will to read them.

---

If you want to understand how colonial decisions still shape modern conflicts—subscribe.
