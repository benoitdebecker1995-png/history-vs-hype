## OPENING HOOK (0:00-1:30)

**[MAP: Horn of Africa, Somaliland highlighted]**

Thirty-five countries recognized Somaliland in 1960. Then they all forgot.

**[MAP: Zoom to Gulf of Aden / Red Sea chokepoint]**

Somaliland sits here—at the mouth of the Red Sea. Twelve percent of global trade passes through the Gulf of Aden. Every ship traveling between Europe and Asia funnels through this corridor.

**[MAP: Ethiopia highlighted as landlocked]**

Ethiopia—120 million people—is landlocked. They're desperate for port access. China, the UAE, Turkey, and now Israel are all competing for influence in this region.

**[MAP: Return to Somaliland]**

And right in the middle of all of this sits a functioning democracy with its own government, its own currency, its own borders—that almost no one officially recognizes.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

In December 2025, Israel became the first UN member state to formally recognize Somaliland. Somalia called it an attack on sovereignty. The African Union rejected it within hours.

But here's what makes this strange.

**[DOCUMENT: AU Fact-Finding Mission 2005, page 4]**

In 2005, the African Union itself investigated Somaliland's case. Their conclusion:

"The union between Somaliland and Somalia was never ratified... makes Somaliland's search for recognition historically unique and self-justified in African political history."

That's not my interpretation. That's the AU's own fact-finding mission.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

So why did the same organization reject Israel's recognition twenty years later?

The answer is in the documents: a 1960 treaty that was never signed, a 1963 court ruling that found no valid union existed, and an AU report that's been ignored for two decades.

So how did this happen? How did a country get recognized, then merged, then erased—and why is the world only now starting to remember?

## ACT 1: THE FIVE DAYS (1:30-4:00)

**[MAP: British Somaliland Protectorate, 1960 boundaries]**

**[VO]** Britain controlled the Somaliland Protectorate from 1884. By 1960, decolonization was sweeping Africa.

**[DOCUMENT: British Somaliland Independence Act 1960, page 1279]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

On June 26, 1960, the Somaliland Order in Council came into effect. The exact language:

"The territories known as the Somaliland Protectorate shall become an independent country on the 26th day of June, 1960."

Not a promise. Not a transition. Independence.

**[MAP: Countries that recognized Somaliland, animated]**

**[VO]** Within hours, the telegrams started arriving.

**[DOCUMENT: Chen Yi telegram, June 25, 1960]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

China's Foreign Minister sent congratulations before independence even took effect. The US followed on June 26th. Soviet Union. Britain. France. Egypt. Ethiopia. Ghana. Israel.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: "Within days, Somaliland exchanged diplomatic recognition with more than 35 states"]**

More than 35 states extended formal recognition. Bilateral agreements. De jure statehood.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Now—one caveat. Some sources note this figure is "reportedly 35" and certain UN archives from this period were destroyed. I'm presenting the historical record as widely accepted.

What's not disputed: Somaliland existed as an independent, recognized state for five days.

Then came July 1st.

## ACT 2: THE UNION THAT WASN'T (4:00-7:00)

**[MAP: British Somaliland + Italian Somalia merging into Somali Republic]**

**[VO]** The plan was unification. British Somaliland in the north, Italian Somalia in the south. Same ethnic group, two colonies, one new republic.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

On July 1, 1960, Italian Somalia gained independence. The same day, both territories declared themselves unified.

But here's where the legal problem begins.

**[DOCUMENT: Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law, June 27, 1960]**

Somaliland's legislature passed the "Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law" on June 27th.

**[DOCUMENT: Atto di Unione, June 30, 1960]**

Somalia's legislature passed the "Atto di Unione" on June 30th.

Two parliaments. Two laws. One union.

Except they weren't the same law.

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, page 1012]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Eugene Cotran was a legal scholar who studied this in the 1960s. Here's what he found:

"The Union of Somaliland and Somalia Law, and the Somalia Act of Union were both drafted in the form of bilateral agreements, but neither of them was signed by the representatives of the two territories."

Neither was signed by both sides.

It gets worse.

"The Somalia Act of Union was approved 'in principle' but never enacted into law."

**[DOCUMENT: Act of Union 1960, page 1]**

Somalia's parliament even passed a resolution requesting—quote—"a definitive single text of the Act of Union, to be submitted to the National Assembly for approval."

That text was never created.

**[DOCUMENT: Prunier, page 21]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

This isn't just academic. It was tested in court.

In 1963, British Judge Hazelwood ruled: "In the absence of an Act of Union, the court had no jurisdiction over Somaliland."

A sitting judge. 1963. No valid union.

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, page 1013]**

**[VO]** Somalia did try to fix this. On January 31, 1961, the new Somali Republic passed Law No. 5—attempting to retroactively validate the union.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Cotran's analysis: "The Act of Union, deemed to have come into effect on July 1, 1960."

"Deemed." Retroactive legislation, curing a void agreement.

Whether you can retroactively fix a defect this fundamental—that's where legal scholars disagree. But the defect itself isn't disputed.

## ACT 3: THE COUNTER-ARGUMENT (7:00-8:30)

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Somalia's Legal Arguments]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Let me present Somalia's side fairly. The strongest version of this argument matters.

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 14]**

**First:** The union was voluntary.

"Northerners can in no way claim that the 1960 merger was a shotgun wedding—by all accounts unification was wildly popular."

That's true. There were celebrations. Pan-Somali nationalism was the dominant force. This wasn't imposed by conquest.

**[DOCUMENT: Cotran, page 1012]**

**Second:** The international community recognized the union.

"A full union had in fact been effected by the will of the peoples of Somaliland and Somalia... and such union was recognised by the international community."

The UN admitted the Somali Republic on September 20, 1960. Every nation accepted the unified state.

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 23]**

**Third:** Thirty-one years of practice.

"The state continued to exist as a single national entity for more than two decades, regardless of any legal shortcomings."

These are legitimate arguments. The question is whether they outweigh the documentary evidence of a treaty that was never properly ratified.

## ACT 4: THE 1991 RESTORATION (8:30-9:45)

**[MAP: Somali Civil War, 1991]**

**[VO]** The Somali Republic didn't just fail. It imploded.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

By 1991, the Siad Barre regime had collapsed. The south descended into warlord chaos. But the north—former British Somaliland—took a different path.

**[DOCUMENT: 1991 Burao Declaration]**

On May 18, 1991, elders and leaders gathered in Burao. Their declaration was carefully worded.

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: Original Somali + Translation]**

"Dalkii hore logu yiqiin Somaliland... ayaa dib loogu soo celiyey Madaxbanaanidii."

Translation: "The country previously known as Somaliland... has restored its independence."

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Not "declared." Restored.

**[DOCUMENT: Crawford, page 375]**

That framing matters. James Crawford—the most cited scholar on statehood—defines secession as "the creation of a State by the use or threat of force without the consent of the former sovereign."

Somaliland's argument: they're not seceding. They're restoring sovereignty that existed on June 26, 1960—from a union that was never legally completed.

## ACT 5: THE PRECEDENT (9:45-10:15)

**[MAP: Baltic States, 1940 → 1991]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

There's a precedent for this: Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania.

In 1990-91, the Baltic states declared independence from the Soviet Union. But they called it restoration—recovery of independence suppressed in 1940.

The international community accepted that framing. They were recognized as continuing states, not new ones.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

But there's a key difference. The world never recognized the 1940 Soviet annexation as legal. The 1960 Somali union was recognized for 31 years.

Whether that difference is fatal to Somaliland's case—scholars disagree.

---

## ACT 6: THE AU PARADOX (10:15-11:45)

**[DOCUMENT: AU Fact-Finding Mission, 2005]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Remember the AU report from the opening?

In April 2005, the African Union sent a fact-finding mission to Somaliland. Deputy Chairperson Patrick Mazimhaka led a team that spent five days investigating.

**[DOCUMENT: AU Report, page 4 - full display]**

Their conclusion:

"The fact that the union between Somaliland and Somalia was never ratified... makes Somaliland's search for recognition historically unique and self-justified in African political history."

But there's more.

"Objectively viewed, the case should not be linked to the notion of opening a Pandora's box."

**[DOCUMENT: AU Report, page 3]**

The AU distinguished Somaliland from other secessionist movements. Unlike Biafra or Katanga—regions breaking from their original colonial unit—Somaliland is a complete colonial unit leaving a failed merger.

Their recommendation: "The AU should find a special method of dealing with this outstanding case."

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: 2005 → 2025: No AU Action]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Twenty years. No method found. No action taken.

**[DOCUMENT: De Jure essay, page 5]**

Here's the irony. The AU refuses to recognize Somaliland based on uti possidetis juris—colonial borders at independence must be preserved.

But Somaliland's borders ARE the colonial borders. June 26, 1960.

By refusing to recognize those borders, the AU is arguably violating its own doctrine.

## ACT 7: THE RECOGNITION CASCADE (11:45-13:00)

**[MAP: Ethiopia, landlocked]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Ethiopia broke the logjam in January 2024.

Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed had called Red Sea access an "existential question." The country's reliance on Djibouti's port was expensive and risky.

**[DOCUMENT: MOU, January 2024]**

The deal: Ethiopia would lease a port at Berbera for 50 years. In exchange, Ethiopia agreed to an "in-depth assessment" toward recognition.

Not a guarantee. But the clearest signal yet from a major African power.

**[NEWS: December 2025]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Then Israel went further.

Prime Minister Netanyahu announced full diplomatic recognition. Ambassadors. Embassies. The works.

**[QUOTE ON SCREEN: "In the spirit of the Abraham Accords"]**

Israel is the first UN member to formally recognize Somaliland in 34 years.

**[NEWS: Somalia response]**

Somalia called it an "attack" on sovereignty. They vowed to pursue "all necessary diplomatic, political, and legal measures."

**[DOCUMENT: AU statement, December 2025]**

The AU responded within hours: "Firmly rejects any initiative aimed at recognizing Somaliland as an independent entity."

The same AU that called the case "unique and self-justified" now calls recognition a "dangerous precedent."

## CONCLUSION (13:00-14:00)

**[TEXT ON SCREEN: The Country That Already Existed]**

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

So what's actually happening here?

Somaliland isn't trying to become independent. They're arguing they already were—for five days in 1960, recognized by 35 nations, before a union that was never properly ratified.

The documents support this. The 1963 court ruling supports this. The AU's own investigation called it "unique and self-justified."

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

That first narrative—Somaliland is just a separatist region with no legal basis? The 1960 independence, the 35 recognitions, the never-ratified treaty, the 2005 AU report—that undercuts it.

But the second narrative—Somaliland was never part of Somalia at all? The 31 years of practice, the international recognition of that union, the voluntary merger—that complicates it.

The legal case is stronger than most people realize. The political obstacles are real.

**[DOCUMENT: Mahmoud, page 25]**

"The AU fears that recognition would spur more secessionist cases across the continent."

South Sudan is the only modern African secession—and that required parent state consent. Somalia will never consent.

**[DIRECT TO CAMERA]**

Which means the international community has to decide: Does documentary evidence trump political convenience? Does a never-ratified treaty from 1960 matter more than 31 years of unified statehood?

**[DOCUMENT: De Jure essay, pages 1-2]**

"Somaliland is not merely de facto. It is de jure—by virtue of its independence on June 26, 1960. The failure to acknowledge this is not ambiguity. It is a political choice."

Israel just made that choice impossible to ignore.

The documents exist. The precedents exist. The AU's own investigation supports the case.

The question is whether anyone will act on them.
