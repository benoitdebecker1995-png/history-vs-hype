📄 WHAT THE DOCUMENTS SHOW

The 1859 Wyke-Aycinena Treaty established the boundary in Article 1 with specific coordinates. Article 7 said Britain would use "best efforts" to build a road from Guatemala City to the Atlantic coast. Britain never built it.

Guatemala's ICJ memorial (December 2020): "No road" means the treaty's void.
Belize's counter-memorial (June 2022): The road clause was separate from the boundary - a breach of Article 7 doesn't void Article 1.

The legal test comes from the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, Article 60: Was the broken promise "essential to the accomplishment of the object or purpose of the treaty"?

The historical twist: From 1859 to 1939, Guatemala cooperated in marking boundary points, recognized British Honduras on official maps, and engaged in cross-border relations. 80 years of accepting that boundary.

🎯 WHY THIS MATTERS

If Guatemala wins, Belize loses nearly 50% of its territory - including areas with significant natural resources and population centers. If Belize wins, it confirms sovereignty over disputed lands it has controlled since independence.

The ruling will set precedent for how the ICJ interprets colonial treaties where one party claims the agreement was conditional and unfulfilled.

When this ruling comes out in 2027, thousands of Maya people will still have had zero say in a process deciding whether their homeland gets divided. Both Guatemala's and Belize's narratives erase how international courts actually work - applying law (Vienna Convention, Uti possidetis juris, treaty stability), not fairness or political preference.
