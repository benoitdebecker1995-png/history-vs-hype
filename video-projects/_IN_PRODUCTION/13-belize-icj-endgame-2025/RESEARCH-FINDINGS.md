## CRITICAL CORRECTION: Material Breach Case

**❌ INCORRECT (from initial PROJECT-BRIEF):**
- Burkina Faso v Mali (1986) demonstrates material breach test

**✅ CORRECT:**
- **Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros (Hungary/Slovakia) 1997** is the ICJ case addressing material breach
- **Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties Article 60** is the primary legal source
- **Burkina Faso v Mali (1986)** IS valid for uti possidetis juris principle, but NOT for material breach

**Impact:** Script structure needs adjustment. Replace Burkina Faso v Mali in Section 2 with Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros for material breach discussion.

**Full text obtained:**
> "The Court, whose function is to decide in accordance with international law such disputes as are submitted to it, shall apply:
> a. international conventions, whether general or particular, establishing rules expressly recognized by the contesting states;
> b. international custom, as evidence of a general practice accepted as law;
> c. the general principles of law recognized by civilized nations;
> d. subject to the provisions of Article 59, judicial decisions and the teachings of the most highly qualified publicists of the various nations, as subsidiary means for the determination of rules of law."

**Relevance:** THE case for material breach of treaties

**Why this case is crucial:**
- ICJ's most comprehensive analysis of material breach under Vienna Convention Article 60
- Hungary claimed Slovakia materially breached 1977 treaty

- Directly applicable to Guatemala's claim that Britain's road breach voids 1859 treaty

**Quote from search:**

### ✅ SOURCE 6: Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties Article 60 (VERIFIED)

**Status:** Fully accessible
**Official source:** https://legal.un.org/ilc/texts/instruments/english/conventions/1_1_1969.pdf

**Article 60 content:**
- Defines material breach of bilateral/multilateral treaties
- States when breach justifies termination vs. suspension
- Exception: Treaties of humanitarian character cannot be terminated for breach

**Relevance to Belize:**
- Guatemala claims Britain's failure to build road = material breach voiding treaty
- Article 60 sets legal test: breach must be "essential to the accomplishment of [treaty's] object or purpose"
- Question: Was Article 7 road "essential" to boundary treaty's purpose?

### SECTION 2: Three Past Cases - What ICJ Actually Did (2:00-5:00)

**Case 1: Nicaragua v Colombia (2012) - Treaty Validity**
- Nicaragua claimed 1928 treaty should be void
- ICJ: Treaty valid, BUT Colombia violated maritime rights anyway
- **Lesson:** Treaty breach ≠ automatic voidance
- **Parallel:** Guatemala claims 1859 treaty void, but ICJ may uphold it anyway

**B-roll:** Display excerpt from 2012 ruling

**Case 2: Cameroon v Nigeria (2002) - Colonial Boundaries Stick**
- Nigeria had effective control of Bakassi Peninsula for decades
- ICJ: Colonial treaty supersedes effective control (uti possidetis juris)
- **Lesson:** "We've been here longer" doesn't override colonial treaties
- **Parallel:** Belize has controlled territory since 1859, but if treaty void, possession doesn't save them

**B-roll:** Display uti possidetis analysis from ruling

**Case 3: Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros (Hungary/Slovakia) 1997 - Material Breach Test**
- Hungary claimed Slovakia breached 1977 dam treaty
- ICJ applied Vienna Convention Article 60: What's a "material" breach?
- **Test:** Breach must go to "object and purpose" of treaty
- **Lesson:** Not every violation voids a treaty
- **Parallel:** Was Article 7 road "essential" to 1859 treaty's purpose?

**B-roll:** Display Article 60 text + excerpt from 1997 ruling
