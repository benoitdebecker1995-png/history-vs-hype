Niger powers one in five French nuclear reactors. Yet Niger ranks dead last in global human development. 189th out of 191 countries.

The poorest country on Earth keeps the lights on for one of the richest.

### THE PATTERN

Let me start with a pattern that demands explanation.

Since 2000, Africa has seen twenty-three successful military coups. Sixteen occurred in former French colonies. Seventy percent.

**[PAUSE]**

Since 2021 alone, thirteen coups. That equals the entire previous decade compressed into three years.

Political scientists call them "coupvolutions." The pattern: months of mass protests, government ignores demands, military intervenes claiming popular mandate.

Most analysis frames these as random instability. But the geographic concentration suggests something more systematic.

**[PAUSE]**

To understand what's happening now, we need to hear from someone who saw how this began.

### A VOICE FROM 1949

1949. Eleven years before independence.

Senegalese Deputy Lamine Guèye addressed the French National Assembly. He condemned what he called the "Colonial Pact."

**[SLOW]**

African territories had a duty to "produce a lot, to produce beyond their own needs, and to produce to the detriment of their more immediate interests."

Then: "sell their products below world prices."

His conclusion: "What have we received in return? Only promises."

**[PAUSE]**

That was seventy-five years ago.

As we examine the evidence, consider whether those promises were ever fulfilled.

**[NATURAL TRANSITION]**

Which brings us to the first myth. The one that underpins all the others.

### MYTH ONE: FRENCH COLONIALISM BROUGHT DEVELOPMENT

The narrative claims that French colonial rule, despite its problems, brought infrastructure, education, and progress to the region.

Let me show you what the evidence reveals.

French aid worker Sophie Pétronin spent twenty-five years in Mali's remote regions. After her release from kidnapping in 2020, she documented conditions in nomadic communities.

These communities had requested basic healthcare facilities, schools, and wells. Not for years. For decades.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what she found.

"Women still die in childbirth, after being transported on donkeys to the nearest dispensary, sometimes more than 40 kilometers away."

**[PAUSE]**

Forty kilometers on a donkey while in labor. For context, that's Manhattan to New Haven, Connecticut.

**[PAUSE]**

Remember Lamine Guèye's words from 1949? "What have we received in return? Only promises."

Seventy-five years later, women still die making forty-kilometer journeys for basic medical care.

Now the quantitative evidence.

**[SHORT, CRISP DELIVERY]**

UN Human Development Index, 2024.

Niger ranks 189th.

Out of 191 countries.

Last place globally.

**[PAUSE]**

Some defend colonial involvement by pointing to infrastructure. Roads were built. Schools were established.

This is factually accurate. But consider: development for whom?

Cameroonian Deputy Louis-Paul Aujoulat revealed French attitudes in 1952. The establishment believed "overseas populations are not ripe for democracy."

**[PAUSE]**

The infrastructure connected extraction points to ports. The schools trained colonial administrators. The development served metropolitan France.

**[PAUSE - ENGAGEMENT]**

So I'm asking: Would you call this development? Or extraction with infrastructure?

**[SMOOTH TRANSITION]**

That addresses the colonial period. Now, what about after independence? Surely 1960 marked the end of French control. African nations gained sovereignty.

Or did they? That's the second myth.

### MYTH TWO: INDEPENDENCE BROUGHT ECONOMIC SOVEREIGNTY

The standard narrative: 1960 ended French control. African nations achieved sovereignty and self-determination.

Let's examine the monetary evidence.

Fourteen African countries use the CFA franc. This currency is directly controlled by France.

Until 2019, these nations deposited fifty percent of their foreign exchange reserves with the French Treasury.

**[PAUSE]**

Consider what this means.

These countries cannot set interest rates without French approval. Cannot print money independently. Cannot pursue monetary policy suited to their economic needs.

African leaders understood this immediately.

1949. Before independence. Sourou-Migan Apithy addressed the French National Assembly.

"The CFA franc is not an independent currency, but a multiple of the metropolitan franc, to the same extent that a decilitre is a multiple of the litre."

**[PAUSE]**

Not sovereignty. A mathematical fraction of someone else's sovereignty.

Here's what citizens say today.

Recent survey: 66 percent of Togolese believe the CFA franc primarily benefits French interests, not their own.

Two-thirds. In their own country. Saying precisely what leaders said in 1949.

**[PAUSE]**

When leaders attempted reform, they faced consequences.

**[SHORT, CRISP DELIVERY]**

1969. Niger's President Hamani Diori requests monetary reform.

France refuses.

Shortly after, France delays uranium payments.

Suspected punishment.

2015 to 2017. Economist Kako Nubukpo dismissed twice for criticizing the CFA franc system.

You cannot question your own currency without professional consequences.

**[PAUSE]**

But here's the evidence that demonstrates the system most clearly.

Economists analyzed forty years of Niger's trade data.

**[SLOW, EMPHATIC]**

Niger earned no more from uranium exports than from onion exports.

**[LONG PAUSE]**

Let me provide the full context.

**[SHORT LINES]**

Niger provides:

Thirty percent of France's civilian nuclear power.

One hundred percent of France's military nuclear fuel.

Twenty-five percent of Europe's uranium supply.

Over forty years, this strategic resource generated the same revenue as selling onions.

**[PAUSE - ENGAGEMENT]**

Is that sovereignty? Or is it control operating under a different name?

**[PAUSE]**

Defenders argue the CFA franc provides stability. Low inflation. Currency convertibility. French financial backing.

Technically accurate. But stability for whom? When monetary policy prevents job creation, when it makes exports uncompetitive, when questioning the arrangement costs careers.

**[SMOOTH TRANSITION]**

So colonial extraction continued through monetary mechanisms. But what about military interventions? Those were justified as humanitarian. Protecting civilians. Combating terrorism. Providing stability.

That's the third myth. And it requires careful examination.

### MYTH THREE: FRENCH MILITARY PRESENCE PROVIDES STABILITY

The narrative: French military interventions protect civilian populations and prevent extremist violence.

Let's examine what the evidence shows.

First, context.

The borders we're discussing aren't natural. Drawn at the 1884-85 Berlin Conference. Straight lines cutting through existing communities, cultures, territories.

Resistance began immediately.

1949. Women in Côte d'Ivoire organized mass demonstrations. Boycotted French businesses. Refused to sell food to Europeans.

French military commander documented: "I was afraid of the women."

**[PAUSE]**

The response: 52 killed. 3,000 imprisoned.

Algerian leader Ferhat Abbas warned French legislators in 1946: "One can do anything with bayonets... except sit on them."

**[PAUSE]**

That's historical context. Now modern interventions.

In Central Mali, researchers documented that people from the lowest social classes joined armed groups. Not primarily for ideology. For social elevation.

Decades after independence, the state structure offered them nothing.

**[PAUSE]**

Now the quantitative evidence from Operation Barkhane.

**[SHORT, IMPACTFUL DELIVERY]**

Global Terrorism Index 2024.

Niger terror-related deaths:

2021: 595 deaths.

2022: 198 deaths. Appeared to show progress.

2024: 930 deaths.

**[PAUSE]**

Highest number on record. Deaths increased nearly fivefold after French withdrawal.

Mali. UN Panel of Experts, August 2023. Islamic State in the Sahel doubled controlled territory.

Burkina Faso. Global Terrorism Index 2024. Most terrorized country globally. Second consecutive year.

**[PAUSE]**

The military juntas were correct that French intervention failed. The data confirms this.

But they were incorrect that removing French forces would solve the problem. Violence reached record levels.

January 2021. French airstrike targeted what intelligence identified as militants. It was a wedding celebration. Twenty-one civilians killed.

France initially denied the incident. Later acknowledged "errors."

**[PAUSE]**

This explains Malian public sentiment.

**[SHORT DELIVERY]**

2013. Malians welcomed French intervention. Widespread support.

November 2019. 79 percent dissatisfied with Operation Barkhane.

January 2022. 96 percent held negative views.

Welcome to rejection in nine years.

**[PAUSE]**

Some argue this proves intervention was necessary. Violence increased when France left, therefore French forces should have remained.

This reasoning misses the fundamental point.

The intervention was failing while French forces were present. Violence was already increasing. And the structural conditions enabling extremism were created by the very systems France had established.

The choice was never "French intervention or chaos." The actual choice: Who determines the solutions? External powers protecting strategic interests? Or Sahelian institutions addressing root causes?

**[SMOOTH TRANSITION]**

So we've examined three myths. French development that left Niger ranked last globally. Economic sovereignty requiring French approval. Military intervention coinciding with record violence.

What does this evidence actually tell us?

This isn't because French forces should have stayed. It's because the problem isn't simply the presence or absence of foreign troops.

The problem is the entire system. The borders drawn in Berlin. The currency controlled from Paris. The economic relationships designed for extraction.

Scholars describe this as "the coloniality of power." Colonial systems that outlive the colonizers themselves.

**[PAUSE]**

Removing France didn't solve the crisis because the structures France established remain intact.
