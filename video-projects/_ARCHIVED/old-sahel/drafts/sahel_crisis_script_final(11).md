### **LIE #1: FRANCE BROUGHT DEVELOPMENT (1:15 - 4:00)**
[ON CAMERA - Text overlay: "COLONIAL LIE #1"]

First lie: France helped develop Africa.

You still hear this today. The CFA franc brings stability. French institutions prevent chaos.

Here's what happened when one African country actually tried to leave.

**[VO with archival footage]**

Nineteen fifty-eight. Charles de Gaulle offers colonies a choice. Partnership with France... or independence. Only Guinea votes "No."

**[VISUAL: Sékou Touré speaking, voting results]**

Guinea's leader Sékou Touré delivers a defiant response. One that still echoes today: "We prefer to be poor in freedom... than rich in slavery."

**[VISUAL: Guinea highlighted, crowds cheering]**

France's response wasn't just withdrawing aid. French intelligence launched "Operation Persil." Economic warfare... disguised as policy.

**[VISUAL: Counterfeit currency, document close-ups]**

They printed fake Guinean money. And flooded the country with it. French archives show officials bragging about it. Quote: "The collapse of the Guinean economy... never fully recovered."

**[ON CAMERA - pause]**

Counterfeit money. To punish independence.

**[VO continues]**

This control never ended. Fourteen African countries still use the CFA franc. As economist Joseph Tchundjang Pouemi put it: "Monetary dependence is the foundation... of all other forms of dependence."

**[VISUAL: CFA currency, rope imagery binding France and Africa]**

Think of it as a rope. Still intact. Continuing to bind two ships together. Even after political independence symbolically cut some ties... the economic rope keeps African countries tethered to France.

**[ON CAMERA]**

Here's the part nobody talks about. Until recently, Central African CFA countries had to deposit fifty percent of their foreign reserves... with the French Treasury. In Paris. 

Reforms have begun for West Africa, but the structural dependence remains.

**[VO with financial flow graphics]**

We're talking billions. In twenty-nineteen, that was roughly twenty billion dollars in African money... sitting in French government accounts. France pays them point-seven-five percent interest. Less than inflation.

**[VISUAL: Economic performance charts]**

The results speak for themselves. Between nineteen-sixty and twenty-sixteen, major CFA franc economies had dismal growth. Côte d'Ivoire grew at less than half a percent annually. Cameroon at less than one percent. Senegal at basically zero. 

These figures vary by source, but the overall pattern is clear. Ten franc zone states recorded their highest income levels... before the two-thousands.

**[ON CAMERA]**

And here's how France maintains control. Countries under IMF programs—which franc zone states frequently are—can't take loans from other countries without IMF consent. This helped France curtail Chinese influence... when China offered low-interest loans at preferential rates.

**[VISUAL: Historical footage - post-WWII Africa]**

But this struggle for economic control has deep roots. After World War Two, African leaders like Lamine Guèye fought for genuine equality in the French Republic. They won citizenship rights in nineteen forty-six. All overseas residents were finally citizens.

**[VISUAL: Document showing Lamine Guèye law, colonial officials]**

But French officials immediately sabotaged this equality. They created the "double college" electoral system. Separate voting for Europeans and Africans. Colonial hardliners warned this was a necessary "security lock." Without it, they admitted, the French Union would fall apart.

**[ON CAMERA]**

This institutionalized racism proved something important. France actively resisted any African aspiration to genuine equality. Political equality was too dangerous to French control.

**[VO continues]**

Even Madagascar tried to break free. In nineteen seventy-three, Foreign Minister Didier Ratsiraka demanded Madagascar's exit from the CFA zone. He said "without a national currency, independence would always be shaky." He demanded the return of seven billion CFA francs... parked in Paris.

France's response? Economic pressure until Madagascar backed down. The pattern was clear. Political independence was acceptable. But economic control... was non-negotiable.

But it gets worse.

### **LIE #2: FRENCH INTERVENTION STOPS TERRORISM (4:00 - 6:30)**
[ON CAMERA - Text overlay: "COLONIAL LIE #2"]

Second lie: French military intervention prevents terrorism.

But here's what France actually learned from its failures in the two-thousands. Going it alone was politically toxic. So they got smarter.

**[VO with UN footage]**

Twenty-thirteen. Before launching Operation Serval in Mali, France secured UN Security Council approval. Got regional backing from the G5 Sahel. Made it look multilateral.

**[VISUAL: UN meetings, international support]**

As French officials admitted, securing UN mandates was arduous. But essential to "increase the military operation's legitimacy and deflect accusations of neocolonialism."

**[ON CAMERA]**

Translation: same French control. Better international cover to avoid the "neocolonial" label.

**[VO continues]**

But here's how France maintains this influence. The CFA franc system provides France with a privileged role at the UN Security Council. France often serves as the "penholder." That's the country responsible for drafting resolutions concerning former colonies like Côte d'Ivoire. That means France often gets to draft... the rules of the game.

**[VISUAL: UN Security Council sessions, voting graphics]**

This gives France a "reservoir of votes" from African countries. Allowing it to steer UN decisions while appearing multilateral.

**[VO continues]**

But there was another motive Western officials rarely discuss openly. The Sahel had become what Europeans call their "southern border." A buffer zone to control migration from Black Africa to Europe.

**[VISUAL: Migration routes map, EU border controls]**

The EU treats the region as a "laboratory for experimentation on the security-migration-development nexus." This securitization fundamentally distorts priorities. Education, healthcare, and development... get framed through the lens of counterterrorism and migration control.

**[VISUAL: EU security programs, CVE initiatives]**

Instead of addressing underlying social and economic needs, policies focus on "countering violent extremism." Real governance challenges get militarized... rather than addressed through institution-building.

**[VISUAL: French military deployment]**

So Operation Serval launched with overwhelming force. Five thousand troops. Fighter jets. Drones. Mission: eliminate terrorists, restore stability, control migration.

After a decade in what's now the terrorism capital of the world... here's what happened.

**[VO with striking graphics]**

Violence exploded. Tens of thousands killed. Terrorism spread to neighboring countries.

**[VISUAL: Violence escalation charts, regional spread]**

Western policymakers often blame this on climate change. Droughts creating competition for resources. But academics warn against drawing straight lines between environmental stress and violence.

**[ON CAMERA]**

While temperatures are rising one-point-five times faster than the global average here... the conflicts are fundamentally driven by politics and history. State policies that marginalize pastoralist communities. Rent-seeking by corrupt officials. Not just drought.

**[VO continues]**

March twenty-twenty-two. French forces and Malian troops conducted a joint operation in the village of Moura. Targeting suspected jihadists.

**[VISUAL: Moura location on map]**

**[ON CAMERA]**

Over three hundred civilians were killed. UN investigators found evidence of summary executions. Villages that were neutral... started supporting armed groups for protection.

**[VO continues]**

The Malian Prime Minister put it bluntly. Quote: "Hundreds of Malians massacred despite fifty thousand soldiers"—including French forces.

**[ON CAMERA]**

France had every advantage. International legitimacy. Overwhelming firepower. Regional support. The result? More terrorism. Less democracy. Regional chaos.

When your intervention creates recruits for the groups you're fighting... maybe you weren't there to solve the problem.

And here's the strangest part.

### **LIE #3: COUPS ARE JUST CORRUPTION (6:30 - 8:45)**
[ON CAMERA - Text overlay: "COLONIAL LIE #3"]

Third lie: These coups are typical African corruption.

Explain this pattern then.

**[VO with map visual]**

Twenty-three military coups across Africa since two thousand. Guess where sixteen of them happened? Former French colonies.

**[VISUAL: Coup locations on colonial map]**

Seventy percent of coups... in twenty-five percent of countries. All doing identical things.

**[ON CAMERA - counting]**

Expel French ambassadors. End military agreements. Demand troop withdrawals. Cut economic ties with Paris.

Every. Single. One.

**[VO continues]**

Research shows "French presence in the country" is a main trigger for opposition protests. But here's what's actually happening. Military leaders are hijacking genuine popular revolutionary moments.

**[VISUAL: Massive protests, then military announcements]**

What political scientists call "coupvolutions." Military forces deliberately seizing power following massive protests and confrontations. Exploiting the pre-existing dissatisfaction caused by civilian government failures.

**[ON CAMERA]**

The military doesn't create the anger. They hijack it. This is a transfer of power among elites, not a popular revolution. The protests were real. The grievances were real. But the colonels step in... to serve their own interests.

**[VO continues]**

History shows populations often welcome military takeovers. Expecting better security and governance. But the long-standing pattern is this: successful coups initially create "popular optimism about the future." Followed by quick disappointment when there are no "political deliveries."

**[VISUAL: Military parades, infrastructure promises vs reality]**

Military regimes typically rely on military-focused strategies. And it remains unclear how or if this can improve the human security situation... that drove people to support coups in the first place.

**[VO continues]**

Here's how the populist playbook works. Military leaders don't just attack France. They attack domestic critics too. In Mali, religious leader Mahmoud Dicko, who backs the junta, claims secular reforms are "maladapted to Malian values." He attacks "the so-called civil society financed by the Westerners."

**[VISUAL: Religious gatherings, anti-reform protests]**

This exploits tensions over "laïcité"—French-style secularism. Military regimes frame concepts like human rights and family code reforms as "foreign imports." Rallying religious support while discrediting internal liberal opposition who advocate democratic reforms.

**[ON CAMERA]**

It's a deliberate strategy. Appear patriotic by attacking both Western influence and domestic reformers simultaneously. Frame any criticism as foreign manipulation. This allows military regimes to silence internal opposition... while claiming anti-colonial legitimacy.

**[ON CAMERA]**

The Alliance of Sahel States talks about building alternative institutions. Joint military commands. Plans for a common currency. Mutual defense agreements.

**[VO with institutional graphics]**

But look at their actual partnerships. Mali turns to Russia's Wagner mercenaries. Niger pivots to opaque Russian uranium deals. Burkina Faso embraces Turkish military support.

**[VISUAL: Wagner operations, Turkish investments, Chinese infrastructure]**

Turkey has actively positioned itself as an anti-colonial partner. Capitalizing on anti-French sentiment. China offers infrastructure investments. Gulf countries provide alternative financing.

The Sahel remains "permeable to external influence." It's just not French influence anymore.

### **THE REAL STORY (8:45 - 10:30)**
[ON CAMERA - analytical tone]

Here's what's actually happening. The last functioning colonial system on Earth collapsed. But what's replacing it... is far more complex than liberation.

**[VO with timeline graphics]**

For sixty years after "independence," France maintained control through economic dependency, military intervention, political pressure. Popular anger finally exploded.

**[VISUAL: Protest footage leading to military announcements]**

But military forces didn't create this anger. They exploited it. Classic pattern: civilian governments fail, people protest, military steps in claiming to represent the people's will.

**[ON CAMERA]**

The grievances driving this are absolutely legitimate. French colonial control was real. Economic exploitation was systematic. Military intervention made terrorism worse.

**[VO continues]**

But history shows us something troubling. Popular optimism about military "liberation" often leads to disappointment. The underlying problems—lack of basic services, corruption, absence of impartial governance—don't disappear when colonels take power.

**[VISUAL: Empty hospitals, broken schools, non-functioning infrastructure]**

The real complaint in the Sahel isn't just about French control. It's about the absence of the state itself. No reliable healthcare. Education. Justice systems. Mali, despite years as a "donor darling," suffered from burgeoning state corruption and poor governance. This created empirical state failure... long before the coups.

**[ON CAMERA]**

The military regimes face the same fundamental challenge their civilian predecessors couldn't solve. How to build impartial institutions that deliver services and establish public confidence. The historical pattern shows political elites—whether civilian or military—often scramble for office perks... rather than tackle these systemic issues.

**[VO continues]**

If the new regimes fail to deliver basic services, they risk discrediting democracy itself. In an alarming way. People lose faith not just in specific governments... but in the whole idea of accountable governance.

**[VISUAL: Religious rallies, family code protests]**

But there's another troubling pattern. The military regimes actively exploit anti-Western rhetoric to silence African civil society advocating for democratic reforms. Religious leaders like Mali's Mahmoud Dicko claim secular reforms are "maladapted to Malian values." And attack liberal reformers as "the so-called civil society financed by the Westerners."

**[ON CAMERA]**

This reveals the complex nature of the internal conflict. Concepts like secularism and human rights get framed as "foreign imports." The new regimes don't just fight external enemies. They use anti-colonial rhetoric to silence domestic critics... pushing for genuine democratic progress.

**[VO continues]**

Look at the timeline. Niger kicks out French forces. But replaces them with Russian partnerships that are even less transparent. Mali expels France. But brings in Wagner mercenaries with worse human rights records.

**[VISUAL: Resource flows - from France to Russia/Turkey/China]**

Senegalese leader Mamadou Dia warned about this exact trap in nineteen fifty-nine. Quote: "Apparent success of seesawing and blackmail could only lead to catastrophe. The establishment of very narrow economic dependence... stifling the country and hidden under the shell of pretend independence with an international facade."

**[ON CAMERA]**

Sixty years later, we're watching that pattern repeat. The choice facing the Sahel is stark. Genuine self-reliance... or the inevitable outcome Modibo Keïta predicted. Narrow economic dependence hidden behind the facade of independence.

**[VO continues]**

Real independence, as anti-colonial theorists understood, is about the colonized "becoming man." Achieving genuine human dignity through the process of freeing themselves from all forms of dependency. Internal and external.

**[VISUAL: Global spillover effects]**

But this isn't genuine independence. It's replacing the close, often tainted relationship of Françafrique... with new, possibly more cynical dependencies.

**[VO with current footage]**

The risk is that focusing too heavily on expelling French influence... distracts from the harder work of building functional states that serve their citizens. The new military regimes may fail for the same reasons their civilian predecessors did. Inability to deliver basic services and establish impartial governance.

Understanding this complexity is crucial for understanding the world's deadliest conflict zone. And whether these dramatic changes will actually improve people's lives... or simply create new forms of dependency with different flags.
