# The Sahel Crisis: Colonial Myths Fueling Modern Coups
## Documentary Script | Runtime: 9-11 minutes

---

Since 2021, Africa has experienced thirteen military coups. Twelve of those thirteen happened in former French colonies. 

That concentration demands explanation.

Most global terrorism no longer centers in Afghanistan or Iraq—it's concentrated in Africa's Sahel region. Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso. The same countries experiencing this coup wave. Political scientists have started calling these events "coupvolutions"—coups driven by popular protest rather than elite power struggles.

Today I'm examining three myths that obscure what's actually driving this crisis. Not to pick sides, but to understand the pattern.

---

## MYTH ONE: FRANCE BROUGHT DEVELOPMENT TO ITS COLONIES

Here's the standard narrative: French colonial rule, despite its problems, built infrastructure and institutions that benefited African development. France's continued involvement maintains that constructive relationship.

Let me show you what happened when one country tested that narrative.

In 1958, French colonies faced a choice: join the new French Community or vote for immediate independence. Only Guinea—under Sékou Touré—voted no.

France's response was immediate. They withdrew military support, administrative staff, infrastructure—telephones, light bulbs, medical supplies. But declassified documents reveal something more calculated.

French intelligence launched "Operation Persil." They used government printing presses to mass-produce counterfeit Guinean currency and flooded Guinea's economy with fake money.

Maurice Robert, who directed this operation, later described it: "The operation was a total success: Guinea's economy, already very weak, never fully recovered."

Now, Robert claimed this wasn't punishment for Guinea's independence vote. He said it was necessary Cold War strategy—preventing the Soviet bloc, which was "interested in Guinea's mineral resources," from gaining "a bridgehead for the spread of subversion."

But here's what matters: whatever the stated motive, the effect was economic collapse. Currency depreciation. Soaring prices. And French Prime Minister Georges Pompidou used Guinea as an explicit warning to other African countries considering leaving the French monetary system: let Guinea's "suicidal policy" serve as an example of what happens when you choose full sovereignty.

That brings us to the system that replaced direct colonial control: the CFA franc.

Fourteen African countries still use this currency today—Communauté Financière Africaine, African Financial Community. Until 2019, West African countries had to deposit fifty percent of their foreign exchange reserves with the French Treasury. Central African nations still maintain that requirement.

France effectively controls monetary policy for these countries—setting interest rates, managing reserves, maintaining fixed exchange rates with the euro.

Now, this system has defenders. President Alassane Ouattara of Côte d'Ivoire—former governor of the regional central bank—argues CFA countries are "better off than Anglophone countries" because of this stability. He points to inflation rates: Côte d'Ivoire averaged six percent inflation compared to Ghana's twenty-nine percent. Ouattara claims the CFA franc has fostered "the most continuous growth in the long term."

French officials echo this. They describe the arrangement as France acting "in a completely disinterested manner, out of pure friendship"—guaranteeing unlimited convertibility to prevent currency collapse.

But critics—including a growing number of African economists—argue those benefits mask structural problems. The fixed exchange rate creates what they call "low competitiveness." It makes African exports more expensive while making imports from Europe cheaper. That undermines local manufacturing and agriculture.

And here's one concrete example: Niger supplies France with thirty percent of its civilian uranium needs and one hundred percent of its military uranium. Niger's royalty rate: 5.5 percent. The same French company operating in Canada pays thirteen percent. In Kazakhstan: 18.5 percent.

In 2013, that French company's global revenue reached 9.3 billion euros. Niger's entire national budget: 1.9 billion euros. Nearly ninety percent of Niger's population lacks electricity, despite uranium from Niger powering one in three lightbulbs in France.

A 2019 poll found sixty-six percent of Togolese citizens believe the CFA franc primarily benefits French interests, not African development.

So the question isn't whether the CFA franc provides some stability. It does. The question is: stability for whom, and at what cost?

## MYTH TWO: FRENCH MILITARY PRESENCE PREVENTS REGIONAL CHAOS

The second myth: French military operations in Mali serve as necessary stabilizing forces preventing terrorist expansion and state collapse.

Let's examine the evidence.

In 2013, armed groups seized towns across northern Mali. France deployed Operation Serval—five thousand troops, air support, advanced military technology. The mission: eliminate terrorist threats and restore stability.

A decade later, Mali's Prime Minister described the outcome: "Hundreds and hundreds of Malians have been massacred over the years despite the presence of over fifty thousand soldiers"—combining Mali's forces, French operations, the G5 Sahel joint force, and EU training missions.

By 2021, the region experienced over 2,500 violent incidents and nearly 6,000 deaths. Violence didn't just continue—it spread from Mali into Niger and Burkina Faso during French military deployment.

Academic analysis concludes the strategy was "doomed to fail" because it prioritized killing militants over addressing why people join militant groups—economic inequality, governance failures, ethnic grievances.

French tactical decisions made this worse. To fight terrorist groups, French forces collaborated with local militias. Some committed atrocities against civilian communities. That drove civilians to join armed groups for protection from the very militias France supported.

Even French analysts recognized this. Internal assessments noted that unilateral interventions "fuel anti-French sentiment, undermining France's regional influence."

So if French presence provided stability, why did violence increase and spread? And why did Mali's democratic government collapse under military rule while French forces remained deployed?

The evidence suggests the intervention created conditions for the instability it claimed to prevent.

---

## MYTH THREE: THE COUPS ARE SIMPLY ABOUT CORRUPTION AND WEAK GOVERNANCE

Standard analysis treats each military takeover as isolated domestic failure. Corrupt officials, weak institutions, poor leadership.

But watch the pattern.

Mali terminated defense agreements with France, expelled the French ambassador, demanded withdrawal of French troops. Burkina Faso did the same. Then Niger. All three suspended economic agreements with Paris and shifted toward Russia, Turkey, China.

That's not three isolated events. That's coordination.

The military leaders explicitly used anti-French sentiment to justify their takeovers and build popular support. Research on these coups found that "one of the main triggers for opposition protest is French presence in the country."

Now, we need to be clear about what these military governments have actually delivered.

In Mali, the pivot toward cooperation with Russia's Wagner group has "seen increased violence against civilians" with "little counterinsurgency impact." Security analysts note it "remains unclear how or if this can improve the human security situation."

In Burkina Faso, the coupvolution raises a fundamental question: how likely is it that the same army and the same military leadership will be more successful fighting armed groups now than they were before the coup?

These military governments have restricted press freedom, postponed elections, committed human rights violations. They're pursuing power, not purely liberation.

But their success reveals something about French policy: when seventy percent of African coups occur in twenty-five percent of African countries—specifically former French colonies—that geographical concentration indicates systematic problems, not random governance failures.

In September 2023, these countries formalized their resistance through the Alliance of Sahel States—explicitly described as "against neocolonialism" and "anti-French in outlook."

Mass protests accompanied government positions. Thousands in Niger marching toward the French Embassy. Crowds in Burkina Faso burning French flags. Not government-organized demonstrations—popular sentiment that French military presence failed to provide security while maintaining economic arrangements that extracted wealth.

## WHAT THE PATTERN REVEALS

What connects these three myths?

Each obscures systemic patterns by framing them as isolated events or random chaos. Economic arrangements get presented as aid despite extraction. Military intervention gets framed as stabilization despite increasing violence. Coordinated resistance gets dismissed as simple corruption.

France maintains its interventions prevented complete jihadist takeover. Possibly true. Military governments manipulate anti-French sentiment for legitimacy. Definitely true. Both points matter.

But neither addresses the fundamental question: why do policies designed to help consistently generate resistance?

The evidence suggests French involvement systematically prioritized external interests—uranium access, migration control, geopolitical influence—over domestic development in partner countries.

Here's what makes it systematic: Operation Persil wasn't one bad decision in 1958. The CFA franc arrangements continue today. Military interventions from the 1960s through 2023 followed similar patterns. When policies fail for sixty years across multiple countries, that's not accident.

The Sahel crisis isn't an anomaly requiring external management. It's the outcome of relationships never designed to produce genuine sovereignty or self-sustaining development.

Whether these new military governments will deliver better results remains uncertain. Early signs aren't promising—violence continues, democratic freedoms contract, economic challenges persist.

But the pattern of resistance itself reveals something important: the relationship between France and its former colonies faces a legitimacy crisis that military force and economic leverage can't resolve.

The question isn't whether these particular coup leaders deserve support. It's whether we'll analyze African politics through myths that serve external interests, or through understanding documented patterns that drive genuine political change—even when that change looks messy and uncertain.

Because the coup belt isn't random chaos. It's the geography where post-colonial arrangements are being rejected—violently, imperfectly, but systematically.
