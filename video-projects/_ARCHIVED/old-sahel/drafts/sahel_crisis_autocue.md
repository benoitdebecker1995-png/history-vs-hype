### THE PATTERN

Let me start with a pattern that demands explanation.

Since 2000, Africa has experienced twenty-three successful military coups. Sixteen of them occurred in former French colonies.

That's seventy percent.

**[PAUSE]**

Since 2021 alone, thirteen coups have occurred. That equals the entire previous decade compressed into three years.

Political scientists have identified a specific pattern. They call them "coupvolutions."

The pattern works like this: Months of mass protests. Government ignores demands. Military intervenes, claiming popular mandate.

Most analysis frames these as random instability. But the geographic concentration and timing suggest something more systematic.

**[PAUSE]**

To understand what's happening now, we need to hear from someone who saw how this began.

### A VOICE FROM 1949

1949. Eleven years before independence.

Senegalese Deputy Lamine Guèye addressed the French National Assembly. He condemned what he called the "Colonial Pact."

Listen carefully to his description.

**[SLOW]**

African territories had a duty to "produce a lot, to produce beyond their own needs, and to produce to the detriment of their more immediate interests."

Then: "sell their products below world prices."

His conclusion: "What have we received in return? Only promises."

**[PAUSE]**

That was 1949.

As we examine the evidence, consider whether those promises were ever fulfilled.

### MYTH ONE: FRENCH COLONIALISM BROUGHT DEVELOPMENT

The first myth centers on development.

The narrative claims that French colonial rule, despite its problems, brought infrastructure, education, and progress to the region.

Let me show you what the evidence reveals.

French aid worker Sophie Pétronin spent twenty-five years in Mali's remote regions. After her release from kidnapping in 2020, she documented conditions in nomadic communities.

These communities had been requesting basic healthcare facilities, schools, and wells.

Not for years. For decades.

**[PAUSE]**

Here's what she found.

"Women still die in childbirth, after being transported on donkeys to the nearest dispensary, sometimes more than 40 kilometers away."

**[PAUSE]**

Forty kilometers on a donkey while in labor.

For context, that's the distance from Manhattan to New Haven, Connecticut.

**[PAUSE]**

This connects directly to Lamine Guèye's 1949 testimony. "What have we received in return? Only promises."

Seventy-five years later, women still die making forty-kilometer journeys for basic medical care.

Now consider the quantitative evidence.

UN Human Development Index, 2024. Niger ranks 189th out of 191 countries. Last place globally.

Not last in Africa. Last globally.

**[PAUSE]**

Some defend colonial involvement by pointing to infrastructure. Roads were built. Schools were established. Administrative buildings were constructed.

This is factually accurate. The question is: Development for whom?

Cameroonian Deputy Louis-Paul Aujoulat revealed French attitudes in 1952. He stated that the French establishment believed "overseas populations are not ripe for democracy."

**[PAUSE]**

The infrastructure connected extraction points to ports. The schools trained colonial administrators. The development served metropolitan France, not populations deemed "not ripe for democracy."

**[PAUSE]**

Would you call this development? Or extraction?

**[PAUSE - transition]**

That addresses colonial-era policy. But what about the post-independence period? Surely after 1960, genuine sovereignty was achieved.

This brings us to the second myth.

### MYTH TWO: INDEPENDENCE BROUGHT ECONOMIC SOVEREIGNTY

The standard narrative claims that 1960 marked the end of French control. African nations gained sovereignty and self-determination.

Let's examine the monetary evidence.

Fourteen African countries currently use the CFA franc. This currency is directly controlled by France.

Until 2019, these nations were required to deposit fifty percent of their foreign exchange reserves with the French Treasury.

**[PAUSE]**

Consider what this means in practice.

These countries cannot set their own interest rates without French approval. They cannot print money independently. They cannot pursue monetary policy suited to their economic needs.

African leaders understood this immediately.

In 1949, before independence, Sourou-Migan Apithy addressed the French National Assembly. He stated: "The CFA franc is not an independent currency, but a multiple of the metropolitan franc, to the same extent that a decilitre is a multiple of the litre."

**[PAUSE]**

Not sovereignty. A mathematical fraction of someone else's sovereignty.

Here's what citizens say about this arrangement today.

A recent survey found that 66 percent of Togolese believe the CFA franc primarily benefits French interests, not their own.

Two-thirds. In their own country. Saying precisely what leaders said in 1949.

**[PAUSE]**

When leaders attempted reform, they faced consequences.

1969. Niger's President Hamani Diori formally requested monetary reform. France refused. Shortly after, France delayed payments owed to Niger for uranium exports.

Nigerien authorities suspected deliberate punishment.

2015 to 2017. Togolese economist Kako Nubukpo was dismissed from government positions twice for publicly criticizing the CFA franc system.

You cannot question your own currency without professional consequences.

**[PAUSE]**

But here's the evidence that demonstrates the system most clearly.

Economists Fanny Pigeaud and Ndongo Samba Sylla analyzed forty years of Niger's trade data.

Their finding: Niger earned no more from uranium exports than from onion exports.

**[LONG PAUSE]**

Let me provide the full context.

Niger provides thirty percent of France's civilian nuclear power. One hundred percent of France's military nuclear fuel. Twenty-five percent of Europe's uranium supply.

Over forty years, revenue from this strategic resource equaled revenue from selling onions.

**[PAUSE]**

Defenders argue the CFA franc provides stability. Low inflation. Currency convertibility. French financial backing.

This is technically accurate. But consider the question: Stability for whom?

When monetary policy prevents job creation, when it makes exports uncompetitive, when questioning the arrangement costs careers—is that sovereignty?

Or is it control operating under a different name?

**[PAUSE - transition]**

So colonial-era extraction continued through monetary mechanisms. But what about military interventions? Those were justified as humanitarian operations. Protecting civilians. Combating terrorism. Providing stability.

This is the third myth. And it requires the most careful examination.

### MYTH THREE: FRENCH MILITARY PRESENCE PROVIDES STABILITY

The narrative here is straightforward. French military interventions protect civilian populations and prevent extremist violence.

Let's examine what the evidence shows.

First, essential context.

The borders we're discussing aren't natural. They were drawn at the 1884 Berlin Conference. Straight lines cutting through existing communities, cultures, and territories.

Resistance to this system began immediately.

1949. Women in Côte d'Ivoire organized mass demonstrations. They boycotted French businesses. They refused to sell food to Europeans.

A French military commander documented his response: "I was afraid of the women."

**[PAUSE]**

The documented response: 52 killed. 3,000 imprisoned.

Algerian leader Ferhat Abbas warned French legislators in 1946: "One can do anything with bayonets... except sit on them."

**[PAUSE]**

That's historical context. Now consider modern interventions.

In Central Mali, researchers documented that people from the lowest social classes joined armed groups. Not primarily for ideology. For social elevation.

Decades after independence, the state structure offered them nothing.

The colonial architecture created conditions for extremism to flourish.

**[PAUSE]**

Now the quantitative evidence from Operation Barkhane.

Global Terrorism Index 2024. Niger terror-related deaths:

2021: 595 deaths.

2022: 198 deaths. This appeared to show progress.

2024: 930 deaths.

**[PAUSE]**

The highest number on record. Deaths increased nearly fivefold after French withdrawal.

Mali. UN Panel of Experts report, August 2023. Islamic State in the Sahel doubled the territory they controlled.

Burkina Faso. Global Terrorism Index 2024. Ranked as the most terrorized country globally for the second consecutive year.

**[PAUSE]**

The military juntas were correct that French intervention failed. The data confirms this.

But they were incorrect that removing French forces would solve the problem. Violence reached record levels.

January 2021. A French airstrike targeted what intelligence identified as militants. It was a wedding celebration. Twenty-one civilians were killed.

France initially denied the incident. Later acknowledged "errors."

**[PAUSE]**

This explains Malian public sentiment. But it doesn't explain why violence escalated after French withdrawal.

Here's the documented shift in public opinion.

2013. Malians initially welcomed French intervention with widespread support.

November 2019. 79 percent expressed dissatisfaction with Operation Barkhane.

January 2022. 96 percent held negative views.

From welcome to rejection in nine years.

**[PAUSE]**

Some argue this proves intervention was necessary. Violence increased when France left, therefore French forces should have remained.

This reasoning misses the fundamental point.

The intervention was failing while French forces were present. Violence was already increasing. And the structural conditions that enabled extremism were created by the very systems France had established.

The choice was never "French intervention or chaos." The actual choice is: Who determines the solutions? External powers protecting strategic interests? Or Sahelian institutions addressing root causes?

**[PAUSE]**

Does this evidence support the stability narrative?

**[PAUSE - transition]**

So we've examined three myths. French development that left Niger ranked last globally. Economic sovereignty that requires French approval to change. Military intervention that coincided with record violence.

What does this evidence actually tell us?

### SYNTHESIS

Let me synthesize what the evidence demonstrates.

Trade data covering forty years: Niger's uranium revenue equaled onion revenue.

UN Development Index 2024: Niger ranks 189th of 191 countries.

Terrorism Index 2024: Niger recorded 930 deaths in 2024. The highest number ever documented.

Public opinion polling in Bamako: 96 percent wanted French withdrawal by January 2022.

**[PAUSE]**

But here's what requires understanding.

Violence didn't decrease after French withdrawal. It increased. To record levels.

**[PAUSE]**

This isn't because French forces should have stayed. It's because the problem isn't simply the presence or absence of foreign troops.

The problem is the entire system. The borders drawn in Berlin. The currency controlled from Paris. The economic relationships designed for extraction.

Scholars describe this as "the coloniality of power." Colonial systems that outlive the colonizers themselves.

**[PAUSE]**

Removing France didn't solve the crisis because the structures France established remain intact.
