# The Sahel Crisis: Colonial Myths Fueling Modern Coups
## Autocue Script | Runtime: 12 minutes

---

Most global terrorism no longer happens in Afghanistan or Iraq. It's concentrated in Africa's Sahel region—Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso. Since 2021, these same countries have experienced thirteen coups and coup attempts, with all but one in the francophone Sahel. This pattern reveals something fundamental about France and its former colonies.

Today I'm examining three myths that obscure what's driving this crisis. Each myth serves a specific function: obscuring systematic patterns as random events while protecting interests that benefit from current arrangements.

---

## MYTH ONE: FRANCE BROUGHT CIVILIZATION AND DEVELOPMENT

The first myth suggests French colonial rule and continued involvement have benefited African development through infrastructure, education, and institutional support. This narrative frames French influence as fundamentally constructive.

When Guinea chose independence in 1958, rejecting de Gaulle's partnership offer, France revealed the true nature of their "civilization." French intelligence launched "Operation Persil"—using SDECE printing presses to create false Guinean banknotes and flood the country with counterfeit currency.

Maurice Robert, director of SDECE's African section, later admitted: "The operation was a total success: Guinea's economy, already very weak, never fully recovered."

The result was economic collapse—currency depreciation and soaring raw material prices. This wasn't development aid but economic warfare designed to make Sékou Touré "vulnerable and unpopular" for choosing independence.

Georges Pompidou stated in 1962, concerning other franc zone countries considering national currencies: "Let the experience of Sékou Touré take its course. Many Africans are beginning to realize that Guinea's policy is suicidal and contrary to the interests of Africa."

This reveals the underlying logic of French policy: independence would be punished to deter others from seeking genuine sovereignty.

The CFA franc system continues this pattern. Fourteen African countries remain locked into a monetary system where the franc zone serves as what economists call a "relief valve for the French economy"—forcing restrictive monetary policies that prioritize French interests over local development needs.

---

## MYTH TWO: FRENCH MILITARY INTERVENTION PREVENTS CHAOS

The second myth presents French military operations—particularly Operation Serval and Barkhane in Mali—as necessary stabilizing forces preventing regional collapse and terrorist expansion.

Academic analysis concludes French military strategy was "doomed to fail." The interventions prioritized counterterrorism while failing to address underlying political dynamics.

Mali's Prime Minister Choguel Kokalla Maiga stated: "Hundreds and hundreds of Malians have been massacred over the years despite the presence of over fifty thousand soldiers: the armed forces of Mali, Barkhane, Takuba, G5 Sahel, EUCAP, EUTM."

The conflict spread from Mali to Niger and Burkina Faso during active French deployment. French combat losses reached fifty troops, civilian casualties far higher.

French policymakers themselves recognized that unilateral interventions "fuel anti-French sentiment among African audiences, undermining France's regional influence." The interventions created the very instability they claimed to prevent.

This failure directly enabled the third myth—that these coups represent random governance breakdowns rather than coordinated resistance.

---

## MYTH THREE: COUPS ARE PURELY ABOUT CORRUPTION AND GOVERNANCE

The third myth treats each military takeover as an isolated domestic governance failure, ignoring external factors and systematic patterns.

The evidence reveals coordinated resistance to French influence. All three Sahelian military governments—Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger—took identical actions: terminated defense agreements with France, expelled French diplomats, and demanded complete withdrawal of French troops.

This wasn't coincidental. Military leaders explicitly exploited widespread anti-French sentiment to build legitimacy. The coups succeeded precisely because they tapped into genuine popular frustration with policies that had demonstrably failed to deliver security or development.

The geographic concentration of coups in francophone countries cannot be explained by corruption alone. When thirteen coups occur almost exclusively in former French territories, that indicates systematic rejection of neocolonial structures.

This brings us to the critical question: if these are isolated governance failures, why do they follow identical patterns of resistance to French influence?

The Alliance of Sahel States formalized this resistance, creating institutional mechanisms for coordinated opposition to what they explicitly identify as neocolonial influence.

---

## CONCLUSION: UNDERSTANDING THE REAL CRISIS

These three myths share a common function: obscuring systematic resistance as random chaos. From Operation Persil's economic warfare to failed military interventions to coordinated diplomatic expulsions, the pattern reveals states attempting to break free from dependency structures.

France maintains its interventions prevented jihadist takeovers. Military governments may manipulate anti-French sentiment for legitimacy. But these arguments don't address the core question: why do policies designed to help consistently generate resistance?

The evidence suggests French involvement has systematically prioritized external interests over domestic development. When those policies fail—demonstrably in security, economics, and politics—resistance becomes inevitable.

The Sahel crisis isn't an anomaly requiring external management. It's the logical outcome of policies never designed to succeed on their own terms. Recognizing this clarifies what these challenges actually are rather than what various actors claim they are.

The question isn't whether resistance movements will succeed—it's whether we'll analyze African politics through myths serving external interests or understand the systematic patterns driving genuine political change.
