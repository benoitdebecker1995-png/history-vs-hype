### **ACT 1: THE LIE THAT BROKE A COUNTRY (0:45–2:30)**

**[VOICEOVER with medical documents]**

But Colonel Novak Ivanović investigates the case. His conclusion? **[Back to camera]** Martinović is lying. The medical evidence shows the injuries were self-inflicted.

**[TALKING HEAD - Matter-of-fact tone]**

Now in any normal world, that would be the end of it. **[Hand gesture]** One bizarre incident. Case closed. **[Beat, slight shift in tone]** But this wasn't just any province. This was Kosovo.

**[More serious]** And for Serbs, Kosovo isn't just land. **[Pause]** It's sacred ground.

#### **The Mythology Machine Starts (1:30–2:30)**
**[TALKING HEAD - Building energy, concerned tone]**

**[Slow down, quote delivery]** Listen to what they wrote: **[Deliberate pace]** "The case of Djordje Martinović has become that of the **whole** Serb nation in Kosovo."

**[Normal pace, building emotion]** They compared it to medieval Turkish torture. They called it proof of Albanian genocide. And the Serbian public? **[Beat]** They believed every word.

**[Direct to camera, understanding but warning tone]** Because this wasn't just about one farmer. **[Pause]** For Serbs, this was about losing their Jerusalem.

**[Slight lean forward, setting up complexity]** Now, you might be wondering: **[Beat]** Jerusalem? **[Pause]** Why would a province in Yugoslavia matter that much? **[Beat]** The answer takes us back 600 years, to a battlefield, a choice, and a story that shaped an entire people.

### **ACT 2: THE SACRED GROUND (2:30–6:00)**

#### **Why Kosovo Matters to Serbs (2:30–4:00)**
**[TALKING HEAD - Explanatory, respectful tone]**

**[Connecting back to setup]** So let's answer that question: **[Beat]** Why do Serbs call Kosovo their Jerusalem? **[Pause]** And why did one farmer's lie trigger such massive emotions?

**[Building understanding]** It starts with what Kosovo contains. **[Beat]** And it's not nothing.

**[VOICEOVER with medieval maps and monastery images]**

Kosovo contains some of Serbia's most sacred Orthodox monasteries. Dečani, built in the 14th century. Gračanica, with its stunning Byzantine frescoes. The Patriarchate of Peć, seat of the Serbian Orthodox Church.

**[TALKING HEAD - Building the narrative]**

But above all, there's the Battle of Kosovo. **[Pause]** June 28th, 1389. Prince Lazar chooses to fight the Ottoman Empire knowing he'll likely die. **[Beat]** According to Serbian epic poetry, he chose heavenly glory over earthly defeat.

**[Slight lean forward, engaging tone]** For 600 years, this story shaped Serbian identity. **[Hand gesture]** The idea that Serbs would rather die than surrender their faith and freedom.

**[VOICEOVER with Milošević speech footage]**

By 1989, Slobodan Milošević stood on that same battlefield and told a million Serbs: "Through the play of history and life, it seems as if Serbia has, precisely in this year, regained its state and national dignity."

**[TALKING HEAD - Understanding but analytical]**

**[Direct eye contact]** This isn't fabricated emotion. **[Beat]** These are real historical sites. Real cultural memory. **[Pause]** Real fears about demographic change.

By 1981, Serbs were only 13% of Kosovo's population. **[Beat]** Many were leaving for jobs in Belgrade and elsewhere. **[Slight pause]** The question haunting Serbian intellectuals was simple: **[Emphasis]** "Are we losing our sacred heartland?"

#### **The Competing Narrative (4:00–6:00)**
**[TALKING HEAD - Balanced, fact-checking tone]**

But here's where history gets complicated. **[Beat]** Because Albanian families have their own 600-year-old memories of this place.

**[VOICEOVER with Ottoman records and maps]**

Ottoman tax records from 1488 show mixed Albanian-Serbian populations already living together. Both peoples had called Kosovo home for centuries, moving around following work, fleeing wars, intermarrying.

**[TALKING HEAD - Methodical tone]**

Even that sacred Battle of Kosovo? **[Pause]** Prince Lazar's army included Albanian warriors fighting **alongside** Serbs against the Ottomans. **[Emphasis]** They weren't ancient enemies. They were allies facing a common threat.

**[Building tension]** And those Serbian monasteries? **[Beat]** They're absolutely real and historically significant. **[Pause]** But they were built in a region that was **already** ethnically mixed.

**[VOICEOVER with demographic data]**

Serbian researchers studying one district in the 1930s found something fascinating: 706 families who'd lived there for generations, and 1,819 families who were recent immigrants. **[Back to camera]** Both Serb and Albanian families showed the same pattern.

**[TALKING HEAD - Direct, conclusive]**

The uncomfortable truth? **[Beat]** Both peoples are indigenous to Kosovo. **[Hand gesture]** Both have legitimate historical claims. **[Longer pause]** Both have suffered real injustices.

**[Shift in tone, more serious]** But in the 1980s, Serbian intellectuals decided that acknowledging this complexity wasn't politically useful. **[Beat]** So they simplified the story.

**[Direct stare]** And simplified stories make excellent propaganda.
