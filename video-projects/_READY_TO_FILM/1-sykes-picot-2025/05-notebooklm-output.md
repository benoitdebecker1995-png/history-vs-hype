Pattern Escalation: Reconciliation and Resistance (1918-1920s)
The existence of these three incompatible commitments—Arab independence (1915), Franco-British carve-up (1916), and Zionist national home (1917)—created an "irreconcilable" position [36].
4. San Remo (1920) - How did Britain/France try to reconcile these contradictions?
Britain and France attempted to formalize the colonial spheres while giving a nod to the Wilsonian rhetoric of self-determination via the League of Nations Mandate system.
• Formal Mandate Assignment: The Supreme Council allocated the mandates, effectively confirming the core territorial carve-up of S-P. France was chosen for "Syria" (which included Lebanon), and Great Britain for "Mesopotamia, and the Palestine" [37].
• Incorporating the Balfour Declaration: The mandate for Palestine required the mandatory (Britain) to be "responsible for putting into effect the declaration originally made on November 2, 1917, by the British Government" (Balfour Declaration) [38, 39].
• Superseding Arab Independence: The territories (Syria and Mesopotamia) were provisionally recognized "as independent States, subject to the rendering of administrative advice and assistance by a mandatory" [40, 41]. This framed colonialism as "trusteeship" [42]. This formal Mandate System was born as a "project of imperial reconciliation and legitimation" [43].
5. Arab Resistance - What did local populations do?
The attempt at reconciliation only confirmed the betrayal, leading to immediate violent resistance against the Mandatories.
• Anger and Retreat of Freedom: The Arabs "reacted angrily as the freedom they had been promised continually receded before them like a mirage" [42].
• Rejection of French Rule: When General Allenby announced that France was to be the protecting power in Syria (in accordance with the S-P framework), Feisal flatly rejected the proposal [44]. Feisal told a British officer he had been "handed over tied by feet and hands to the French" [45].
• Violent Protest: French forces forcibly dismantled the Faysali Arab state in Damascus in July 1920 [46]. Both Britain and France "resorted to violent tactics to crush protest, which only enraged the Arabs further" [42]. In Iraq, the British imposition of an "Indian" style of direct administration led to a major rising in the summer of 1920 [47].
• Opposition to Zionism: News that the Balfour Declaration was incorporated into the Palestine mandate aroused "great excitement" among the Arab population, and officials warned that the appointment of a British Jew (Samuel) as the first Governor would be seen as "handing country over at once to a permanent Zionist Administration" [48, 49].
6. Final Outcomes (1920s) - Which promise "won"? Who was betrayed?
The colonial powers' interests, formalized at San Remo and enforced through military and administrative power, largely superseded the conflicting wartime promises.
• The S-P Framework "Won": The final borders and mandates reflected the broad S-P spheres of influence: French dominance over Syria/Lebanon and British dominance over Palestine/Iraq [50, 51].
• The Balfour Declaration "Won" (Temporarily): The pledge to Zionists was enforced, leading to the rapid increase in the Jewish population in Palestine (from 10% in 1922 to roughly one-third by 1939) [52].
• The Arabs were Betrayed: The comprehensive promise of Arab independence (Commitment 1) was completely undermined. McMahon's ambiguous phraseology allowed the British to maintain that "on proper construction of the Correspondence Palestine was in fact excluded" from the promises [53]. Moreover, the commitment was conditional on a successful general revolt, which largely failed to materialize in the north [54, 55].

Consequences Leading to Current Conflicts
The fundamental incompatibility of the three commitments—especially Commitment 1 and Commitment 3 over Palestine—led directly to conflict that endures today.
• Palestine/Israel: The "struggle between Britain and France for the mastery of the Middle East" led directly to the S-P agreement, and British "dissatisfaction over the outcome of this deal that led the British, fatefully, to proclaim their support for Zionist ambitions in the Balfour Declaration" [35]. The conflict between Jews and Arabs in Palestine was described as "irrepressible" by the Peel Commission [58]. The Declaration’s legacy is that the "Jews’ right to a country of their own became dangerously associated with a cynical imperial maneuver" [35].
• Instability and Polarisation: The French and British subsequent policies of "favoritism of the Christians in Lebanon" and "sponsorship of the Jews in Palestine" were designed to strengthen colonial control but "deeply antagonized the predominantly Muslim Arab population... with irreversible effects", leading to oscillating alliances that only "polarized Arab and Jew, Christian and Muslim, further" [59].
Historical Analysis of this Pattern
Historians emphasize the deceit, shortsightedness, and profound negative impact of this chain of promises:
1. On the Unilateral Nature of Arab Claims: Historian Isaiah Friedman argues that the Arab side exaggerates the British commitment. While the slogan of the "twice promised land" infected some intellectuals, official records "fully confirm this conclusion" that Palestine was intended to be excluded from McMahon's promises [53, 60].
2. On the Duplicity and Sham: Lawrence of Arabia argued that S-P was "ten thousand times better than the eventual settlement" because the final mandates were a greater betrayal of the principle of self-determination [61]. 3. On Sykes's Intentions: Mark Sykes, one of the chief architects of Commitment 2 and a key figure in Commitment 3, later confessed that his deal with Picot was "a reactionary measure" that was "the sooner scrapped the better" [63].

5. On the Impossibility of Reconciliation: The lead-up to the Balfour Declaration, characterized by "contradictions, deceptions, misinterpretations, and wishful thinking," "sowed dragon’s teeth" and produced a "murderous harvest, and we go on harvesting even today" [64].

1. What did the Sykes-Picot Agreement actually propose in 1916?
The Sykes-Picot Agreement (S-P), finalized in an exchange of letters in May 1916 [1-3], was a secret pact between Britain (represented by Sir Mark Sykes) and France (represented by François Georges-Picot) to divide the Ottoman Empire's Middle Eastern territories [4-6].
Exact Territorial Divisions (Zones A, B, Blue, Red, Brown)
The agreement split the territory using a diagonal "line in the sand" [4] that ran "from the ‘e’ in Acre to the last ‘k’ in Kirkuk" [7-9]. Territory north of this line would fall under French protection or influence, and south of it, British [9]. The divisions were defined as follows:
1. Blue Area (France - Direct Control): France was allowed to establish "such direct or indirect administration or control as they desire" [10]. This zone encompassed the Syrian and Lebanese coast [1, 11], extending north into modern Turkey [1].
2. Red Area (Britain - Direct Control): Britain was allowed to establish direct or indirect control [10]. This expanded the existing British bridgehead in southern Iraq up to Baghdad [1], covering the port of Haifa [1, 12] and the port of Acre [12]. This zone encompassed Mesopotamia/Iraq [5, 11].
3. Brown Area (International Administration): This zone covered Palestine [1, 13]. It was intended to have an international administration [9, 10, 13, 14], decided upon after consultation with Russia, other Allies, and the representative of the Sherif of Mecca [10]. This was a compromise Sykes and Picot reached because neither power could agree over Palestine’s future [9, 14]. Britain reserved the right to construct a railway connecting Haifa and Acre to Area B [15, 16].
4. Area A (French Sphere of Influence): This region was part of the future "independent Arab State or a Confederation of Arab States" under an Arab chief's suzerainty [17, 18]. France had priority rights of enterprise and supplied advisers at the request of the Arab state [17-19].
5. Area B (British Sphere of Influence): This region was also part of the future Arab State/Confederation [18]. Great Britain had priority rights of enterprise and alone supplied advisers [17-19].
Which modern borders MATCH Sykes-Picot vs. which DON'T
The Sykes-Picot agreement was "never implemented" [6, 20] and was merely a hypothetical division of a region that neither signatory yet controlled [21].
• DON'T Match: The Sykes-Picot map "does not look very much like the present-day map of the region" [22]. Sykes himself later admitted the deal was "a reactionary measure" that was "the sooner scrapped the better" [23]. The boundary lines were drawn "without regard to the territory of the tribes or the lie of the land" [24], and T. E. Lawrence called the boundaries "absurd" [25, 26]. The actual borders came from subsequent negotiations, such as the Cairo Conference of 1921 or London in 1922 [27].
• MATCH/Foundation: Although S-P was not implemented, it did provide the framework for later decisions [28]. The struggle between Britain and France over this deal led to subsequent decisions, such as the Balfour Declaration [29]. The division established two general spheres—one for Britain in the south and east (Mesopotamia/Iraq) and one for France in the north and west (Syria/Lebanon)—which largely influenced the later mandate assignments at San Remo in 1920 [30].

4. What do historians say about the "artificial borders" claim?
Historians largely agree that while the Sykes-Picot line itself was arbitrary, the resulting borders were highly complex and emerged from overlapping factors, complicating the common narrative that S-P alone caused current conflicts.
Scholars who SUPPORT the claim (Counter-evidence)
Some accounts acknowledge that the process led to inherently artificial constructs:
• The partition of the Ottoman Empire involved creating frontiers like Sykes’s, which were "drawn with a stroke of a pencil across a map of the world" [78], a method considered arrogant when applied to the Ottoman Empire [78].
• Some scholars readily admit that all nation-states are artificial, but suggest that a country like Iraq, arising from the post-WWI settlements, is "more artificial than most" [33].
• David Fromkin, in his widely read A Peace to End All Peace, claimed the borders of Iraq, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Iraq were established by British politicians after WWI and were "lines drawn on an empty map" [33].
• The lines were drawn "without regard to the territory of the tribes or the lie of the land" [24].
Scholars who DEBUNK or COMPLICATE the claim
Many experts argue that S-P was never implemented and that the final borders resulted from many factors:
• T. E. Lawrence called the S-P Agreement "absurd in its boundaries" but pointed out that it "did recognize the claims of Syrians to self-government" and was "ten thousand times better than the eventual settlement" [25, 26].
• Historian Margaret MacMillan summarized the imperial perspective in Peacemakers: the Sykes-Picot Agreement "was reasonable enough, if you were a western imperialist" [79, 80].
• The final, extant borders were shaped by many overlapping factors that went far beyond the simple S-P line, including: "mountains, deserts, rivers, and ports; known and suspected oil deposits; population densities; existing Ottoman borders, both international and provincial; previous treaty agreements and current diplomatic relations and balances of power; military strategies, gains, and losses, especially given the active wars over several of the borders through the late 1920s; and perceived local desires, demands, and conflicts as well as perceived ethnicities, languages, and religious sects" [61].
• Historian Isaiah Friedman addressed the controversy directly, noting that those who emphasize British duplicity use the slogan of the "twice promised land" [81]. However, he concluded, based on diplomatic correspondence, that the exclusion of Palestine from the McMahon promises was definitively intended by the British, demonstrating that the foundation of the post-war system was more complex than a simple betrayal [41].

1. What did the Sykes-Picot Agreement actually propose in 1916?
The Sykes-Picot Agreement (S-P), finalized in an exchange of letters in May 1916
, was a secret pact between Britain (represented by Sir Mark Sykes) and France (represented by François Georges-Picot) to divide the Ottoman Empire's Middle Eastern territories
.
Exact Territorial Divisions (Zones A, B, Blue, Red, Brown)
The agreement split the territory using a diagonal "line in the sand"
that ran "from the ‘e’ in Acre to the last ‘k’ in Kirkuk". Territory north of this line would fall under French protection or influence, and south of it, British
. The divisions were defined as follows:
1. Blue Area (France - Direct Control): France was allowed to establish "such direct or indirect administration or control as they desire"
. This zone encompassed the Syrian and Lebanese coast, extending north into modern Turkey
.
2. Red Area (Britain - Direct Control): Britain was allowed to establish direct or indirect control
. This expanded the existing British bridgehead in southern Iraq up to Baghdad, covering the port of Haifa and the port of Acre. This zone encompassed Mesopotamia/Iraq
.
3. Brown Area (International Administration): This zone covered Palestine
. It was intended to have an international administration, decided upon after consultation with Russia, other Allies, and the representative of the Sherif of Mecca. This was a compromise Sykes and Picot reached because neither power could agree over Palestine’s future. Britain reserved the right to construct a railway connecting Haifa and Acre to Area B
.
4. Area A (French Sphere of Influence): This region was part of the future "independent Arab State or a Confederation of Arab States" under an Arab chief's suzerainty
. France had priority rights of enterprise and supplied advisers at the request of the Arab state
.
5. Area B (British Sphere of Influence): This region was also part of the future Arab State/Confederation
. Great Britain had priority rights of enterprise and alone supplied advisers
.
Which modern borders MATCH Sykes-Picot vs. which DON'T
The Sykes-Picot agreement was "never implemented"
and was merely a hypothetical division of a region that neither signatory yet controlled
.
• DON'T Match: The Sykes-Picot map "does not look very much like the present-day map of the region"
. Sykes himself later admitted the deal was "a reactionary measure" that was "the sooner scrapped the better". The boundary lines were drawn "without regard to the territory of the tribes or the lie of the land", and T. E. Lawrence called the boundaries "absurd". The actual borders came from subsequent negotiations, such as the Cairo Conference of 1921 or London in 1922

The division established two general spheres—one for Britain in the south and east (Mesopotamia/Iraq) and one for France in the north and west (Syria/Lebanon)—which largely influenced the later mandate assignments at San Remo in 1920

4. What do historians say about the "artificial borders" claim?
Historians largely agree that while the Sykes-Picot line itself was arbitrary, the resulting borders were highly complex and emerged from overlapping factors, complicating the common narrative that S-P alone caused current conflicts.
Scholars who SUPPORT the claim (Counter-evidence)
Some accounts acknowledge that the process led to inherently artificial constructs:
• The partition of the Ottoman Empire involved creating frontiers like Sykes’s, which were "drawn with a stroke of a pencil across a map of the world"
, a method considered arrogant when applied to the Ottoman Empire
.
• Some scholars readily admit that all nation-states are artificial, but suggest that a country like Iraq, arising from the post-WWI settlements, is "more artificial than most"
.
• David Fromkin, in his widely read A Peace to End All Peace, claimed the borders of Iraq, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Iraq were established by British politicians after WWI and were "lines drawn on an empty map"
.
• The lines were drawn "without regard to the territory of the tribes or the lie of the land"
.
Scholars who DEBUNK or COMPLICATE the claim
Many experts argue that S-P was never implemented and that the final borders resulted from many factors:
• T. E. Lawrence called the S-P Agreement "absurd in its boundaries" but pointed out that it "did recognize the claims of Syrians to self-government" and was "ten thousand times better than the eventual settlement"
.
• Historian Margaret MacMillan summarized the imperial perspective in Peacemakers: the Sykes-Picot Agreement "was reasonable enough, if you were a western imperialist"
.
• The final, extant borders were shaped by many overlapping factors that went far beyond the simple S-P line, including: "mountains, deserts, rivers, and ports; known and suspected oil deposits; population densities; existing Ottoman borders, both international and provincial; previous treaty agreements and current diplomatic relations and balances of power; military strategies, gains, and losses, especially given the active wars over several of the borders through the late 1920s; and perceived local desires, demands, and conflicts as well as perceived ethnicities, languages, and religious sects"
.
• Historian Isaiah Friedman addressed the controversy directly, noting that those who emphasize British duplicity use the slogan of the "twice promised land"
. However, he concluded, based on diplomatic correspondence, that the exclusion of Palestine from the McMahon promises was definitively intended by the British, demonstrating that the foundation of the post-war system was more complex than a simple betrayal
.
