**Section 1: Episode Structure Template**

Define the standard episode flow for document-based videos:
1. **Cold Open / Hook** (1-2 min): Modern consequence or myth that the untranslated document contradicts. "Everyone says X about this document. But when you actually read it in the original [language]..."
2. **Document Introduction** (2-3 min): What the document is, when/where it was created, why it matters. Show the physical document or archival scan on screen.
3. **Clause-by-Clause Walkthrough** (bulk of video): For each section/article/clause:
   - Context setup: Why this clause exists, what problem it addressed
   - Read original: Show original-language text on screen, narrator reads or paraphrases
   - Translate: Show English translation alongside original (split-screen approach)
   - Explain significance: What this clause actually means vs. common misconception
   - Connect to myth: How this specific provision contradicts or supports the narrative
4. **Synthesis / "What They Got Wrong"** (3-5 min): Aggregate what the full document reveals vs. common narrative. Pattern: "So when people say X, the document actually says Y, Z, and W."
5. **Modern Relevance Close** (1-2 min): Why accurate understanding matters today. Not preachy — factual consequences of the misunderstanding.

- Usually one document per video, but related documents can be grouped when they tell a connected story (e.g., a decree and its implementing regulations).
- Video length: as long as needed. No arbitrary caps. A 10-article statute might be 25 min; a complex treaty might be 45+ min. Completeness matters more than brevity.
- Support both full-document and excerpt-based episodes. Some documents warrant full coverage; others have 3-4 key clauses worth examining.

**Section 2: Visual / Staging Standards**

- Split-screen is the core visual approach: original-language text on one side, English translation on the other.
- Exact layout (left/right split, top/bottom, proportions) intentionally left unspecified — will be determined during production.
- Filming technique details deferred — guide establishes structure, not specific production methods.
- Key visual principles:
  - Document text must be readable on screen (font size, contrast, highlight active clause)
  - Original language text is shown as-is, never "cleaned up" or modernized
  - Active clause/section should be visually distinguished from surrounding text (highlight, zoom, or isolation)
  - Archival scans/photos of original documents shown at least once per video (authenticity marker)
  - Maps/context visuals used during setup sections, document visuals during walkthrough
- Talking head vs. document display follows existing HYBRID_TALKING_HEAD_GUIDE.md principles but with heavier document display ratio (~50-60% document, ~40-50% talking head) compared to standard videos.

**Section 3: Quality Bar & Source Rules**

- **Source selection**: Academic/critical editions preferred over raw scans. If critical edition exists (Brill, Cambridge, Oxford, etc.), use it. Reference ISBN and purchase link alongside any free archive URL.
- **Translation accuracy**: Primary translation by Claude, cross-checked against at least one independent source (DeepL, Google Translate, or second AI model). Legal/technical terms annotated with dictionary definitions.
- **Translation display**: When existing English translations are misleading, show the contrast — bad translation vs. correct translation — so viewers can see exactly where distortion occurs. When no prior translation exists, present the new translation with annotation.
- **Qualification criteria**: Document qualifies if (a) no full English translation exists, OR (b) existing translations are misleading/distort the original. Partial translations or summaries do not disqualify.
- **Citation standard**: Every translated clause must reference the specific article/section number in the original document. Page numbers from critical editions where available.
- **Surprise clause detection**: Flag clauses where translation contradicts common English-language narratives about the document. These are highlight moments — spend extra time on these.
- All standard channel quality rules from STYLE-GUIDE.md apply (real quotes, academic sources, intellectual honesty).

**Section 4: Tone & Framing Rules**

- Same "Calm Prosecutor" voice as main channel (reference STYLE-GUIDE.md).
- Framing: "Let's read what it actually says" — not "they lied to you." Intellectual curiosity, not gotcha.
- Acknowledge translation difficulty openly: "This word has no perfect English equivalent, but the closest is..." — this is intellectual honesty, not weakness.
- When showing translation disagreements between tools, present it as discovery: "Interestingly, DeepL renders this as X while the direct translation is Y — here's why that distinction matters."
- Steelman the common narrative before correcting it (Knowing Better / Alex O'Connor style): "The popular summary isn't entirely wrong — clause 3 does say X. But clauses 7 through 12 add context that completely changes the meaning."
- No political editorializing about the document's morality. Present what it says, explain what it means, connect to modern misunderstandings. The document speaks for itself.
- Language of the original is treated with respect — pronounce terms correctly, explain etymologies when relevant.

**Include at end:** Brief example video outline for "Statut des Juifs (October 3, 1940)" showing how the template applies to a real document — hook through close, ~25 min estimate for a 10-article statute.
