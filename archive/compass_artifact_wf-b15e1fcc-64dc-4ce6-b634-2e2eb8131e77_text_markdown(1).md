## Critical Fixes Requiring Immediate Attention

### 1. Pope Gregory XVI's Mirari Vos (1832): Both Versions Are Correct

**VERDICT: NEEDS NUANCE** - The Latin text supports both characterizations.

The encyclical's paragraph 14 contains **both** the phrases found. The Latin reads: "absurda illa fluit ac erronea sententia, **seu potius deliramentum**" (absurd and erroneous proposition, **or rather delirium/insanity**). The phrase "most contagious error" (**pestilentissimo errori**) appears in the following sentence.

**Exact translation:** "This shameful font of indifferentism gives rise to that absurd and erroneous proposition which claims that liberty of conscience must be maintained for everyone."

**Fix needed:** Both "insanity" and "absurd and erroneous...most contagious error" accurately reflect different parts of the same passage. The script should acknowledge both translations exist.

**Source:** Vatican Archives, *Epistola Encyclica Mirari Vos* (August 15, 1832), available at papalencyclicals.net

### 2. Cyrus Cylinder (539 BCE): "First Charter of Human Rights" Is False

**VERDICT: FALSE/ANACHRONISTIC** - This is 20th-century political propaganda rejected by all serious scholars.

The "human rights charter" interpretation originated in **1971** when Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi promoted it during celebrations of the 2500th anniversary of Persian monarchy to legitimize his regime. Iran presented a replica to the UN that year, and UNESCO adopted this interpretation despite it contradicting historical evidence.

**Scholarly consensus unanimously rejects this characterization:**

British Museum Assyriologist Irving Finkel stated bluntly in a 2021 Yale lecture: "**There are no human rights in antiquity. There were never human rights in antiquity.**" C.B.F. Walker wrote in 1972 that it is "simply **a building inscription, in the Babylonian and Assyrian tradition**," not a human rights declaration. Josef Wiesehöfer describes the portrayal as "**as illusory as the image of the 'humane and enlightened Shah of Persia'**" and characterizes it as political propaganda.

The cylinder follows standard Mesopotamian foundation deposit conventions used for two millennia before Cyrus, serving as royal propaganda to legitimize Persian rule by portraying Cyrus as chosen by Marduk.

**Fix needed:** Describe it as a "standard Mesopotamian foundation inscription" and note that the "human rights charter" label is anachronistic 20th-century political propaganda.

**Sources:** Walker, C.B.F., "The Cyrus Cylinder and Achaemenid Imperial Policy," *Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society* (1972); Finkel, Irving, Yale Peabody Museum lecture (May 2021); Wiesehöfer, Josef, *Ancient Persia*

### 3. Ashkelon Infant Burials: Brothel Theory Is Contested/Likely False

**VERDICT: CONTESTED/DEBUNKED** - Recent scholarship (2020) proposes alternative explanations that better fit the evidence.

The 1988 discovery of approximately 100 infant skeletons in a Roman-era sewer initially led to a "brothel with infanticide" interpretation. However, this theory faces **severe methodological and evidentiary problems**:

**DNA evidence contradicts the theory:** Of 19 successful tests, **14 were male, only 5 female**—unexplained by the prostitute-infanticide hypothesis which would expect roughly equal sex ratios.

**Historical evidence undermines the premise:** Historian John M. Riddle notes that "literary evidence—classical, medieval, and early modern—is virtually united in claiming that **prostitutes knew what to do to prevent full-term pregnancies**." Roman prostitutes used contraceptives and abortifacients extensively.

**Archaeological reassessment:** Simeon Ehrlich (Concordia University, 2020) argues the structure was a **baptistery, not a brothel**, stating: "The true nature of the excavated remains was overlooked in pursuit of a sensationalized narrative." Cambridge University Press's *Britannia* journal concluded interpretations "**move far beyond any credible reading of the evidence**" with "**little independent evidence for the use of the bathhouse as a brothel**."

**Fix needed:** Present as "contested and likely incorrect" with recent scholarship proposing alternative interpretations (baptistery, general infant cemetery).

**Sources:** Ehrlich, Simeon, "The brothel or, rather, baptistery of Ashkelon in late antiquity," Concordia lecture (October 2020); Riddle, John M., North Carolina State University; *Britannia* journal, Cambridge Core

## A. Aztec Sacrifice Scale

### Claim: Spanish sources said 80,400 victims in single ceremony

**VERDICT: VERIFIED**

Fray Diego Durán reported this exact figure in *Historia de las Indias de Nueva España* (c. 1579) for the 1487 dedication of Templo Mayor under Emperor Ahuitzotl over four days. The number also appears in the Annals of Cuauhtitlan (1558-1570) and works by Ixtlilxochitl and Mendieta.

**Source:** Durán, Diego, *Historia de las Indias de Nueva España* (c. 1579)

### Claim: Archaeological finds show 126 individuals at Templo Mayor

**VERDICT: VERIFIED** 

Alfredo López Austin and Leonardo López Luján published this figure in "The Aztec World" (2008), stating: "If we add up the data from four successive archaeological projects in the area, the total reaches 126 individuals."

**Critical context:** This represents remains found across **all construction stages** of Templo Mayor and its thirteen adjoining buildings spanning roughly 1390-1521, not a single ceremony. Breakdown includes 42-43 children, 47 adult heads, and various skull artifacts.

**Source:** López Austin & López Luján, "The Aztec World" (Brumfiel & Feinman eds., 2008)

### Claim: This represents 600x inflation

**VERDICT: MATHEMATICALLY ACCURATE BUT METHODOLOGICALLY PROBLEMATIC**

The math checks out (80,400 ÷ 126 = 638x). However, **this comparison is invalid** because it contrasts a single four-day event claim against multi-century accumulated archaeological finds.

**Problems with the comparison:**
- Temporal mismatch: 80,400 refers to one 1487 ceremony; 126 covers centuries
- Archaeological incompleteness: Only preserved remains in excavated areas
- Not all sacrifices were buried at Templo Mayor

**Alternative numbers:** Codex Telleriano-Remensis (mid-16th century) reports ~4,000 victims from elderly Aztec informants; Sherburne Cook calculated maximum ~14,100 possible in four days (2 minutes per sacrifice). Modern scholarly estimates: 4,000-20,000 for the 1487 event.

**Fix needed:** Note the comparison conflates different temporal scales. A more valid comparison: 80,400 Spanish claim vs. ~4,000 from Indigenous sources (20x inflation) or vs. scholarly estimates of 10,000-20,000 (4-8x inflation).

**Sources:** López Austin/López Luján (2008); Pennock, Caroline Dodds, "Mass murder or religious homicide?" *Historical Social Research* 37:3 (2012)

## B. George Washington 1790 Letter

**VERDICT: NEEDS CORRECTION** - Multiple errors in the claimed quote

**Date:** VERIFIED - August 18, 1790 letter to Hebrew Congregation of Newport (Touro Synagogue), responding to Moses Seixas's letter of August 17.

**Quote errors identified:**

**Claimed version:**
"no more that toleration is spoken of as if it were the indulgence of one class of people that another enjoyed the exercise of their inherent natural rights"

**Correct version:**
"It is now no more that toleration is spoken of, as if it was by the indulgence of one class of people, that another enjoyed the exercise of their inherent natural rights."

**Four discrepancies:**
1. Missing opening: "**It is now**"
2. Verb error: Changed "**was**" to "**were**" 
3. Missing preposition: Should read "**was by the indulgence**"
4. Missing punctuation

**Historical context:** Washington rejected the European concept of religious "toleration" (where a majority permits minorities to worship) in favor of recognizing religious freedom as an inherent natural right belonging to all citizens equally. Written one year before First Amendment ratification (December 1791).

**Full correct passage with context:**
"The Citizens of the United States of America have a right to applaud themselves for having given to mankind examples of an enlarged and liberal policy: a policy worthy of imitation. All possess alike liberty of conscience and immunities of citizenship. **It is now no more that toleration is spoken of, as if it was by the indulgence of one class of people, that another enjoyed the exercise of their inherent natural rights.** For happily the Government of the United States, which gives to bigotry no sanction, to persecution no assistance requires only that they who live under its protection should demean themselves as good citizens."

**Source:** "From George Washington to the Hebrew Congregation in Newport, Rhode Island, 18 August 1790," *Founders Online*, National Archives

## C. British 1833 Abolition Act Details

### All five claims VERIFIED with primary sources

**1. £20 million compensation (40% of annual budget): VERIFIED**

Section XXIV of the Slavery Abolition Act 1833 states "the Sum of Twenty Million Pounds Sterling." UK Treasury FOI response (2018) confirms: "equivalent to approximately 40% of the Government's total annual expenditure" in 1833.

**Source:** Slavery Abolition Act 1833 (3 & 4 Will. 4. c. 73); UK Treasury FOI2018-00186

**2. Bishop of Exeter received £12,700 for 665 enslaved people: VERIFIED WITH CONTEXT**

Henry Phillpotts, Bishop of Exeter, received £12,728 18s 4d for estates in Jamaica. **Important context:** He received this as **trustee and executor** of Earl of Dudley's will, not as direct owner. UCL research confirms "there is no evidence" he was a direct slave owner (unlike his brother Thomas who was).

**Source:** UCL Legacies of British Slavery database, Person ID 18347; Parliamentary Papers 1837-8

**3. Apprenticeship system until 1838: VERIFIED**

Started August 1, 1834; ended August 1, 1838 (two years before originally scheduled 1840 termination due to colonial voluntary early termination).

**4. Required 45 hours unpaid work per week: VERIFIED**

Apprentices worked "no more than 45 hours per week without compensation" (described as "seven and a half hours a day"). Masters provided food, clothing, lodging, and medicine in exchange.

**Source:** Parliamentary Papers 1833-1835; Brill Academic Journal, *New West Indian Guide* 97:3-4 (2023)

**5. Flogging continued during apprenticeship: VERIFIED**

Multiple sources document apprentices who refused work were "arrested, flogged under judicial authority, and sent to prison." Parliamentary Committee recommended in 1837 that flogging of women should cease. James Williams's 1837 narrative provides first-hand account.

**Sources:** Parliamentary Papers 1838, Part 5, Jamaica (1), 154; Williams, James, *A Narrative of Events, since the First of August, 1834* (1837)

**Key academic sources:** Drescher, Seymour, *Abolition: A History of Slavery and Antislavery* (2009); Holt, Thomas C., *The Problem of Freedom* (1992); Temperley, Howard, *British Anti-Slavery 1833-1870* (1972)

**3. Southern Baptist Convention founded 1845: VERIFIED**

Founded May 1845 in Augusta, Georgia, explicitly over slavery after northern Baptist mission boards refused to appoint slaveholders as missionaries in 1844-1845. In 1995, the SBC formally apologized for its pro-slavery founding.

**4. Methodist Church split 1844: VERIFIED**

1844 General Conference voted 111-69 to ask Bishop James Osgood Andrew to desist while owning slaves. Southern delegates met in Louisville in May 1845 and formed the Methodist Episcopal Church, South. Churches remained separate for 94 years until 1939 reunion.

**5. Pope Nicholas V 1452: VERIFIED**

Papal bull ***Dum Diversas*** (June 18, 1452) granted King Afonso V of Portugal permission "to invade, search out, capture and subjugate the Saracens and pagans and any other unbelievers and enemies of Christ...and to reduce their persons into perpetual slavery." Reinforced by *Romanus Pontifex* (1455).

**Source:** Original bull text available through Vatican archives; confirmed in multiple Catholic historical sources

## E. Pre-Christian Human Rights Traditions

### All dates VERIFIED, but content NEEDS SIGNIFICANT NUANCE - "human rights" framing is anachronistic

**1. Stoic philosophy (3rd century BCE): DATE VERIFIED**

Founded by Zeno of Citium around 300 BCE; developed by Cleanthes and Chrysippus.

**What they taught:** Universal reason (logos), cosmopolitanism, natural law, equality based on shared rationality. Seneca: "the wise man will esteem the community of rational beings far above any particular community."

**Is this "human rights"? NO - ANACHRONISTIC**

Stoics emphasized **living according to nature/reason**, not asserting rights against authority. Focus was on **virtue, duty, and cosmic order**—not individual rights claims. Natural law prescribed how to live virtuously, not what one could claim from others.

**Source:** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy on Stoicism

**2. Buddhist ethics (6th century BCE): DATE VERIFIED**

Buddha lived c. 563-483 BCE.

**What they taught:** Universal compassion (karuṇā) for all sentient beings, Five Precepts, reduction of suffering (dukkha).

**Is this "human rights"? NO - ANACHRONISTIC**

Buddhism frames ethics in terms of **reducing suffering and karmic consequences**, not rights. Emphasis on **duties, compassion, and spiritual development**—not rights claims. No concept of individual subjective rights against authority.

**Source:** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy on Buddhist Ethics

**3. Confucian philosophy (5th century BCE): DATE VERIFIED**

Confucius lived 551-479 BCE.

**What they taught:** Ren (benevolence/humaneness), benevolent governance, moral limits on rulers (Mandate of Heaven), Five Relationships defining reciprocal duties.

**Is this "human rights"? NO - ANACHRONISTIC**

System is **hierarchical and paternalistic**, not egalitarian. Focus on **duties and roles**, not rights. Confucius emphasized **submission to authority** within moral constraints—not rights against authority. "Benevolent government" is ruler's duty, not people's right.

**Source:** Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy on Confucius

### Scholarly Consensus on Anachronism

**Samuel Moyn** (*The Last Utopia*, 2010): Contemporary human rights discourse only emerged in **1970s**; earlier "rights" language was fundamentally different. Criticizes "teleological" histories tracing human rights to antiquity.

**Lynn Hunt** (*Inventing Human Rights*, 2007): Human rights emerged in **Enlightenment/18th century** with humanitarian sentiment, connected to empathy from novel-reading and American/French Revolutions.

**Anthony Preus:** "Although there is no single word in classical Greek that captures the sense that modern political thinkers give to the word 'rights.'"

**Fix needed:** These traditions contain ideas that **influenced** later human rights concepts (dignity, equality, limits on arbitrary power), but applying "human rights" terminology is anachronistic. They were systems of **virtue ethics, natural law, and moral duty**—not systems of individual subjective rights against authority. Present as "traditions that influenced modern human rights thinking" rather than actual "human rights traditions."

**Sources:** Moyn, Samuel, *The Last Utopia* (2010); Hunt, Lynn, *Inventing Human Rights* (2007); Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

## F. Additional Verification

**1. Pawnee Morning Star Ceremony: VERIFIED**

The ceremony existed and involved human sacrifice, practiced by the Skidi band of Pawnee as a fertility rite involving sacrifice of a captive girl (usually from enemy tribe). Last known ceremony: April 22, 1838, when Haxti, a 14-15-year-old Oglala Lakota girl was sacrificed. Not conducted annually but only when a warrior had specific dream/vision.

**Sources:** Linton, Ralph, "The Origin of the Skidi Pawnee Sacrifice to the Morning Star," *American Anthropologist* 28:3 (1926): 457-466; Dorsey, George A., "The Skidi Rite of Human Sacrifice," *International Congress of Americanists*, Vol. XV (1906): 66-70

**2. Archaeological surveys - Eastern woodland tribes: CANNOT VERIFY SPECIFIC CLAIM**

No specific archaeological survey claiming "NO sacrifice" was found. Evidence indicates human sacrifice was uncommon in Eastern woodland tribes but sources don't make categorical "proof of absence" claims. Human sacrifice in pre-Columbian North America was primarily limited to Mesoamerican cultures, some Great Plains groups (Pawnee), and select others (Natchez, some Huron practices).

**Fix needed:** Soften to "archaeological evidence indicates human sacrifice was uncommon among Eastern woodland tribes" rather than categorical "NO sacrifice."

**3. Matthew Restall - "savage Americas" narrative: VERIFIED (CONCEPTUALLY)**

While the exact phrase wasn't located, Restall's *Seven Myths of the Spanish Conquest* (2003, updated 2021), especially Chapter 7 "The Myth of Superiority," directly addresses this concept. He challenges myths that conquistadors succeeded due to Spanish superiority and demonstrates native Americans did NOT take Spaniards for gods.

**Source:** Restall, Matthew, *Seven Myths of the Spanish Conquest* (Oxford, 2003/2021), Chapter 7

**4. Eric Williams - economic factors in British abolition: VERIFIED**

Core thesis of *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944): "the motivations for abolition of the slave trade (1807) and of slavery (1834) being economic rather than humanitarian."

**Key quote:** "When British capitalism depended on the West Indies, they ignored slavery or defended it. When British capitalism found the West Indian monopoly a nuisance, they destroyed West Indian slavery."

**Source:** Williams, Eric, *Capitalism and Slavery* (University of North Carolina Press, 1944)

**5. David Brion Davis - Christianity used for both sides: VERIFIED**

Central theme of his Pulitzer Prize-winning trilogy (*The Problem of Slavery in Western Culture*, 1966; *The Problem of Slavery in the Age of Revolution*, 1975; *The Problem of Slavery in the Age of Emancipation*, 2014). Davis demonstrated Western culture "associated [slavery] with certain religious and philosophical doctrines that gave it the highest sanction" while Christianity also became central to antislavery movements.

**Source:** Davis, David Brion, *The Problem of Slavery in Western Culture* (1966)

**6. Samuel Moyn - "civilizational triumphalism": PARTIALLY VERIFIED**

The exact phrase wasn't located, but Moyn extensively uses "triumphalism" to describe problematic historiography. In *The Last Utopia* (2010), he criticizes "the general flaws of teleology, tunnel vision, and triumphalism" and challenges "triumphalist or hagiographic 'church history.'"

**Fix needed:** Use "triumphalism" or "historiographical triumphalism" rather than "civilizational triumphalism" unless the exact phrase can be sourced.

**Source:** Moyn, Samuel, *The Last Utopia: Human Rights in History* (2010); *Christian Human Rights* (2015)
