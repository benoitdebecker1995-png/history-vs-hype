# Spain's White Legend: Colonial Apologetics in the 21st Century

## How the White Legend frames colonialism today

Modern White Legend rhetoric employs sophisticated strategies refined for 21st-century audiences. **The "imperiofobia" concept** itself represents a rhetorical innovation, transforming Spain from perpetrator to victim. By claiming all empires face systematic demonization from rivals, defenders argue criticism of Spanish colonialism reflects not historical reality but geopolitical propaganda that Spaniards unfortunately internalized.

**Comparative relativism** dominates the argumentative structure. Every White Legend text systematically compares Spanish actions to British, Dutch, Belgian, and German colonialism, emphasizing Protestant propaganda origins. Roca Barea frames European history as Catholic-Protestant conflict where anti-Spanish sentiment arose from anti-Catholicism. This deflection avoids confronting Spanish colonial violence directly, instead asking "why do they criticize us more than others?"

The **mestizaje argument** serves as the movement's empirical centerpiece. High rates of racial mixing in Spanish Americaâ€”contrasted with Anglo settler-colonial genocide in North Americaâ€”supposedly prove Spanish tolerance and non-racism. Gullo writes that accusations of genocide are "incompatible with the reality of mestizaje." This argument ignores historians' documentation that mestizaje often involved sexual violence, that Spanish colonial society maintained rigid racial caste systems, and that Indigenous populations still declined by 80-90%.

**Legal protections receive disproportionate emphasis**. Defenders highlight the Leyes de Indias (Laws of the Indies), the 1550-1551 Valladolid debate where Spanish theologians debated Indigenous rights, Francisco de Vitoria's writings on universal human rights, and Spanish Crown regulations protecting Indigenous peoples. They catalog 23 universities founded in Spanish America versus few in British colonies, hospitals built, and urban planning regulations. This creates what historian Benjamin Keen in 1969 called a "White Legend" that "substitutes the texts of laws and pious expressions of goodwill, generally unimplemented, for the reality of Indian-Spanish relations."

The **victimization narrative** runs through all White Legend literature. Spain suffered centuries of anti-Catholic, anti-Hispanic propaganda from Protestant northern Europe, these authors claim. Roca Barea writes: "If we deprive Europe of its hispanophobia and anti-Catholicism, its modern history becomes nonsense." IbÃ¡Ã±ez asks why Spaniards have "such a bad opinion of their past" despite starring in "the greatest feats of History" that saved "Christianity and the West." This frames any criticism as psychological pathologyâ€”what Roca Barea terms "fracasologÃ­a," a culture of failure and self-hatred.

**Civilizational mission defenses** emphasize Spanish cultural contributions. Universities, hospitals, cities, baroque churches, Spanish language spread across continents, Catholic evangelization, and architectural heritage demonstrate Spanish beneficence, defenders argue. Pedro Insua claims that "when one arrives at real, documentary history, the Black Legend is completely diluted." The argument positions Spanish colonialism as state-building rather than extraction, integration rather than exploitation.

Defenders employ **new terminology** to reframe debates. "Imperiofobia" entered Spanish discourse as a clinical-sounding diagnosis. "Hispanofobia" labels critics. "Imperio generador" versus "imperio depredador" creates categorical distinctions. "FracasologÃ­a" pathologizes Spanish self-criticism. These neologisms shift ground from historical evidence to psychological interpretation, from colonial actions to modern attitudes.

Contemporary framing connects to **present-day politics**. White Legend proponents explicitly link Black Legend to Catalan independence movements, EU policies, progressive politics, indigenous rights movements in Latin America, and demands for historical apologies. They position themselves as defending Spanish national unity against internal and external enemies who weaponize historical guilt. The 2019-2021 controversy with Mexican President LÃ³pez Obrador crystallized these dynamics.

## Spanish schools and museums shape collective memory

Spanish textbooks consistently minimize colonial violence while emphasizing Spanish contributions, according to multiple academic studies. Carretero et al.'s 2002 comparative study of Spanish and Mexican textbooks found Spanish texts labeled Columbus's arrival as "discovery" (Mexico uses "encounter"), portrayed Columbus as a "hero" with "courage, experience, steadfastness, intelligence, and great intuition," and **contained "NO information about the socio-economic, cultural, or political life of people living in America before Columbus's arrival."** Indigenous voices and agency were rejected and neglected.

A 2013 Pousa and Facal study analyzing six Spanish history textbooks from five major publishers found texts **consistently minimize or omit massacres and genocide**, omit fates and lives of Indigenous peoples, and identify "the West" with "progress and freedom" despite long-standing slavery. Visual representations most frequently showed "natives performing physical tasks or reiterating stereotypes of poverty or exoticism" while colonizers appeared in "benevolent or seemingly neutral roles." Maps reflected the concept of *terra nullius*â€”empty land with resources but no people.

From these textbooks, Spanish students learn that Columbus's voyage represents discovery and exploration, that **"by the mid-sixteenth century, in less than fifty years, a handful of Spaniards had travelled the length of and colonized an entire continent,"** emphasizing heroic Spanish suffering while Indigenous suffering remains largely absent. One textbook stated: "Spain respected those who were there, created universities, created prosperity, built entire cities."

Curriculum battles reveal sharp political divisions. The **conservative LOMCE law (2013)** under Partido Popular established greater central government control over history teaching. Education Minister JosÃ© Ignacio Wert sought to end what he called "ultra-parochial approaches" and "blatantly indoctrinating and false content" in autonomous regions, especially Catalonia and the Basque Country. The **progressive LOMLOE law (2020)** under the PSOE-Unidas Podemos coalition reversed this centralization, re-establishing collaboration between ministry and autonomous communities, eliminating standardized learning standards, and emphasizing competency-based learning over encyclopedic knowledge. LOMLOE addresses "cultural diversity and identities" in Spain and includes assessment criteria about understanding "different collective identities that have developed in Spain," the "richness of multiple identities," and "shared multicultural identity."

Museums present Spanish colonialism through lenses of cultural exchange while avoiding detailed discussion of violence. ## European colonial apologetics follow similar patterns

Spain's White Legend fits a broader pattern of European colonial revisionism employing remarkably similar rhetorical strategies despite different contexts. All involve "balance sheet" arguments, claims of exceptionalism, comparative deflection, civilizing mission narratives, and connection to contemporary far-right politics.

**British Empire defenses** center on historian Niall Ferguson's "Empire: How Britain Made the Modern World" (2003), arguing the British Empire was "one of the world's great modernising forces" and "midwife to modernity." Ferguson credits it with spreading capitalism, telecommunications, English language, and representative government. He acknowledges British "brutality and racism" but argues Germany and Japan "were far more brutal." Critics including Richard Drayton call Ferguson a "court historian for the imperial American hard right" attempting to "rehabilitate empire for contemporary great power interests."

Bruce Gilley's "The Case for Colonialism" (2017) argued colonialism was "objectively beneficial and subjectively legitimate," proposing revival through "charter cities." The article in Third World Quarterly was withdrawn after death threats and 15 editorial board resignations. Nigel Biggar's "Ethics and Empire" project at Oxford called for Britons to "moderate our post-imperial guilt" and conduct "cost-benefit analysis" of imperial history, drawing sharp criticism for his "balance-sheet" approach.

## Contemporary significance and novel insights

The White Legend's 21st-century revival reveals fundamental patterns about how colonial memory functions in democratic societies facing identity crises. Spain's experience offers three novel insights applicable beyond Iberian contexts.

First, **colonial apologetics emerge strongest not from confidence but from anxiety**.
