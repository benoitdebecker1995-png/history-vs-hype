# The rocks smaller than Grand Central Terminal dividing America's Asian allies

**Dokdo** (Korean) and **Takeshima** (Japanese)—neutrally known as Liancourt Rocks—measure just 0.187 square kilometers total, yet these volcanic outcroppings have sparked 300 years of conflict. South Korea physically controls them with 40 Coast Guard personnel rotating every two months, plus one 79-year-old fisherman resident. Japan claims legal sovereignty but has no physical presence. The immediate stakes are modest: about $94,000 in annual fishing revenue, though broader Exclusive Economic Zone implications could affect tens of thousands of square kilometers of ocean. But the symbolic weight is crushing—for Koreans, these islands represent the first territory seized during Japanese colonization in 1905, the symbol of restored sovereignty after liberation. For Japanese, they represent legitimate territorial incorporation under international law. The dispute undermines U.S. strategic interests by dividing its two most important Asian allies at precisely the moment Washington needs Tokyo-Seoul cooperation against North Korea and China.

Neither government can compromise without domestic political suicide. In 2005, when Japan's Shimane Prefecture designated "Takeshima Day," 2.2 million commemorative stamps sold out in three hours; protestors sliced off fingers and self-immolated outside Japan's embassy. When South Korea's President Lee visited in 2012—the first presidential visit ever—shuttle diplomacy between leaders ceased for over a decade. The frozen conflict persists because South Korea controls the islands now and has everything to lose from international arbitration, while Japan cannot rescind claims without appearing to validate colonial-era territorial seizures. America maintains studied neutrality, using the French name "Liancourt Rocks," because taking sides would fracture the alliance architecture containing China.

## The geography and occupation reality on the ground

Two main volcanic islets rise from the Sea of Japan (East Sea in Korean usage), formed 4.5 million years ago with steep 10-20 meter cliffs. The West Islet reaches 168.5 meters elevation; the East Islet features a dramatic vertical hole to the sea. Together they span just 187,554 square meters—genuinely smaller than Grand Central Terminal. Coordinates: 37°14'N, 131°52'E. Critically for legal claims, Dokdo sits **87.4 km from Korea's Ulleungdo Island** (visible on clear days) but **157 km from Japan's Oki Islands** (not visible). This geographic proximity matters under international law's equidistance principles.

The islands support 49-80 plant species, 107-183 bird species including black-tailed gulls and streaked shearwaters. Harsh weather allows ferries to dock only once every 40 days on average. Annual precipitation reaches 1,324mm; fog is common. Water temperatures range from 10°C in spring to 25°C in late summer. The convergence of the warm East Korea Current and cold North Korea Current creates plankton-rich waters supporting commercially valuable squid, pollock, cod, anchovy, and abalone.

South Korea's current presence includes 30-40 Coast Guard personnel on two-month rotations, 3-6 lighthouse keepers, 2 Ulleung-gun Office staff, and fisherman Kim Shin-yeol plus his wife as civilian residents. Facilities on East Islet: Coast Guard station, guard posts, radar station, helipad, staffed lighthouse (completed 1998). West Islet: residents' quarters, fishermen's lodge, government offices. Infrastructure includes docking facilities for 500-ton vessels, desalination plants, and mobile phone coverage from three carriers. Tourism opened in 2005; 280,312 visitors came in 2022 (about 500 daily), each getting exactly 30 minutes in designated areas. Natural Monument #336 designation.

Japan maintains no physical presence but conducts regular diplomatic protests—over 70 since 1954. Occasional Coast Guard patrol vessels approach but don't land. Japan proposed International Court of Justice referral three times (1954, 1962, 2012); South Korea rejected all three, stating "no territorial dispute exists" and refusing to legitimize Japanese claims.

## The strategic value: resources, fishing, and exclusive economic zones

**EEZ implications remain deeply ambiguous.** Under UNCLOS Article 121(3), "rocks which cannot sustain human habitation or economic life of their own" generate no EEZ—only a 12 nautical mile territorial sea (452 sq km). If classified as proper "islands," they generate a full 200 nautical mile EEZ. The distance between Korea and Japan is only 289 nautical miles, so both countries' 200nm EEZ claims overlap significantly. Control of Dokdo/Takeshima fundamentally shifts the median line calculation.

**No authoritative third-party calculation exists** for total disputed ocean area in square kilometers. Estimates range wildly from 40,000 to 400,000 sq km depending on delimitation method and island classification. What's certain: Japan's claimed EEZ globally ranks 7th at 4.5 million sq km, with 2.6 million sq km generated from small outlying islands—Japan aggressively classifies tiny features as EEZ-generating islands (see Okinotorishima atoll). South Korea currently enforces only a 12nm limit around Dokdo, essentially treating them as rocks rather than islands, ironically weakening its own maritime claims to maintain legal consistency elsewhere.

**Fishing rights carry moderate but real economic value.** The 1998 Korea-Japan Fisheries Agreement established a "Provisional Measures Zone" without resolving sovereignty, allowing: Japanese vessels to catch 94,000 tons in Korean EEZ; Korean vessels to catch 149,200 tons in Japanese EEZ. 2002 actual output totaled $500 million. The immediate Dokdo area fishing generates only **$94,000 annually** (2009 data)—Ulleungdo's entire fishing industry grosses $10.3 million from 250 vessels. But broader disputed EEZ fishing rights are worth significantly more. Commercially valuable species: squid (primary), Alaska pollock, codfish, octopus, anchovy, abalone, sea cucumber, seaweed. The area is described as having "the tastiest squid and shrimp in all of Korea." **Content warning:** Global warming is transforming the region into subtropical waters, changing species populations—blue damselfish and tilefish increasing while sea cucumbers and abalone dwindle.

**Undersea resources are highly speculative.** Korean government surveys (June 2005) identified approximately **600 million tons of natural gas hydrates** buried in the Ulleungdo Basin seabed ~100km from Dokdo at 300+ meter depths. Estimated value: $9.4 billion annually or $149 billion total (estimates vary). This equals roughly 30 years of South Korea's natural gas consumption. Korea Gas started a $225 million, 10-year exploration project in 2004; target commercialization was end of 2020s. **Critical content warning:** As of 2006, "expected reserves had not been found" according to multiple sources. Extraction remains technologically infeasible—material buried too deep for current technology, requiring 10-20 years minimum before viability. Over 200 million tons of phosphate minerals also estimated in East Sea seabed (South Korea imports 1.5 million tons annually for fertilizer).

**Tourism actually generates more confirmed revenue than fishing:** At $377 average per person × 130,000 annual tourists = **$49 million annually**. The islands' value is overwhelmingly symbolic rather than economic. As the Lowy Institute notes, there's "no economic value to what are essentially glorified rocks."

## Legal claims: South Korea's historical and modern arguments

**Ancient historical foundation (6th-17th century):** South Korea's case begins with the *Samguk Sagi* (1145), Korea's oldest extant history, recording that in **512 AD**, General Yi Sabu conquered Usan-guk (Usan Kingdom), which Korea claims comprised Ulleungdo and Usando (modern Dokdo). The *Sejong Sillok Jiriji* (1454) explicitly mentions Ulleungdo and Usando. Other supporting documents: *Dongguk Yeoji Seungnam* (1531), various Joseon Dynasty annals. Korean historical names included Usando (于山島), Sambongdo, Gajido; fishermen called it "Dokseom/Dolseom" (rocky island); written as Dokdo (獨島) or Seokdo (石島).

**Critical dispute:** Japan counters that Usando refers to Jukdo, Ulleungdo itself, or a non-existent island—not modern Dokdo. Japanese scholars note Korean maps often show "Usando" between the peninsula and Ulleungdo, not east of Ulleungdo where Dokdo actually sits, and depict it similar size to Ulleungdo, inconsistent with tiny Dokdo.

**The 1696 diplomatic recognition:** Korean fisherman An Yong-bok confronted Japanese officials in 1693-1696, claiming Ulleungdo and "Jasando" (variant of Usando) as Korean territory. The Tokugawa Shogunate's December 1695 inquiry to Tottori clan asked whether Takeshima (Ulleungdo) and Matsushima (Dokdo) belonged to their domain. Response: **"Neither belongs to the domain."** On **January 28, 1696**, the shogunate banned Japanese passage to Takeshima (Ulleungdo). South Korea interprets this as implicit recognition of Korean control over Dokdo; Japan argues the ban applied only to Ulleungdo, not Matsushima.

**Modern incorporation (1900):** Korean Empire's **Imperial Ordinance No. 41 (October 25, 1900)** placed under Uldo County jurisdiction "Ulleungdo, Jukdo, and **Seokdo**." South Korea argues Seokdo (石島 = "rocky island") equals Dokdo, representing formal incorporation under modern international law. Japan counters: no evidence "Seokdo" refers to Dokdo; why different name if same island?

**Post-WWII legal framework:** **SCAPIN 677 (January 29, 1946)**—Supreme Commander Allied Powers directive—explicitly separated "Utsuryo-to, Liancourt Rocks (Takeshima), Quelpart" from Japanese administrative control. Article 6 cautioned this was "not final determination," but no subsequent reversal occurred. **SCAPIN 1033 (June 22, 1946)** prohibited Japanese fishing within 12nm of Dokdo. South Korea cites the Cairo Declaration (1943) and Potsdam Declaration (1945) aiming to return territories "stolen by violence and greed"—arguing Dokdo was unlawfully annexed during the Russo-Japanese War.

**The San Francisco Treaty ambiguity (September 8, 1951):** Article 2(a) states "Japan...renounces...Korea, including...Quelpart, Port Hamilton and Dagelet" but **does not mention Dokdo/Takeshima**. South Korean arguments: (1) already recognized as Korean, no need to list; (2) impossible to list every Korean island; (3) SCAPIN 677 had separated it; (4) treaty drafts 1-5 explicitly listed Dokdo as Korean; (5) removal due to Japanese/US lobbying doesn't equal Japanese territory.

**The secret Rusk Note (August 10, 1951):** US Assistant Secretary Dean Rusk rejected Korea's request to add Dokdo, stating islands were "according to our information never treated as part of Korea" and "since about 1905, has been under the jurisdiction of...Shimane Prefecture of Japan." South Korean response: (1) not official Allied Powers position, only US opinion; (2) not released to Japan in 1951—kept secret; (3) based on false Japanese information; (4) no legal effect on territorial determination.

**Rhee Line and effective occupation:** President Syngman Rhee's "Peace Line" (**January 18, 1952**) incorporated Dokdo within South Korean maritime boundaries **before** the San Francisco Treaty took effect (April 28, 1952). August 1954: government established permanent military outpost. **1954-present: continuous effective control for 70+ years.** South Korea's official position: **"No territorial dispute exists—Dokdo is clearly Korean territory historically, geographically and under international law."**

## Legal claims: Japan's historical incorporation and sovereignty arguments

**17th century awareness and activity:** Japanese maps from the 1600s show the islands, originally called "Matsushima" (current Takeshima). The Tokugawa government granted Murakawa and Oya families passage rights to Ulleungdo in the early 1600s; they used Takeshima as "navigational port and anchorage," conducting sea lion hunting and abalone fishing. Japan argues this constituted effective occupation.

**The 1695-1696 inquiry controversy:** When the shogunate inquired whether Takeshima (Ulleungdo) and Matsushima (Dokdo) belonged to Tottori Province, the response was neither belonged. However, the January 1696 ban on passage applied only to "Takeshima" (Ulleungdo). **Japanese argument:** The diplomatic issue with Korea concerned Ulleungdo only, not sovereignty over Matsushima (Dokdo). The 1877 Dajokan Order stating "Takeshima and one other island have nothing to do with Japan" referred to Ulleungdo by both names, showing name confusion rather than sovereignty acknowledgment, Japan contends.

**The 1905 incorporation—Japan's core claim:** Nakai Yozaburo petitioned (September 1904) to lease the islands for sea lion hunting. Following his request, the Cabinet decided (**January 28, 1905**) to incorporate based on **terra nullius** (ownerless land). Decision language: "Uninhabited island...no trace of occupation by any other country." Nakai's activities since 1903 constituted "occupation under international law." Named "Takeshima," placed under Shimane Prefecture, Oki Islands. **Shimane Prefecture Notice No. 40 (February 22, 1905)** publicly announced incorporation in newspapers—Japan argues not secret.

**Administrative actions (1905-1941):** Added to State Land Register; license system for sea lion hunting established; Nakai plus three others licensed (June 5, 1905); land usage fees collected; operations continued until 1941. **Licenses still renewed** (most recently September 1, 2013). Japan argues these actions demonstrate peaceful, continuous sovereignty exercise without protest, constituting valid occupation of terra nullius under international law.

**San Francisco Treaty interpretation:** Article 2(a) lists renounced territories—**"Quelpart, Port Hamilton and Dagelet"**—deliberately **excluding** Takeshima. The **Rusk Note** explicitly stated islands were "never treated as part of Korea" and since 1905 "under jurisdiction" of Shimane Prefecture. US **rejected** Korean request. The **Van Fleet Report (1954)** confirmed US view Takeshima is Japanese. Japan argues: SCAPIN 677's Article 6 stated "not indication of Allied policy relating to ultimate determination"—it was temporary administrative measure. San Francisco Treaty is definitive—it excluded Takeshima from Japanese renunciations. US even used Takeshima as bombing range 1952-1953, which Japan designated publicly, proving international recognition of Japanese sovereignty.

**Rebuttal of Korean evidence:** Usando/Usan-do refers to Jukdo, Ulleungdo, or non-existent island—not Dokdo. Imperial Ordinance No. 41's "Seokdo" has no proven connection to Dokdo. No effective Korean control evidence exists pre-1905. Syngman Rhee Line (January 1952) was illegal under international law—unilateral with no legal basis. Japan protested immediately and continuously, negating any prescriptive claims.

**ICJ proposals:** Japan proposed **1954, 1962, 2012**—all rejected. Japan accepts ICJ compulsory jurisdiction (since 1958); South Korea does not. ROK rejection suggests weak legal claim, Japan argues. Japanese official position: **"Takeshima is indisputably an inherent part of Japanese territory in light of historical facts and based on international law."**

## Why South Korea refuses international arbitration despite having physical control

South Korea officially dismisses Japanese ICJ proposals as "not worth attention." Foreign Ministry (August 30, 2012): "Dokdo is clearly Korean territory...no territorial dispute exists...proposal not worth attention." Three stated reasons: (1) no dispute exists—going to ICJ would legitimize Japanese claims; (2) Korea controls islands—can only lose at ICJ, not gain; (3) Japanese proposals are political posturing.

**Deeper legal concerns center on unfavorable ICJ precedents.** The **Minquiers and Ecrehos Case (UK v. France, 1953)** ruled based on effective occupation and administrative actions—France had historical claim but UK had detailed continuous administration, so Court ruled for UK. Korean concern: Japan's 1905 incorporation plus administrative actions (licenses, fees, Land Register) might prevail. **Preah Vihear Temple Case (Thailand v. Cambodia, 1962)** relied on maps over treaty language; Thailand's failure to protest meant abandonment of rights. Korean concern: Korea couldn't protest during colonial rule (1910-1945), but ICJ may not consider imperial aggression context. **Island of Palmas Case (1928)** emphasized "continuous and peaceful display of state functions." Korean concern: Japan might argue better pre-1945 administrative record.

**ICJ pattern: does not heavily weigh imperial aggression/colonial context.** Courts focus on legal titles, effective occupation, administrative actions, protests—not historical injustice. Korea controls islands NOW; ICJ ruling could only LOSE them (no upside). Legal precedents suggest uncertain outcome. Political cost of losing would be catastrophic domestically—no Korean government could survive after ICJ loss.

**Historical/emotional factors are overwhelming.** Dokdo represents the first territory seized during Japanese colonization—powerful symbol of colonial resistance and sovereignty recovery. Deep nationalist sentiment unifies all Koreans politically across partisan lines. 89% of Koreans cited Dokdo issues when asked about Japan concerns (2005). In 2005, when Shimane Prefecture designated "Takeshima Day," protestors sliced off fingers and self-immolated outside the Japanese embassy; 2.2 million commemorative stamps sold out in three hours. The dispute connects to broader unresolved grievances: comfort women, forced labor, Yasukuni Shrine visits, textbook controversies. Losing at ICJ would be perceived as legitimizing the colonial past.

If Japan filed unilaterally, ICJ would request South Korean consent; South Korea would refuse; ICJ cannot proceed without both parties' consent. Could escalate to UN Security Council for recommendations—neither side wants this.

**US position complicates matters.** Publicly neutral, using "Liancourt Rocks." But the 1951 Rusk Letter and 1954 Van Fleet Report suggest US privately believes Japanese claim stronger. US policy: "Should be referred to ICJ." Yet US will NOT intervene or support either ally militarily—Dokdo is NOT covered under US-Korea Mutual Defense Treaty. In 2014, when Korea asked if Dokdo was covered under US-ROK defense treaty (parallel to US stance on Senkaku under US-Japan treaty), US firmly stated: **would NOT back South Korea against Japan on Dokdo.**

## Historical timeline: from ancient records through the 2025 diplomatic bluebook

**512 AD:** *Samguk Sagi* records Usan-guk submitted to Silla Kingdom. Korean interpretation: Usan-guk included Dokdo. Japanese counter: referred only to Ulleungdo.

**1454:** *Sejong Sillok Jiriji* mentions "Usan and Muleung" islands "one can see each other when weather is clear." Korean position: Usan = Dokdo. Japanese counter: vague descriptions may refer to different islands.

**1696:** After Korean-Japanese fishermen clashes, Tokugawa shogunate banned Japanese passage to Takeshima (Ulleungdo) on **January 28, 1696**. Korean interpretation: recognition that islands not Japanese. Japanese counter: ban applied only to Ulleungdo.

**1877:** Japanese Dajokan (Grand Council) concludes Takeshima "and one other island" are "of no concern to Japan." Korean interpretation: confirmation islands not Japanese. Japanese counter: both names referred to Ulleungdo due to name confusion.

**1900:** Korean Empire's **Imperial Ordinance No. 41 (October 25)** placed "Ulleungdo, Jukdo, and Seokdo" under Uldo County. Korean claim: Seokdo = Dokdo, formal incorporation. Japanese counter: no evidence Seokdo refers to Dokdo.

**January 28, 1905:** Japanese Cabinet formally incorporates Dokdo/Takeshima claiming terra nullius during Russo-Japanese War. **February 22, 1905:** Shimane Prefecture Notice No. 40 announces incorporation. **March 1906:** Korean officials on Ulleungdo notified; Korean government protests but suppressed under Japanese pressure. **1910:** Japan annexes entire Korean Peninsula; Dokdo becomes part of colonized territory.

**January 29, 1946:** **SCAPIN 677** separates Dokdo from Japanese administrative control. **June 22, 1946:** **SCAPIN 1033** prohibits Japanese vessels within 12nm of Takeshima.

**September 8, 1951:** **San Francisco Peace Treaty** signed; Dokdo/Takeshima not explicitly mentioned in renunciations. **August 10, 1951:** **Rusk Note** rejected Korean request to include Dokdo, stating islands "never treated as part of Korea" and "since about 1905" under Japanese jurisdiction. **January 18, 1952:** President Syngman Rhee declares "Peace Line" incorporating Dokdo—**three months before Peace Treaty takes effect** (April 28, 1952).

**June 8, 1948:** American B-29 bombers drop seventy-six 1,000-pound bombs during "routine training exercise," killing **16-30 Korean fishermen officially** (survivor testimony suggests 150-320 deaths). US apologizes and pays compensation to 33 claimants. **Content warning:** Casualty numbers highly disputed—official vs. survivor accounts differ by order of magnitude.

**July 12, 1953:** First armed incident—South Korean authorities fire on Japanese Coast Guard demanding Koreans leave; Japanese vessel damaged. **June 1954:** Korean government establishes permanent Coast Guard battalion—beginning of continuous South Korean physical occupation.

**September 1954:** Japan proposes ICJ referral—**rejected by South Korea** (October 28): "Dokdo was first territory victimized by Japanese aggression...serious doubt if Japan repeating same course." **March 1962:** Second ICJ proposal—rejected. **1952-1965:** Syngman Rhee Line enforcement: 328 Japanese ships seized, 3,929 arrested, 44 casualties.

**June 22, 1965:** Treaty on Basic Relations normalizes Japan-South Korea diplomatic relations. Syngman Rhee Line abolished but Dokdo/Takeshima unresolved—both sides agree to disagree.

**March 16, 2005:** Shimane Prefecture passes ordinance designating February 22 as "Takeshima Day" (100th anniversary of 1905 incorporation). Korean protestors slice off fingers, self-immolate outside Japanese embassy; 2.2 million commemorative stamps sell out in three hours. Major escalation transforming dormant issue into active dispute.

**August 10, 2012:** President Lee Myung-bak becomes **first South Korean president to visit Dokdo**, arriving by helicopter stating islands "worth staking our lives to defend." Japan recalls ambassador; shuttle diplomacy between leaders ceases for 10+ years. **August 2012:** Japan proposes third ICJ referral—**officially rejected August 30, 2012.**

**December 20, 2018:** **Radar lock-on incident**—South Korean destroyer allegedly directs fire-control radar at Japanese P-1 patrol aircraft near Noto Peninsula during North Korean vessel rescue. Major military incident straining relations (not directly over Dokdo but in context of broader tensions). **June 2024:** Both countries agree on steps to prevent recurrence without resolving facts.

**August 2019:** South Korea threatens to terminate GSOMIA (intelligence-sharing with Japan) amid trade dispute over forced labor compensation. After US pressure, agrees to continue in November 2019.

**March 16-17, 2023:** First bilateral summit in 10+ years—Yoon visits Tokyo, resuming shuttle diplomacy. **May 2023:** Kishida reciprocates with Seoul visit (first PM visit since 2018). Relations at warmest level in over decade despite domestic opposition. **August 18, 2023:** **Camp David Summit**—first standalone trilateral summit with US; establishes annual Leaders' Summits, multi-year military exercises, real-time missile warning data sharing, commitment to consult on threats.

**January 2024:** Japan includes Dokdo in tsunami advisory after major earthquake—Seoul lodges "stern protest." **May 14, 2024:** Cho Kuk (former Justice Minister, opposition leader) visits Dokdo, stating "Japan's claim is assertion that Japan's horrific wartime crimes were justified." Japan lodges strong protest before expected Japan-SK-China trilateral summit.

**April 8, 2025:** Japan's **Diplomatic Bluebook 2025** reiterates Takeshima "clearly inherent part of Japan's territory" and "South Korea has continued its illegal occupation." South Korean Foreign Ministry lodges strong protest; term "provocation" reappears in commentary after being omitted 2023-2024.

## Strategic significance: EEZ, fishing, resources, nationalism, and regional security

**The symbolic weight far exceeds practical value.** As noted above, confirmed fishing revenues total $94,000 annually from immediate Dokdo vicinity (broader disputed EEZ worth more). Gas hydrate reserves remain speculative, technologically unfeasible for 10-20 years minimum. Yet tourism generates $49 million annually—the islands' value is primarily symbolic rather than economic.

**For Koreans, Dokdo represents decolonization and restored sovereignty.** Korean government official position: Dokdo is "the symbol of restoration of Korea's sovereignty" and "first victim of Japan's aggression against the Korean Peninsula." The dispute "strikes at the heart of national identity." Survey data (2019): **84.5% of Koreans** cite "Dokdo issue" as main concern with Japan, ranking even higher than comfort women. **89% of South Koreans** (2005) said they "cannot trust Japan." Nearly 30% believe military conflict with Japan could eventually occur. Historical disputes with Japan increase presidential approval ratings by ~4.4 percentage points on average—politicians use nationalism strategically. Airport propaganda: "Every time you arrive in South Korea and take the train from the airport, there are videos talking about Dokdo."

**For Japanese, the issue represents legal order and post-war territorial integrity.** Conservative politicians use territorial disputes to strengthen political base and support military expansion. Shimane celebrates February 22 annually as "Takeshima Day" since 2005 (100th anniversary of incorporation). Survey data (2023): **56.7% of Japanese** cite "Takeshima issue" as concern about South Korea; **50.1%** cite "continued opposition on Takeshima." However, Japanese prioritize China relations and North Korea threat higher—territorial disputes rank lower in foreign policy priorities than for Koreans.

**The dispute directly undermines US strategic interests in Northeast Asia.** GSOMIA (General Security of Military Information Agreement), signed November 2016 as first military pact between Japan and South Korea, nearly collapsed in August 2019 amid territorial/historical tensions—South Korea gave 90-day notice of withdrawal. US Ambassador Harry Harris stated termination "affects the U.S. and our ability to defend Korea and puts our troops at risk." The dispute prevents South Korea from joining the Quad (Australia, India, Japan, US), weakens regional coalition against China's assertiveness. When Russian and Chinese aircraft entered disputed airspace above Dokdo (July 2019), Korean jets fired 360 warning shots, but Japan and Korea had conflicting interpretations, preventing unified response. China and Russia exploit lack of coordination.

**US efforts at Camp David (August 2023) compartmentalized the dispute.** The first standalone trilateral summit established annual leaders' meetings, "Freedom Edge" multi-domain military exercises, real-time missile warning data sharing, trilateral hotline for crisis communication, annual ministerial meetings. Biden administration frames trilateral cooperation as "backbone of U.S. alliances in Indo-Pacific," part of "latticework" with Quad and AUKUS. Yet the islands dispute remains completely unresolved—US maintains studied neutrality using "Liancourt Rocks" because taking sides would fracture alliance architecture.

**Regional precedent concerns center on island classification under UNCLOS.** The Senkaku/Diaoyu dispute (Japan-China) involves similar questions about whether features are "islands" (generating EEZ) or "rocks" (only 12nm territorial sea) under Article 121(3). Both disputes stem from 1951 San Francisco Peace Treaty ambiguity. If Japan ceded Dokdo/Takeshima, could "weaken its claims to islands it disputes with China." Japan aggressively claims even tiny Okinotorishima atoll generates 400,000 sq km EEZ; China argues it's "rocks" not entitled to EEZ. Similar debates affect South China Sea disputes. Korea's ICJ refusal creates precedent: stronger party can block adjudication, weakening "rules-based international order."

**ICJ precedents favor effective control over historical claims.** Pedra Branca (Malaysia-Singapore, 2008), Preah Vihear Temple (Cambodia-Thailand, 1962), Ligitan and Sipadan (Indonesia-Malaysia, 2002) all show ICJ tends to favor effective occupation and administrative actions. This pattern partly explains Korea's ICJ refusal despite physical control—concern that Japan's 1905 incorporation and pre-1945 administrative actions might prevail over historical claims, especially since ICJ doesn't heavily weigh colonial/imperial aggression context.

## Modern geopolitics: the 2023-2025 rapprochement amid frozen territorial conflict

**March-May 2023 marked a diplomatic breakthrough.** President Yoon Suk Yeol pursued improved relations with Japan despite 60% South Korean public opposition. First bilateral summit in 10+ years occurred March 16-17 when Yoon visited Tokyo; Kishida reciprocated with Seoul visit in May (first PM visit since 2018). Shuttle diplomacy resumed after over decade-long freeze following Lee Myung-bak's 2012 Dokdo visit. The warming came despite Yoon's domestic approval rating hitting historic low of 20%, partly due to his Japan policy.

**Security cooperation fully normalized.** GSOMIA (intelligence sharing) fully normalized March 2023 after 2019 near-termination. June 2023: military relations normalized at Shangri-La Dialogue, with 2018 radar lock-on incident formally resolved. Trilateral "Freedom Edge" multi-domain exercises established as annual events. Real-time missile warning data sharing became operational by end 2023. No military incidents occurred 2024-2025—both sides actively avoiding escalation.

**Economic ties remained strong.** 2024 trade: Japan exported $46.38B to South Korea; South Korea exported $29.6B to Japan. March 2023: trade dispute fully resolved—Japan lifted export controls, South Korea withdrew WTO complaint. May 2023: both countries restored each other to "whitelist" of trusted trading partners. Economic security cooperation focuses on semiconductors, batteries, supply chains. South Korea's exports to China fell 20% to 19.7% of total in 2023—first time below 20% in 20 years, indicating diversification toward US/Japan amid China concerns.

**Cultural exchange flourished.** 2023: 5.25 million South Koreans visited Japan (record); only 2.32 million Japanese visited South Korea. K-pop and dramas remain popular in Japan; Japanese anime and manga popular in South Korea. Despite cultural exchange, deeper people-to-people ties remain limited. Japan has 35% "never travelers"—highest rate globally.

**The Dokdo dispute remained completely unresolved but compartmentalized.** July 2024: South Korea urged Japan to rescind territorial claims in defense white paper. August 2024: opposition accused Yoon of "erasing Dokdo" when his Liberation Day speech focused on freedom and reunification without mentioning Japan's colonial violence or Dokdo. January 2024: Seoul lodged "stern protest" when Tokyo included Dokdo in tsunami advisory after major earthquake. May 14, 2024: Cho Kuk's visit prompted Japan to lodge strong protest through diplomatic channels before expected trilateral summit. Dispute largely compartmentalized from economic/security cooperation—diplomatic protests continue but don't derail broader relationship.

**Camp David institutionalized trilateral cooperation despite territorial tensions.** The August 18, 2023 summit at Camp David—first-ever standalone trilateral meeting, first foreign leaders summit at Camp David under Biden—produced "Camp David Principles," "Commitment to Consult" to coordinate responses to threats, annual Leaders' Summits, multi-year trilateral military exercise plan, trilateral hotline. NSC Advisor Kurt Campbell emphasized working to make cooperation "enduring feature of American policy" beyond current leaders. November 2024: Trilateral Coordinating Secretariat established.

**North Korea cooperation improved.** In 2022, North Korea conducted 38 ballistic missile launches. October 2024: North Korea deployed thousands of troops to Russia/Ukraine. Improved trilateral ties enabled direct intelligence sharing, coordinated sanctions, joint exercises. Challenges remain: different threat perceptions; potential leadership changes could reverse gains; South Korean progressive president in 2027 could undo rapprochement.

**China emerged as shared security concern beyond North Korea focus.** Camp David statements directly referenced China and Taiwan Strait—major shift. Concerns include Chinese military exercises near Taiwan, economic coercion, South China Sea aggression. Risk: China-Russia-North Korea trilateral forming in response. Economic security focus: semiconductors, quantum computing, AI, supply chains. Challenge: China remains South Korea's largest trading partner—Seoul balances security alignment with US/Japan against economic ties with Beijing.

**Public opinion data reveals asymmetric intensity.** 2023 EAI-Genron NPO Survey: **84.5% of Koreans** cite "Dokdo issue" as main concern with Japan; **77%** cite "inadequate repentance over history"; **70.3%** want "territorial dispute over Dokdo" addressed in bilateral talks. In Japan: **56.7%** cite "Takeshima issue" as concern about South Korea; **50.1%** cite "continued opposition on Takeshima." But 2023 marked first time in decade that Japanese with favorable impression of South Korea outnumbered unfavorable—attributed to cultural exchange (K-pop, dramas) and improved government relations. Military threat perception remains asymmetric: **38.3% of Koreans** (2019) view Japan as military threat vs. only **12.3% of Japanese** viewing South Korea as threat.

**International community maintains strict neutrality.** No countries have expressed support for either side. UN has no involvement—no Security Council or General Assembly resolutions, no Secretary-General mediation attempts. Issue considered bilateral dispute outside UN mandate. Both Japan and South Korea are major economies, and both are US allies—taking sides damages relations with one. Most countries have their own territorial disputes and avoid creating precedents. Academic consensus leans toward South Korea having stronger claim—Prof. Jon M. Van Dyke (University of Hawaii): "Korea's claim substantially stronger than Japan's" based on proximity (88km from Ulleungdo vs. 158km from Oki Islands), historical evidence, colonial context of 1905 incorporation, effective control since 1952, post-WWII intent to strip Japan of colonial acquisitions. But governments maintain neutrality regardless of academic assessments.

**The April 2025 Diplomatic Bluebook continues the pattern.** Japan's annual Diplomatic Bluebook (April 8, 2025) reiterated Takeshima is "clearly an inherent part of Japan's territory" and claimed "South Korea has continued its illegal occupation." South Korean Foreign Ministry lodged strong protest; term "provocation" reappeared in commentary after being omitted in 2023-2024 under Yoon administration. ## Comparison to Turkey-Greece Aegean islands: similar alliance dilemmas, different conflict intensity

**Both disputes divide allied democracies and involve post-colonial/post-imperial grievances.** Turkey-Greece are both NATO members; Japan-South Korea are both US allies. Turkey-Greece dispute traces to Ottoman Empire collapse and 1923 Lausanne Treaty; Dokdo/Takeshima traces to Japanese colonization and 1951 San Francisco Treaty. Both involve treaty ambiguity, small islands with outsized strategic/emotional importance, EEZ implications for maritime resources, and nationalist fuel making domestic compromise politically costly.

**Key similarity: militarization despite cooperation.** Greece militarized Aegean islands after 1974 Cyprus crisis despite demilitarization treaties (1923 Lausanne, 1947 Paris). South Korea militarized Dokdo with Coast Guard despite Japan's protests. Both disputes involve military presence and periodic standoffs, yet both pairs cooperate in broader alliance frameworks.

**Critical difference: conflict intensity.** Turkey-Greece nearly went to war in 1987 and 1996 (Imia/Kardak crisis); fighter jet confrontations and naval standoffs are routine; military build-up ongoing. Dokdo/Takeshima has no serious military risk—only Coast Guard personnel, no direct military threat, status quo accepted as essentially permanent. Turkey-Greece tensions are ESCALATING (2020-2022 major crises over energy exploration); Japan-South Korea are DE-ESCALATING (2023-2025 rapprochement despite symbolic disputes).

**Geographic scope differs dramatically.** Turkey-Greece involves hundreds of islands across entire Aegean Sea in multiple categories (territorial waters, continental shelf, airspace, "grey zones" of disputed sovereignty). Dokdo/Takeshima involves just two small islets totaling 19 hectares—singular focus point.

**Resource economics differ.** Aegean Sea has massive proven natural gas deposits—potential 70-90 trillion cubic feet in Greek waters; 2020 tensions over Turkish seismic vessel Oruç Reis near Kastellorizo involved real energy stakes. Dokdo area has only speculative gas hydrates (not yet proven, technologically unfeasible) and modest fishing ($94,000 annually immediate vicinity).

**Third-party involvement differs.** Turkey-Greece involves Libya, Egypt, Israel, Cyprus (divided since 1974), EU, France—multilateral complexity. Dokdo/Takeshima remains strictly bilateral—US maintains neutral stance, no other countries involved.

**What makes Dokdo/Takeshima unique:** Inseparable from colonial legacy in Korean mind—historical grievances matter more than economic value. South Korea's effective control is essentially irreversible—Japan has tacitly accepted this while maintaining legal claims for domestic politics. Dispute hasn't significantly worsened militarily in 70+ years despite periodic flare-ups. Pop culture paradox: extensive cultural exchange coexists with unresolved dispute. Asymmetric priority: matters much more to Koreans (84.5% cite as concern) than Japanese (56.7%). South Korea refuses ICJ—unique among democracies with effective control.

**Both likely remain unresolved indefinitely.** Common factors: domestic political utility (leaders gain from hard line), historical identity formation (disputes central to national narratives), zero-sum perception (viewed as win-lose not win-win), legal ambiguity (no clear international law answer), alliance constraints (prevent war but remove urgency for resolution), media amplification (nationalist sentiment spreads rapidly), no crisis forcing resolution (economic costs manageable). Resolution appears neither necessary nor achievable—managed through crisis prevention rather than solved.
