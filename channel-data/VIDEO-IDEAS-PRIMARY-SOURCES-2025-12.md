## Top Picks (Ancient/Medieval Sources)

### 🥇 TIER 1: High Volume + Strong Primary Sources

#### 1. The Diary of Merer (Pyramid Construction)
- **Search Terms:** "How were the pyramids built" / "Pyramid construction"
- **Primary Source:** Diary of Merer (Papyrus Jarf) - c. 2560 BC
- **The Myth:** Slaves built the pyramids (or aliens)
- **The Reality:** Oldest papyrus ever found - a logistics spreadsheet from construction manager Merer detailing limestone deliveries and paid workforce
- **Hook:** "Movies tell us slaves built the pyramids. But in 2013, we found the actual logbook of a construction manager. He wasn't whipping slaves; he was managing a paid payroll."
- **Visual:** The papyrus itself - looks like an ancient Excel spreadsheet
- **VidIQ Assessment:** Highest viral potential - contradicts biggest myth in human history

#### 2. Viking Law Code (Grágás)
- **Search Terms:** "Viking society" / "Viking culture"
- **Primary Source:** Grágás (The Grey Goose Laws) - oral tradition recorded c. 1117 AD
- **The Myth:** Vikings were lawless raiders/berserkers
- **The Reality:** Obsessively litigious society with detailed legal codes for everything including insults
- **Hook:** "We think of Vikings as lawless raiders. But their society was actually more litigious than modern America. Here is the legal code they used to sue each other over bad poetry."
- **Visual:** Manuscript pages showing specific fines (e.g., calling a man "beardless")
- **Channel Fit:** High - legal/forensic angle, builds on existing Birka content

#### 3. Crusader Constitution (Assizes of Jerusalem)
- **Search Terms:** "Kingdom of Jerusalem" / "Crusades"
- **Primary Source:** Assizes of Jerusalem (Livre au Roi) - c. 12th-13th century
- **The Myth:** Crusader states were religious chaos/constant warfare
- **The Reality:** Constitutional monarchy with complex legal code limiting royal power
- **Hook:** "The Kingdom of Jerusalem wasn't a religious anarchy. It was a constitutional monarchy with a legal code so complex it makes the US Constitution look like a napkin doodle."
- **Visual:** Constitutional text, court system diagrams
- **Channel Fit:** High - territorial/legal niche, state-building angle

#### 4. Bald's Leechbook (Medieval Medicine)
- **Search Terms:** "Medieval medicine" / "Dark Ages medicine"
- **Primary Source:** Bald's Leechbook - c. 9th century AD (Anglo-Saxon)
- **The Myth:** Medieval doctors just used leeches and superstition
- **The Reality:** Contains eye salve recipe that killed MRSA superbugs when recreated by Nottingham University in 2015
- **Hook:** "We joke that medieval doctors just used leeches. But this 9th-century manuscript contains a recipe that kills modern superbugs better than our best antibiotics."
- **Visual:** Manuscript page with the specific recipe
- **Modern Relevance:** Antibiotic resistance crisis

#### 5. Spartan Police State
- **Search Terms:** "Sparta" / "Spartan warriors"
- **Primary Source:** Thucydides Book IV (Pylos Campaign) + Great Rhetra fragments (Plutarch/Tyrtaeus) - c. 425 BC / 7th century BC
- **The Myth:** Sparta was a warrior paradise of super-soldiers
- **The Reality:** Paranoid police state terrified of Helot slave revolts; foreign policy paralyzed by internal fear
- **Hook:** "Pop culture says Sparta was a warrior paradise. Primary sources reveal it was actually a paranoid police state terrified of its own slaves. Here is the text of the annual declaration of war they made against their own farmers."
- **Note:** Sources are secondary texts (Thucydides, Plutarch) - less "show the document" potential

### 🥈 TIER 2: Medieval Legal/Social Records

#### 6. Peasants Suing Lords (Court Rolls)
- **Primary Source:** Manorial Court Rolls - c. 1290 AD
- **The Myth:** Lords had absolute power; peasants had no legal recourse
- **The Reality:** Peasants could drag lords to court and WIN via custom law
- **Hook:** "Here's what people assume: The Lord's word was law. Here is a court record from 1290 where a peasant drags his Lord to court."
- **Visual Challenge:** Faded Latin manuscripts - need zoom/highlight technique
- **VidIQ Assessment:** #1 pick for channel audience (loves disputes/legal frameworks)

#### 7. Medieval CSI (Coroner's Rolls)
- **Primary Source:** Medieval Coroner's Rolls (London) - c. 1276 AD
- **The Myth:** Medieval justice was trial by combat or religious superstition
- **The Reality:** Detailed forensic reports - wound depth, weapon type, witness testimony
- **Hook:** "People think medieval murder investigations didn't exist. Here is an actual government report from 1276 detailing a crime scene."
- **Visual Strategy:** Overlay with terrain maps, weapon sketches
- **VidIQ Assessment:** "True Crime" mechanics work historically

#### 8. Roman Female Business Owners (Inscriptions)
- **Primary Source:** Latin Funerary Inscriptions & Pompeii Shop Signs - 1st century AD
- **The Myth:** Roman women stayed in the domus with no financial power
- **The Reality:** Stone carvings naming women as "negotiatrix" (merchant) and brick factory owners
- **Hook:** "Here's the assumption: Roman women had zero financial rights. Here is a 1st-century shop sign from Pompeii naming the woman who owned it."
- **Visual:** Carved stone inscriptions - high contrast, authoritative
- **Framing:** Economic history, not social history (better CTR for male audience)

#### 9. Brehon Law (Irish Divorce Rights)
- **Primary Source:** Brehon Law Manuscripts (Old Irish) - c. 700 AD
- **The Myth:** The "Dark Ages" were uniformly oppressive for women everywhere
- **The Reality:** Detailed divorce rights and property retention in early medieval Ireland
- **Hook:** "Here's the myth: The Dark Ages stripped women of rights. Here is an Irish law book from 700 AD explaining exactly how a woman can divorce her husband."
- **Angle:** Anglo-centric history ignores Celtic legal systems

#### 10. Egyptian Will of Naunakhte
- **Primary Source:** The Will of Naunakhte (Papyrus) - c. 1100 BC
- **The Myth:** Ancient women were property, they didn't own property
- **The Reality:** 3,000-year-old legal contract where a woman distributes her estate and disinherits a lazy daughter
- **Hook:** "People assume ancient women couldn't own anything. Here is a 3,000-year-old legal contract where a woman disinherits her lazy daughter."

- **Hook:** "The assumption is that ancient people couldn't read. Here is a birthday party invitation sent between two army wives in 100 AD."

#### 12. Pompeii Graffiti

- **The Myth:** We only know what emperors thought

- **Assessment:** Good but feels like trivia rather than paradigm shift

#### 13. Medieval Guild Records (Female Masters)

- **The Myth:** Guilds were "brotherhoods" - women couldn't be masters
- **The Reality:** Ledgers listing "Magistra" and women paying dues
- **Note:** Could combine with Roman women topic for "women in historical economies" video

#### 14. Manumission Rolls (Buying Freedom)
- **Primary Source:** Manumission Rolls & Borough Court Records - c. 1200 AD
- **The Myth:** Born a peasant, die a peasant - no social mobility
- **The Reality:** Receipts showing peasants buying their freedom
- **Assessment:** Similar to "Suing Lords" but less conflict/action

## Production Notes

### The "Boring Document" Problem
For text-heavy manuscripts (Court Rolls, Law Codes), use this technique:

1. Show full document (0:45) → "This looks like a boring tax list."
2. Zoom 200% → "But look at line 14."
3. High-Contrast Overlay → Invert colors of just that line (white text on black)
4. Translation → Fade in English directly over the handwriting

### Retention Strategy
- Primary source must appear on screen by 0:45
- Don't spend 2 minutes on the myth - state it in 10 seconds
- Immediately cut to document: "Here is the document that proves that wrong."
